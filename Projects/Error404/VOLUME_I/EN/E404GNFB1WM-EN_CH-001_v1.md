# Chapter One. Error 404

That evening, Vlad prayed not because he believed.

That would have been too beautiful.

He prayed because all other methods had already run out. First, the reasonable arguments ran out. Then the money. Then the strength. Then the usual masculine ability to tell himself, "It’s fine, we’ll get through it," although inside, no one had been getting through anything for a long time. Someone was simply sitting among the wreckage and pretending it was only temporary furniture rearrangement.

By midnight, the apartment had gone quiet.

Not that good kind of quiet in which people with a clean conscience, paid rent, and no new messages from the bank get to rest. No. This was another kind of quiet — sticky, watchful, almost administrative. The kind you find in hospital corridors, empty stairwells, and the heads of people who have held themselves together too long and now no longer know whom to present the bill to.

Vlad sat in the kitchen.

In front of him stood a cup of tea, long cooled into the state of a philosophical reproach. On the table lay his phone, a notebook, a pen, several bills, and one small cross on a thin chain. He did not wear the cross. Not out of principle. It simply had not worked out somehow. It lay in a drawer together with batteries, old keys, and other objects whose meaning had once been obvious and then quietly died.

Today Vlad had taken it out.

Without knowing why.

Perhaps to strengthen the appeal.

Or to show that he had not come empty-handed. Like in an institution: here is the application, here are the documents, here is the evidence, here is a small religious object confirming that the applicant attempted to cooperate with the system.

People have strange logic in moments of despair. As long as everything is more or less bearable, they talk about free will, maturity, psychological boundaries, and the need to take responsibility. But let life carefully place a knee on their chest, and a person suddenly remembers all the ancient communication protocols: candles, crosses, prayers, promises, threats, bargaining, tears, "Lord, please," and other forms of spiritual technical support.

Vlad looked at the cross.

Then at the bills.

Then back at the cross.

The cross looked more honest.

The bills did too, but in another sense.

He sighed and ran a hand over his face. The face was tired. Not old, not broken, not tragic — simply the face of a man who had spent too long explaining to himself that everything was going normally, and had finally stopped believing himself.

"Well then," he said into the emptiness. "Let’s do this like adults."

The emptiness did not object.

That was a good sign. Or a bad one. It is difficult to correspond with emptiness in general: it rarely clarifies its position.

Vlad did not know a single prayer in full. He had heard something in childhood. Later, he had seen things in films. Then a couple of times he had been present in church, where everyone around him confidently performed movements whose meaning was about as clear to him as the settings of an industrial machine in Japanese.

He crossed himself.

Awkwardly.

Too quickly.

Then again, more slowly, so it would not look entirely like the gesture of a desperate user before a frozen computer.

"Lord," he said.

And fell silent.

The word turned out to be heavier than he expected. Not exalted, not luminous, not solemn. Just heavy. As if he had spoken the name of someone he had not written to for too long, and now had suddenly come not with love, but with a complaint.

He gave a crooked smile.

"A beautiful beginning. Spiritual maturity at its finest. Straight in with a grievance."

He wanted to say something proper. Something noble. For example: "Thy will be done." Or: "Teach me to accept." Or: "Show me the way."

But something entirely different rose inside him.

Not a prayer.

More like an application.

Not even an application — a formal complaint.

And the longer he remained silent, the more clearly he understood: if he started speaking beautifully now, he would lie from the very first word. And he was tired of lying. Especially to the place where, in theory, everything was visible anyway.

"I tried to live properly," he finally said.

The phrase sounded pathetic.

He winced.

"No, not like that."

He got up, paced around the kitchen, returned, and sat down again.

"I tried to live properly. I endured. I tried. I pretended I understood. I even crossed myself."

He looked at the cross on the table.

It lay there silently and did not help in any way.

"And now please explain to me: why does none of this work?"

After those words, it became very quiet.

Even the refrigerator, which usually hummed with the persistence of an old prophet, fell silent for several seconds. A car passed outside. Somewhere upstairs, someone dropped something heavy and then said a short word not intended for liturgical use.

Vlad waited.

He did not know for what exactly.

A voice from heaven? Light? A sign? Inner warmth? That very feeling written about by people who have survived an encounter with the higher, and then for some reason sell a course for ninety-nine euros?

Nothing happened.

Of course.

He gave a crooked smile.

"Right. That’s what I thought."

He leaned back in his chair and felt anger return to its rightful place.

"Wonderful," he said, louder now. "So suffering — please. Enduring — please. Pretending that everything has meaning — please. But the moment an answer is needed, immediately silence. Very convenient."

The emptiness again did not object.

"I’m not asking for miracles, by the way," Vlad said. "Not anymore. I just want to understand by what logic all this works. Or at least who is responsible here for quality of service."

He heard himself say it and winced.

But he no longer wanted to stop.

"I pay for this life with my suffering. Can anyone issue me a receipt?"

And at that moment, the phone on the table vibrated.

Vlad jerked so sharply he nearly swept the cup off the table. The screen lit up.

New notification.

Sender undefined.

Subject: Response to appeal

For several seconds Vlad stared at the screen.

Then he raised his eyes to the ceiling.

"Seriously?"

The ceiling was ordinary. White, with a small crack near the lamp. No angels. No trumpets. No radiance. Only a crack resembling a thin river that had long ago decided to leave this apartment first.

The phone vibrated again.

A message appeared on the screen:

Your appeal has been registered.

Vlad did not move.

He read the line once.

Then a second time.

Then a third, more slowly, as if the speed of reading could change the content.

Below it, the next line appeared:

Case number: 777-404-13.

He blinked.

"No."

The phone, apparently, disagreed, because the message continued:

Please do not create duplicate appeals.
This will not speed up the verification of your soul’s algorithms.

Vlad put the phone down.

Then picked it up again.

Then put it down again.

Stood up.

Sat down.

Looked at the tea.

The tea, like any honest witness, explained nothing.

"Right," Vlad said. "This is either a nervous breakdown or spam on a new level."

He took the phone and tapped the notification.

The screen went dark for a moment.

Then it turned white.

In the center appeared the words:

Error 404: God Not Found

Below, in small print:

Possible causes:

1. Invalid address of appeal.
2. Request sent to the wrong addressee.
3. User is searching for God outside the zone of internal access.
4. Requesting subject demands an answer without confirming his own existence.

Vlad felt everything inside turn cold.

Not frightened.

Cold exactly.

That is what happens when reality suddenly takes one step aside, and you see that there is no wall behind it. Only a service passage, poorly lit, with a sign reading: "Unauthorized persons may enter, but they will regret it."

He slowly set the phone on the table.

"What does ‘without confirming his own existence’ mean?"

The phone answered immediately.

To continue, select appeal category:

1. Pain.
2. Injustice.
3. Why me?
4. Why me again?
5. I understand everything, but can we do it without consequences?
6. Other.

Vlad closed his eyes.

Opened them.

The options did not disappear.

He pinched his arm.

It hurt.

So either this was not a dream, or it was a dream with good detail.

"And if I don’t want to choose?"

A new line appeared on the screen:

Unwillingness to choose will also be registered as a choice.

Vlad laughed quietly.

The laugh came out wrong. Dry. Short. The kind of laugh people give when they are suddenly informed that their personal tragedy fits perfectly into a general administrative procedure.

"Wonderful," he said. "Even God has bureaucracy."

The answer came instantly:

Bureaucracy was created by humans.
We are merely forced to use an interface you understand.

Vlad stopped laughing.

That was already too much.

He leaned closer to the screen.

"Who is writing this?"

A pause.

Then:

Temporary access operator.

"Name?"

Names are used when distinction is necessary.
In your case, it is premature.

"What do you mean, premature?"

You do not yet distinguish even yourself.

Vlad leaned back in his chair.

The kitchen was the same as before. Cup, notebook, bills, cross, phone. Everything in place. But the air had changed. It had become denser, as if someone invisible had entered the room and had not introduced himself because he was already at home.

"Listen," Vlad said carefully. "If this is a joke, it’s sick."

Confirmed.

"What is confirmed?"

The joke is sick. The world as well. Please continue.

Vlad ran a hand through his hair.

"I want to understand what is happening."

Your appeal has been redirected to the section "Understanding".

"No. Stop. That is not what I meant."

Registered: user employs words in a meaning different from the one in which he wishes to be understood. Standard error of prayer appeal.

Vlad stared at the screen.

"Are you mocking me?"

No.
We are responding in an accessible form.

"This is an accessible form?"

Judging by your reaction, yes.

He wanted to throw the phone. Not hard. Just toss it onto the sofa, turn away, tell himself it was time to sleep, that tomorrow everything would be explained: app glitch, virus, idiotic prank, exhaustion, overheated brain, anything at all. Humans are very fond of the phrase "anything at all" when they are afraid to name the obvious.

But his finger hovered over the screen on its own.

The menu was still glowing there.

Pain.
Injustice.
Why me?
Why me again?
I understand everything, but can we do it without consequences?
Other.

Vlad looked at the option "Why me?"

There was something humiliatingly precise in it.

Not "what is the meaning of suffering." Not "how is evil structured." Not "why is the world imperfect." No. Everything was much more honest. Simpler. Smaller. More human.

Why me?

Here was the true center of most prayers.

Not God.

Not truth.

Not salvation.

But a small, offended, exhausted "I" standing in the middle of a burning world and still asking not "what is happening?" but "why is this happening specifically to me?"

Vlad tapped the third option.

The screen went dark.

The light in the apartment flickered.

The refrigerator began humming again, but now its hum sounded like a distant mechanism somewhere beneath the floor.

A new message appeared on the screen:

Category selected: "Why me?"

Transferring appeal to Window No. 7.

Please wait.

Vlad exhaled.

"How long do I wait?"

The answer did not appear immediately.

For three seconds, nothing happened.

Then the screen softly lit up.

Average waiting time: eternity.

Vlad closed his eyes.

"Of course."

However, an expedited procedure is provided for users in a state of partial despair.

He opened his eyes.

"And what do I need to do?"

Admit that you did not come for God.

Vlad stared at that sentence for a long time.

Then said quietly:

"Then what did I come for?"

Only one line appeared on the screen:

That is what will be reviewed.

The kitchen light flickered again.

Somewhere deep inside the wall, behind pipes, behind concrete, behind all the layers of the building, there came a short sound. Not a knock. Not a creak. Not the rush of water.

More like a click.

As if somewhere a small window had been opened.

The phone vibrated one last time.

Your number in the queue: 1.

Vlad wanted to smile crookedly, but could not.

The screen displayed:

Please proceed to Window No. 7.

"What window now?" he whispered.

And then the kitchen window, the one facing the courtyard, slowly darkened.

Outside there was no longer the neighboring building, no streetlamp, no parked cars.

Only a narrow corridor.

Gray.

Long.

With a sign on the far wall:

WINDOW NO. 7
COMPLAINTS UNDER THE SECTION "WHY ME?"

Vlad sat motionless.

The cross on the table glinted quietly in the light of the phone.

Then the screen went dark.

And in the darkness of the kitchen, a calm, polite voice sounded:

"Next."
