# Chapter Two. Window No. 7

The corridor was longer than it had looked from the kitchen.

Vlad had been walking for several minutes already, though he could not hear his steps — the floor was covered with something soft, gray, and indifferent, like the carpeting in government offices, replaced once every thirty years and only when it begins asking for it itself. The walls were gray too, with thin horizontal stripes, as if they had been built from layers of other people’s expectations, pressed into sheets. Long lamps shone beneath the ceiling — evenly, shadowlessly, with the dead conscientiousness only fluorescent light in non-residential spaces can produce.

It was warm. Not cozy. Precisely warm — in the sense that it was not cold, and that was all that could be said in favor of the air temperature.

Somewhere ahead came a low hum. Not mechanical. More human — dense, background noise, like the murmur of a queue that had learned to wait and stopped being surprised by it.

Vlad stopped.

Before him was a hall.

Large. Very large — so large that the opposite wall dissolved into the whitish haze of distance, or perhaps simply did not exist, and the hall went on into places the eye could not reach. Rows of plastic chairs — blue, like in every waiting room in the world where people have time to change their minds — stretched inward in disciplined lines. The chairs were occupied.

There were many people.

Vlad stopped at the entrance and looked.

Different people. Completely different. A young man in an expensive coat, sitting straight, as if posture could help under the circumstances. A woman of about fifty with a shopping bag on her knees — she held it with both hands, the way one holds something alive that might run away. A teenager in headphones that clearly did not work, but which he had no intention of removing. An old man, sleeping or pretending to — Vlad was not sure, because sleepers and philosophers of a certain age have the same expression. A woman in a business suit, not yet having understood that here it meant nothing. A man in sweatpants, who had already understood it and therefore looked calmer than everyone else.

No one looked at anyone else.

People sat beside one another, but not together. Each was locked inside his own posture, his own waiting, his own “why.” Their faces were turned forward, downward, into bags, into palms, into emptiness. Sometimes someone moved their lips. Sometimes someone uttered a phrase. But the phrases did not fly toward listeners. They left people and immediately sank into the air, dense and soft as gray batting.

Vlad felt irritation.

Not fear.

Precisely irritation.

Even here, a queue.

Even here, one could not simply arrive normally, humanly. A person reaches the final point, opens an incomprehensible corridor from his own kitchen, ends up God knows where, and is still offered a seat, a wait, and preferably not to create unnecessary noise.

Of course, he had not expected a personal reception.

Naturally, he had not.

But somewhere inside, in the most indecent corner of his soul, that was exactly what he had expected.

On the wall near the entrance hung an electronic display.

Now serving: 31 204.

Vlad lowered his eyes to the phone he was still holding.

Your number: 1.

He looked at the display.

Then at the phone.

Then back at the display.

"Wait," he said aloud.

His voice left him, traveled a short distance, and disappeared. It did not echo. It did not return. It attracted no attention. It was as if someone had placed it between layers of thick gray cotton and carefully left it there.

No one turned around.

In the waiting hall, apparently, conversations with the air were not considered interesting.

The phone vibrated.

*Number 1 is the sequence number within your category of appeals.*

*The general queue for the category “Why me?” consists of 4,109,887 people.*

*Your appeal is not urgent. Urgency was indicated independently by the applicant.*

*We apologize for the inconvenience.*

Vlad slowly put the phone into his pocket.

Then took it out again.

*We apologize* — he reread it once more, not because he had failed to understand it the first time, but because he wanted to make sure that this was exactly what was written.

"For the inconvenience," he repeated quietly.

A woman of about thirty-five sat down on the chair beside him. She put a bag on her knees, took out a thermos and a sandwich wrapped in cling film. Her movements were precise, habitual, as if she had done this many times before and had long since stopped hoping that today it would not be necessary.

"Are you here for long?" Vlad asked.

The question sank almost at once.

The woman did not look at him. She unscrewed the thermos cap, blew on the steam, and said:

"Hot things run out quickly if you wait without a lid."

Vlad turned his head toward her.

The phrase was not an answer. But for some reason, it answered.

"Have you been here before?" he asked.

The woman bit into the sandwich. She looked straight ahead, where there was nothing but rows of chairs and identical light.

"The first time I didn’t bring a thermos," she said. "Then I understood."

She fell silent so naturally, as if she had not spoken at all.

For several more seconds Vlad looked at her, waiting for at least a glance, a confirmation, some trace of conversation.

Nothing.

The woman chewed. The thermos stood between her knees. The bag lay on her lap, pressed down by both hands, like something alive that might run away.

He sat down.

The chair was the same as everywhere. Plastic. With a slight backward tilt that made it impossible to sit upright and feel like a respected person at the same time. The designers of these chairs had obviously understood something important about the nature of waiting.

Vlad looked around.

By the far wall — the one that did exist after all, though very far away — there were windows. Not windows in the usual sense. More like service counters. Frosted glass, narrow slots for documents, small signs. Most of the signs read: “Temporarily unavailable.” One: “Break.” Another: “Technical reasons.” And one, the farthest, was lit: “Window No. 7.”

A living queue stretched toward it — separate, not connected to the chairs. People stood calmly, without pushing, with that particular expression people get when they have long since moved beyond hope for a quick resolution and are simply waiting because there is nowhere else to go.

Vlad stood up and went there.

Not because he had resigned himself.

Simply because if he had been dragged here, he intended at least to find out who was responsible for this outrage.

---

The queue moved.

Slowly, but it moved — which in itself offered a certain consolation.

Vlad stood and looked at the people ahead of him. They did not speak to one another. Not because silence was mandatory — simply because each had brought enough of his own not to need anyone else’s.

In front of him stood a man of about forty-five, wearing a jacket with the logo of a construction company. His neck was tanned. His hands were folded in front of him — not prayerfully, simply so he had somewhere to put them. He looked ahead.

"Have you been standing long?" Vlad asked.

The man did not turn around.

The question fell through between them. First there was a voice, then a soft deaf place, then nothing.

The man continued looking toward Window No. 7.

"At first I thought it would end on its own," he said.

Vlad froze.

It was not an answer to the question.

And yet it was one.

"In the ordinary sense," Vlad said, more quietly now, as if testing whether one could use a voice here at all.

The man shifted his shoulders slightly, but did not turn.

"Then not long ago. My son died. Three years ago. Eighteen years old."

Vlad said nothing.

His own ready-made phrase, already almost on his tongue, quietly stepped back and pretended it had never been there.

The man spoke forward, into the frosted glass, not to Vlad and not to the queue.

"I didn’t come here right away. At first I thought I would manage. Then I thought I wouldn’t manage, but I would hold on. Then I stopped thinking. And then — here."

He gave the slightest nod toward the window.

Vlad wanted to ask what he wanted to hear there, behind the glass.

He did not have time.

"I don’t want to ask anything," the man said. "I want there to be somewhere to come. That’s all."

The queue moved forward by one person.

Vlad stood and thought that his own question — “why does none of this work” — had suddenly become a little lighter. It had not disappeared. It had simply changed weight in comparison with the question beside it. This was an unpleasant discovery, because he had come here not for perspective, but for an explanation.

And certainly not so that his personal tragedy could suddenly turn out to be standing in a common corridor next to someone else’s.

The queue nudged him from behind.

He turned around.

Behind him stood a girl of about twenty-five. Disheveled, in an oversized sweater, with shadows under the eyes of a person who does not sleep not because she is busy, but because she cannot. In her hands was a crumpled sheet of paper. She unfolded it, looked at it, crumpled it again.

"Did you write something?" Vlad asked.

The girl did not raise her eyes.

Whether his voice reached her or not was impossible to know.

"If I write ‘I’m not guilty,’" she said into the paper, "they’ll ask who is."

Vlad felt an unpleasant chill pass down his back.

"I only asked..."

She smoothed the sheet against her knee again.

"I’m trying to formulate it. So it’s right. So it’s clear."

Vlad tightened his grip on the phone in his hand.

"They’ll probably accept it anyway here."

The girl crumpled the sheet again.

"I know. But I want it to be exact. So they won’t ask again."

She said it as if she were answering not him, but the one inside her who had already returned the application for revision a thousand times.

Vlad turned away.

Now he began to understand: people did not talk here.

Here, everyone spoke to themselves.

It was just that sometimes someone else’s “to themselves” landed exactly inside your question.

And sometimes it missed.

But even when it missed, it still touched.

And his question was his question.

Even if four million such questions lay here, that still did not make his question any less his.

Vlad stubbornly held on to that thought.

It was small, but convenient.

---

Up close, Window No. 7 looked modest.

Frosted glass. A narrow slot. On the side, a call button that had clearly been pressed often: the plastic around it had been worn down to white. Above the window hung a sign written in small, even type:

*Window No. 7. Complaints under the section “Why me?”*

*Related appeals also accepted:*

*“Why exactly like this,” “Why exactly now,” “Why exactly this much.”*

*Appeals of the “Why at all” type should be directed to Window No. 12 (temporarily unavailable).*

Vlad stood before it.

Behind the glass was a shadow — indistinct, as happens when one looks through frosted glass and tries to see more than the construction has provided for.

"Next," said the voice. Polite. Genderless. The kind of voice used by systems capable of imitating concern without experiencing it.

Vlad bent toward the slot.

"Good evening."

"Appeal category registered," the voice said. "‘Why me?’ State the context."

"Meaning?"

"Why me — in what sense? ‘Why did misfortune befall me specifically?’ ‘Why did I specifically have to go through this?’ ‘Why me and not someone else?’ ‘Why at this particular period of life?’ Please specify."

Vlad was silent for a moment.

"Probably... all at once."

"A complex appeal requires an additional form."

"Fine. Give me the form."

"The form will be sent to your address."

"What address?"

"Internal."

"What does that mean?"

The voice held a pause that elsewhere might have seemed human.

"The address at which you receive what you do not want to realize."

Vlad straightened.

Then bent toward the slot again.

"Listen. I just want to understand why life is arranged the way it is. Why a person tries and receives — who knows what. Why silence. Why injustice. Why I tried and it didn’t work. Can’t that be called a specific topic?"

"It can," the voice said. "It is called ‘standard category A appeal.’ The most common one."

Vlad blinked.

"The most common?"

"Yes."

"So for you this is just... a typical complaint?"

"We do not have simple complaints," the voice said. "We have recurring ones."

"Very comforting."

"Comfort is not included in the function of this window."

"And what is included?"

"Registration, sorting, and redirection."

"And who will answer?"

"Please specify: answer, or confirm your version?"

Vlad fell silent.

Behind him someone said:

"I didn’t ask for much."

The voice was male, old or simply very tired.

No one reacted.

The phrase hung for a second and dissolved into the cottony air.

"Answer," Vlad said.

"Then the appeal has been accepted."

"And what now?"

"Await review."

"How long?"

A pause.

"That depends on when the applicant stops waiting for an answer and begins asking another question."

"What other question?"

"That is already the next window."

Vlad looked at the frosted glass.

Behind it was the shadow. Calm, even, in no hurry.

"Do you answer questions at all?" he asked.

"We register, sort, and redirect," the voice said. "Answering is not our function."

"Whose function is it?"

Silence. Long. Almost respectful.

Somewhere in the queue, a woman said softly:

"If he had called then, I wouldn’t have left."

Then everything became even again.

"That is a good question," the voice finally said. "Write it down. It will be useful to you later."

The slot closed.

Above the sign, a small red lamp blinked quietly and went out again. Perhaps it meant something. Perhaps not.

Behind him, someone coughed delicately.

Not at him.

Simply inside their own waiting.

Vlad stepped away from the window.

---

He found a chair away from the main rows. Sat down. Took out his phone.

New message:

*Your appeal has been accepted for processing.*

*Status: “In queue for review.”*

*Estimated time: undefined.*

*Note: the average processing time for appeals in the “Why me?” category cannot be calculated, because most applicants change the question before receiving an answer to the original one.*

Vlad looked at the screen.

Then raised his eyes.

The hall was still the same. People sat. Waited. Each with their own. The man with the tanned neck had returned to his seat and folded his hands as before. The girl in the sweater was still unfolding and crumpling the sheet of paper. The woman with the thermos was finishing something hot with the look of a person whom it helped less than it should.

Someone in a distant row said:

"I did try."

A little farther away, another voice answered:

"I didn’t want it to turn out that way."

They did not look at each other.

Perhaps they did not hear each other.

Perhaps these were not even parts of different conversations, but fragments of one and the same conversation scattered across different people.

Vlad suddenly thought that he hated this thought — that his question was standard. Category A. The most common one. All his life he had tried not to be standard. Not to be part of the general mass. He thought better, felt more precisely, formulated more subtly. And here it was — a queue. A number. Frosted glass.

*Standard category A appeal.*

Still, it explained nothing.

The queue was not an answer.

Other people’s pain was not an answer.

The frosted glass even less so.

Vlad stubbornly felt that he had been deceived on some new level: not refused, not thrown out, not mocked, not even judged. Simply placed carefully into a common row. And for some reason that turned out to be more insulting than if he had been sent away directly.

He put the phone away.

Stood up.

Went toward the exit.

The corridor accepted him back — gray, even, indifferently warm. His steps still made no sound.

Halfway through, the phone vibrated again.

Vlad took it out.

*Dear applicant.*

*You are leaving the hall before the procedure is complete.*

*This is your right.*

*Please note: leaving does not close the appeal.*

*The question remains in the system until an answer is received or until the applicant stops asking it.*

*Have a good evening.*

Vlad stopped in the middle of the corridor.

Read it again.

*The question remains in the system until an answer is received or until the applicant stops asking it.*

Behind him, very far away, someone said:

"I just wanted to be heard."

The phrase did not catch up with him.

It only dented the air slightly.

He put the phone away. Walked on.

Around the bend, the corridor ended — and ordinary light returned. Kitchen light.

The cup stood on the table. Cold tea. The cross. The bills.

Everything in place.

Outside the window, the courtyard had returned — the building opposite, the streetlamp, parked cars, one lamp not burning and not intending to.

Vlad sat down.

Picked up the cup.

Put it back.

Thought of the man with the tanned neck.

Thought of the girl with the sheet of paper.

Thought of the woman with the thermos.

Thought that perhaps none of them had spoken to him.

And that for some reason it changed nothing.

His question — “why does none of this work” — had not gone anywhere. It simply now stood in line not alone, but with four million people, most of whom had come with the same question and apparently also considered it their own, special, unlike all the others.

It was inconvenient knowledge.

Not destructive. Simply inconvenient — like a chair in a waiting hall. You can sit. But you cannot sit straight.

And yet somewhere inside, Vlad immediately placed a small support under that knowledge.

Yes, there were many people.

Yes, the questions were similar.

Yes, the category was standard.

But his case was still his.

That meant they still had not understood it completely.

The thought was weak, petty, and absolutely necessary.

Vlad looked at the cross.

The cross lay silently.

This, perhaps, was the only being in the apartment that was not trying to register him, redirect him, or notify him.

He picked up the cross. Held it in his palm. Put it back.

The refrigerator hummed.

"I know," Vlad said.

The refrigerator did not clarify what exactly he knew.
