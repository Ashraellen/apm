# Book2_Na_Vse_Volya_Ego — Error404

This file fixes the current canon for **Book II** of the Error404 cycle.

Working Russian title:

**На всё воля Его**

English working sense:

**All Is His Will** / **It Is All His Will**

Final English title is not locked yet.

---

## 1. Status of Book II

Book II is planned as a separate novel in the Error404 cycle, not as a direct Vlad-centered continuation.

Main protagonist:

**a priest / clergyman of undefined confession**

The confession should remain deliberately undefined enough for the book to work across languages and cultures.

He may carry Orthodox resonance in the Russian source layer, because the author's lived experience includes service as a deacon and departure from Orthodoxy after the realization that if absolutely everything is God's will, then sin in the ordinary church-instructional sense cannot exist as an independent force outside that will.

But the novel should not become a memoir, confession, or direct autobiographical statement.

The priest is a separate character.

He is not:

- a caricature;
- a stupid fanatic;
- an anti-religious target;
- a clown in vestments;
- a mouthpiece for attacking religion.

He is:

- sincere;
- disciplined;
- frightened;
- intelligent;
- pedantic;
- faithful to form;
- used to spiritual language;
- used to giving answers;
- trapped inside the form through which he learned to understand God.

## 3. Core premise

The priest has repeated and used the formula:

```text
На всё воля Его.
```

At first this phrase functions as:

- consolation;
- discipline;
- spiritual restraint;
- explanation of suffering;
- pastoral tool;
- a way to keep people from falling apart;
- a way to keep people holding themselves back.

But one day the phrase turns absolute.

Main question:

```text
If absolutely everything is His will, where does sin come from?
```

The priest enters Window №7 not through Vlad's legal complaint, but through a theological fracture.

His question is not simply:

```text
Why me?
```

His question is:

```text
If all is His will, what exactly have we been calling sin?
```

Or:

```text
If absolutely everything is His will, where can something against His will exist?
```

---

## 4. The personal source nerve

The author served as a deacon and left Orthodoxy after realizing the absolute consequence of the formula:

```text
На всё воля Его.
```

If everything is truly His will in the absolute sense, then sin, in the simplified form in which churches often speak about it, cannot be an autonomous power outside God.

In this view, the church concept of sin often functions as a system of restraint, checks and balances, and social-spiritual discipline.

It works because people hold themselves back:

- by fear;
- by shame;
- by guilt;
- by obedience;
- by hope of salvation;
- by fear of punishment;
- by need for belonging;
- by desire not to fall out of the accepted order.

The phrase from conversation:

```text
People hold themselves by the little pants.
```

This is not to be used as a crude philosophical conclusion in the novel unless transformed artistically, but it captures the mechanism: people often need a restraining form because without it many would not become free; they would simply become uncontrolled.

Book II must not preach this directly.

It must show the priest discovering that the system he served may restrain people effectively while not being the Absolute Truth.

---

## 5. The dangerous theological fracture

The priest's reasoning:

```text
If sin is outside His will, then there is a place outside His will.
If there is a place outside His will, His will is not absolute.
If His will is absolute, sin cannot exist as an independent force against Him in the simple church-pedagogical sense.
If sin exists only as a system of restraint, then what was I teaching, confessing, warning against, and naming for all these years?
```

This must not become an anti-religious pamphlet.

But the book must go along the edge of rupture.

Rule:

```text
Do not attack God.
Do not flatten faith.
Do not mock living belief.
But do not soften the crack to protect the form.
```

The truth of the book comes from the fact that the question is born inside faith, not outside it.

---

## 6. The main artistic boundary

Book II must not become:

```text
Religion is false.
The Church lies.
There is no sin, do whatever you want.
```

That would be flat and weak.

The correct boundary:

```text
A religious system may be useful, protective, educational, and restraining.
But the useful form is not the Absolute.
When the form is brought before the absolute idea of divine will, it begins to crack.
```

Book II should show:

- the usefulness of form;
- the danger of form;
- the tenderness of form;
- the violence of form;
- the way form preserves people;
- the way form also protects itself from living truth.

Key rule:

```text
Do not attack religion from outside.
Let the question arise inside the priest's own faith.
```

This is what makes the book dangerous.

---

## 7. What the book is really about

The book is not about proving that religion is false.

It is about the moment when a working religious system of restraint collides with the absolute idea of divine will.

Key distinction:

```text
A system may restrain people and still not be the Absolute Truth.
A form may be useful and still not be God.
A doctrine may function as a fence and still fail when brought before infinity.
```

The priest begins to see that the concept of sin, as commonly used in church instruction, may operate as a system of checks and balances.

It works because people hold themselves back by fear, shame, duty, guilt, hope, obedience, and the desire to remain acceptable.

But the priest begins to see that this useful system does not fully survive the phrase:

```text
На всё воля Его.
```

Central conflict:

```text
If the system is not absolute Truth but still keeps people from chaos, what is he supposed to do with that knowledge?
```

This is the real pressure of the novel.

Not simply:

```text
Is sin real?
```

But:

```text
What happens to the shepherd when the fence he uses to protect the flock no longer looks like the horizon?
```

## 8. The priest's internal rupture

The priest does not immediately leave, reject, or mock the system.

He tries to stay inside it and save the formula.

He tries to classify the question as:

- temptation;
- demonic thought;
- spiritual delusion;
- pride of mind;
- dangerous reasoning;
- attack on faith;
- lack of humility;
- forbidden curiosity;
- intellectual arrogance;
- enemy suggestion;
- false light.

But the question does not disappear.

Formula:

```text
He does not become possessed by a demon.
He becomes possessed by the defense against the demon.
```

Or:

```text
He fights not the devil, but the question he cannot exorcise.
```

Or:

```text
He prays not for God to answer,
but for the question to stop.
```

This is the inner collapse.

## 9. His "raging" / besnovanie

The priest begins to accept the Chancellery-thoughts as temptation and starts to "rage" inwardly.

This must not be clownish possession.

The point is subtler and more dangerous:

```text
He behaves like someone fighting demons,
but the demon is his fear of the living question.
```

He may:

- cross himself more often;
- interrupt his own thoughts with prayer;
- repeat protective formulas;
- grow irritated with ordinary parishioners;
- become harsher in confession;
- become suspicious of silence;
- fear calmness because the thoughts arrive through calmness;
- label every exact question as possible delusion;
- write everything down to regain control;
- try to submit the thoughts to spiritual classification;
- fear that the thoughts are too peaceful to be dismissed as simple darkness.

The humor comes from the collision between his spiritual discipline and the absurdly precise living question.

The pain comes from the fact that he is not foolish. He understands too much.

## 10. Chancellery manifestation in Book II

There should be no screen as the main interface.

The screen belongs more naturally to Vlad's legal-digital world.

For the priest, the Chancellery manifests as a **second layer of thought**.

Not a voice.
Not a vision.
Not text on a screen.
Not a hallucinated external speech.

It appears as a thought inside thought — already complete, fuller than his own thought, clearly felt as arriving from outside the ordinary thinking process.

Working formula:

```text
A thought appears in him, but not from him.
It does not sound.
It is simply already there.
```

The priest experiences these thoughts as potentially supernatural, dangerous, or demonic because they are too calm, too precise, and too impossible to dismiss.

They are not emotional.
They do not seduce.
They do not flatter.
They do not threaten.
They ask.

That is why he fears them.

## 11. Difference between ordinary thoughts and Chancellery-thoughts

Ordinary thoughts of the priest may be:

- anxious;
- circular;
- defensive;
- quoting authority;
- searching for labels;
- emotionally coloured;
- trying to close the question;
- trying to classify the danger.

Chancellery-thoughts should be:

- complete;
- still;
- clear;
- inward;
- paternal;
- spiritually exact;
- fuller than thought;
- not produced by his ordinary reasoning;
- impossible to dismiss without dishonesty.

They do not behave like intrusive noise.

They are more like a completed line placed into consciousness.

## 12. The squared notebook

The priest is a pedant.

He begins recording these "other" thoughts in a squared notebook.

The notebook may be labelled cautiously, for example:

```text
Notes on thoughts
For discernment
Private observations
Do not open without necessity
```

Russian possible labels:

```text
Наблюдения за помыслами
Для рассуждения
Частные наблюдения
Не открывать без необходимости
```

The notebook is important because he tries to make the impossible manageable.

Vlad wanted screenshots and attachable evidence.

The priest wants a disciplined notebook.

Function:

```text
He tries to put infinity into a grid.
```

The squared notebook is funny, human, and spiritually exact.

The grid is essential:

```text
He tries to put the living abyss into little squares.
```

This object can become one of the core physical symbols of Book II.

## 14. Tone of the Chancellery-thoughts

Book II must move away from Vlad's dry digital-bureaucratic tone.

For the priest, the answers should be more inward, paternal, almost ecclesial, but still sharp.

Not sentimental.
Not syrupy.
Not sermon-like.
Not theatrical.

Correct tone:

```text
soft, but unsafe;
paternal, but cutting;
clear, but not explanatory;
gentle, but impossible to evade.
```

The thought should not flatter him.
It should not comfort him too quickly.
It should ask the exact question he is trying to avoid.

Example:

```text
Сын мой, уточни: грех находится вне воли Его или внутри неё?
```

This is better than a dry technical notice because the priest's interface is not digital bureaucracy, but spiritual inwardness.

## 15. Canonical forms of address

The following address forms are approved for Book II.

They should not be varied randomly.
Each form should correspond to the layer of the priest being addressed.

```text
Сын мой          — базовое отеческое обращение.
Ищущий Меня      — когда речь о самом поиске Бога.
Служитель мой    — когда вопрос касается его служения.
Пастырь          — когда речь об ответственности за других.
Человек божий    — когда с него снимается сан и роль.
Чадо моё         — редкий церковно-отеческий оттенок.
Возлюбленный сын — очень редко, в самых глубоких местах.
```

Do not use:

```text
Раб мой
```

Reason:

It is too risky for a multilingual cycle and creates the wrong social/historical resonance. The book needs theological danger, not unnecessary wording damage.

## 16. Use of each address form

### Сын мой

Basic paternal address.

Used when the thought approaches him as a person before God.

Example:

```text
Сын мой, уточни: грех находится вне воли Его или внутри неё?
```

### Ищущий Меня

Used when the question concerns his search for God.

Example:

```text
Ищущий Меня, почему ты ищешь Меня только там, где Мне заранее разрешено быть?
```

### Служитель мой

Used when the question concerns his service, function, role, or religious duty.

Example:

```text
Служитель мой, если ты называешь вопрос искушением, исчезает ли его правда?
```

### Пастырь

Used when the question concerns responsibility for others, guidance, flock, confession, teaching, or spiritual authority.

Example:

```text
Пастырь, куда ты ведёшь других, если сам боишься войти?
```

### Человек божий

Used when the thought removes his protective role and leaves him as a human being before God.

This does not mean humiliation.
It means the stripping away of function.

Example:

```text
Человек божий, кто останется от тебя, если снять сан, страх и правильные слова?
```

### Чадо моё

Rare church-paternal shade.

Use sparingly in Russian, because it may not transfer cleanly into other languages.

Example:

```text
Чадо моё, не всякий страх есть смирение.
```

### Возлюбленный сын

Very rare.

Use only in the deepest places, where the thought does not cut first, but calls.

Example:

```text
Возлюбленный сын, Я не пришёл разрушить твою веру. Я пришёл снять с неё страх.
```

## 19. The edge of rupture

Book II must walk along the edge of rupture.

It must not become a safe respectful theological essay.
It must not become an anti-religious pamphlet.

The correct place is between them:

```text
The book should be dangerous because the question is honest.
```

The priest's breakdown must feel real.

He begins to "rage" not because he is possessed, but because he cannot understand how a thought that feels more alive than his defense can be dangerous, and how a question that threatens doctrine can also feel closer to God.

The deeper horror:

```text
He prays not for God to answer,
but for the question to stop.
```

## 20. Humor rules

Humor must arise from truth, not from mocking faith.

Correct humor sources:

- the squared notebook trying to contain the infinite;
- the priest classifying living truth as suspicious;
- excessive spiritual self-monitoring;
- protective prayer becoming avoidance;
- the gap between calm Chancellery-thoughts and his panicked reaction;
- the absurdity of trying to file the living God under "possible temptation";
- the contrast between sacred vocabulary and very human fear.

Wrong humor:

- making him stupid;
- cheap jokes about church rituals;
- cartoon exorcism;
- external anti-clerical mockery;
- turning the priest into a religious buffoon.

Book II should be funny because the human being is funny under pressure.

Not because faith is stupid.

## 21. Possible scene directions

These are not a fixed chapter plan.

They are future-use material.

### First crack

He says "На всё воля Его" to someone else as consolation.

Later he hears the phrase inside himself too literally.

The word "всё" becomes heavier than the sentence.

### First Chancellery-thought

After prayer or pastoral duty, the thought appears:

```text
Сын мой, уточни: грех находится вне воли Его или внутри неё?
```

He is frightened because the thought is calm and not obviously blasphemous.

### First notebook entry

He begins to record the thought in a squared notebook.

The act is both disciplined and ridiculous.

### First misclassification

He tries to classify the thought as temptation.

But the thought remains true even after classification.

### Escalation

He increases prayer, crossing, self-watchfulness, reading, discipline.

The thoughts become not louder, but clearer.

### Pastoral contradiction

He hears someone's confession and suddenly realizes he is using the same concept of sin he himself can no longer hold without fracture.

### Responsibility crisis

He sees that removing the concept of sin too early may not free people, but unrestrain them.

This creates the conflict between truth and pastoral utility.

### Window №7

He reaches Window №7 through theological fracture, not legal complaint.

For him, Window №7 may not appear as Vlad's exact waiting hall. It may appear as a state, a second layer of mind, a prayer-space, a silence, or an inner room of unclosed questions.

### Encounter with cycle-world

Possible light crossing with Vlad, woman with thermos, or a phrase from Book I, but Book II must remain centered on the priest.
