# Chapter Twenty-One. Participation

In the morning, the porch was darker than it had been in the evening.

The rain had not grown stronger during the night, but it had not left either. It worked finely, patiently, without a voice. Dripped from the edge of the roof. Gathered in the grooves between the boards. Ran down the steps into the mud where yesterday the fish had lain, the fish that was no longer there.

The tracks by the porch had blurred.

The boar pits had grown softer, wider. Where the hooves had pressed into the earth, now there stood cloudy water. The broken piece of mushroom stem had disappeared. Or had been pressed into the mud so thoroughly that it could no longer be told apart from everything else.

Sofia saw it first not with her eyes.

With her foot.

She came out into the entryway, opened the door, and stepped onto the upper board of the porch. The board slipped under her sole so shortly and sharply that the body had time to be frightened before thought. Sofia grabbed the doorframe.

“Careful,” Eva said from inside.

“Too late for advice, but thank you.”

Nick, sitting on the bench and trying to pull a dry sock onto an insufficiently dry foot, raised his head.

“Has the porch entered into morning dialogue?”

“It won.”

“Then I’ll wait for a diplomatic window.”

Sofia looked down.

The water was going the wrong way.

Not as a turbulent stream. Not frighteningly. Simply as a thin cloudy path from the edge of the porch to the lower board, then under it. There the water disappeared into the dark beneath the planking, as if it knew a small passage people had not yet seen.

Marek came out after her.

He said nothing at first.

Put one foot on the side board, crouched, looked under the porch. Then went to the edge, where the earth had been torn up after yesterday. With the toe of his boot he moved a wet leaf aside. The water immediately occupied the leaf’s place.

“What?” Sofia asked.

“It went toward the threshold.”

“And what?”

“Then it’ll be under the house.”

Nick finally stood up, holding the second sock in his hand.

“That sounds like a phrase after which it is better to stop being a spectator.”

Marek was already walking to the shed.

Not fast.

But in such a way that the question of spectators became unnecessary.

Thomas came out the door, fastening his jacket.

“What happened?”

“Water,” Sofia said.

“What water?”

Marek returned from the shed with a shovel, a short board under his arm, and an old iron hoe in his other hand.

“This one,” he said.

Thomas looked at the path under the porch.

“We need to remove the water?”

“Divert it.”

“Where?”

Marek pointed toward the bushes.

“There.”

“Is it lower there?”

“A little.”

“How much ditch do we need?”

Marek drove the shovel into the soft earth by the step.

“Until the water goes.”

Thomas closed his eyes for a second.

“Where?”

“Not here.”

Nick stepped carefully onto the porch, tested the board with the toe of his boot.

“I see morning has decided to continue education without coordinating the schedule.”

“We need stones,” Marek said.

“Stones are already better. Stones, at least, are honestly heavy.”

“There, by the barrel.”

Nick looked toward the barrel.

“Of course. The most honest things always lie farther away than one would like.”

Marek did not answer. He had already begun removing the upper layer of mud by the step. The shovel went in easily, too easily. The wet earth dragged after it in a heavy chunk, then slid back almost to the same place.

At first everyone stood.

Not out of laziness.

Rather because Marek’s movement seemed to belong to him. He knew where to dig, where to look, which board to take. It was convenient to stand beside that confidence, as beside a person who was already engaged instead of you.

Marek set the board on its edge by the washout and tried to press it with his foot while drawing earth toward it with the shovel. The board slipped. Not much. But enough.

Sofia stepped forward.

“Hold this?”

Marek did not even raise his head.

“Hold it.”

She crouched and took the edge of the board.

The wet wood was cold, with fine mud on its side. Her hand became slippery at once.

“Like this?”

“Lower.”

She lowered her palm.

“Like this?”

“Hold it.”

This was not an answer to “is it right.” It was something else. While she held it, the board did not slide.

Marek pulled earth in again.

For a second the water stopped at the board, gathered, darkened, then found a crack on the side and ran there.

“It isn’t holding,” Thomas said.

“Stones,” Marek said.

Nick was already walking toward the barrel.

“I wanted to say something majestic about drainage troops, but I suspect the stone will object.”

A minute later he returned with the first stone.

Not large.

But wet enough to look heavier.

He set it by the board, straightened, and inhaled.

“The stone is against everything.”

“More,” Marek said.

“I feared democracy would be brief here.”

But he went.

Thomas did not go for stones at once.

He crouched by the water.

“If we dig from here to here, the water will go to the bushes. But there’s a root. We need to go around it.”

“Yes,” Marek said.

“Then first we need to understand the line.”

“We’ll understand as we go.”

“If we do it wrong, it will be worse.”

“If we don’t do it, it will be worse.”

Thomas looked at him.

Then at the water.

Then at Sofia, who was holding the board and already felt her fingers beginning to ache.

“What should I do?”

Marek nodded toward the barrel.

“Stones.”

Thomas wanted to ask how many.

Sofia saw it in his face.

The question had already risen, prepared to become full, precise, demanding a limit.

But Thomas only said:

“More?”

Marek nodded.

“More.”

And Thomas went.

Sofia held the board.

At first it seemed to her that she was helping Marek. Then this became too complicated for the hand. The hand was not helping a person. The hand was holding a wet edge that wanted to slide. The fingers grew tired. The thumb began to slip. Mud packed under her nails. Water crawled along the board toward her sleeve and found the gap between glove and skin.

She wanted to let go for a second.

Just to change fingers.

The board trembled immediately.

“Hold it,” Marek said.

Sofia did not let go.

Not because she had decided to be useful.

Because the board would slide.

David came out of the house with the notebook.

Why was clear from his face even before he understood it himself. He wanted to see how the water went. Probably draw a line. Fix the place where it dove under the boards. Name the cause so it would not be repeated later.

He stood on the upper step.

Water dripped from the roof straight onto the cover of the notebook.

A dark stain spread across the corner.

Then onto the water.

Then he closed the notebook and put it in a dry place in the entryway, on the overturned basket.

Nick came back with another stone and saw this.

“This is hard to write down,” David said.

Nick set the stone by the board.

“Then carry.”

David nodded.

The word did not offend him.

He took the empty bucket by the door and went to the place where water stood in the boar pit. Scooped clumsily. Half of it poured back. He looked at Marek.

“Like this?”

“Tilt it more.”

David tilted it.

The bucket filled with cloudy water, leaves, and something dark from the bottom.

“Where?”

“To the bushes.”

David carried it.

The bucket beat against his leg. Water splashed over the edge. He walked carefully, too carefully, but reached it.

Eva came out with gloves.

“Nora found them,” she said.

Nora stood behind her in the doorway, holding another pair — small, old, with a hole by the thumb.

“These are bad,” Nora said.

“Everything fits,” Nick said, passing by with a stone. “Today we have a usefulness contest for damaged objects.”

Nora held the gloves out to Sofia.

“For you?”

Sofia looked at her hands.

They were already in mud.

“Too late.”

Nora thought.

Then placed the gloves nearby, on a dry board.

“Then later.”

And stepped back into the entryway so as not to get in the way.

Lina appeared without words.

She did not ask what had happened. Looked at the water, at the board, at the stones, at Sofia’s hands, at the line Thomas was trying to understand with his foot in the mud.

“Not there,” she said.

Thomas turned around.

“What?”

Lina pointed to the stone Nick had just set down.

“It will block it.”

“It’s holding the board.”

“And holding the water.”

Marek looked.

Nodded.

“To the right.”

Nick picked up the stone again.

“The stone has already begun building a career in the wrong place.”

He moved it.

Lina crouched, took a small flat stone from the mud, and slid it under the edge of the board.

“Now.”

Marek let go of the shovel for a second, tested with his foot.

The board held better.

The water gathered at the edge, searched for the old crack, did not find it, stood there, and went along the new little ditch Thomas had managed to dig with the hoe for half a palm.

“It’s going,” Thomas said.

“Too little,” Marek said.

“More?”

“More.”

Thomas took the hoe again.

Now he did not ask how much in all. He dug in short movements, removing roots, leaves, clumps of wet earth. When the hoe stuck, he did not get angry at once, but looked at what was holding it. Sometimes Marek pointed with his foot. Sometimes Lina said “don’t press” or “put it under.” Sometimes Thomas moved a stone himself, then stepped back and watched where the water went.

Sofia was still holding the board.

Her shoulder had begun to ache.

Her knee pressed into the wet step. Her sleeve was already soaked to the elbow. It seemed to her that if she stood up now, the body would fall apart into separate complaints: fingers, back, knee, cold, mud.

“Want me to take over?” Nick asked.

He stood beside her, breathing heavily, but without the usual theater.

Sofia wanted to say yes.

But Marek was just then pulling earth under the lower edge.

“Not now,” she said.

Nick nodded.

Did not joke.

Simply took the second edge of the board and pressed it together with her.

His hand was beside her hand. Dirty, red from cold, with a cut by the knuckle.

“Now?” he asked.

“Now yes.”

She changed fingers.

The board did not slide.

It was a small event almost no one but her noticed.

But for her hand it was large.

David returned with the empty bucket.

“More water?”

Marek looked at the pit.

“Yes.”

David went.

Eva held the door open, but not all the way, so the cold would not pull from the house. Each time someone passed with a stone or a bucket, she opened it wider. Then closed it partly. With one hand she held the edge of the door; with the other, her hair, which the wind kept throwing into her face.

“Can I hold it?” Nora asked from inside.

“I’ve got it,” Eva said.

“I can.”

Eva looked at her.

“You can hand out gloves.”

Nora nodded.

And began handing them out.

Not always at once to the one who needed them. Sometimes the wrong ones. Nick received the little pair with the hole and put it on two fingers so as not to offend the intention.

“I feel elegant and helpless,” he said.

“Stone,” Lina said.

“I return to rough matter.”

He took the glove off and went.

After a while, Ivar appeared by the shed.

Not from the house.

As if he had already been there for a long time or had come from the side where the trees stood thicker. He wore a dark jacket, wet at the shoulders. In his hands he carried two short boards.

Nick saw him first.

“Ah, here comes supreme command.”

Ivar looked at the stone in Nick’s hands.

“Carrying it?”

“I am already carrying it.”

“Carry it.”

Nick blinked.

“Cruel, but fair.”

Ivar went to Marek, laid the boards nearby.

Marek looked.

“This one will do.”

Ivar nodded.

Took the other and set it himself against the edge of the ditch, where the earth had begun to crumble. Did not explain. Did not look at everyone like a teacher. Simply stepped into the mud, put his boot on the board, and held it while Marek drove a flat stone beside it.

“Lower,” Ivar said.

Marek moved the stone lower.

“Now.”

And that was all.

After that, Ivar was silent.

The work became different not because of him.

Rather because by then everyone was already doing something.

Marek dug and set.

Sofia held.

Nick carried stones and sometimes simply stayed silent, which was stranger than any joke.

Thomas deepened the ditch and now asked only:

“More?”

David scooped the pit and poured water into the bushes.

Eva held the door.

Lina adjusted stones, removed extra earth, said “not there,” “now leave it,” “let it settle.”

Nora handed out gloves, and then began stacking small stones by the edge herself — not in the ditch, simply beside it, where they could be taken.

Nick had been about to say something about organizing a stone archive, but at that moment lifted too heavy a stone and changed his mind.

“What?” Sofia asked.

“Nothing. The stone convinced me of brevity.”

He set the stone in place.

Lina moved it a palm to the left with her foot.

“Like this?”

“Like this.”

The water gathered by the board again.

For a moment everyone thought it would go back under the porch. Thomas even cursed quietly, almost without sound. Marek pressed the edge of the board with the shovel. Sofia strengthened her hand. Nick leaned in with his shoulder. Ivar set his boot by the lower edge. Lina slipped a flat stone into the gap.

The water trembled.

Stood.

Went toward the ditch.

Not beautifully.

Not at once.

First as a thin thread.

Then as a cloudy ribbon, picking up small leaves, sand, a piece of pine needle. It passed along the board, went around the first stone, lingered by the root, then spilled over a small threshold of earth and moved toward the bushes.

“It’s going,” David said.

He said it as if he did not want to frighten it off.

Nick exhaled.

“The water has accepted our modest administrative proposal.”

“Don’t relax,” Lina said.

“I’m not relaxing. I am solemnly standing in mud.”

“Stone.”

“Yes.”

He went for the next one.

Sofia was still holding the board.

But now the board no longer pulled downward as strongly. The stones had taken part of the weight. The earth under it had grown denser. The water went past, and Sofia’s hand suddenly felt not tension, but trembling after tension.

She let go for a second.

The board stayed.

Sofia looked at her palm.

The fingers were dirty, red, trembling slightly. Earth darkened under the nail of her thumb. Water ran down her sleeve, cold, foreign, and already somehow not entirely foreign.

She did not think anything clear.

For now there was only this: the board held.

“More here,” Marek said.

Thomas brought a stone before Marek showed where.

“This one?”

Marek looked.

“It will do.”

Thomas placed it.

“More?”

“Small one.”

Thomas went for a small one.

Nick watched him go.

“Man has evolved from ‘how much is needed’ to ‘small one.’ I am proud of the species.”

Thomas did not turn around.

“Carrying?”

“Carrying.”

“Then don’t get distracted.”

Nick opened his mouth.

Closed it.

“Fair.”

The rain began to quiet closer to noon.

It did not end, but became rarer. Drops from the roof no longer fell in a solid thread, but in separate strikes. The earth by the porch was ruined completely: kneaded, opened, with pieces of moss, roots, and boot tracks. The new ditch looked ugly. The board stood crooked. The stones did not make a wall — they simply held what could slide again.

But the water went past.

Not far.

Past.

Marek straightened first.

Set the shovel against the wall.

Then looked under the porch one more time. Bent, ran his hand along the lower board where earlier the water had disappeared into the dark.

“Normal?” Thomas asked.

“For now.”

Thomas nodded.

The word “for now” no longer angered him so much.

Or he was tired of being angry.

Nick sat straight down on the overturned barrel.

“If anyone now says ‘there, we fixed everything,’ I will throw a small stone at him. I won’t manage a large one. I have grown as a person, but not that much.”

Eva stood in the doorway, still holding it with her hand.

“No one will say it.”

“Thank you.”

David poured the last bucket into the bushes and set it upside down. Then came to the entryway, looked at the notebook lying on the overturned basket.

It was dry, except for the dark stain in the corner.

David took it.

Opened it.

Looked at the empty page.

Then closed it.

“Won’t write?” Sofia asked.

He looked at his hands.

Mud was on his fingers, on his sleeve, even on his cheek — a thin dark stripe he had not noticed.

“Not now.”

“And later?”

“Later, maybe I’ll lie.”

Sofia smiled.

“Then don’t write.”

“I won’t for now.”

Nora came out of the entryway and set a mug on the step.

“The tea is cold,” she said.

“That is wonderful,” Nick said. “It corresponds to the condition of the troops.”

“There’s hot one too.”

“Then the troops are partially restored.”

Nora handed him another mug.

He took it with both hands.

“My thanks.”

“It’s hot.”

“Then I can still feel.”

Lina looked at everyone.

“Wash your hands before going inside.”

“I thought we were now part of the earth,” Nick said.

“Not all of the earth comes into the house.”

“Excellent border policy.”

But he went to wash his hands.

Sofia stayed by the porch a few seconds longer.

Her wrist hurt. When she unclenched her fingers, they did not obey at once. Under her nails was mud. On her sleeve — a dark stripe from the board. Her knee was soaked. Her back wanted to complain separately, in detail and with evidence.

She looked at the ditch.

The water flowed.

Quietly.

Without gratitude.

Without acknowledgment.

The water had not become cleaner.

Had not become obedient.

It simply now went not to where it was worse.

Sofia suddenly understood that she was tired not from helping.

From holding.

From the fact that one could not let go before the board had stopped sliding.

Ivar came out from behind the shed empty-handed.

Stopped nearby.

Looked not at Sofia, but at the water.

“It holds,” he said.

“For now.”

“For now also holds.”

She wanted to ask something.

Did not ask.

Because the answer, most likely, would again not be words.

Ivar went to the shed, lifted the second board that had not been used, and carried it back under the shelter.

Sofia looked at the porch once more.

The mud remained.

The tracks remained.

The stones lay crooked.

The board darkened from water.

The house looked not saved, but simply a little less open to what could enter beneath it at night.

That turned out to be enough.

By evening the water was still flowing.

Only now not under the house.
