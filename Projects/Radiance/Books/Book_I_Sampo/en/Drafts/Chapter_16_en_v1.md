# Chapter Sixteen. Watch

In the morning they did not go to the jar at once.

At least that was how it seemed.

First someone passed the shelf with a mug. Then came back, as if remembering a spoon. Then lingered one breath longer than was needed for a spoon.

It was Eva.

She did not touch the cloth. Only looked at how the edge lay, whether it had dried out overnight, whether it had slipped, whether it had become too heavy with damp. Then she looked at the glass. The white tape held. The English letters were slightly blurred from the moisture, but still readable.

STARTER. DO NOT THROW.

Below it was the Finnish inscription.

Still lower — the date.

Everything was in place.

Eva nodded to herself and went to the table.

A few minutes later Thomas stopped by the same shelf.

He too did not touch the jar.

At first.

Then he leaned closer. Then straightened. Then leaned again, like a person who does not want to check, but is already checking what he did not want to check.

Sofia came into the kitchen at exactly that moment.

On the table lay bread wrapped in a towel. Lina was cutting apples into thin, uneven slices. Marek by the door was shaking chips and small bark out of a bucket. In the stove there was grey warmth, not fire yet, only the memory of it. Outside the window hung rain, too fine to count as rain and too persistent to be fog.

Thomas heard her steps and moved away from the shelf too quickly.

“I was just looking,” he said.

“I didn’t ask anything,” Sofia answered.

“That is why I explained.”

Nick, sitting at the table with a mug, raised his eyes.

“Good strategy. Explaining before accusation. Legally elegant, spiritually doubtful.”

“Nick,” Eva said.

“I’m quiet.”

He really said it quietly.

Sofia went to the shelf.

The jar stood where Lina had moved it yesterday. Not by the stove. Not on the old circle. By the side wall, where the air was cooler but not draughty. The cloth on top lay evenly. The mass behind the glass seemed calm. Not dead. Not especially alive. Simply the way the living can be in the morning, when something is already expected of it.

Sofia reached out.

Not all the way.

Only her fingers went forward, by themselves, as if they wanted to lift the cloth just a little. To check. To make sure. To see that something was still continuing there.

She stopped her hand in the air.

It came out awkwardly.

That is how one stops not a thought, but a body that has already given the thought away before you.

Lina looked at her.

Said nothing.

And because of that Sofia felt even more awkward.

She withdrew her hand and took a knife from the table, although she did not need the knife.

“Apple?” Lina asked.

“Yes.”

“Then cut it, don’t hold it like evidence.”

Nick chuckled quietly.

“I am beginning to love this kitchen. Here even fruit gives testimony.”

Sofia put the apple on the board.

The knife entered the flesh too loudly.

Thomas was still standing near the shelf. He was no longer looking at the jar now, but at the people around it.

“If everyone thinks someone else will look,” he said, “we will lose it again.”

Nick did not answer at once.

And that was noticeable.

Because usually he answered such phrases faster than they had time to settle on the table.

David raised his head from his mug.

“There is sense in that.”

He said it carefully.

Without the old pleasure with which he had once laid a map out on the table. Without that small inner click when a person feels that the world is finally ready to become understandable, if only one takes the right pencil.

“What sense?” Eva asked.

“Not to control,” David said.

He paused.

As if checking his own words for smell.

“So as not to leave it without attention.”

Thomas nodded at once.

“Yes. Exactly. Not control. Just someone has to be responsible.”

“For what?” Lina asked.

Thomas turned to her.

“That someone looks.”

“Looks at what?”

“The place. The cloth. The condition. If it is cold. If it is warm. If it needs feeding.”

“And if it doesn’t?”

“Then not feed.”

“How will you know?”

Thomas opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Then said honestly:

“That is why one has to look.”

Lina continued cutting apples.

She did not argue.

That was worse for Thomas than an argument.

David stood and went to the shelf with the list by the door. The sheet with yesterday’s points was still hanging there. ASK BEFORE THROWING. CHECK PLACE. Check the place. Overnight the paper had softened and bent slightly. The pencil lay on the windowsill beside a small shell that someone had long ago brought from the lake and left there, as if the lake needed a dry copy.

David took the pencil.

Then did not begin writing at once.

Sofia could see his shoulders.

Before, in such moments, he used to become more collected. Now — less. As if writing no longer gave him support, but he did not yet know another way not to drop what he was holding.

“We can make a simple rotation,” he said.

“A queue to the jar,” Nick said.

“Nick.”

“I’m not against it. Just clarifying the level of civilization. We now have a small northern patient.”

Thomas looked at him.

Nick raised his palms.

“That’s all. Last time.”

“You said that yesterday,” Eva said.

“Yesterday was yesterday.”

Lina raised her eyes.

Nick understood that he had stepped into someone else’s phrase, and fell silent with the look of a person who had accidentally stepped in the track of a larger animal.

David wrote:

MORNING.

Then below:

DAY.

Then:

EVENING.

He stopped.

“No night,” Lina said.

“Why?” Thomas asked.

“Because at night it also needs not only to live, but to be left in peace.”

“But if it gets cold?”

“Then the whole house will get cold.”

That phrase passed by the jar and remained somewhere near the stove.

Marek raised his head.

“The wind will change today.”

“How do you know?” Thomas asked.

“You can hear it.”

Thomas almost asked: where exactly can you hear it.

But did not ask.

David wrote in the names.

Morning — Eva.

Day — Thomas.

Evening — Lina.

Then he looked at Lina.

“Or better not you?”

“Better not me.”

“Why?”

“Because then you will think everything is fine if I have looked.”

David erased her name.

A grey stripe remained on the paper.

“Then who?”

Sofia felt everyone look not at her, but beside her.

It was almost the same thing.

“I can do evening,” she said.

She said it before she had decided.

Nick looked at her, but said nothing.

And that too was noticeable.

David wrote:

EVENING — SOFIA.

Thought, and added beside it in Russian:

evening — Sofia.

Then looked at the list.

It hung crookedly.

David straightened the sheet.

The sheet slid slightly again.

He straightened it once more, already more slowly.

“Leave it,” Lina said.

“It’s hanging crooked.”

“It isn’t for beauty.”

David let go of the paper.

It hung slightly uneven.

And for some reason everyone looked at it as at a small concession to the world.

Breakfast passed almost calmly.

Almost — because calm was now measured by how many times someone raised their eyes toward the side wall.

Eva checked the jar after breakfast.

She went to it very carefully. Did not remove the cloth. Only bent over, smelled the air beside it, looked at the glass, and stepped back.

“Normal?” Thomas asked.

“I don’t know.”

“Then why did you check?”

Eva shrugged.

“Because it’s my turn.”

Thomas said nothing.

Later David approached the jar out of his time.

He took a cup from the table, carried it toward the sink, and stopped on the way. Just for a second. Looked at the cloth. At the list. At his name, which was not there. Then moved away.

Sofia noticed.

David noticed that she noticed.

“I didn’t touch it,” he said.

“I saw.”

“Just looked.”

“So did I.”

He smiled slightly.

Not merrily.

“Strange, isn’t it?”

“What?”

“That now even simply looking feels like interference.”

Sofia did not answer.

Because he had said too close to what she herself did not want to pronounce.

Around noon Thomas approached the jar officially.

Not deliberately officially.

He simply had that kind of back.

He washed his hands, wiped them on a towel, then once more — with the dry edge. Went to the shelf. Looked at the list, as if checking with permission. Lifted the cloth with two fingers.

Too little to see everything.

Too much not to disturb.

The smell came out warm and sour.

Not bad.

But after yesterday everyone for a moment stopped doing what they were doing.

Lina raised her eyes.

“What are you looking for?” she asked.

“Signs.”

“What kind?”

“That it is alive.”

“And if you see them?”

“Then I’ll write it down.”

“And if you don’t see them?”

Thomas held the cloth in the air.

The mass behind the glass was damp, uneven, with small bubbles at the edge. Not many. A few. Or it only seemed so to Sofia because she wanted to see them.

“Then I’ll think what to do,” Thomas said.

Lina stood.

Not sharply.

But in such a way that Thomas immediately let go of the cloth.

She came over, adjusted the edge that had not lain right, and said:

“Look. Don’t wake.”

Thomas reddened.

A little.

Not even from shame. From the effort to accept the difference.

“I didn’t mean to.”

“I know.”

Lina did not soften the phrase.

And did not harden it.

I know.

That was enough.

After lunch the wind did change.

The house heard it first.

Not the people.

The house creaked in the corner where it had been silent that morning. Then something in the pipe sighed shortly. Then the door to the entryway stopped closing on the first try. The air coming through the cracks became different — lower, wetter, with the smell of water, stone, and distant needles.

Marek went out to check the wood.

Came back with wet sleeves.

“We need to bring in more,” he said.

“Now?” Eva asked.

“Before it’s completely wet.”

“But it’s raining.”

“That’s why.”

This logic needed no decoration.

Several people stood.

Thomas looked at the list.

It was already after his daytime check, but not yet evening. According to the schedule, the jar was supposed to be left in peace.

The house, obviously, did not read the schedule.

Sofia went out with Marek.

Not because many hands were needed.

Or exactly because of that.

Outside the rain was fine, but in a few steps it had time to become part of clothing. The wood lay under the shelter, but the wind drove the water sideways, and the edge of the stack had darkened. Marek silently began moving the top row inward, closer to the wall. Sofia took two logs, then three, then two again, because wet wood lied with its weight.

Nora brought a second bucket.

“That’s for water,” Marek said.

“Then it will be for wood,” she answered.

He looked at her.

Nodded.

Nick appeared in the doorway, fastening his jacket.

“I came out not to help, but to suffer together. However, it may look similar.”

“Take the small ones,” Marek said.

“You have just diminished me as an epic.”

“But your back will remain.”

“All right. Convinced.”

They carried wood into the entryway. Water ran from sleeves onto the floor. Boots left dark tracks. The door opened and closed, letting in cold each time someone passed through. In the kitchen it was becoming cooler. The stove, which in the morning they had barely noticed, suddenly became the center not of conversation, but of need.

Thomas came to the door and said:

“Don’t keep it open.”

Nick, passing by with two logs, answered:

“We are trying to close the passage between the elements and the kitchen, but the elements have poor manners.”

Thomas did not smile.

He was looking at the jar.

Sofia looked too.

The cloth lay evenly.

But the air around it had become different.

Not because of the jar.

Because of the door, the wet wood, the cold jackets, the water on the floor, the wind in the pipe, the stove that had not yet gained strength, and the people who, saving the wood, were breaking the warmth in the house.

The schedule hung on the wall.

Morning.

Day.

Evening.

It knew nothing about wet sleeves.

When the wood had been moved, Lina set the kettle closer to the fire. Marek opened the damper. The stove hummed more quietly than it should have. Then louder. Then more quietly again.

“The draught is wandering,” he said.

“Because of the wind?” David asked.

“Yes.”

“Can anything be done?”

“Wait. And not open the door unless needed.”

Thomas looked at the jar.

Lina noticed.

“It is the same with it,” she said.

“What?”

“Don’t open unless needed.”

He nodded.

Faster than in the morning.

In the second half of the day water came into the house.

Not as an event.

Marek simply asked someone to bring another bucket from the pump by the old shed, because the clean water in the kitchen was running out. Nora went first. Sofia went with her. Nick said he was going for the balance of the group, because two women and one bucket was “dangerously close to symbolism.”

Eva threw him a second bucket.

“Then carry it.”

Nick looked at the bucket.

“Symbolism has grown heavier.”

Outside it had become colder.

The path to the shed was not long, but the mud had had time to make it convincing. The water in the pump did not come at once. Nora pumped the handle slowly, as if listening to the metal. Then air burst from the spout, then a rusty trickle, then clean cold water that struck the bottom of the bucket so sharply Sofia flinched.

She put her fingers into the stream.

The cold was real.

Not “cool.”

Precisely cold, of the kind that had no intention of becoming more convenient because someone needed to bake bread or preserve the starter.

“Too cold,” Nick said.

“For what?” Nora asked.

He wanted to answer.

Did not find anything.

Shrugged.

“In general.”

Nora looked at the water.

“Water is not in general.”

She said it and was embarrassed herself.

Nick lifted the bucket.

“I am not going to comment right now, because I might accidentally spoil a good phrase.”

“You already spoiled it,” Sofia said.

“But carefully.”

They returned to the house slowly. The buckets pulled their hands downward. The water splashed near the rim, and each time Sofia caught the movement with her shoulder, hip, fingers. One could not simply “bring water.” One had to walk so that it remained in the bucket.

In the kitchen Thomas stood by the shelf.

Not opening.

Just standing.

Sofia set the bucket on the floor.

“Your shift is over,” Nick said.

Thomas did not react.

“I know.”

“Then why are you here?”

Thomas exhaled slowly.

“Because the door was opened many times.”

Nick looked at the door.

Then at the jar.

Then at the wet floors, the wood by the wall, the bucket of water, Lina by the stove, Marek at the damper, David with the list, which was still hanging not quite straight enough.

“This is no longer a question for the jar, is it?” he said.

Nick said it almost without mockery.

Thomas did not answer.

David went to the list.

Sofia saw him lift the pencil.

And saw how he did not write.

He stood with the pencil in his hand, looking at the three words: MORNING, DAY, EVENING.

Then put the pencil back on the windowsill.

A small movement.

But for some reason it turned out to be more important than if he had written ten more points.

By evening Sofia began to feel that people were walking not around the kitchen, but in a circle.

Not a real one.

Nick from the door to the table.

Thomas from the table to the shelf.

David from the list to the window.

Eva from the stove to the sink.

Lina from the dough to the knife, from the knife to the cloth, from the cloth back to the dough.

Marek from the entryway to the stove, from the stove to the door, from the door to the wood.

Nora almost did not walk. She sat by the window and watched water on the glass gather into paths and suddenly change course when one drop became heavier than another.

Sofia caught herself waiting for evening.

Not just for the time.

For her turn.

It was funny.

And unpleasant.

She was waiting for the moment when she could approach the jar lawfully.

As if the living thing had become something for which one needed a pass.

When the room began to darken, David said:

“Evening.”

Not loudly.

Just reminded.

Sofia stood.

At once, too quickly.

Nick noticed it.

Said nothing.

Sometimes he was merciful after all, though he carefully hid it under a layer of verbal dirt.

Sofia went to the shelf.

The jar stood calmly.

The cloth was damp, but not wet. The edge had not lifted. The labels held. On the surface behind the glass there were a few bubbles. Or more than in the morning. Or the light simply fell that way.

She raised her hand.

Again.

This time by right.

Evening — Sofia.

That was what was written.

She could remove the cloth.

Could check.

Could smell, look, call Lina, tell Thomas that everything was normal, or the opposite, not normal. Could finally do what she had been waiting all day to do.

Her hand stopped.

Not because she had decided.

Because she suddenly heard the stove.

Not a voice.

Not a sign.

Just the quiet uneven breathing of fire behind iron. The wind pressed into the pipe and let go. The house accepted cold, gave off warmth, held water in wet wood, the smell of apples on the table, sour air by the jar, damp wool on the back of a chair, human steps, breath, weariness.

All of that was condition.

Not only the shelf.

Not only the cloth.

Not only her.

Sofia lowered her hand.

“Aren’t you going to look?” Thomas asked.

There was no reproach in his voice.

Only fear that had already grown tired of looking reasonable.

Sofia turned to Lina.

“Is it needed?”

Lina came over.

Not to the jar at once.

First to the shelf. Then to the window. Then to the stove. Then ran her fingers over the cloth without lifting it.

“No.”

“Why?” Thomas asked.

Lina looked at the jar.

“Too many hands.”

The words lay down quietly.

Nick did not joke.

Even he understood that the phrase had already done all the work, and adding his wit to it would be like sprinkling sugar over moss.

Thomas sat down.

David looked at the list.

Eva covered the bread with a towel.

Marek closed the stove door.

And at that moment Sofia saw the glint.

Not in the fire.

In the dark glass of the window, where the kitchen was reflected.

First the modern one: shelf, jar, people, list by the door, wet jackets, wood, bucket.

Then — another room.

Not quite a house.

Not quite a hut.

A low dark place where the ceiling holds smoke and the walls smell of earth, wool, and old grain. By the hearth sits a boy. Or a young man. Sofia could not tell. He has thin wrists and the adult face of someone who was too early entrusted not to let something die.

Before him are embers.

He touches them with a stick.

Once.

Again.

A third time.

The older woman sleeps by the wall, but not all the way. That is how those sleep who long ago learned to leave one ear for the world. She opens her eyes.

“Do not disturb it,” she says.

He freezes.

“I’m watching so it doesn’t go out.”

“I see.”

“If I don’t watch, it will go out.”

The woman is silent for a long time.

In the corner someone coughs. Behind the wall the wind pushes at the roof. Near the door stands a vessel of water, and on its side the trace of a hand darkens.

The boy moves the embers again.

They flare briefly, nervously, as if the fire had awakened not from strength, but from being shoved.

The woman rises.

She is not angry.

That, as always, is worse.

She comes to the hearth, takes the stick from him, and lays it beside them.

“Fire is kept with wood,” she says. “Not with eyes.”

The boy looks at the embers.

Then at the stick.

Then at his own hands.

And for the first time understands that all night he had been guarding not the fire, but his own fear of darkness.

The glint left.

In the kitchen someone set a mug on the table.

The sound was too loud.

Sofia blinked.

On the window glass there was again only their kitchen. Evening. Damp. A little tired.

Nora was looking at her.

Not at the window.

At Sofia.

“What?” Sofia asked.

Nora shook her head slightly.

“Nothing.”

Then looked at the stove, where the log was still hissing.

“It needs wood,” she said.

Sofia did not move.

Nick slowly turned his head.

“I am going to try very hard not to participate.”

“In what?” Nora asked.

“I don’t know. That is the problem.”

Sofia wanted to ask Nora why she had said it.

She did not ask.

She already knew that the question would make the phrase an object. And today they had touched objects too often as it was.

Lina took a log from the basket and handed it to Marek.

“Add it.”

Marek put it in the stove.

The fire did not accept it at once.

First the log hissed, releasing water. Then the edge blackened. Then a thin line of flame appeared, small, almost timid. It moved along the bark slowly, as if testing not the wood, but its right to be.

For some reason everyone watched.

Then Thomas was the first to look away.

“I thought if there was a schedule, it would become calmer,” he said.

“Did it?” Eva asked.

He smiled.

Very tiredly.

“No.”

“Then it’s an honest schedule,” Nick said.

This time no one stopped him.

David went to the list.

“Take it down?” he asked.

Lina shook her head.

“Why?”

“If it gets in the way.”

“It doesn’t.”

“But...”

“Hands get in the way.”

David looked at his hands.

There was no pencil in them.

That was good.

And still they did not know where to go.

“Then leave it?” he asked.

“Leave it.”

“And what do we do?”

Lina smiled slightly.

Not softly.

Almost tiredly.

“Live beside it.”

The phrase could have become too beautiful.

But at that moment Marek dropped a chip, Nick sneezed from the smoke, and the door in the entryway opened from the wind again.

Beauty did not have time to put on clean clothes.

Marek went to close the door.

Cold passed over the floor in a thin layer. Sofia felt it through her socks.

The jar stood on the shelf.

No one touched it.

And still everything around touched it: the stove, the door, the wet wood, cold water, people coming in and out, bread being cut thinner, air that did not know how to stay the same.

In the evening they ate soup.

Simple. Potatoes, onions, a little fish, herbs Lina added at the end. There was less fish than one would have liked. Nick said it was “soup with the memory of fish,” but ate two portions and did not develop the theme. Thomas ate slowly. David crumbled bread into his bowl and stopped himself when he wanted to take another piece.

Sofia noticed that.

Probably because all day everyone had been noticing everything unnecessary.

After eating, no one went to the jar at once.

That was almost an event.

Then Thomas looked after all.

From afar.

Lina saw.

Did not stop him.

Sometimes looking from afar was exactly the distance the living thing could bear.

Later, when the table had been cleared, David went to the list and straightened it.

Not because it had slipped.

Just because.

The sheet hung straight.

Too straight for a wet wall, old tape, and a northern evening.

David stepped back.

Looked.

Did not smile.

“Good?” Nick asked.

“Straight.”

“Those are different things, but a worthy beginning.”

David nodded.

“Yes.”

He was not offended.

That too was new weather.

Ivar appeared late.

So quietly that it was not Sofia who noticed him first, but the house: the door closed differently, footsteps entered the entryway without fuss, a wet jacket hung on a hook. He brought in from outside the smell of cold bark and water. Looked at the people, at the wood by the wall, at the floor by the door where dark tracks still remained, then at the list.

“Watch,” he said.

Not as a question.

Thomas straightened.

“So we don’t lose it again.”

Ivar nodded.

“Clear.”

He came to the jar.

Not close.

Looked at how the cloth lay.

Then at the stove.

Then at the door.

Then at the wet logs by the wall.

It seemed to Sofia that he was looking not at the objects separately, but at the distances between them.

“Give it time,” he said.

And nothing more.

Thomas waited for continuation.

David, it seemed, did too.

Nick definitely waited, because he had already prepared the face of a person who would suffer from wisdom, but bravely.

There was no continuation.

Ivar took a bowl, poured himself some cold soup, and sat at the table.

It was almost cruel.

Because everyone had to remain beside the unsaid themselves.

Sofia stepped to the window.

The kitchen was reflected in the glass. People moved slowly. The stove burned. The jar stood. The list hung by the door. Beyond the glass was darkness in which the rain could no longer be seen, only heard, if one stayed silent long enough.

She looked at the list.

MORNING — EVA.

DAY — THOMAS.

EVENING — SOFIA.

The letters were careful. Not too large. Not imperious. Rather frightened of their own usefulness.

Sofia thought that tomorrow someone would look out of turn anyway.

Not from malice.

Not from distrust.

She did not think further.

Behind the wall the wind changed again. In the pipe it pulled dully downward, and the flame in the stove bent briefly. Then rose. Then lowered again.

Marek said:

“I’ll have to get up in the night.”

“To the jar?” Thomas asked too quickly.

Marek looked at him.

“To the stove.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“This is how growing up is born. Painfully and without a certificate.”

Thomas smiled tiredly.

This time — almost for real.

Later everyone began to disperse.

Not to sleep yet.

Just into different corners of the house: someone for warm socks, someone for a dry jacket, someone to check the window in the far room, someone to carry wet towels out of the entryway. The house was no longer a background where the jar stood. It had become what constantly required small participation and promised no clarity in return.

Sofia stayed in the kitchen last.

Almost.

Lina was still putting the knives away.

“You really won’t look?” Sofia asked.

Lina put the knives into the drawer.

“I will.”

“When?”

“When it’s needed.”

“And how will you know?”

Lina closed the drawer.

Thought.

Not choosing a beautiful answer.

Simply letting an honest answer arrive on foot.

“I won’t always know.”

Sofia nodded.

This was unexpectedly easier to accept than certainty.

Lina wiped the table.

She left.

Sofia stayed.

By the side wall the jar stood under cloth. The labels whitened on the glass. The words still protected it from one old mistake. The list by the door protected it from another.

And still, between the jar and the house, there remained something that held neither on words, nor on rotation, nor on the right place.

Heat left through the cracks.

Water stood in the bucket, cold to the bottom.

The wood hissed in the stove because there was still damp inside it.

Wet jackets hung heavily by the door.

Sofia listened.

The house was not quiet.

It was working.

Creaking, breathing, cooling, accepting fire, releasing warmth, holding people, water, bread, the sour smell under cloth, fears that walked in circles and called themselves care.

She went to the list.

Did not straighten it.

Only looked.

By evening the schedule hung straight.

The house was cooling anyway.
