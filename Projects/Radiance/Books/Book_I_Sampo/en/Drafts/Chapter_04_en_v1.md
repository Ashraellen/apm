# Chapter Four. Surface

They did not go to the water at once.

And for some reason that was right.

After Ivar’s words, everyone seemed to expect the house to gather itself immediately, put on jackets, zip up zippers, and move toward the lake like a group that had been given a route and a meaning. But the house did not like abrupt transitions. It still held breakfast inside itself: crumbs on the board, the faint smell of butter, warm air by the stove, cups set down not where they had stood before the meal, and damp traces from firewood on the floor.

Marek was washing the knife.

Lina wiped the table without hurry, as if she were not cleaning after breakfast, but returning its own surface to the wood. Eva gathered plates. David was trying to find a place where his phone would at least imitate signal, and stood by the window with one hand raised, looking like a person receiving a message from a very ancient and dissatisfied civilization.

Thomas sat quietly.

Before him was an empty plate.

Sofia saw white traces of crumbs, a small butter stain, the thin line from the knife at the edge. Nothing unusual. And still Thomas’s empty plate was no longer simply empty. The slice that no one wanted to call evidence had already been there.

Nick stood by the door, fastening his jacket, and could not get the button into the buttonhole.

“The north is testing fine motor skills,” he said.

“You’ve been fighting for a whole minute,” Sofia noted.

“I am not fighting. I am negotiating.”

“With a button?”

“With circumstances.”

The button finally went in.

Nick looked at it suspiciously, as if the victory had happened too easily.

Ivar was in no hurry.

He stood by the stove, slightly to one side, and waited until the little tasks in the house came to an end. He did not urge anyone on. Did not explain why they could not go out immediately. Did not create the pause on purpose. He simply allowed things to finish. This was different from the waiting of people who want to look patient. Their patience always rings a little. With Ivar, nothing rang.

Nora brought a small enamel mug from the shelf. White, with a blue rim. On its side was a chip that looked like a small map of an unknown shore.

“What for?” David asked.

Nora looked at the mug as if she herself were not sure.

“It may come in useful.”

Nick turned.

“After this morning, that phrase is high-risk.”

“Not for reserve,” Nora said.

“Then for what?”

She lifted one shoulder.

“For water.”

It sounded too simple to argue with.

Sofia noticed that Thomas looked at the mug longer than the others.

Not with interest.

With wariness.

After the slice, any object that could hold something “for later” became suspicious.

Ivar took an old dark jacket from the bench, put it on, adjusted the collar, and was the first to step out onto the porch.

Not like a guide.

Rather like someone who knows the door opens outward.

Outside, the day had not grown lighter. It had simply stopped being morning. The grey sky hung low and even. There was no rain, but the air was so saturated with damp that it seemed enough to pass a hand through it and a thin film would remain on the palm. The house stood among the wet grass like a large dark thing the forest had temporarily permitted to be placed here, but had not recognized as its own.

A path led to the lake from the back porch.

Not wide. Not tended. Not touristic. It was the kind of path that does not so much lead as agree to be walked on. First it passed between the shed and the low bushes where Nora had gathered berries that morning. Then it turned toward the birches, crossed a damp patch where boards had been thrown straight onto the ground, and descended toward where water was already appearing between the trunks.

Not everyone came out.

Someone stayed in the house to wash dishes. Someone said they would catch up. Someone simply said nothing and did not come. Ivar did not count. Marek did not either. There was relief in that: no one turned the walk to the water into a rite of attendance.

Sofia walked beside Eva.

A little ahead were Ivar and Nora. Nora carried the enamel mug by its handle with two fingers, and the mug knocked softly against her thigh. Thomas walked behind them, silent, his hands in his pockets. Nick walked to the side of Sofia, but this time did not try to keep the conversation on the surface. That too was noticeable. Several times he opened his mouth as if about to say something, then looked at the path and closed it.

David caught up with them by the birches.

“I’m coming after all,” he said.

“We noticed,” Nick replied.

“Just reporting.”

“For the record?”

“For inner order.”

“Then order is accepted.”

The joke came out weaker than usual.

And strangely, because of that, it was better.

The path sprang underfoot. Somewhere beneath the moss there was water. It did not show itself, but answered the steps with a barely audible squelch. Almost nothing here was dry. Even the stones seemed damp from within. On one dark, flat stone lay a yellow leaf. Its edges had already begun to darken, but the middle still held its colour. For some reason Sofia thought that autumn did not arrive here all at once. It first laid down one leaf, watched how it was received, and then decided whether it was worth continuing.

The lake did not open all at once.

First there was more grey between the trunks. Then the air ahead lost depth. Then the water appeared as a strip, so still that for the first few seconds it seemed not water, but an empty place where someone had forgotten to draw in the forest.

Sofia stopped.

The lake lay low.

It did not shine. Did not call. Was not beautiful in the sense in which beautiful places like to be immediately understood. Grey, cold, broad, with a dark band of forest on the far shore. Near the bank the water was almost transparent, but not with the cheerful transparency of southern rivers; it was northern transparency: showing stones and weeds without wanting to be liked.

The surface was even.

Almost.

Sometimes a slight ripple passed over it, though no wind could be felt. The ripple was born somewhere in the middle, moved slowly toward the shore, and disappeared by the grass, leaving the water flat again, like a closed face.

Nick stood beside Sofia.

“An unfriendly lake,” he said quietly.

“It isn’t obliged to be friendly.”

“Neither am I. Yet people still criticize me.”

Sofia looked at him.

He shrugged.

“I said it quietly. That’s progress.”

Ivar went down to the water itself.

The shore was low, with wet grass and flat stones. He did not begin to speak. He simply crouched and touched the water with the tips of his fingers. Not checking the temperature, not performing a gesture, not calling anyone to look. He touched it — and took his hand away.

Nora set the mug on a stone.

The white enamel immediately became too visible against the grey shore.

Thomas looked at the mug.

“Are we going to drink from this?” he asked.

“You don’t have to drink,” Ivar said.

“That isn’t what I mean.”

“Then what do you mean?”

Thomas did not find the answer at once himself.

“I am trying to understand what we are doing here.”

Ivar looked at the water.

“Trying to understand.”

“Is that an answer?”

“No.”

Nick breathed out softly through his nose, but did not insert a joke.

Sofia noticed this and for some reason was grateful to him.

Eva sat on a fallen trunk a little higher above the shore. David stood beside her, hands in his pockets. He looked as if he were trying not to look like a person who was cold.

“Just looking?” he asked.

Ivar picked up a small stone from the shore. Grey, flat, with a black vein.

“At first, yes.”

“And then?”

“Then the hand will do something by itself.”

He threw the stone into the water.

Not far. The stone entered almost without sound. Circles moved over the surface. One, then another, then a third. They spread slowly, each next one wider, weaker, thinner. Then the circles reached the grass, touched the reflection of the birches, and disappeared.

The water became even again.

But Sofia could already see that the evenness was not the same.

It was not the absence of movement.

It was what knows how to close after movement.

“You may scoop,” Ivar said.

He spoke quietly, almost past them.

“You may not take away.”

Nick turned his head.

Thomas too.

Ivar did not explain.

Nora took the mug and held it out to Sofia.

Sofia did not understand at once what she was supposed to do.

“To me?”

“If you want.”

Sofia took the mug.

The enamel was cold, the handle thin. The mug looked too small before the lake. Even ridiculous. A human thing that could be forgotten in the kitchen, dropped, chipped, washed, placed on a shelf. The lake did not notice it. Or noticed it the way the sea notices a shell: without offence, without interest, without the need to acknowledge it.

Sofia crouched by the water.

The stone was wet. Cold passed at once through the fabric of her trousers to her knee. She tilted the mug and scooped.

The water entered easily.

Too easily.

Just now it had been lake. Now it had become a mug of water.

That was almost indecent.

Sofia lifted the mug.

The surface inside it trembled, reflecting the grey sky, a piece of her face, and the dark line of the shore. Everything fit. Sky, face, forest — everything became small, round, trembling, enclosed inside the blue rim.

“There,” David said. “Taken away.”

Ivar looked at him.

Not sharply.

“The mug?”

David wanted to object, but stopped.

Sofia looked at the water in the mug.

She suddenly understood that she was not holding the lake.

Not even part of the lake.

She was holding water that had accepted the shape of the mug for a few seconds.

“Drink, if you want,” Nora said.

“Is it cold?”

“I don’t know.”

“You brought the mug.”

“The mug does not report in advance.”

Nick said quietly:

“Nora, today you are almost practical.”

“No.”

“Right. I got carried away.”

Sofia brought the mug to her lips.

The water smelled of iron, stone, and something green. Not strongly. Barely. She took a small sip.

Cold entered her mouth, her throat, her chest. Not icy. Simply clear. Such cold does not punish. It separates one thing from another. Before the sip and after. Inside and outside. I want and I take.

Sofia lowered the mug.

For one second it seemed to her that the water inside remained higher than the level it should have had after a sip.

She blinked.

The level was ordinary.

Of course.

She held the mug out to Thomas.

Thomas did not take it.

“Later.”

Nick looked at him, but said nothing.

After the slice, the word “later” had become too loud.

Thomas heard that himself.

He took the mug after all.

His fingers touched Sofia’s fingers. Quickly, almost by accident. The mug trembled, and small circles moved across the surface of the water inside it. Thomas looked at them so closely, as if he feared one of the circles would say more about him than he was ready to hear.

He did not drink.

Simply held it.

“It is heavy,” he said at last.

David gave a short laugh.

“A mug of water?”

“Yes.”

David wanted to say something, but looked at Thomas and changed his mind.

Ivar picked up another stone, but did not throw it. Turned it in his fingers and set it back down.

“The palm is lighter,” he said.

Thomas looked at him.

“What?”

Ivar reached his hand to the water and scooped with his palm.

For a moment the water lay in his broad palm, transparent, trembling, with a tiny sky inside it. Then it began to leave between his fingers. First in thin threads. Then faster. After a few seconds the palm was wet and empty.

“The palm takes for a short while,” Ivar said.

He wiped his hand on the grass.

Sofia looked at his fingers.

She remembered the bread.

A slice could be left on a plate. One could not eat it. One could turn it into reserve, into shame, into warm waiting. Water in the palm could not be left. It left at once, without accusation and without question. Even if a person wanted to hold it — there was nothing to hold with.

Nora crouched beside the water and also scooped with her palm.

It went badly. The water almost immediately spilled over the edge of her palm; her sleeve became wet.

“There,” she said.

“What there?” Nick asked.

“I hold badly.”

Nick looked at her wet sleeve.

“But honestly.”

Nora nodded, as if that were an entirely sufficient result.

Thomas finally drank from the mug.

Not much. A small sip. Then another. Then he set the mug on the stone.

“If you pour it into a bottle,” David said, “you can carry it away.”

“You can,” Ivar said.

“So you can take it after all.”

“The bottle.”

David frowned.

“But the water will be inside.”

“For a while.”

“And if you close it?”

Ivar looked at him calmly.

“Then it will stop being lake sooner.”

David fell silent.

Nick raised his eyebrows, but said nothing. Apparently he understood that a joke now would be too cheap.

Sofia looked at the mug.

Blue rim. Chip. Water inside. Lake outside.

She suddenly felt that it was here something new began. Not in the lake, even, but in the person who does not want to bend down to water every time. Who is tired of depending on the shore, the weather, the path, someone else’s permission. Who wants the water to remain.

At least a little.

At least in a form.

She raised her eyes.

The birches were reflected on the water.

Their trunks trembled, broke on the ripples, gathered again. The sky lay between them like grey skin. In the reflection everyone stood longer than in life: Ivar — a dark vertical by the shore, Thomas — a leaning figure with the mug at his feet, Nora — a patch of grey sweater, Nick — slightly to the side, as if even his reflection kept distance.

Sofia looked at herself.

Her face in the water was not a face, but the possibility of a face. The features blurred when the ripple touched the surface. The eyes darkened, the mouth disappeared, the chin broke against the water’s movement. She leaned closer.

The reflection of her hand on the water, for a moment, became someone else’s.

Not completely.

Not enough to cry out.

It was simply that the fingers in the reflection had become longer, the joints rougher, the skin darker, and instead of her palm, above the water, there seemed to hang that same hand from the snow — the hand that could still have left, but was already reaching toward the warm earth.

Sofia blinked.

In the water was her hand.

With the sleeve of her jacket.

With the thin strip of earth under the nail, almost gone but not entirely.

“What?” Nick asked.

He had noticed.

Of course he had noticed.

“Nothing.”

He looked at the water.

“That expensive word again.”

“Quiet.”

He really became quiet.

Circles appeared in the middle of the lake.

Not from a stone. Not from a fish — at least Sofia saw no splash. Simply circles. One, then another, then a third. They spread from a point where the surface had been even before, and moved slowly, as if someone below had touched the water with a finger.

Everyone watched.

Even David stopped pretending that he was merely interested.

The circles reached the space between the shore and the far forest, weakened, and disappeared.

“A fish?” Nick asked.

“Maybe,” Eva said.

“I like that ‘maybe.’ There is still a chance of normality in it.”

Nick said this quietly. Not for laughter.

Ivar did not answer.

He was looking not at the circles, but at those who were looking at them.

Sofia suddenly understood that the lake was not showing them anything. Not in the human sense. It was not making a sign. Not proving. Not comforting. Not frightening. It was simply water: depth, surface, cold, movement, stones on the bottom, roots by the shore, reflection of the sky.

And people already brought their questions to it themselves.

Thomas lifted the mug and unexpectedly splashed the remaining water back into the lake.

The water from the mug struck the surface in a small bright patch. Circles spread. They mixed with the others, already almost gone, and after a few seconds it was impossible to understand which circles had come from the lake and which from Thomas’s hand.

“Why?” David asked.

Thomas looked at the empty mug.

“I don’t know.”

Nora took the mug from him and rinsed it in the lake.

“Now it’s empty again,” she said.

“Is that good?” Nick asked.

“I don’t know.”

She set the mug upside down on the stone.

Water ran down the white enamel in thin paths. On the chip one drop held longer than the others, trembled, did not let go.

For some reason Sofia looked precisely at it.

The drop held on the damaged place.

Where the enamel was missing.

Then it fell after all.

Onto the stone.

And disappeared, leaving a dark point.

“And if the vessel is damaged?” Sofia asked.

She had not meant to ask.

The question came out by itself.

Ivar looked at the mug.

Then at Sofia.

“Then it shows what it held.”

“Through the crack?”

“Through loss.”

Sofia felt Nora beside her stop moving.

Even Nick did not joke.

Thomas lowered his eyes.

David turned toward the water.

Ivar said it dryly, almost wearily, like a person who would rather not have said it at all, but since the question had come out, something honest had to be set beside it.

The wind finally reached the shore.

Weak. Real. It passed over the water and crumpled the reflections. The birches trembled. Sofia’s face disappeared. Ivar became a dark patch. Nora — grey. Nick — a breakable line. For one second no one was reflected as a person.

Then the wind stilled.

The surface gathered itself again.

“Shall we go farther?” Eva asked.

“Where?” Nick said.

“Along the shore.”

Ivar nodded.

They did not move at once. First each of them seemed to return the body to himself: someone adjusted a sleeve, someone fastened a jacket, someone picked up the mug, someone looked at their boots. Then they set off along the water.

The shore was uneven.

In places the grass came right down to the water, and they had to walk over hummocks. In places flat stones opened out, wet and smooth, easy to slip on. Several times Sofia grabbed at branches. One branch turned out to be dry and broke in her hand. The sound was short, unpleasantly clear.

Nick walked beside her, but now he stayed silent longer.

For the first time that morning Sofia did not try to understand whether that was good or bad.

Thomas fell behind. He walked closer to the water and sometimes looked at his own steps, as if checking whether he left traces where there should be none. Nora walked behind him with the mug. For some reason this looked almost like care, although she was doing nothing for him.

David took out his phone after all and photographed the lake.

Then looked at the screen.

“Strange,” he said.

Nick came alive.

“At last technology enters into dispute with antiquity.”

“In the photo everything is ordinary.”

“What a twist.”

“No, I mean it. Too ordinary. Even prettier than it is.”

Sofia came over to look.

On the screen was the lake: grey, calm, correct. Birches, stones, water, sky. The photo really was prettier. Cleaner. More convenient. There was none of the wet cold in it, none of the heaviness of air, none of Thomas’s uneven breathing, none of Nora with the mug, none of Ivar, who was silent too precisely. In it the lake had become a place.

Here it was a presence.

“Delete it,” Nora said.

David raised his head.

“Why?”

She became embarrassed.

“I don’t know. Don’t delete it if you need it.”

“I don’t need it.”

He himself looked at the screen for another second and put the phone away.

He did not delete it.

But he did not take any more photographs.

The narrow path brought them to a small wooden landing. Apparently in summer people entered the water here. The landing was old; the boards had darkened, some lifted at the edges. At the end lay an overturned boat. Low, wooden, with peeling green paint. Beneath it smelled of damp, fish, and old summer.

“Does the boat work?” Nick asked.

Marek, who somehow was already standing by the landing, although Sofia had not noticed how he had caught up with them, answered:

“If one does not expect too much from it.”

“Is that still about the boat?”

“For now, yes.”

Nick nodded.

“It is pleasant to have clarity at least in navigation.”

Ivar came up to the boat and ran his hand along the side. Green paint remained on his fingers as barely visible dust.

“Do they launch it in summer?” Sofia asked.

“Sometimes,” Marek said.

“And now?”

“Now it is heavier.”

“Because of the water?”

“Because of autumn.”

Sofia did not ask for clarification.

Here the shore was deeper. The water by the landing no longer showed stones. The surface became darker. Not black, no, simply deprived of transparency. Here it no longer allowed one to look down.

Thomas approached the edge of the landing.

“If you throw something here,” he said, “it will sink quickly.”

“Not everything,” Eva replied.

“I mean something heavy.”

“Heavy things almost always know where they belong.”

Nick looked at her.

“That was unexpectedly severe.”

Eva shrugged.

“After breakfast I am not in the best shape for sweet observations.”

Thomas picked up a small rusty nail from the landing.

Nick said at once:

“Don’t.”

Thomas froze.

“What?”

“Don’t throw it.”

“I wasn’t going to.”

“You already were.”

Sofia saw that Nick was right.

Thomas held the nail between his fingers exactly the way one holds an object before throwing it. Perhaps he had not wanted to. Perhaps he had wanted to test it. Perhaps his hand had simply chosen faster than his head.

Ivar stood behind them.

He did not interfere.

Thomas looked at the nail.

“Iron again,” he said quietly.

Nick lowered his eyes.

The nail was small. Nothing like the hook from the night’s manifestation. Just an old nail that had fallen out of a board. Rust had eaten its head; the tip was blunt. But in Thomas’s hand it suddenly became too intentional.

“Put it down,” Sofia said.

Thomas put it down.

Did not throw it.

Onto the board.

The sound was small.

But everyone heard it.

Ivar finally said:

“Sometimes the beginning is not when a person takes. It is when he checks whether he can throw.”

Marek looked at him.

Ivar fell silent.

After that they walked more slowly.

The lake accompanied them on the left. Sometimes close, sometimes behind bushes. The surface changed. Where the shore was open, the water seemed metallic. Where branches hung over it, it became green and almost motionless. In one place yellow leaves floated on it. They moved not together but each separately, like small boats without purpose.

Sofia grew tired.

Not in the body. In the body the walk was easy. She was tired from the fact that every ordinary phenomenon could now turn out not to be ordinary, and there was no way to know in advance where one could simply look, and where looking already meant participating.

She fell behind the group.

Nick noticed, but did not come up. Only slowed ahead.

Eva turned around.

Sofia waved to her: everything was fine.

Nothing was fine, but the gesture was useful.

She came to the very edge of the water, where alder roots came out of the earth and went into the lake. Between the roots the water was quieter. Almost standing still. On the surface lay a thin film — not dirt, not oil, simply smoothness separating air from depth.

Sofia crouched.

The water reflected her face in pieces.

One eye. Half a mouth. The line of hair. Grey sky between them.

She stretched out her hand and touched the surface.

The skin entered the water without resistance.

And everything immediately fell apart.

Face, sky, roots, shore.

Circles moved from her finger.

Sofia held her hand in the water.

The cold was stronger than at the first shore. Or it seemed so to her. Her fingers quickly began to go numb. She could have taken her hand away, but did not.

The water did not hold her.

Did not push her away.

Simply flowed around her.

That was worse than resistance.

Resistance at least recognizes you as a force. The water did not argue. It allowed the hand to be inside and at the same time did not become hers.

Sofia slowly closed her palm.

Caught water.

Lifted it.

It immediately ran between her fingers. First cold, then almost warm — from the skin. Drops streamed down her wrist into the sleeve. Very little remained in her palm. She looked at the small trembling puddle in which a piece of grey sky fit.

“You can take it,” she said to herself.

The water left.

“For a short while.”

She did not know whether she had said it or remembered it.

Ivar came up quietly behind her.

She did not turn around.

“Did you hear?” he asked.

“What?”

“How the hand stopped arguing.”

Sofia looked at her wet palm.

“It did not stop.”

“Then good.”

“Why good?”

“Then it is still alive.”

He did not explain.

And that was enough.

They returned to the others when Marek was already turning the group back toward the house. Nick stood by the landing pretending to be looking at the boat. Thomas looked at the nail on the board. Nora held the mug by the handle; it was already empty, but for some reason Nora carried it more carefully than if it had been full.

On the way back almost no one spoke.

The silence was neither heavy nor light. It simply walked with them, putting its feet on the damp path. Sometimes a branch cracked. Sometimes someone slipped. Once David did swear after all when water from the grass got into his boot. Nick opened his mouth, but Eva looked at him and he changed his mind.

By the house Sofia turned around.

The lake had almost disappeared behind the trees.

Only a strip of grey was visible between the trunks. Ordinary water. A northern lake. No sign. No answer. No machine, no miracle, no revealed law.

But Sofia knew: if she closed her eyes now, she would not see the whole lake.

She would see the mug.

The chip in the enamel.

The drop that had lingered on the damaged place.

The nail on the board.

The palm from which water had left before it had time to become hers.

At the porch Nora stopped and turned the mug upside down on the railing. The last drop flowed out of it. Fell onto the wood and vanished instantly.

“Empty?” Nick asked.

Nora looked inside the mug.

“No.”

He lifted an eyebrow.

“There’s nothing in it.”

“It’s wet in there.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“Nothing to object with.”

Ivar climbed the porch last.

Before going in, he shook water from his fingers. Several drops fell onto the boards. One fell into the dark crack between them and disappeared.

Sofia thought that the crack had held it longer than the palm.

And was immediately frightened by the thought.

Because somewhere nearby, still without form, a desire had already stirred.

For the source not to leave between the fingers.

For there to be something nearby capable of holding.

The house accepted them with warm air, the smell of the stove, wet wool, and the bread left after breakfast.

The loaf still lay on the table.

Sofia looked at it and for the first time that morning did not immediately want to understand how much could be taken.

She wanted water.

But Nora’s mug stood upside down on the porch.

And the lake remained there, behind the trees.

Whole.

Grey.

Not carried away.
