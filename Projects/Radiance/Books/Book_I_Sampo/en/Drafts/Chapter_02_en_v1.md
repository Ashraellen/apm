# Chapter Two. Warm Snow

At first Sofia decided it was a root that had cracked.

That happens, after all. Old trees do not stand only above the earth. Beneath the earth they hold whole invisible cities: they pull water, argue with stones, interlace, rot, grow through their own death, and sometimes, perhaps, break in such a way that a person above takes it for a sign.

A root could have cracked.

Then she decided it had been a stone.

Somewhere under the moss, under the damp soil, under that soft layer that seemed kind only to someone who had never fallen onto northern ground with his knees, a stone could have shifted. Stones, too, are not obliged to lie forever in the way convenient to human balance.

Then Sofia remembered that stones do not turn that slowly.

And certainly not as if, before turning, they had listened.

The fire sank lower.

It did not go out. It did not become frightened. It simply gathered inward. The flame no longer rose merrily and unevenly, no longer grabbed at the air, no longer crackled with small domestic sounds. It lay between the stones denser, darker, like an animal that had stopped moving not because it had fallen asleep, but because it had heard a step beyond the light.

Nick beside Sofia was silent.

That was the most convincing proof that what was happening had crossed the boundary of the ordinary. While Nick joked, the world still retained the possibility of being called the world. While he chose a cutting phrase, reality could pretend it was merely badly lit. But now he sat motionless, looking into the fire and keeping his mouth closed so carefully, as if he were afraid that what might escape from it would not be a word, but a confession.

Thomas raised his head.

“Was that beneath us?”

Nick wanted to answer. Sofia saw it in his shoulder: a slight movement, habitual, almost automatic. The phrase had probably already reached his tongue, put on its boots, and prepared to go out.

But it did not go out.

Ivar sat slightly to the side on a low chopping block. He was not looking at Thomas. He was not looking at the ground. He was not doing what a person ought to do after arranging an impressive effect and now waiting for the proper fear. His palms lay calmly on his knees. The fire lit his large face unevenly: cheekbone, silver in the hair, shadow by the shaved temple, the heavy line of the mouth.

He was silent.

And that silence did not ask for respect.

It simply weighed more than questions.

Thomas opened his mouth again, but Marek looked at him once, and the question remained where it was. Not out of fear. Rather from the sudden understanding that any clarification now would become smaller than what had already happened.

Sofia smelled bread.

It did not return at once. At first she thought it was the remnant of the previous sensation, the trace of that impossible warm crust that had already once passed through the circle and spoiled everyone’s right to simple smoke. But the smell did not dissolve. On the contrary, it grew deeper. Not stronger — deeper. As if the bread were not nearby, not behind them, not in the kitchen of the house, but somewhere below, under the roots, and its warmth was rising slowly through the earth.

She looked at her hands.

Her fingers were clean.

Of course they were clean.

Still, she rubbed her thumb against her forefinger.

“Do not start by looking for what turned,” Ivar said.

His voice was low, slightly hoarse, but not tired. Rather, it was the kind of voice that comes after long silence, when words still remember the price of coming out.

Nick exhaled quietly.

“What should we do?”

It did not sound like a joke.

Ivar looked at him.

“First, hear.”

After that it became quieter.

Not only around them. Inside too.

Sofia suddenly understood that until now she had not been listening to the movement under the earth, but to her own attempts to explain it. Root. Stone. Soil. Water. Tiredness. Road. North. Atmosphere. Words ran between her and what had happened like frightened people between a door and a window.

When they grew tired, something else remained.

Under the moss it was not quiet.

Something there continued to be.

Not to move. Not to creak. Not to sound. Simply to be present so deeply that the whole surface of the earth seemed thin and too trusting.

“At first there was no object,” Ivar said.

The flame trembled.

“At first there was a rumor.”

Sofia blinked.

The fire remained fire.

The stones remained stones.

People sat in a circle, wrapped in blankets, with travel-tiredness in their shoulders, smoke in their hair, and awkward hands that had nothing left to do.

And still something had already shifted.

Not under the earth now.

In the deepest part of attention itself.

“A rumor about Sampo?” Eva asked.

She said it quietly, almost without a question. That is how people ask not because they do not know, but because they allow a word to pass through the circle and become shared.

Ivar shook his head.

“About enough.”

Lina lowered her eyes.

David stopped turning the dry blade of grass in his fingers.

Nora, who until then had been looking into the forest, turned toward the fire.

“Are those different things?” Thomas asked.

The question was careful. Thomas’s first truly careful question of the evening.

Ivar did not answer at once.

“For the well-fed — yes.”

He added nothing more.

And that proved enough.

Sofia looked at the fire and suddenly saw snow.

She did not imagine it. Did not remember it. Did not picture it the way one pictures an illustration to a story. The snow did not appear over the flame, and not in place of it. It appeared where, for one instant, the fire became deeper than itself.

White.

Grey.

Almost blue.

A night in which there was not yet any beautiful northern silence, because hunger spoils beauty before a person has time to call it landscape.

Snow lay on the roof of a low house, on a crooked fence, on a woodpile poorly covered, on an empty barrel by the wall. It lay on the shoulders of a man standing at the door of a neighbouring house.

The man was not a hero.

That was clear at once.

Not because he was ugly, weak, or pitiful. No. He did not have that convenient mark by which a story tells the reader in advance: this is the one to worry about. He stood with his head drawn into his shoulders and held his hands empty.

For some reason that was the most frightening thing of all.

Empty hands.

There was no guilt in them yet.

And that made it easier for them to believe they had the right.

The door of the neighbouring house did not open at once. First light passed behind it. Then a shadow. Then a man, older than the one standing outside, came out onto the threshold and did not invite him in.

Sofia did not hear the words.

And still she understood.

The request was not the first.

Nor was the refusal.

Snow fell between them sparsely, as if even the sky were saving itself.

The man on the threshold shook his head.

The one standing outside smiled.

The smile was small, tired, almost funny. With such a smile a person tries to leave himself a door back into dignity when he already stands before someone else’s store and knows he has not come to bargain.

He said something. Probably a joke.

The neighbour did not smile.

Then he closed the door.

He did not slam it.

Not rudely.

He simply closed it.

That made it worse.

The man remained in the snow.

For a while he looked at the door. Then he lowered his eyes to his hands, as if only now noticing they were empty.

The fire snapped.

Sofia flinched and saw the circle again.

The fire. The lake beyond the trees. Faint smoke. People. The blanket on her knees. Nick beside her, too motionless. Thomas with his glasses in his hand. David leaning forward. Ivar sitting slightly to the side, as if he had not shown anything, but had merely allowed them to see what had already been standing behind the thin wall of fire.

“Was he hungry?” Sofia asked.

She had not meant to speak.

The question came out by itself. Quieter than she expected.

Ivar looked at her.

“No.”

Sofia felt irritation. Almost a relieving irritation: at last, something could be refused.

“Then why did he come?”

“Because soon he would be hungry.”

Nick turned his head slightly.

“That is worse.”

Ivar did not answer him.

And rightly so.

Nick’s phrase did not require an answer. It required space.

Space was found.

The flame became deeper again.

The man was walking home.

Now Sofia saw him from behind. The snow creaked under his feet, but the sound was not winter-clean, not pure, not the kind with which beautiful stories about frost like to begin. There was too much calculation in that creaking. Each step seemed to ask: how much farther to the house, how much farther to morning, how much farther to the day when a request becomes humiliation, and humiliation becomes a right.

His house was not poorer than the others.

That, too, became clear.

A low roof. Smoke from the chimney. Dry firewood by the wall, but not much. A fish net put away badly. Several sacks in the corner of the entryway. A woman by the stove, neither young nor old, with the face of someone who knows how to count not numbers, but consequences. A child slept on a bench and coughed in his sleep.

Not dying.

Not doomed.

Simply coughing.

And because of that, it became easier for the man to assemble future misfortune inside himself.

He took off his hat, shook off the snow, said something to the woman. Smiled again. She looked at his hands.

Empty.

The smile went out before he had time to finish the phrase.

The woman said nothing.

She only turned to the stove and adjusted a log so carefully, as if the log were the house’s last argument against winter.

Sofia smelled smoke.

Not campfire smoke. Another kind. Stove smoke. Domestic. With a trace of sour bread, damp wool, old wood, and that special human air in which live those who no longer argue with the cold, but negotiate with it every day.

The man sat down by the wall.

The child coughed again.

The woman opened a sack, looked inside, closed it.

Not dramatically.

Not theatrically.

Simply closed it.

And in that movement there was everything.

Sofia blinked.

Before her was the fire again.

David spoke first.

“But if the child is sick, if the stores are low... he had to do something.”

It was the only sentence David could have spoken just then. And it did not sound like a defence of logic, but like a defence of a human being from too easy a judgment.

Ivar nodded.

“Yes.”

David seemed to have expected something else. An argument. A correction. An indirect answer. Not simple agreement.

“Yes?” he repeated.

“Yes.”

Ivar took a thin branch and laid it into the fire.

“That is why the mistake became stronger.”

The branch did not catch at once. First it blackened. Then a small red nerve ran through one place. Then the fire took it whole.

“If he were simply greedy,” Ivar said, “the story would be poorer. And there would be less use in it.”

He did not explain further.

Sofia was grateful.

Because an explanation now would have been almost indecent.

The fire lowered.

This time she almost did not notice the passage.

The world by the fire did not disappear. It remained: warmth on her face, cold at her back, Nick beside her, the blanket on her knees, the lake in the darkness. But over it, within it, through it, something else appeared.

Not the past.

Not memory.

Rather, that which had always lain in the world, waiting until a person stopped shutting his eyes too quickly.

An old woman sat by the stove.

Not from that man’s house. Another. She had broad palms, narrow shoulders, and a face in which wrinkles did not adorn wisdom, but simply showed that life had used this face for a long time without much caution.

Before her stood a bowl of grain.

She sorted it with her fingers. Slowly. Without holiness. Sometimes she threw aside what was dark, empty, damaged. Sometimes she held a good grain in her palm a little longer than necessary.

Beside her a boy held a sack open.

He yawned.

The old woman struck him on the hand.

Not cruelly. Precisely.

He woke up and held it better.

At the door stood the man.

He had not come to ask.

That was visible at once.

Asking makes a person softer, even when he is angry. There was no softness in him. He stood straight, too straight for someone else’s house, and looked not at the old woman, not at the boy, not at the stove.

At the grain.

The old woman did not raise her head.

“You have come late,” she said.

Sofia did not know the language.

And still she understood the words.

Not as translation. As one understands salt when it touches the tongue.

The man answered.

“So has winter.”

The old woman gave a dry smile.

“Winter comes on time. It is people who remember late that they do not live alone.”

He said nothing.

The boy looked at him with curiosity. Then at his hands.

They were empty.

At last the old woman raised her head.

“You were given the rumor.”

The man started.

Very slightly. Almost invisibly.

But the old woman noticed.

“So you were.”

“I did not come for a rumor.”

“It is for that that people come when they say they have not come for it.”

He took a step closer.

The boy gripped the sack more tightly.

“Where is the place that feeds?” the man asked.

The old woman looked at him for so long that Sofia felt: the answer now would not be a refusal. A refusal would have been easier.

“Under your feet,” the old woman said.

The man smiled slowly.

This time the smile was different.

There was no request in it now, no attempt to save face before the neighbour. There was the irritation of someone who had come for a road and been given a circle.

“I am not a child.”

“Then why do you ask like a child promised hidden bread?”

He clenched his fist.

The empty hand became not empty.

Intention appeared in it.

Sofia felt her own fingers tighten.

The old woman took a handful of grain and poured it into the sack. The boy stepped carefully so as not to spill any.

“The place that feeds,” she said, “is not hidden. It is recognized by the fact that after you it can still feed.”

The man was silent.

“If you are looking for a thing, you are already late.”

Sofia heard the door creak behind the man’s back.

Another entered.

Young. Cheeks red from the cold, eyes too quick. The kind who could laugh where others keep silent, not because he is braver, but because inside him something is always trembling and looking for a way out.

Behind him — a third.

Broad-shouldered, with an iron hook at his belt. Not a weapon. A tool. But iron is not obliged to know what it was first made for.

Then a woman with a bandaged hand.

Then an old man with the eyes of someone who has long since counted other people’s hope as a variety of weather.

Then two more in silence.

Seven.

Sofia did not count.

The number laid itself inside her, like a stone into water.

The old woman looked at them all.

Now for the first time tiredness appeared in her face.

Not fear.

The tiredness of one who sees that a person has come not for an answer, but for permission not to listen.

“You will not find what you are looking for,” she said.

The young man laughed.

“Then we will find something better.”

The old woman closed her eyes.

The boy dropped one grain.

It rolled across the floor.

Nick beside Sofia drew in a sharp breath.

The fire became fire again.

The circle by the lake returned at once, almost roughly. Blanket, smoke, damp moss, faces in half-darkness. But for an instant it seemed to Sofia that a small dark grain was rolling over the ground between the stones of the fire circle.

She lowered her eyes.

Nothing.

Only moss.

Dark, damp, alive.

“Seven,” Nora said.

It was her first phrase in a long time.

And the only one that was needed.

Ivar looked at her.

“Yes.”

“Could they still have left?” Sofia asked.

Ivar turned to her.

The question hung between them without protection.

“Yes.”

For some reason Sofia felt pain from that answer.

If he had said no, everything would have been simpler. Fate. Myth. Inevitability. Old stories love corridors like that: a person walks because he must; takes because it is written; falls because otherwise the story will not receive its end.

But yes left room.

And room is always responsibility.

Nick said quietly:

“I hate places where it is still possible to leave.”

Nick was Nick. He had found a phrase after all. But now it did not break the circle. It did not become a lid over fear. It lay beside it, human, tired, almost honest.

Ivar did not smile.

“That is why a person often waits until leaving becomes too late.”

The fire rose.

Now the snow was no longer by the houses.

Forest.

Northern, dark, long.

Not the forest Sofia had seen from the window of the minibus, though perhaps the same. Here the trees stood denser, older, bent lower over the path. The snow did not lie evenly. In some places it was deep, in some blown by the wind down to black earth, in some covered by an icy crust that broke underfoot and sounded too loud.

The seven walked in a chain.

First — the man with the empty hands.

Now his hands were no longer empty. In one he held a stick. Not only for support. For testing. For pointing. For that future gesture in which a person jabs at the world and says: here.

Behind him walked the young man, the one who had laughed. He laughed less now. Cold takes excess sounds faster than a person is ready to admit.

The third carried the iron hook.

The woman with the bandaged hand walked steadily, without complaint. The old man lagged behind, but caught up each time. The last two almost had no faces. Not because they were unimportant. Because sometimes people follow another person’s desire for so long that their faces stop differing from a trace.

Snow fell slantwise.

Not beautifully.

Stubbornly.

They were not going toward a miracle.

Sofia understood this suddenly.

None of them looked as if he was going toward a miracle. A person goes toward a miracle with expectation. These people went with calculation, fear, and an already almost ready justification. They did not need the miracle as a miracle. They needed a point where the world could finally be made to answer.

The first stopped by a fallen tree.

The stick went into the snow.

Deep.

He frowned, pulled it out, drove it in nearby.

Again deep.

The young man came closer.

“What?” he asked.

The first did not answer.

He took off his mitten.

The old man said sharply:

“Don’t.”

But too late.

The bare hand lay on the snow.

Sofia felt it with her own palm.

The snow was not cold.

It was warm.

Not thawed. Not wet with spring. Not heated by the sun. Warm from within, like bread under the crust, like the body of a sleeping animal, like earth that had not yet managed to pretend it was dead.

The first man did not pull his hand away.

That was what was frightening.

He was not frightened.

He smiled.

Slowly.

That is not how one smiles at a discovery.

That is how one smiles at being right.

The woman with the bandaged hand went down on her knees and touched the snow too. The young man laughed, but the sound came out dry. The third man with the iron hook took it from his belt.

The old man stood behind.

He looked not at the snow, but at their faces.

“You can still leave,” he said.

The first raised his head.

“Will you leave?”

The old man did not answer.

And by that answered.

Sofia felt something damp beneath her own hand.

She was sitting by the fire. She was not touching snow. She was not in the forest. She was not standing among the seven.

But her palm lay on the blanket, and under her fingers there was warmth.

Too living a warmth.

She slowly raised her hand.

There was nothing on her skin.

Almost nothing.

Under the nail of her thumb a thin strip of earth had darkened.

Sofia stared at it.

Not dirt.

Dirt would have been salvation.

She had not dug. Had not touched the earth. Had not fallen. Had not taken moss in her hands. She was sitting by the fire, holding the blanket and listening to a story that, as it turned out, did not know how to remain outside.

“Sofia?” Eva said quietly.

Sofia closed her hand.

“Later.”

Eva did not ask.

Good.

Very good.

The fire opened the forest again.

The iron hook lay on the snow.

It did not strike.

Not yet.

It simply touched.

And because of that the whole forest seemed to hold its breath.

The first man raised his hand.

“Here,” he said.

The word was small.

But there was already more than a place in it.

There was mine.

Unspoken.

Still too early to confess itself.

But already alive.

The woman with the bandaged hand wept soundlessly. Not from fear, it seemed. From relief. The young man was on his knees, raking the snow aside with his palms. The old man still did not move. The third held the iron so carefully, as if he himself understood: the tool was now becoming something more than a tool, and for the iron too there would be no way back.

Dark earth showed under the snow.

It was not black.

Rather reddish, deep, damp. Steam rose from it. Very thin. Almost invisible. But in it there was a smell.

Bread.

Root.

Milk.

Smoke.

Salt on skin.

Water that has passed through stone.

That which a person usually divides into food, home, body, winter, labour, tiredness, and morning rose here together, without asking whether it was convenient to be one word.

The young man whispered:

“It is alive.”

The first looked at him as if he had said too much.

“Then it will give.”

The old man closed his eyes.

“The living does not ‘give’ like that.”

The first turned to him.

“How, then?”

The old man was silent.

Because in such moments the right answer almost always sounds weaker than the wrong desire.

The iron hook entered the warm earth.

Not deeply.

Only to the thickness of a finger.

But Sofia felt something inside her tighten.

Not her heart. Lower. Deeper. Where the body still remembers things the mind has not permitted it to know.

In the circle by the fire someone drew in a sharp breath.

David.

Or Thomas.

Or herself.

The fire did not go out completely, but for one instant it became black at the very centre.

When the light returned, the forest was gone.

There was no snow.

There were no seven.

Only the fire pit, moss, stones, the lake in the dark, the house behind them, twelve guests, and Ivar, who sat slightly to the side and looked not at them but into the place in the fire where the earth had just first accepted iron.

Nick slowly passed his palm over his face.

“Now,” he said quietly, “I really don’t like this story at all.”

No one laughed.

And Nick, it seemed, had not expected anyone to.

Thomas wanted to ask something. It was visible in him: the question had already gathered itself, found a form, risen to his throat.

But Thomas looked at Ivar and kept silent.

This time he understood.

Not everything that can be asked needs to be pulled immediately into the air.

Ivar picked up a small branch from the ground, but did not put it into the fire. He held it in his hands, looking at the broken end.

“At this point,” he said, “they had not yet taken Sampo.”

Sofia listened.

“They had only decided that if the living could be hooked, then it could be taken.”

The flame trembled.

“That is where it all began.”

Under the moss it became warm again.

Not enough to scream. Not enough for everyone to jump up and start checking the ground with their hands. The north does not give a person such gifts. It rarely gives obviousness. It gives just enough for denial to become work.

Sofia opened her palm.

The strip of dark earth under her nail was still there.

She ran her thumb over the pad of her forefinger.

Her skin smelled of bread.

She became afraid.

Not because of what she had seen.

Because a part of her had understood the man in the snow too well.

The one who came for an answer.

The one who received a warning.

The one who could still leave.

The one who smiled when the warm snow confirmed not the truth, but his right.

Ivar rose.

Not abruptly. Not solemnly. He simply stood, like a person who had finished not a story, but part of the work.

“Inside,” he said.

Nick looked at him.

“Already?”

Ivar turned toward the house.

“While you still remember that you have a door.”

He went first.

Not as a leader.

As one who knows the way in the dark and is not obliged to prove it every time with a lantern.

People began to rise.

Slowly. Carefully. Without the usual bustle after long sitting. Even the blankets were taken off more quietly than necessary. Thomas lifted his glasses from his knee and did not put them on for a long time. David brushed his palms off, though there was nothing on them. Lina looked at Nora, Nora at the moss. Marek stamped down the edge of an ember with his boot, unhurriedly, practically, as if exactly that had been the most important thing after everything they had seen.

Eva came up to Sofia.

“Let’s go.”

Sofia nodded.

But before rising, she touched the earth beside one of the stones of the fire circle with her fingers.

Only for a second.

On top the moss was cold.

Damp.

Ordinary.

And inside — warm.

She took her hand away and stood.

The house shone between the trees with yellow windows. Inside waited soup, cups, wet shoes, creaking floorboards, human voices, the possibility of saying “I am tired” instead of “I am frightened.”

Sofia followed the others.

Earth remained under her nail.

And somewhere deep under the moss, under the roots, under the water, where a person should never have hooked anything with iron, warmth lay motionless.

Like bread not yet broken.
