# Epilogue. The First Sound

The morning of departure began without solemnity.

The house did not become quieter because they were supposed to leave. It simply returned to its usual work: the floor creaked underfoot, water in the kettle murmured softly, someone opened and closed the door to the entryway, cups, a knife, cloth, crumbs, paper moved across the table.

Outside it was gray.

Not rainy.

After rain.

That was a different state: everything had already fallen, but had not yet dried. The boards of the porch were dark. The stones by the little ditch lay crookedly. The water was still going past the house, thin, almost invisible if one did not know where to look.

Bags began appearing by the door one after another.

First Thomas’s large black backpack. Then Eva’s soft bag with a wet corner. Then a case with something long inside, which someone set against the wall and immediately moved so it would not be in the way. Then a bag with dirty boots, tied too tightly, as if the dirt might change its mind and come out.

Nick stood in the middle of the entryway with one sock in his hand.

“I officially declare that departure is an ancient rite of losing small things.”

“That is your sock,” Eva said.

“Exactly. The ritual has begun.”

“The second one is on you.”

He looked down.

“Then the rite has not been lost. It has merely been distributed.”

Sofia sat at the table, folding her warm sweater.

Badly.

Her hand still ached a little, especially the thumb. Under the nail there remained a dark point of earth, smaller than yesterday, but stubborn. She was no longer trying to wash it away completely. Not because she was preserving it. She had simply understood that if the earth went away, it would not change the fact that the hand had held the board.

On the table before David lay a sheet.

Сампо —

After the dash there was still nothing.

David sat before it in his jacket, already half-fastened, as if the body had gathered to leave before the paper. The pencil lay nearby. Not on the sheet. Separately.

He took the sheet with two fingers.

Lifted it.

Put it back.

Then took out a folder.

Opened it.

Did not put the sheet into it at once.

Sofia watched, trying not to watch too openly.

“Will you take it?” she asked.

David raised his eyes.

“I don’t know.”

“Will you leave it?”

“I don’t know that either.”

Nick, passing by with the found sock, said:

“For the first time in history, paper has won an argument by saying nothing.”

David looked at him.

“It hasn’t won.”

“All the more dignified.”

Eva took the sock from Nick.

“Put it in the bag before it becomes philosophical again.”

“Too late, but I’ll try.”

David did put the sheet into the folder after all.

Not in the middle.

Not on top.

Between the map and the sheet with the recipe, so that the edge with the dash remained visible. Then he put the pencil into another pocket. Not next to the papers.

Sofia noticed this.

And for some reason she felt calm.

Not joyful.

Simply exact.

Lina stood by the kitchen table and wrapped bread in a clean cloth.

There was not much bread. Several slices, one end no one had taken in the evening, and crumbs poured into a small paper twist. She wrapped it all as if the bread were not a remainder. As if a remainder, too, required form.

Thomas came up to her.

“Is that to take with us?”

“Yes.”

“For everyone?”

“For whoever needs it.”

He looked at the cloth.

“It still doesn’t make it easier for me.”

Lina tied the knot.

“Take the bread.”

He raised his eyes.

“Is that an answer?”

“It is bread.”

Nick said quietly from the entryway:

“Please record: the best answer of the day has already been wrapped.”

Thomas took the bundle.

Held it awkwardly, with both hands, as something more fragile than bread.

“Thank you.”

Lina nodded.

“Don’t put it with wet things.”

“I understand.”

“That is also important.”

He almost smiled.

“Yes. I understood that better.”

Marek came in from outside.

There were fine drops on his jacket, not rain, but water from the branches. He looked at the bags by the door, at the boots, at the people, at the clock.

“In ten minutes.”

Nick lifted his head.

“In ten minutes is an ancient form of self-deception.”

“Seven, if you talk.”

“I am silent out of respect for time.”

Marek put another bag by the door, checked his pocket, took out a key, placed it on the shelf. Then looked at the porch through the opened door.

“The road will be slow.”

“Because of the rain?” Thomas asked.

“Because of the road after rain.”

Thomas nodded, as if this were more exact.

Sofia stood.

Her bag was already packed, but the zipper did not close on the first try. The things inside lay no worse than usual, but today it seemed strange that they could be placed inside at all and zipped shut. Sweater. Socks. Notebook. Charger. Gloves. Everything fit.

The house did not.

She stopped at this thought before it became too large.

Not the house.

Not as a thing.

No need.

Just close the bag.

She pulled the zipper. It went stiffly, then gave way.

Eva noticed.

“Help?”

“Already.”

“You’re quiet.”

“Everyone is quiet.”

Eva looked at Nick, who at that moment was trying to push the sock into a side pocket and pretending it was a complex engineering operation.

“Almost everyone.”

“He is quieter too.”

“Yes.”

They both looked at him.

Nick felt the look.

“I do not know what you have understood about me, but I ask that you not formalize it in a document.”

Eva smiled.

Sofia too.

A little.

By the wall Nora was carrying someone else’s glove. She held it with two fingers, like a wet bird.

“Whose is this?” she asked.

Nick raised his hand.

“If it is lost, possibly mine. If useful, someone else’s.”

Nora came up to him and held it out.

“It is still damp.”

“Like many decisions in my life.”

“Don’t put it with the bread,” Lina said, without turning around.

“We already have a school of packing ethics.”

“Nick.”

“Yes. Glove separately. Bread separately. Soul uncertainly.”

Nora looked at him seriously.

“The soul also not with the wet.”

Nick froze.

Eva closed her eyes.

Sofia waited for someone to make a phrase out of it.

Nick did not.

He only took the glove and said:

“Fair.”

Nora went to the door and began tying the lace on her own boot.

Ivar had been nearby almost all morning, but not in the center. He closed the stove damper, checked that the firebox door was not open, lifted someone else’s scarf from a chair and placed it on a bag. Then carried a small box with empty jars out to the porch; it needed to be taken to the van.

Nick followed him with his eyes.

“If he says something at the last moment now, I will lie down on the floor.”

“He won’t,” Eva said.

“That is what worries me.”

Ivar returned, heard the last words, and looked at Nick.

“The floor is cold.”

“There. That is worse than wisdom. It is applicable.”

Ivar passed by.

Said nothing more.

Sofia went to the window.

The ditch could be seen at an angle. The water in it was already very thin. Not a brook. Not a sign. Simply the remainder of movement after rain. The stones held. The board held. The earth around was torn up, but it did not spread toward the threshold.

On the porch stood two bags. One had already grown wet underneath. Marek noticed, lifted it, and put it on the bench.

“Not here,” he said to himself.

Sofia heard it and for some reason remembered it.

Not here.

Not under the house.

Not with wet things.

Not in the center.

Not after the dash.

She almost smiled.

Then stopped the sequence.

It was time to gather.

Not understand.

In the corner of the large room stood the case.

Sofia had seen it before and had not seen it. Long, dark, with worn edges, closed with two metal latches. It stood by the wall so quietly that all this time it could have seemed part of the furniture or someone else’s bag. Now Aila came up to it.

She did not hurry.

First she ran her hand over the lid, as one checks whether cloth is damp. Then took the strap and adjusted it so it would not twist. The metal clasp softly struck the edge of the case.

The sound was small.

But Sofia turned around.

Aila noticed this.

Said nothing.

She lifted the case by the handle.

The weight became visible at once in her shoulder. Not great. Familiar. The hand knew how to hold. The body knew how to distribute the weight. Aila tilted her head slightly, as if she were listening not to the case, but to what had become audible after its movement.

Outside the window, the water was going past the house.

So quietly that it could truly be mistaken for ordinary noise.

And almost everyone did mistake it.

Marek opened the door.

Cold entered the entryway at once, without asking.

“Time,” he said.

Not loudly.

Simply enough.

The bags began to be lifted.

Thomas took his backpack and the bread bundle. Then moved the bread into the upper pocket, separate from the wet jacket. David fastened the folder and pressed it to his side, as if it were not valuable, but unfinished. Eva checked whether any cups had been left on the table. Lina covered the knife with cloth and put it away in a drawer. Nora stood by the door, then went back for the glove someone had again put in the wrong place.

Nick looked around the room.

“Does anyone know how to leave a place that does not pretend it is letting you go?”

No one answered.

“I see,” he said. “We improvise.”

Sofia took her bag.

Her shoulder answered with pain after yesterday’s board. She adjusted the strap and felt the remaining point of earth under her nail. She did not look. Simply felt it.

At the door she stopped.

Not to say goodbye.

She did not know what exactly one says goodbye to.

The house?

The water?

The stove?

The sheet that was leaving in David’s folder and still remained unfinished?

The silence that was no longer empty?

No.

She simply stopped because ahead was the threshold.

And the threshold had to be crossed.

Ivar stood beside the door. Not seeing them off. Not blessing them. Simply holding the door so it would not close from the wind before its time.

Sofia looked at him.

He nodded.

Nothing more.

It was enough.

She stepped out onto the porch.

The boards underfoot were damp, but no longer slipped. The stones by the little ditch lay in their wrong places. Water went beside them, almost soundlessly. The minibus stood by the road, gray, wet along the lower edge. The engine was not yet running. The windows were cold, clouded in the corners.

People came out one after another.

Not like a procession.

Like people with bags, wet boots, bread, papers, gloves, unsaid words, and a road ahead.

Sofia went down to the lower step and turned around.

The house stood.

Did not hold them.

Did not let them go.

Stood.

Marek passed her on the way to the vehicle.

“Careful on the step.”

“I know.”

“Still careful.”

She nodded.

Behind her, Nick said:

“That is, I think, the best brief course on life I have ever heard.”

Marek did not turn around.

“Get in the van.”

“And the second lesson is immediately practical.”

But he went.

Aila came out last of those who had been standing in the room.

Not last altogether — Ivar was still holding the door, Lina had delayed by the table, Eva was checking something in the entryway. But Sofia saw Aila specifically.

She stood by the door with the closed case in her hand.

The strap lay on her shoulder. Her free hand touched the lid of the case not from above, but on the side, where wood or leather might remember a palm. Aila did not look at the people. Not at the van. Not at Ivar.

She listened.

Outside, the minibus waited.

The water went past the house so quietly that it could be mistaken for ordinary noise.

Aila did not mistake it.

She said nothing.

And stepped out.
