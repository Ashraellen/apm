# Chapter Twenty-Four. Sampo

After the dash, nothing appeared.

David was still holding the pencil above the sheet. The hand no longer trembled, but it did not move either. The sharp tip hung above the white place after the word “Sampo,” as if there were a thin crust of ice there and he did not dare be the first to touch it.

Nick did not joke.

Thomas was no longer looking at Ivar, but at the sheet.

By the window, Lina was folding the dry rag. Folded it once. Then a second time. Then laid it on the back of the chair where the wet one had hung before. Eva gathered the cups closer to the edge of the table so no one would catch the papers with an elbow. Marek stood by the stove and held a log in his hand, not hurrying to put it into the fire.

The stove crackled quietly.

Not like a sign.

Like a stove.

Outside the window, the water was going along the little ditch. It could hardly be heard, but after yesterday everyone knew it was there. Past the porch. Not under the house. For today.

Sofia looked at the white place after the dash.

Before, an empty place had asked to be filled. Demanded a word. Promised relief if the word finally appeared. Now it did not ask. It lay calmly, like a place on the table where nothing had yet been set, because the hand did not know what could be set there without lying.

“Leave it like that?” Thomas asked.

Not David.

Not Sofia.

Rather, the room.

David lowered the pencil a little.

Did not touch the paper.

“For now,” he said.

Thomas gave a short laugh.

“That word has won.”

“No,” Marek said from the stove. “It holds.”

Nick raised one finger very carefully.

“I would like to note that the distinction between ‘won’ and ‘holds’ may save civilization today. But I promised to be almost human.”

Eva took the empty mug from him.

“It’s working.”

“That frightens me.”

But his voice was soft.

Sofia did not smile at once. She was still looking at the sheet. At the word. At the dash. At the place after the dash. Then, without thinking, she looked around.

The room had not changed.

The table stood in the same place. The papers lay crookedly. The map partly covered the edge of the recipe. The sheet “gift —” still did not know where to lead its continuation. At the edge of the table lay the knife Lina had brought back after cutting the dark place off the bread. Crumbs had gathered near the board in a thin uneven line.

By the wall stood the bucket.

Empty.

But on the bottom there remained a cloudy stripe — the memory of yesterday’s water, which David had carried to the bushes. On the hook by the door hung the gloves. One pair was drying with palms outward, as if someone were still holding an invisible board.

On the floor by the entrance, tracks darkened.

Not fresh. Already dry at the edges. From Marek’s boots, Nick’s dirty sole, Thomas, David. Her own print was probably somewhere among them too. Impossible to recognize. No longer important.

From the kitchen came the smell of bread.

Someone had left a thin slice on a plate. Not new. Yesterday’s. Dry flour whitened on the crust. Beside it stood the little bowl of crumbs Eva had gathered from the board in the morning.

Nothing was full.

And nothing was empty.

Sofia slowly inhaled.

The air smelled of smoke, wet wool, warm wood, sour water after berries, bread, bucket iron, dry cloth, earth under a nail. All this had been near before. Now “near” had stopped being an accurate word.

“You’ve gone pale,” Nick said quietly.

She looked at him.

“No.”

“A convincing answer. Almost medical.”

“I just...”

She stopped.

The words did not come.

Not because there was no thought.

Because the thought, if made into a word, immediately became smaller.

Ivar stood by the wall, almost in the shadow of the cupboard. He was not looking at her directly. Or was looking in such a way that she did not have to answer.

Sofia moved her gaze to the window.

The glass was cloudy from yesterday’s dampness. Behind it one could see part of the porch, the edge of the little ditch, the wet stone Nick had carried yesterday with both hands although he had called it “not very big.” The board still stood crookedly, darkened by water. The stones lay unevenly. The water went around them not like something obedient, but like something able.

It was going.

Did not ask.

Did not thank.

Did not submit.

And still it went in such a way that the house remained dry underneath. For now.

The stove cracked softly.

Marek set the log beside the firebox, then still moved it closer. Not into the fire at once. He let the edge dry. The movement was so small that Sofia would not have seen it before. Now she saw it.

The log did not warm.

For now.

But it already lay where it could become warmth.

Lina put bread on the table.

Not solemnly.

Simply put it there. Several thin slices. One edge burnt. One almost transparent from being cut thin. Beside it, a small bowl of crumbs.

“Is that all?” Thomas asked.

Lina looked at the bread.

“For now.”

Thomas nodded.

Did not argue.

Then still said:

“It is still hard for me.”

“What?” Eva asked.

He pointed to the papers, to the dash, to the window, to the bread. The gesture came out too large and did not land on any one thing.

“This.”

Nick lowered his eyes.

“Yes.”

Thomas looked at him, as if expecting continuation.

But there was no continuation.

The short “yes” turned out heavy enough not to joke.

David placed the pencil beside the sheet.

Did not close the notebook.

Did not put the paper away.

Simply laid down the pencil. It remained beside the unfinished word, like a tool that had not been thrown away, but was no longer being forced to work before its time.

“I don’t know what to do with it,” David said.

“With the pencil?” Nick asked.

“With this.”

He pointed to the dash.

Nick opened his mouth.

Then closed it.

“I don’t know,” he said. “And, to my horror, that is more honest than everything I wanted to say.”

Eva nudged his shoulder gently.

“Get used to it.”

“I promise nothing.”

Sofia went to the window.

Not quickly.

She did not want to turn the movement into an event. It had simply become hard to stand by the table. The papers lay too close to words. The window was closer to water.

The glass cooled her forehead when she almost touched it.

Outside the window, the house did not see itself.

It simply stood.

With its roof still holding drops. With the porch where the fish scale had almost disappeared into the mud. With the ditch they had made badly, but enough. With the shed where the firewood lay. With wet stones. With the barrel. With the path to the water. With the forest, which did not come close and still was inside the smell of the house.

Sofia looked and suddenly stopped distinguishing where “outside” ended.

Not because the walls disappeared.

The walls were there.

The door was there.

The threshold was there.

On the threshold there was even an old rag someone still had to shake out.

But cold came in through the door if it was held too long. Heat left through cracks if the stove was forgotten. Water went under the boards if not diverted. Dirt came in on boots. Bread lay on the table because grain had once been in the earth, hands had ground it, someone had kneaded, someone had cut, someone had gathered crumbs. The gift had come from Laura through Matti, and no one could put it in the center of the scheme without spoiling it. Lack remained in the basket. Participation remained in the pain of the hand. The bucket stood empty, but was not empty if one remembered what it had carried.

Sofia closed her eyes.

Not in order to see more.

Simply in order not to hurry to name.

In the dark behind her eyelids, the house did not disappear.

On the contrary.

It became more audible.

The stove cracked.

Someone moved a cup.

Nick inhaled, preparing to say something, and did not say it.

A floorboard creaked by the door.

Outside the window the water went thinly.

Someone was cutting bread — the knife slid over the board with a short dry sound.

David moved the pencil. Very quietly. But Sofia heard.

Everything was ordinary.

And precisely because of that it no longer hid.

“It sounds different,” the woman by the window said.

Sofia opened her eyes and turned.

The woman sat by the second window, where the light was grayer. All these days she had hardly spoken. Sofia had heard her name rarely, as if the name itself kept away from the common noise. Aila.

She was looking not at the table.

At the glass.

Behind the glass, the water went past the house.

“What?” Thomas asked.

Aila moved one shoulder slightly.

“I don’t know. Just different.”

Nick turned his head toward Sofia, as if wanting to check whether the phrase had sounded too strongly.

But it had not sounded strongly.

It had sounded exactly the way a drop falls from the roof after rain: small, separate, but no longer accidental.

And Aila said nothing more.

That was good.

No.

Not good.

Enough.

Sofia looked back at the window.

The water went past the house.

The stove held heat not by itself, but because firewood lay nearby and someone put it in. The bread lay on the table not whole, but as much as remained. The crumbs had been gathered not because there were many of them, but because there were few. David’s papers did not give an answer, but they no longer demanded the old arrow. It was hard for Thomas, and that did not stop him from standing here. Nick was silent longer than usual, and that too was part of the room. Lina moved the knife so no one would touch it accidentally. Eva set one cup beside another. Nora, by the wall, quietly untied and tied again the lace on the same boot, as if checking whether the knot held.

Nothing led to one place.

Nothing gathered into an object.

Nothing asked: take me.

And yet everything held.

Not forever.

Not by itself.

Not for them.

But held.

Sofia turned to the table.

The sheet “Сампо —” lay open. The white place after the dash no longer seemed empty. It was not filled, but it was no longer empty.

She came closer.

David looked at her.

“Write?” he asked.

The voice was quiet.

Not insistent.

Rather, he was asking not for permission, but for caution.

Sofia looked at the sheet.

At the dash.

At the pencil.

Then at his hands.

There was almost no dirt on them now. Only under one nail a dark point remained, like hers.

“No,” she said.

David nodded.

Almost with relief.

Thomas came to the table.

“But someday one will have to.”

“What?” Nick asked.

“Say it.”

Nick looked at the sheet.

“Possibly.”

“You said ‘possibly’ without a joke.”

“I am frightened myself.”

Thomas did not smile, but the corner of his mouth moved.

“It is still hard for me,” he repeated.

Sofia looked at him.

“For me too.”

“Then why do you look as if it has become easier?”

She thought.

Not long.

“Because one does not have to hold everything in one’s hands.”

Thomas looked at her hand.

At the thumb.

At the dark earth under the nail.

“Yesterday one had to.”

“Yesterday, yes.”

“And today?”

Sofia looked at the window.

“Today the water goes past.”

Marek gave a low sound by the stove.

“For now.”

“For now,” Sofia said.

And for the first time that word did not make what had been said smaller.

Ivar quietly came to the table.

Nick stepped slightly aside, making room, and had already opened his mouth to say something about sudden appearances, but changed his mind.

Ivar looked at the sheet.

At the dash.

At the white place.

Then at Sofia.

She was not asking.

But the question was there between them all the same.

Not “where.”

Not “what.”

Not even “how.”

Rather: whether now one could avoid hurrying to turn this into a word.

Ivar nodded.

Barely noticeably.

“Yes,” he said.

Nick inhaled.

“Just to clarify: was that an answer to a question no one heard?”

Ivar looked at him.

“You heard.”

Nick froze.

Then said quietly:

“That’s unfair.”

“Yes.”

Eva almost smiled.

Thomas sat on the bench.

Not tiredly.

Rather because standing with this had become uncomfortable. He took one thin slice of bread, but did not begin eating. Simply held it in his hand.

“If this is not an answer,” he said, “then what now?”

Nick looked at him.

“Bread, it seems.”

Thomas lowered his eyes to the slice.

This time he smiled.

Very slightly.

“That is already too simple.”

“The simple also has the right to be suspected.”

Lina set a cup beside him.

“Eat before it dries out.”

“The bread?”

“You.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“Magnificent. I have been removed from humor on grounds of professional superiority.”

Lina did not answer.

Thomas bit into the bread.

Sofia looked at this and suddenly felt that there would be no “moment.”

There would be no flash.

No sign.

No voice that would say that now everything had been understood.

Precisely this was almost frightening.

Because if there would be no moment, then everything had already been happening.

When the bread was cut thin.

When water could not be taken into one’s hands.

When the place turned out to be full, but not an answer.

When the recipe remained and the continuation was thrown away.

When the jar was labeled, but it was not enough.

When the house cooled despite the schedule.

When the water turned gray in the bucket.

When the fish disappeared.

When the potatoes came not as compensation.

When the board could not be let go.

When the arrow did not appear.

She stopped the sequence before it became a list.

No.

No need.

Do not enumerate.

Simply look.

Sofia looked.

At the house.

At the people.

At the bread.

At the stove.

At the window.

At the water.

At the dirt under the nail.

At the unfinished dash.

Things did not disappear into meaning.

Meaning did not settle on top of things.

Things remained things.

And suddenly, precisely because of that, they ceased to be only things.

The sheet on the table moved slightly from the draft. The edge rose and fell. David held it down with his finger.

Not as an answer.

As a sheet.

Sofia would have said something if she could.

But the only phrase rising inside was too large for the voice and too quiet for conversation.

She did not say it.

And no one asked.

The stove breathed warmth.

The water went past the house.

The bread lay on the table.

The board by the porch held.

The stones lay crookedly.

The bucket stood empty.

People breathed.

Aila by the window again listened to what the others for now only heard.

David did not write.

Thomas ate bread and still did not look satisfied.

Nick was silent so seriously that he himself seemed afraid of it.

Ivar stood beside the table, explaining nothing.

Sofia looked at the word.

Sampo.

Now it did not become clear.

It became tight.

Not in the sense of small.

The opposite.

Tight for the sheet.

For the question.

For the place.

For the hand that wanted to take.

For the arrow that wanted to point.

For the center that wanted to hold everything else around itself.

Outside the window a small leaf passed along the ditch. It caught on a stone, stayed there, then the water went around it on both sides, moved it, and it went on.

Sofia watched until it disappeared beyond the edge of the window.

Then looked at the house again from within.

And suddenly everything became very quiet.

Not because the sounds disappeared.

Because there was no longer any need to wait for another sound, the real one, the main one.

This one was.

The stove.

Water.

Breath.

Wood.

Bread.

The pencil that does not write.

The hand that no longer holds the board, but remembers.

The house that does not stand by itself.

Sofia said nothing.

David said nothing.

Thomas had been about to ask, but did not.

Nick lifted his mug, then set it back without a word.

Ivar closed his eyes for one second.

Aila listened.

Sampo did not stand in the house.

The house stood in it.

And no one said it aloud.
