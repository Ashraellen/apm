# Chapter Ten. Share

After breakfast the house began counting by itself.

Not aloud.

No one placed tally marks on the table, wrote down names, or said that now duties had to be distributed. There was no meeting. But quantity entered the rooms before the people did.

The bread lay under a towel.

The water stood in a bucket by the door and was already lower than in the morning.

There were few dry logs left by the stove.

Wet jackets hung on the hooks.

In the basin beneath them, water darkened.

By David’s place lay the closed notebook.

Sofia did not notice it at once. At first it seemed to her that after yesterday’s morning place by the woodshed and the ants everything ought to have become simpler. Not easier, no. But simpler: the body had returned, the earth had accepted, the ants had found work, David had come out to the table and even said a few words without vanishing again into shame. It seemed that after something like that people should become more careful with large words.

They did.

That was exactly why they began speaking in small ones.

“There’s little water,” Marek said.

It was an ordinary phrase.

Completely ordinary.

It could have sounded in any house where there is a bucket, a kettle, people, and the need to wash dishes. But this time it lay on the table beside the bread, the butter, and the closed notebook as if it had a continuation.

Marek was already standing by the door in his jacket. His boots had not had time to dry properly. The newspaper beneath them was covered in brown stains. He lifted the empty bucket, checked the handle, as if overnight it might have changed its opinion about weight.

“I’ll go,” Eva said.

“No need,” Marek answered.

“Why?”

“You’re cooking.”

“I’ve almost finished.”

“Then you’ll wash after.”

“You say that as if the bucket asked you personally.”

Marek looked at the bucket.

“It is silent. That makes it more reliable than most.”

Nick, sitting at the table with a mug, lifted his eyes.

“I could go, but I warn you in advance: I will return with a philosophical report on water, and everyone will be worse off.”

“Sit,” Marek said.

“What pleasant authoritarianism. Almost care.”

“Almost.”

David sat by the stove, wrapped in the same wide sweater. His face was already a normal colour, but not normal. It happens that way after shame: the body returns before the person permits himself to return. He held his mug in both hands and did not interfere.

That too became part of the room.

Someone does not go.

Someone goes instead of him.

Nick looked at David and immediately turned away.

Sofia saw it. Nick did not want to look as if he had noticed. But he had noticed. And because of that he turned away too quickly.

Thomas stood by the window, as if he were again looking for the rise between the firs, although the window faced in a completely different direction. After yesterday he spoke less, but not more softly. There was more silence in him, and that silence was not peace. It was postponed movement.

“I’ll go,” he said.

Marek stopped by the door.

“For water?”

“Yes.”

“Do you know how to walk with a bucket?”

Thomas looked at him.

“Is it harder than a map?”

Nick said quietly:

“Do not provoke a northern man with a bucket.”

Marek handed Thomas a second bucket.

“We’ll go.”

“I said I’ll go.”

“I heard.”

“Then why are you coming?”

“So the water comes back.”

The room grew a little warmer from the stove and a little colder from that phrase.

Thomas took the bucket.

They went out.

The door did not close at once. Cold came in, the smell of wet boards, grey morning, earth beyond the porch. Then the door thudded, and the kitchen became a house again.

But not entirely.

Because two people had gone for water, and one of them might not have gone.

Sofia caught herself on that thought and almost grew angry.

“Might not have gone” — at whom? At Thomas? At Marek? At David? At herself?

She stood and began collecting plates.

That was easier than thinking.

Plates could be taken one by one. On them remained traces of egg, butter, berry liquid, crumbs. On Nora’s plate lay half a berry, crushed by a spoon and for some reason not eaten. On Nick’s — a thin trace of butter, too neat for a person who pretends not to be neat. On David’s almost nothing. His plate had been set closer to the edge, as if it had to be removed faster, without holding the gaze.

“Leave it,” Eva said.

“I can.”

“I know.”

“Then why leave it?”

Eva looked at her.

“Because you went too.”

Sofia did not understand at once.

Then she did: to the hollow. To the woodshed. Into morning. Into the ants. Not for water, not for firewood, but all the same.

And this “too” suddenly became strange.

Can a person grow tired from something he merely saw?

Who decides what a person has the right to sit down from?

“I’ll wash,” Lina said, taking the plates from Sofia.

“But you already cooked.”

“So?”

“Nothing.”

“Exactly.”

Lina said it calmly, but there was fatigue in the calm. Not offence. Simply the fatigue of a person who has long known: if one begins weighing every work, half of what is necessary will have to stop being done.

Nora rose and took a towel.

“I’ll dry.”

“You cleaned the knives yesterday,” Eva said.

“They’re dirty again.”

“Because knives work.”

“Then so do I.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“I think we are witnessing the birth of the state.”

“Don’t start,” Lina said.

“Too late. The water has gone, the plates demand organs of administration, the knives have entered labour relations.”

“Nick.”

“That’s it. I’m silent. But if a butter tax appears, I warned you.”

He said it lightly.

Almost.

Sofia heard that he was joking not only for himself. Yesterday he had joked so as not to fall into David’s shame. Today — so as not to let quantity acquire a face.

On the table remained a small bowl of berries. The berries were from a jar, sour, with dark syrup. They were eaten carefully, because there was little sweetness. Not because anyone had forbidden more. Simply little. And when there is little, the hand begins to know about itself more than one would like.

Nora took a spoon, scooped berries, looked into the bowl, and put half back.

Too noticeably.

Nick saw.

Sofia saw.

Eva saw and pretended not to see.

“What?” Nora asked, without raising her eyes.

“Nothing,” Nick said.

“You are silent too loudly.”

“I am practising.”

“Badly.”

He grinned.

“That is my share of talent.”

The word sounded accidental.

And stopped being accidental.

Share.

Sofia felt it appear in the kitchen without knocking.

Not loud. Not large. Not philosophical.

It smelled of berries, a wet towel, an unwashed plate, and water, which two people had gone to fetch.

How much to take, so as not to take too much.

How much to leave, so as not to look greedy.

How much to do, so as not to feel indebted.

How much to tire, so as to have the right to sit.

How much to suffer, so they release you from the bucket.

Sofia sat down.

Not because she decided.

Because her legs asked by themselves.

David said quietly:

“I can help.”

Everyone looked at him too quickly.

He gave a crooked smile.

“That’s why I was silent.”

“Sit,” Eva said.

“I’m already sitting.”

“Good.”

“Not very.”

Nick raised his mug.

“A person who was sick yesterday has the right to be useless today.”

“I don’t want to be useless.”

“No one does. It is one of the most exhausting forms of pride.”

“Nick,” Sofia said.

“What? I am almost being kind right now.”

David looked at him.

“I know.”

Nick immediately grew embarrassed, as if he had been caught stealing.

Lina set a wet plate on the shelf. Water ran down the edge and fell onto the worktop.

“No one is useless,” Nora said.

Nick sighed.

“Nora, please don’t save everyone with one phrase. We have only just begun to spoil honestly.”

“I’m not saving.”

“Then what?”

“I just don’t like the word.”

“‘Useless’?”

“Yes.”

“And which one do you like?”

She thought.

“None.”

And returned to the towel.

Footsteps sounded outside. The door opened. Marek and Thomas came in with the buckets. Both were wet below the knees. Thomas’s sleeve was smeared with clay, his hair had escaped its usual neatness, and his glasses had fogged.

“The water has returned,” Nick said.

Marek set his bucket by the stove.

“I was there.”

Thomas set the second one down too sharply. Water splashed onto the floor.

“Careful,” Lina said.

“Sorry.”

He wiped the floor with a rag without waiting for anyone to ask. He did it quickly, almost angrily.

Marek took off his jacket.

“Slower next time.”

“I carried it normally.”

Marek looked at the wet floor.

Thomas wrung out the rag again.

Did not answer.

Only then did he notice David sitting by the stove and looking at him.

“Are you better?” Thomas asked.

“Yes.”

“Good.”

A pause.

“I can go again later,” David said.

Thomas looked away.

“No need.”

“Why?”

“Because we already went.”

The phrase was ordinary.

But inside it lay more than water.

Already went.

Someone already did it.

Someone already contributed.

Someone already became part of an invisible count, even if no one wanted to keep one.

David heard it.

Sofia saw how he heard it. He did not take offence. That would have been easier. He only tightened both hands a little around the mug, as he had yesterday just before he became ill.

Ivar entered from outside later than everyone. In his arms were several thin logs, too dry for the common woodpile. Apparently he had found them under the shelter, where they had been lying since last week or last year. He placed them by the stove, looked at the water by the bucket, at the wet trace from the rag, at the closed notebook by David’s place, at the bowl of berries.

Said nothing.

And that became too noticeable.

“Are you going to say something?” Thomas asked.

Ivar looked at him.

“No.”

“Why?”

Ivar sat by the stove, took a knife, and began shaving wet bark from one log. The bark came away in strips. Beneath it opened the pale dry body of the wood.

Thomas waited.

Ivar removed another strip.

Nick said quietly:

“Now it is definitely dangerous. When a man silently peels bark, meaning starts climbing out by itself.”

Ivar did not smile.

“Is there bread?” he asked.

Lina brought another loaf from the pantry.

“There is.”

“This is the last until tomorrow,” Marek said.

And now the bread on the table changed.

Not physically.

The loaf was the same: dark crust, uneven side, flour in the cracks, the trace of a knife on the old edge. But after Marek’s words it stopped being bread in general. It became that which had to be enough.

Sofia almost saw how everyone in the room counted a future piece for a second.

And was immediately ashamed.

And immediately pretended not to have.

“Can we bake?” Eva asked.

“We can,” Marek said. “If someone kneads. And if the yeast is still alive.”

“The yeast too is included in the general moral crisis,” Nick said.

“At least if it is alive, it rises honestly,” Marek answered.

“Listen, you need to be banned from speaking after water. You become deeper than the bucket.”

Marek almost smiled.

“Go yourself next time.”

“You see? It has begun. The distribution of state labour.”

Thomas said:

“I’ll go if needed.”

“We know,” Nick said.

“Is that bad?”

“No. It’s irritating.”

“Why?”

Nick wanted to answer with a joke.

Could not find one.

And that turned out to be a rare moment of honesty.

“Because then I have to decide why I’m not going.”

Thomas looked at him for several seconds.

Then nodded.

“That’s honest.”

“Don’t get used to it.”

Sofia saw something in the room soften a little. It did not disappear. It simply stopped hiding for a second. If one names a small greed, a small fatigue, a small unwillingness to carry the bucket, it does not become noble. But it stops working secretly.

David suddenly reached for the notebook.

Everyone noticed.

He too noticed that everyone had noticed.

“Not the map,” he said.

Nick closed his eyes.

“I ask it be noted: the man now clarifies in advance that he is taking the notebook not for the map. Progress is dubious, but undeniable.”

David opened the notebook to a clean page, not to the map. It was visible. He did not take the pencil at once. First he ran a finger over the clean sheet.

“I wanted to write down who did what.”

The silence became so dense that even the stove cracked more cautiously.

“Why?” Sofia asked.

David heard the question to the end himself.

And grew frightened of the answer himself.

“Not for keeping score,” he said.

“Then for what?” Thomas asked.

“So as... not to forget.”

“What exactly?”

David looked at the pencil.

“That Marek went for water twice. That Lina cooked. That Nora washed the knives. That...”

“That you sat,” Nick said.

Not cruelly.

Rather too precisely.

David raised his eyes.

“Yes.”

Nick set his mug aside.

“Sorry.”

“No need. You’re right.”

“That is not always a reason to continue.”

David put the pencil down.

“I didn’t want to count against anyone.”

“I know,” Sofia said.

“I wanted honestly.”

And that word again became dangerous.

Honestly to the map.

Honestly to the work.

Honestly to the water.

Honestly to the bread.

Honestly to every piece that would fall into a hand.

And suddenly it became clear how close honesty stands to counting. Not always. But often. Especially when a person is afraid of being indebted or offended.

Ivar took the loaf and looked at Lina.

“May I?”

She nodded.

He broke off a small piece. Not large. Ordinary. Placed it before himself on the table.

“This is no longer a gift,” he said. “It is a piece.”

Nick said nothing.

And that was right.

The phrase did not sound like wisdom. It sounded like a kitchen unpleasantness.

The bread really lay as a piece.

Before it had been broken off, it was part of the loaf. Before the loaf was set on the table, it was food for everyone. Before someone said “last until tomorrow,” it was simply bread. And now the piece lay before Ivar, and it had edges. It could be taken, given away, left, hidden, compared with another piece.

A piece knows how to be mine.

A gift is worse.

A gift does not hold its edges so conveniently.

Sofia felt resistance to this thought rising in her. A gift can also be taken. A gift can also be appropriated. A gift can be forgotten as a gift as soon as it ends up in the hand. Perhaps that is exactly why a person loves pieces: they have a boundary one can defend without extra explanation.

Lina took the knife.

“Shall I cut?”

Nick said:

“Now this is a political question.”

“Then I cut,” Lina said.

“And that, dear friends, is how power arises.”

“Nick.”

“I’m silent. But remember: it all began with bread.”

Lina cut the loaf calmly, into even slices. Not too thick, not transparent. She did it as if her hand had long known the size that is enough for a person not to feel either deprived or caught in greed.

But even that did not save it.

Because once the slices lay on the board, they could still be compared.

One with an edge.

One soft from the middle.

One a little thinner.

One with a crack in the crust.

Nora took the one with the crack.

Not because she wanted to be good. Or she did. It was unclear. Sofia did not know which was worse: visible greed, or generosity that a person can also use as a place for himself.

Thomas took the middle one.

Then looked at David and put it back.

“You take it.”

David shook his head.

“No need.”

“Take it.”

“Thomas.”

“You need to eat.”

“Everyone needs to eat.”

“Not everyone was sick yesterday.”

There it was.

The phrase everyone had avoided.

Not cruel.

Almost caring.

But it brought yesterday into today’s table as a right to a different piece.

David turned pale, not strongly, but visibly.

“I don’t want that to count,” he said.

Thomas answered:

“I’m not counting.”

Nick said quietly:

“Already.”

Thomas turned to him.

“What?”

“It is already counted.”

“I wanted to help.”

“I know.”

“Then why are you...”

“Because help too can put a person in a separate stack.”

Thomas fell silent.

Nick looked as if he himself regretted what he had said. But the phrase was already on the table, beside the bread, the berries, and the second bucket of water.

David took not the middle slice, but the end one. The one where the crust was harder.

“There,” he said. “Fine.”

Nora raised her eyes.

“Forbidden word.”

David almost smiled.

“Then tolerable.”

“Better.”

Sofia saw how he ate the piece slowly. Not from hunger. As proof that he was participating again. But participation through bread also had a size.

In the afternoon they had to go for firewood.

Not far. Not into the forest, not to the hollow. To the shelter behind the house, where the logs lay under the roof, but the driest ones had already been chosen over the previous days. What remained closer to the edge was a little damper. What lay deeper required moving the upper row. A small job, but in a house where for three days they had spoken about the living thing, traces, the map, the place, and return, even a small job could become a test.

Marek went first.

This time Nick stood up at once.

“I’m going.”

Marek looked at him.

“For a report?”

“For firewood. And to preserve my personal reputation after my morning confession.”

“Take gloves.”

“You see? The state cares for the working class.”

Lina threw a towel at him.

He caught it.

“Are these gloves too?”

“This is so you take out the rubbish on the way back.”

“And here come the taxes.”

Sofia stood too.

“I’ll go.”

“You washed,” Eva said.

“I didn’t.”

“Almost.”

“Almost doesn’t count.”

Eva looked at her with an expression that made it clear: it counts exactly.

And because of that Sofia stubbornly took the gloves.

David stood too.

Everyone again looked too quickly.

He stopped.

“I can carry the small ones.”

Marek said:

“You can.”

Nick opened his mouth.

Closed it.

David noticed.

“Thank you for not saying it.”

“You’re welcome. That was a great inner labour. Please enter it in the log.”

David took the notebook from the table, lifted it, and looked at Nick.

“In mine?”

The phrase came out almost by accident.

Mine.

And immediately struck.

Thomas, who until then had been silent, turned.

“In ours,” he said.

Sofia felt the air grow thinner.

Even Marek stopped with a glove in his hand.

David looked at Thomas.

“What?”

“I said: in ours. We all took part.”

“In what?”

“In the map.”

“I drew it.”

“From our words.”

“I didn’t say it was only mine.”

“You said ‘mine.’”

“I meant the notebook.”

“The map is in the notebook.”

“There is a lot in the notebook.”

“But the map is common.”

Nick said quietly:

“The firewood can wait. The history of ownership has awakened before us.”

No one smiled.

David set the notebook back on the table. Carefully. Too carefully.

“All right,” he said. “Common.”

“I didn’t mean...”

“I said all right.”

Thomas fell silent.

But “all right” was not all right.

It was a lid.

Sofia looked at the notebook. Grey, slightly bowed from yesterday’s damp. Inside — the map. Lines, dots, the white place, the erased water, what had not matched the hollow, what David had not corrected, what Thomas had wanted to continue. Until that minute the notebook had been David’s thing. The map — their common anxiety. Now those two layers had collided, and it became visible: even anxiety can acquire an owner.

Ivar did not interfere.

He sat by the stove and cleaned a knife. Not an old one, not a special one. An ordinary kitchen knife. Wiping a thin strip of butter from the blade, he looked like a man who knows: if you give people enough bread, water, fatigue, and a common drawing, they will find for themselves what to call theirs.

Sofia suddenly saw a brief glint.

Not like a great vision.

More like a splinter of light behind closed eyelids.

A dark house. Fire. People sit around an unfound object.

The object is not there.

There is only rumour.

There is only a place where, perhaps, it will be.

There is only a road that, perhaps, will lead.

And still they already speak as if a full bowl lay before them.

“First the children,” says a woman.

“First those who walked,” answers a man with a bandaged arm.

“To the one who finds it,” says a young man.

“The elders,” says another.

“Our own,” someone by the wall says.

A pause.

“And strangers?”

“If there is any left.”

The fire crackles.

The bowl does not appear.

But the place for the bowl is already being divided.

The glint disappeared.

Sofia stood in the kitchen, holding gloves, and looked at the closed notebook.

“What?” Nick asked.

“Nothing.”

“With us, that word has long needed a separate room.”

“They were dividing what wasn’t there,” she said.

Nick looked at the notebook.

“I don’t envy them. At least we have paper for that.”

Thomas heard and turned away.

David took the gloves.

“I’m not against the map being common.”

“I know,” Thomas said.

“But the notebook is mine.”

Thomas turned again.

David raised his hand, not letting him answer.

“This is not about Sampo. Not about the map. Not about the place. It is simply my notebook. I need at least something to be simply mine, or I’ll start writing down who breathed how many times.”

Nick said quietly:

“I support private ownership of the notebook in order to avoid respiratory accounting.”

Eva sat on the bench.

“The map can be common even if the notebook is David’s.”

Lina added:

“And it can be no one’s if no one opens it.”

Nora said:

“It is inside anyway.”

Nick looked at her.

“Sometimes I want to ask you to speak later. Or never. But later is more honest.”

Ivar finally laid the knife on the table.

“Firewood,” he said.

One word.

And everything returned to the body.

Boots. Jackets. Gloves. Door. Cold. Shelter. Logs.

Four of them went out: Marek, Nick, Sofia, David. Thomas stayed in the house. Not because he had been forbidden. Because after the argument over the notebook, going out together with David would have been too direct a continuation. He stayed by the window, and Sofia almost physically felt his gaze on her back.

It was colder by the shelter. The firewood smelled of resin, wet bark, and old dust. The logs lay unevenly, some dry and light, others heavy with damp. Marek immediately began shifting the upper row, choosing those that would go into the stove today and those that needed to be left closer to the heat for later.

“These — inside,” he said.

“Not these?” David asked.

“Let these lie.”

“Why?”

“Wet.”

Nick picked up one wet log and weighed it.

“It too, perhaps, wants to participate.”

“Let it dry first.”

“A cruel but just system.”

Sofia took two dry logs. They were lighter than they looked. A chip flew off the bark and stuck to her glove. The firewood too was being divided: these now, these later, these dry, these wet, these good, these will wait. Even wood, after being cut down, continued falling into different queues.

David gathered small logs, as promised. Not many. Exactly enough not to look helpless and not to prove too much. This too was a measure. Also a share.

Nick noticed, but this time said nothing.

Marek said:

“You can take more.”

David looked at the logs in his hands.

“I can.”

And did not.

Marek nodded.

That was better than praise.

When they returned, Thomas was washing knives in the kitchen.

Not very skilfully.

On the table lay washed cups, a towel, the closed notebook, and the bowl of berries, which someone had moved to the middle of the table. Very little remained in the bowl. More than one spoonful. Less than everyone wanted.

Thomas did not raise his eyes.

“I decided to do something useful.”

Nick set the firewood by the stove.

“Careful. Usefulness is contagious. They’ll start comparing symptoms.”

Thomas did smile after all.

Weakly.

“I already compared.”

David put his logs beside the others. Not too neatly. One rolled down. He bent and adjusted it.

Thomas said:

“The notebook is yours.”

David froze.

“The map is common, if you want,” Thomas added.

“And if I don’t want?”

Thomas wiped the knife.

“Then, I suppose, it remains in my head anyway.”

Nick said quietly:

“Splendid. We have moved from ownership of the object to unauthorized copying.”

This time David smiled first.

Thomas too.

Not reconciliation.

But the crack stopped widening.

For now.

Lina set another teapot on the table.

“Who wants berries?”

Nick raised his hand.

“I will morally suffer if I take some. But physically I am ready.”

“Suffer less, take a spoon.”

He took one.

Looked into the bowl.

Put the spoon back.

“I can’t. Too little for my dramatic nature.”

Nora took the bowl and divided what remained into four small spoonfuls: for David, Thomas, Sofia, and herself. She gave none to Nick.

“Hey.”

“You refused.”

“I was artistically wavering.”

“Too late.”

“And that is how civilizations collapse.”

Nora ate her spoonful calmly.

David looked at his.

“Why me?”

“Because sour is useful for you,” Nora said.

“Is that a medical fact?”

“No.”

“Then what?”

She shrugged.

“Share.”

Everyone fell silent.

Nora understood what she had said. But she did not take the word back.

David looked at the spoon. Then at Nora. Then ate the berries.

“Thank you.”

“You’re welcome.”

“For the share?”

“For eating it.”

And that turned out to be right.

Sometimes a gift can be saved only by not explaining it for too long.

Ivar stood, took his mug, and went to the window. Outside, the day remained grey. No radiance. No northern postcard. Wet yard, woodshed, dark grass by the wall where the ants had still been working in the morning, the path to the water, the forest that held its places without their consent.

“Tomorrow we’ll bake bread,” Eva said.

“If the yeast is alive,” Marek reminded her.

“And if not?”

“We’ll make flatbread.”

Nick raised his mug.

“Magnificent. The form of government changes depending on the yeast.”

“Nick,” Lina said.

“I’m almost finished. I only want to note: we have found nothing, and the count is already rustling somewhere.”

Nick said it almost lightly.

But this time no one stopped him.

Because he said what was already lying between them.

Sofia looked at the table.

The loaf had grown smaller. The bowl of berries was empty. The cups were not where they had been in the morning. The notebook lay closed, but no longer simply David’s and no longer simply common. Firewood dried by the stove, and among it were the logs David had brought. Water stood in two buckets: one Marek’s, one Thomas’s, but everyone would drink. Nora dried a knife with which she had cut not only for herself. Lina swept crumbs from the table with her palm, and the crumbs fell into a small bowl for birds or for the stove; Sofia did not yet know.

Everything was common.

And almost everything could already become someone’s.

There was the crack.

Not loud.

The house did not split. No one had truly quarrelled. No one had said “mine” in such a way that there was already no return. But the word had been spoken. Share had appeared. Not as law. As a sensation in the hand.

Sofia took her piece of bread.

Ordinary.

Thin.

With crust.

She looked at it and suddenly understood that she was holding not simply food. She was holding a boundary. A small, temporary, edible boundary between “there is” and “mine.” In a minute it would disappear into the mouth, become body, warmth, work, perhaps fatigue, perhaps excess. Then it would return to the world by another route. And still now, in this second, the hand knew: this is my piece.

She felt unpleasant from this precision.

She broke off half and placed it on the plate beside her.

Nick looked.

“For whom?”

Sofia did not know.

“Later.”

“A dangerous recipient name.”

“Yes.”

Nora took that piece several minutes later, without asking.

And for some reason that was good.

Not because it was right.

Because it had not had time to become a rule.

The day passed without an outing.

That was almost strange. After the map, the place, return, the body had finally held them near the house. They did not go to the water, except for Marek and Thomas. Did not go to the hollow. Did not check the rise between the firs. Did not open the map. But because of that, nothing stopped. Inside the house another movement continued: dishes, firewood, bread, tea, wet socks, the notebook, berries, short glances, the unsaid “mine” and “ours.”

In the evening Sofia went out onto the porch.

Alone.

Light from the kitchen lay on the wet boards. Behind the house it was already growing dark. By the woodshed something moved in the grass — perhaps ants, perhaps only wind. The path to the water went down and then was lost. The forest stood in place, as if it had never taken part in any of their mistakes.

Nick came out after her.

“Escaped from the distribution of duties?”

“From bread.”

“That is more serious.”

He stood beside her, not too close.

They were silent for a while.

Then he said:

“Did you notice?”

“What?”

“We haven’t found anything yet.”

Sofia looked at him.

Nick was not smiling.

“And we have already begun thinking how much goes to whom.”

She nodded.

From the kitchen came a sound: Lina set a bowl on the table, someone laughed — Eva, perhaps — Marek said something short, Nora answered. An ordinary evening. An ordinary house. Ordinary people dividing bread, water, warmth, fatigue, the right to be silent, and the right not to go.

Nick sighed.

“Now I am a little afraid for Sampo.”

“Why?”

“Because it isn’t here yet.”

He did not finish.

And did not need to.

Sofia looked at her hands. On her fingers remained a faint smell of bread and wet wood. Her hands were empty.

Almost.

They had found nothing yet.

But they had already begun to divide.
