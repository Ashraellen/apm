# Chapter Eighteen. Exchange

In the morning, the tracks of the forest still darkened on the floor.

They had dried at the edges overnight, but held moisture in the middle. In some places the mud had turned grey. In some places almost black. By the door the tracks overlapped one another so that one could no longer understand who had entered with water, who with wood, who had simply stood at the threshold and blocked the way.

Eva saw them first.

Or was the first to say it aloud.

“Splendid,” she said. “The forest stayed the night.”

Nick, coming down the stairs in one sock, stopped.

“It had a hard day. Don’t throw it out right away.”

“Take the rag.”

“I have only just entered the morning.”

“Then enter it with a rag.”

Nick looked at Sofia as if searching for a witness to injustice.

Sofia stood by the table and held an empty mug. She had not yet decided whether she wanted tea or was simply holding something warm in the future.

“Don’t look at me,” she said. “I brought forest in yesterday too.”

“Betrayal of the common poetic cause,” Nick said.

But he took the rag.

It lay by the bucket, heavy, yesterday’s, still damp. Nick lifted it with two fingers, like a captured creature of doubtful reputation.

“Cleaning,” he said, “is when dirt officially changes address.”

“Then change it,” Eva said.

Lina, already standing by the stove, smiled briefly but did not raise her eyes. She was raking yesterday’s ash to the edge of the hearth with a long iron scoop. She did it slowly, so as not to raise dust. The stove was still warm. Not hot. Grey inside, with a reddish remainder where the coal did not want to become morning at once.

Marek brought a metal bucket.

“Not into that,” Eva said to Nick.

“Why?”

“That’s for ash.”

“Separation of powers,” Nick said, and took another bucket.

Sofia watched him lower the rag into the water. The clean water immediately became cloudy. Not completely. First a grey cloud spread around the cloth, then a thin brown path, then one small pine needle floated up to the surface.

The tracks on the floor were losing their shape.

The water in the bucket was becoming greyer.

Sofia did not think the thought through. It could too easily become beautiful, and the morning was not for beauty. The morning was for a wet rag, cold water, and a person in one sock trying to wipe the floor without losing the last of his dignity.

“You missed the corner,” Eva said.

“I am negotiating with the corner.”

“It is winning.”

“That is temporary.”

Thomas entered the kitchen, already dressed, with damp hair and the face of a person who had managed to wash with cold water and now regarded it as a personal trial.

He stopped by the bucket.

“This gets poured out later?”

“Yes,” Eva said.

“Where?”

“Not by the threshold.”

“Why?”

Marek raised his head from the stove.

“It’s damp enough there as it is.”

Thomas nodded.

“So farther away?”

“To the bushes.”

“Dirty water?”

“It’s water from the floor.”

“Exactly.”

Marek looked at him calmly.

“The floor was outside yesterday.”

Nick froze with the rag in his hand.

“I ask that it be entered into the protocol: Marek has just said a terrible thing and did not even notice.”

“Wipe under the bench,” Eva said.

“The protocol has been suppressed by household regime.”

He crawled under the bench.

Sofia put down the mug and went to the window. Outside, the yard was grey. Not rainy, but wet after rain. By the bushes, where Marek suggested pouring the water, the ground was darker. There were leaves there, branches, last year’s needles. Everything looked dirty enough to accept a little more dirt without offense.

Lina tipped the first handful of ash into the metal bucket.

The ash fell quietly.

Not like sand.

Dryly and softly, as if something of smoke still remained inside it.

“Is it hot?” Thomas asked.

“In places,” Lina answered.

“Then it can’t be taken out right away?”

“It can. But not where there is dry grass.”

“Everything is wet now.”

Marek turned his head.

“Today it’s wet.”

Thomas heard the difference.

So did Sofia.

Today.

Not in general.

Not always.

Only today.

Lina again drew the scoop along the bottom of the stove. Under the ash there was a small coal, almost black, but still alive inside. It flared into a short red point when the air touched it.

“Leave this one?” Sofia asked.

Lina looked.

“This one — yes.”

“Why?”

Lina moved the coal closer to the edge, where thin chips lay.

“It will catch faster from this.”

Thomas watched silently.

Did not ask.

That was noticeable.

Then he asked after all:

“And the rest?”

“The rest has cooled.”

“So it’s no longer needed?”

“It is needed. Just not here.”

Lina said it so simply that the phrase did not have time to become wise.

Eva brought from the shelf a small bucket with apple peels. Yesterday they had been put there carelessly: peel, cores, dark spots, thin pieces Lina had cut from the bruised apple. On top lay a stem with two shriveled seeds.

“Throw this out?” Sofia asked.

Lina raised her eyes.

“Take it out.”

“Where?”

“Behind the shed.”

“Into the pit?”

“Beside it. Into the pit later.”

Nick peered out from under the bench.

“The difference between ‘throw out’ and ‘take out’ has just saved part of the morning from meaninglessness.”

“Have you wiped there?” Eva asked.

“Almost.”

“So no.”

“Your trust wounds me, but helps the quality.”

David came down later than the others.

He held the notebook under his arm, but this time did not open it at once. He stopped by the door, looked at the floor, at the bucket with grey water, at the metal bucket by the stove, at the apple peels.

“Did I miss the meeting of leftovers?” he asked.

Nick crawled out from under the bench.

“Leftovers do not hold meetings. They have already survived our decisions.”

David looked at him.

“That sounds like a phrase better not written down.”

“At last you are beginning to understand my best sides.”

David sat by the window.

Opened the notebook.

Then looked at the page and did not write for a long time.

Sofia could see only the top of the sheet. Yesterday’s lines were still there: Stove. Wood. Door. Water. People. Several arrows. One unfinished, stopped between forest and water.

David turned the page.

On a clean sheet he wrote:

CLEAN / DIRTY

Below:

INSIDE / OUTSIDE

He looked at the bucket of water.

Then at the floor, which Nick had already almost wiped.

Then at the door.

“What?” Sofia asked.

“I don’t know where to put it.”

“Put what?”

“The water.”

“In the bucket.”

“I can see that.”

He was not even irritated.

That was worse and better at the same time.

“I meant — on the page.”

Nick came over with the rag, from which grey water dripped.

“Write: ‘water with a biography.’”

David looked at the rag.

“It was clean.”

“Nick,” Eva said.

“That’s all. Too early for confessions.”

David wrote nothing after INSIDE / OUTSIDE.

He closed the notebook.

Not sharply.

Simply left the page without a decision.

Marek lifted the bucket with grey water.

“I’ll take it out.”

“I’ll do it,” Nick said.

Everyone looked at him.

“What? I was changing its address. You can’t abandon the process halfway along the bureaucratic path.”

He took the bucket with both hands and immediately understood that it was heavier than it looked.

Sofia went with him.

“I’m the witness,” she said.

“Testimony accepted. Just don’t touch the bucket, otherwise the feat will become collective and lose its advertising value.”

They went out into the entryway. The air there was colder than in the kitchen and smelled of wet boots. Nick set down the bucket, opened the door with his shoulder, picked it up again. Outside, the grey day stood low. There was no rain. Drops hung on the branches, and each held on too long, as if waiting for its own separate order.

Nick carried the bucket to the bushes.

Not very far.

But far enough for the water to splash onto his boot.

“Symbolic,” Sofia said.

“Be quiet. I am suffering practically.”

By the bushes he stopped.

“Here?”

Marek came out from behind the shed with an empty basket for chips.

“A little farther.”

“There’s already ground there.”

“Here it will run toward the path.”

Nick took three more steps.

“Here?”

“Yes.”

Nick poured out the water.

The grey water did not go into the leaves at once. It lay on top, spread, lingered in hollows, then began to darken together with the earth. On the surface two thin blades of grass remained, and a pine needle stuck to the wall of the bucket.

Nick looked inside.

“Even after moving, someone stays in the apartment.”

Sofia reached out, removed the pine needle, and threw it into the leaves.

Marek came closer.

“Don’t put the peels there.”

“Why?” Sofia asked.

“The fox will scatter them.”

“And where?”

“Behind the shed. There’s a pit.”

“The fox won’t find the pit?”

“It will. But not by the door.”

Marek said it without a smile.

Nick looked at Sofia.

“Northern logic: if the world is coming anyway, at least let it not come through the front entrance.”

They returned with the empty bucket.

In the kitchen Lina had already put water on for porridge. Not the one from the floor. Another. Clean water from the bucket by the wall. She dipped it with the ladle, poured it into the pot, set it on the stove. The water at the bottom was transparent and cold. After a few minutes small bubbles appeared on the sides of the pot. Then steam rose toward the window and lay on the glass as a thin cloudy veil.

Sofia watched the window lose transparency.

Outside, the forest became softer.

Did not disappear.

Simply stopped being seen directly.

Lina poured in the grain.

The sound was short, dry, almost sandy. The grain lay in the water and at first remained separate. Then it began to darken, swell, slowly lose its former hardness.

“How much do we need?” Thomas asked.

Lina did not answer at once.

“For everyone.”

“That isn’t a measure.”

“It very much is.”

Thomas looked at the pot.

“If someone wants more?”

“Then we’ll see.”

“Again, we’ll see.”

“Yes.”

He wanted to object.

Did not object.

Instead, he took an apple and a knife from the table.

“Cut this?”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“So it can be eaten.”

Nick, who had returned with the bucket, set it by the door.

“Thomas, today you are dangerously close to understanding the kitchen as a form of indeterminate management.”

“I just asked.”

“The most terrible things usually begin exactly that way.”

Eva handed Nick a towel.

“Wipe the bucket.”

“It’s empty.”

“It’s wet.”

“So am I, but no one wipes me with such care.”

“Because you resist.”

“Fair.”

He wiped the bucket.

Then set it upside down by the wall.

A circle of water quickly appeared on the floor under it.

David raised his head.

“Not empty again.”

Nick looked at the circle.

“You see? I kept silent, and it became smart by itself.”

Meanwhile Marek carried out the metal bucket with ash.

Not alone.

Sofia followed him, because she wanted to see where what had been warmth yesterday went.

He carried the bucket carefully. The ash inside did not move, but from it rose the thin smell of the stove. Not smoke anymore. And not wood. Something dry, old, grey.

Behind the shed, under the shelter, stood two buckets like it. One empty. In the other was old ash, already completely cold and light. Marek put the new one beside them.

“Not pour it out?” Sofia asked.

“Later.”

“Where?”

“There’s a place.”

He pointed behind the shed, closer to the old bushes, where the ground was darker and lower.

“Why not now?”

“It may be hot.”

“You checked.”

“Not all of it.”

Sofia looked at the grey surface.

There was no fire in it.

And still Marek did not touch it with his hand.

That was more convincing than any explanation.

“And if we leave it here?”

“It will get wet.”

“And what then?”

“It will become heavier.”

Sofia nodded.

Ash was not simply what remained after fire. It too had to be handled. Not thrown anywhere. Not placed under rain. Not trusted too early.

Marek lifted the empty bucket.

“Take chips on the way.”

“Which ones?”

“Dry ones.”

He said it and looked at her himself.

Sofia looked too.

Under the shelter lay different pieces: thin dry splinters, wet broken bits, bark that seemed dry outside but darkened inside. Sofia took several of the lightest. Then one heavier.

Marek silently took it from her hand and put it back.

“This one later.”

“I wouldn’t have understood.”

“Now you have.”

“No.”

He looked at her.

Sofia smiled slightly.

“Now I know that you took it out.”

Marek nodded.

“That is also a beginning.”

On the way back by the door they met Nora. She was carrying a bucket with apple peels. She held it carefully in front of her, as if inside was not what remained after food, but something that could still soil the wrong place.

“Where?” she asked.

“Behind the shed,” Marek said.

“Into the pit?”

“Beside it.”

“Then into the pit?”

“Then.”

Nora nodded.

Went.

Sofia wanted to offer help, but Nora had already turned the corner. The bucket was small. Her steps were even. The wind moved the edge of her jacket. She stopped by the shed, looked down, then a little to the left, then set down the bucket and did not shake the peels out at once. First she removed from the rim one piece of apple that had stuck to the metal.

Then shook out everything else.

Sofia watched from a distance.

Nora turned around.

Was embarrassed.

“I didn’t know if it was allowed with seeds,” she said when she returned.

“It’s allowed,” Marek said.

“Can they grow?”

Nick, appearing in the doorway with a towel over his shoulder, said:

“If an apple-tree rebellion begins behind our shed, I am ready to acknowledge my share of responsibility as a consumer.”

Marek looked at him.

“It won’t grow here.”

“Harsh.”

“Cold.”

Nora looked at the empty bucket.

“Then why is it allowed?”

Marek shrugged.

“It’s that kind of place.”

And again this was not an answer for Thomas.

But Thomas was not nearby.

He was standing in the kitchen and cutting apples.

Not very evenly.

But no longer asking every time.

Sofia saw this through the window before she went in. Thomas stood by the table, bent slightly, with the knife in his right hand. Lina stirred the porridge. Eva was taking bowls out. David sat by the window and looked at the closed notebook as if it might itself confess that not everything had to fit inside.

The kitchen smelled different than in the morning.

Not cleaner.

Warmer.

Steam had settled on the glass. Apples lay on the table. In the stove the dry chips, which Sofia had brought correctly only in part, crackled. The wet wood by the wall darkened, but the edge of one log had already begun to grow lighter from the heat.

The porridge thickened.

Lina took off the lid. Steam rose thickly, with the smell of grain and apple. She added the cut pieces, a pinch of salt, then something from a small jar — not sweet, rather spicy. Nick inhaled at once.

“The food has acquired character. This is alarming.”

“Sit,” Eva said.

“I wanted to say a compliment.”

“You’ll say it shorter sitting down.”

He sat.

The bowls went around the table. Not equally full. Lina served the children first — although there were no children here, only Nora, whom no one already called a child, but the hand still served her a little earlier. Then Marek. Then Thomas. Then Eva, David, Nick, Sofia. Herself — last.

Thomas noticed.

“You have too little.”

“It’s enough.”

“I can give some.”

“Eat.”

“But...”

“If there’s any left, I’ll add.”

He looked into his bowl.

Then at the pot.

Then put down the spoon.

“How much is left?”

Nick closed his eyes.

“And here we are again at the sacred edge of the pot.”

Lina raised the ladle.

“A little is left.”

“Then why so much for me?”

“Because you cut apples and got tired from questions.”

Thomas wanted to smile.

It did not happen at once.

But it happened.

“That isn’t a measure,” he said.

“It very much is,” Nick answered.

This time he said it without mockery.

They ate.

The porridge was neither sweet nor bland. The apples in it had grown soft, but had not lost their shape completely. The grain held warmth for a long time. Sofia lifted the spoon to her mouth and suddenly thought of the water that had been cold in the bucket in the morning. Of the wood that hissed. Of the chip Marek had taken from her hand. Of the ash under the shelter. Of the grey water by the bushes. Of the log that yesterday had still been too wet for fire and now lay by the stove waiting its turn.

She did not fold this into a thought.

Simply ate.

The stove burned more evenly.

Beyond the window, the forest stood in the steam settled on the glass.

After the meal the bowls remained on the table.

Empty.

Not equally. At the bottom of Nick’s there was a thin strip of porridge, because he talked while eating and forgot to scrape the edges with his spoon. Eva silently pushed his bowl back to him.

“What?” he asked.

She gave him the spoon.

“Finish the thought.”

He looked into the bowl.

“That was cruel and precise.”

“Eat.”

He scraped up the rest.

David looked at his empty bowl.

Then opened the notebook.

Sofia was almost frightened that he would start another scheme.

But he wrote only one word:

BOWL.

Looked.

Crossed it out.

Wrote beside it in Russian:

миска.

Then closed the notebook.

“Why did you cross it out?” Sofia asked.

“I don’t know.”

“The English?”

“No. The word was simply too clean.”

Nick raised his eyebrows.

“David, you are becoming dangerous. A little more and kitchen objects will stop fearing you.”

David did not answer.

He ran a finger along the edge of the bowl, where warmth remained.

Sofia stood up to help clear.

The bowls were carried to the sink. The water in the basin grew cloudy from porridge. Lina did not pour it out at once. First she wiped the bowls, then the spoons, then the pot where the porridge had stuck a little at the bottom. She poured in water, waited, scrubbed again. What had stuck did not come off at once.

“You can do it harder,” Thomas said.

Lina handed him the pot.

“You can.”

He took it.

Began scrubbing.

Too hard.

The pot scraped.

Lina put her hand on his wrist.

“Don’t tear it off.”

“How then?”

“Wait.”

“But it’s dirty.”

“It’s soaking.”

Thomas looked at the dark spot at the bottom.

“Will it come off by itself?”

Nick had already opened his mouth.

Eva looked at him.

He closed it.

Lina said:

“Not by itself. With water.”

Thomas put the pot back.

The water in it stood cloudy, with several pale grains floating to the surface.

“How long should we wait?” he asked.

“Until it can be done without scraping.”

Thomas nodded.

And left the pot.

At that moment Sofia saw the glint.

Not in the window.

In the water at the bottom of the pot.

The grey surface trembled because Lina had set a bowl nearby. In the trembling, for a moment another bottom appeared, not iron, but clay. Someone’s hands held a vessel by the fire. Inside, thick food had stuck to the walls. A woman poured in water and set the vessel nearby, not back on the fire, but beside warmth.

Beside her a boy reached his hand toward a stone.

“Scrape it?” he asked.

The woman shook her head.

“Let it release.”

“By itself?”

“With water.”

He looked at the vessel as if it were being stubborn.

Meanwhile the woman gathered dry crumbs from the floor and threw them into a small wooden bowl. Not into her mouth. Not into the fire. Into the bowl. Then she went to the door and poured the crumbs onto a flat stone by the wall.

A bird flew down at once.

Not beautiful.

Grey, with a quick angry eye.

It snatched one crumb and flew away.

The boy wanted to smile, but the woman had already returned and placed the vessel closer to him.

“Now.”

He ran a finger along the clay.

What had stuck came away more easily.

The glint trembled.

In the pot there was again cloudy water and grains at the edge.

Sofia blinked.

Nick was looking at her.

“I am silent,” he said.

“You haven’t asked anything yet.”

“I am aging. I’m starting to economize questions.”

Nora, sitting by the window, looked at the pot.

“It’s easier that way,” she said.

And immediately turned away.

Nick raised a finger.

Then lowered it.

“No. That is too clean. I won’t spoil it.”

Eva handed him a wet spoon.

“Then wipe.”

“This is how great silences perish.”

He wiped.

The day moved on through small tasks.

Marek carried the old ash out to the place he had shown in the morning. Not all of it — only what was already cold. He left the new ash under the shelter. Nora helped move the peels closer to the pit, then returned with the empty little bucket and washed her hands for a long time, although there was almost nothing on them. David finally wrote one short word under CLEAN / DIRTY:

PLACE

Then beside it, lower, in Russian:

место.

And nothing more.

Thomas saw.

“Did you find where to write the water?”

David shrugged.

“No.”

“Then what is that?”

“For now, place.”

Thomas wanted to clarify.

Changed his mind.

Instead he went to the stove and moved one log closer to the warmth. The same one that had been too damp in the morning.

Marek noticed.

“Not into the fire,” he said.

“I remember.”

“Good.”

Thomas left the log at the edge.

“When will it be ready?”

Marek shrugged.

“We’ll see.”

Thomas gave a tired half-smile.

“That word works every position here.”

“Yes.”

Nick, wiping spoons, said:

“‘We’ll see’ is the chief manager of the northern house. Without a salary, but with full authority.”

“And no days off,” Eva added.

Lina looked at both of them.

“Spoons.”

They fell silent.

For a while.

By evening the floor by the door was almost clean again.

Almost — because a completely clean floor in this house held about as long as hot water in a mug: until too much hope was placed on it.

Boots stood by the wall. Wet. Some turned upside down, others simply placed as they were tired. The empty bucket dried upside down. On its bottom outside, a pale circle from water remained. By the stove, in the metal bucket, lay the new handful of ash — the one Lina had not taken out at once.

The house smelled of porridge, smoke, damp wool, and apple peel.

Outside the window it was getting dark.

The fire in the stove burned evenly.

Not strongly.

Enough.

By evening the house was warm.

By the wall lay wet boots, an empty bucket, and a new handful of ash.
