# Chapter Seventeen. Inside

The house cooled all night.

Not at once.

Not sharply.

It gave up warmth slowly, like a person who does not want to let go of another’s hand, but can no longer hold it tightly. First the cold came into the corners. Then it lay down on the floor. Then it rose to the edges of blankets, to feet, to the gap between sleeve and wrist.

Sofia woke before dawn.

Not from a sound.

From the fact that the air had become different.

The room was dark. Beyond the window there was neither rain nor light — only thick greyness that could not yet be called morning. Nick slept on the neighboring bed, wrapped in a blanket as if he had concluded a temporary truce with the world and had no intention of signing additional agreements.

Sofia lay and listened.

The house was not silent.

Somewhere below it creaked, dryly and briefly. Then the pipe pulled. Then something clicked dully, as if the wood had remembered that it had been a tree before it became a wall.

Then footsteps could be heard.

Not anxious.

Slow.

A person was walking through the house so as not to wake the others, but the house still knew every movement. The floorboard by the stairs answered quietly. The door below opened not all the way. The metal of the stove door rang. Then in the dark something cracked — a chip or a thin log.

Sofia did not get up.

At first.

She lay and thought about yesterday’s schedule by the door. MORNING. DAY. EVENING. Beautiful, even words. They had hung in the kitchen, where there had been warmth from people, from soup, from the stove, from the fact that everyone had not yet dispersed into rooms.

Night had not entered the schedule.

Lina had said: at night it too needed to be left in peace.

The jar — yes.

The house — no.

Sofia sat up.

The cold found her neck at once.

She put on a sweater, felt for the socks that had fallen from the edge of the bed during the night, and went out into the corridor. Under her feet the boards were cool, but not icy. That meant warmth was still holding below.

Or it was being held.

One small lamp above the table was burning in the kitchen. The yellow circle of light did not reach all the corners, and because of that the kitchen seemed larger. Marek stood by the stove.

In a shirt thrown over a T-shirt.

With bare feet in old slippers.

With the face of a person who had not fully woken yet, but had already done what needed doing.

He held a log in his hand and looked into the open mouth of the stove. Inside, the embers reddened. Not fire. The memory of fire, stubborn and uneven. Marek laid the log closer to the edge, not into the hottest place, pushed the door in, but did not close it all the way. Listened.

The stove moved.

Quietly.

Then sighed.

“Did I wake you?” he asked, without turning around.

“No.”

“The house?”

Sofia thought.

“Probably.”

Marek nodded, as if that were a normal answer.

“The kettle is warm,” he said.

She went to the table. The kettle really was warm, not hot. On the table lay chips, a knife, a small pile of bark, and a black speck of coal dust. By the door the tracks darkened. Not fresh anymore. Night tracks.

“You got up?” Sofia asked.

“Yes.”

“Once?”

Marek looked at the stove.

“Twice.”

Sofia sat on the edge of the bench.

“Did you set an alarm?”

He turned to her at last.

There was almost surprise on his face.

“No.”

“Then how?”

“Woke up.”

He said it as if there was nothing else to say.

And perhaps there was not.

The stove clicked quietly.

Marek closed the door.

“Sleep more,” he said.

“I don’t want to.”

“Then sit. Just don’t open the door.”

He said it without instruction. Simply like a person who had already understood in the night what one extra draught costs.

Sofia stayed.

For a while they were silent.

On the side shelf stood the jar under cloth. In the dark it was almost invisible. The white labels on the glass had turned grey. The list by the door was also in shadow. Yesterday it had seemed important. Now it was simply a sheet of paper in a house where someone got up at night not according to it.

Sofia wrapped her hands around a mug, although there was nothing in it yet.

Marek glanced at her hands.

“Pour some if you want. Just not boiling water.”

“Why?”

“There isn’t any.”

The answer was so simple that Sofia smiled.

Marek did not smile.

He listened to the stove again.

Then to the wall.

Then to the wind outside.

It did not look like checking.

That is simply how one listens to a house when the night is not over yet.

Morning came not with light, but with noises.

Someone coughed upstairs. A board creaked in the entryway. The water in the bucket under the table rang slightly when the house was tugged by the wind. Then Thomas appeared on the stairs, in a sweater put on inside out, with the face of a person who is not ready for the world, but already suspects it of improper organization.

He saw Marek by the stove.

Then Sofia.

Then the tracks on the floor.

“You got up in the night?” he asked.

“Yes,” Marek said.

“At what time?”

“When it was needed.”

Thomas stopped on the bottom step.

“I’m serious.”

“So am I.”

“How did you know?”

Marek crouched, opened the stove door, and looked at the log.

“Woke up.”

Thomas ran a hand over his face.

“But why?”

“It got cold.”

“For you?”

“For the house.”

Nick appeared behind Thomas, wrapped in a blanket like a bad philosophical toga.

“Splendid,” he said. “The house complains, Marek gets up, Thomas demands a protocol. Morning has begun honestly.”

Thomas did not turn.

“I am not demanding a protocol.”

“You were born with an internal folder called ‘methodological guidelines.’ That is not an accusation. Almost.”

“Nick.”

“That’s all. I fall silent until coffee. Or until the first absurdity. Whichever comes first.”

Lina came in from the corridor, already dressed, her hair gathered somehow. She looked at the stove, at Marek, at the tracks by the door, at the bucket of water.

“It didn’t go out,” she said.

“No,” Marek answered.

“Good.”

Thomas turned to her.

“But if he didn’t set an alarm, how do we repeat it?”

Lina took a towel and wiped the edge of the table where coal dust lay.

“Why?”

“So next time we don’t depend on whether a person wakes up or not.”

Marek closed the stove.

“Next time someone else will wake up.”

“And if not?”

“Then it will get colder.”

It was not a threat.

And it was not the answer Thomas wanted to receive.

Day began to enter the kitchen.

First grey light lay on the window. Then the yard became visible: wet, motionless, without rain, but entirely made of rain. It dripped from the shed roof. The side of the woodpile darkened. The path to the water had blurred and become wider than yesterday, as if the earth during the night had slightly changed its mind about where to be solid.

David came down last of those who had woken early. He was carrying a notebook, although he clearly did not want anyone to notice.

Nick noticed at once.

“Good morning, chronicler of the domestic climate.”

David looked at him without the old offense.

“I haven’t written anything yet.”

“That is already maturity.”

David sat at the edge of the table and opened the notebook.

Not to a clean page.

To the one where yesterday’s words remained: MORNING. DAY. EVENING.

He looked at them.

Then below he wrote:

Stove.

Thought.

Wood.

Still lower:

Door.

Then:

Water.

The pencil stopped.

Sofia saw his fingers grip the wood a little harder. The list was simple. Too simple. Each point immediately pulled others after it, the way a wet thread pulls all the cold out of a sleeve.

Stove — draught, wind, damper, and wet logs.

Wood — shelter, rain, and the place by the wall.

Door — people, hands, entryway, and the habit of not closing all the way.

Water — bucket, path, cold, and shoulders.

David did not write all that down.

He only looked at the four words that no longer fit inside the lines.

“You can add ‘people,’” Nick said.

David raised his eyes.

“Where?”

Nick opened his mouth.

Closed it.

“Good question. I have changed my mind about being useful.”

Lina put bread on the table.

Old bread.

There was less of it left than it had seemed in the evening yesterday.

She unwrapped the towel and began cutting it thin. Not deliberately. The knife simply went closer to the edge. The slices came out narrower, lighter. On the plate there seemed to be many of them, but each held less bread than the hand expected.

Eva brought apples.

Three.

Then looked at the table, returned to the shelf, and brought one more, bruised on one side.

“Can this still be used?” she asked Lina.

Lina looked.

“It can.”

“And this?” Eva showed the dark spot.

“We’ll cut it out.”

“So not all of it.”

“Almost all.”

Nick sat closer to the stove.

“I like that breakfast is becoming philosophical without our participation. It saves strength.”

“Eat,” Lina said.

“That is precisely how I serve the world.”

Thomas took a slice and held it in his hand.

“When will there be new bread?”

Nick looked at him.

“A brave man. Straight into the heart of the northern kitchen.”

Lina did not turn toward the jar.

And everyone noticed that she did not turn.

“When there is,” she said.

“Today?”

“No.”

“Tomorrow?”

“We’ll see.”

Thomas nodded, but not as he had yesterday. Without irritation. Rather with the heavy recognition that “we’ll see” is sometimes not an evasion, but the only honest unit of time.

The water in the kettle took a long time to boil.

Longer than one wanted.

The stove was already burning, but the wood was wet inside. It hissed, giving water to the fire. Steam rose thinly, almost invisibly. The kitchen smelled of smoke, apple peel, wet wool, and cold iron.

Nora, sitting by the window, suddenly said:

“The house smells of forest.”

Nick slowly turned.

“Is that an accusation or a compliment?”

Nora was embarrassed.

“It just smells.”

Sofia looked at the floor.

By the door the tracks darkened. No longer clear. Blurred. Earth, water, needles, something small brought on soles. The house smelled of forest not because someone had opened a window for beauty.

The forest entered on feet.

After breakfast Marek said they needed to check the woodpile.

“Again?” Nick asked.

“Not again. More.”

“I hear the difference and suffer from its practicality.”

“Coming?”

Nick looked at the window.

There was no rain behind it.

That promised nothing.

“I’m coming,” he said. “But only so no one decides I am saving myself for great tasks.”

“That would be implausible,” Eva said.

“Eva, the morning has only begun and you are already cutting without a knife.”

Outside the air was damp and low.

Such air did not envelop. It lay on the face, on the hair, on the collar, like a thin wet cloth. After the night the yard had become different. Not destroyed, not flooded, simply a little less convenient for human intentions.

At the woodpile Marek pulled off the upper tarpaulin.

Under it the wood was dry only where they had managed to shift it yesterday. At the edge the logs had darkened, heavy with water. Marek took one and struck it against another. The sound came out dull.

“Is that bad?” Thomas asked.

He had come with them after all.

In a jacket fastened to the chin, with the face of a person who had decided not to argue with the wet world, but was continuing an internal protocol.

“Not good,” Marek said.

“But it will burn?”

“It will.”

“Badly?”

“Smokily.”

“Then we should have brought more in yesterday.”

Marek looked at him.

Not sternly.

Simply looked.

“Yesterday we didn’t know the wind would turn like that.”

“But you said you heard it.”

“After.”

Thomas pressed his lips together.

“So one should have heard earlier.”

Nick lifted a small log.

“Thomas, right now you are demanding yesterday’s Marek be today’s Marek. That is no longer control; that is time travel.”

Thomas wanted to answer.

Did not.

Because the log in Nick’s hands fell apart into two pieces. Inside it was darker than outside, damp, almost soft at the edge.

“Oh,” Nick said. “Wood also has an inner life. Unpleasant.”

Marek took the halves.

“This goes to the end. Let it dry.”

“Where is the end?” David asked.

He stood nearby with the notebook in his pocket. He did not take it out, but the pocket gave him away better than any confession.

Marek pointed with his hand.

“There.”

“Why there?”

“Air moves there.”

David looked.

There was nothing special there. Simply a place between the wall of the shed and an old barrel. Not too open. Not too closed. A dry end that was dry not because of a sign, but because the wind, the roof, the distance, and the old barrel had for some reason agreed better than people.

David slipped his hand into his pocket.

Did not take out the notebook.

Sofia noticed.

He noticed that she noticed.

This time he did not begin to explain.

They shifted the wood in silence.

Not for long.

But long enough for hands to begin smelling of bark and damp. Boots sank into the softened earth by the shed. Each step left a mark that at once filled with water at the edges. At first Nick tried to choose dry places, then stopped and announced that he had officially entered negotiations with the mud, but the mud was the stronger party to the contract.

Nora carried small logs.

Not because she had been given small ones.

Because she chose for herself the ones she could carry and not drop. She did it better than Nick with the large ones.

“Don’t look like that,” he said.

“Like what?”

“Like a person who has understood my true carrying capacity.”

“I wasn’t looking.”

“Exactly. Mercy can be especially cruel.”

When the woodpile was covered again, Marek said they needed to bring water.

“Now?” Thomas asked.

Marek looked at him.

Thomas exhaled himself.

“Because later the path will be worse.”

“Yes.”

Nick took the bucket first.

“I am growing up too quickly. It’s suspicious.”

The path to the pump went past low bushes, along wet moss, then slightly downward, where the earth became softer. The house remained behind. From outside it looked smaller than from the kitchen. Not weaker. Simply smaller.

Sofia stopped for a second and looked back.

The roof was dark with moisture. From the pipe came thin smoke, which did not rise straight but immediately lay sideways toward the forest. The windows reflected the grey sky. The door seemed a dark rectangle that could be closed, but not erased.

The house did not stand in the middle of empty space.

It stood in wet air, on earth, beside water, under trees, between wood and path, beneath a wind that went its own way.

Sofia did not think further.

Nick called to her:

“If you are seeing deep meaning right now, tell it we’re carrying a bucket.”

“I’m coming.”

The pump was cold to the touch.

Marek worked the handle several times. Inside, first the air hummed. Then rusty water came, briefly, reddish, almost ashamed. Then clean water. Cold. It struck the bucket with a sound as if it were falling not from above, but from depth.

Thomas bent over.

“Does it need to be boiled?”

“For tea — yes,” Marek said.

“And in general?”

“We drink it.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“That’s experience.”

Nick looked at the water.

“In Thomas’s defense: experience often looks like a bad answer until someone’s stomach hurts.”

“It doesn’t hurt here,” Marek said.

“A strong scientific argument. Living, verifiable, but with risk.”

David did take out the notebook after all.

Not at once.

First he looked at the pump, at the bucket, at Marek’s hands, at the water that became clean only after the rusty beginning.

Then he wrote:

Water.

And after the word he put not a period, but a dash.

The dash remained hanging.

He did not know what to write after it.

Source?

Pump?

Boil?

Carry?

Do not spill?

Drink?

Wait until the rust leaves?

Sofia looked at his pencil.

David closed the notebook.

“Later,” he said.

Nick lifted the bucket.

“Hurray. The world has won another five minutes before turning into a table.”

But he said it more softly than usual.

They walked back more slowly.

The water pulled their hands down. The bucket hit the knee if one walked too quickly. It could not be held far from the body — too heavy. It could not be held too close — the water splashed onto the boot. Steps had to be adjusted to the bucket, the path, the mud, the other shoulder nearby.

Sofia carried the second bucket together with Nora.

Each held one side of the handle. It was uncomfortable. First one pulled faster, then the other lagged. The water answered every disagreement.

“Stop,” Nora said.

Sofia stopped.

The bucket still swung.

The water reached the edge, but did not spill.

“What?”

Nora looked at the path.

“It’s better from the side here.”

“There’s moss there.”

“It holds.”

Sofia looked. The moss really did hold. Not like a bridge, not like a board. It simply gave the foot down a little less.

They went by the side.

Nora was right.

And was embarrassed by that again before anyone managed to say anything.

“I don’t know,” she said.

“What?”

“It just seemed so.”

“It seemed well.”

Nora did not answer.

Nick, walking ahead with a bucket, said:

“I too would like things to sometimes seem well to me. So far mostly they seem wet.”

At the house the door was closed.

Marek opened it with his elbow. The smell of water, moss, wet bark, and mud on soles entered at once. They set the buckets in the entryway. New tracks appeared on the floor over the old ones. Not identical. Nick’s broad track. Nora’s narrow one. Marek’s heavy one. David’s smeared one, because he slipped at the threshold and pretended it had been almost intentional.

Eva brought a rag.

Looked at the floor.

“How long can one keep bringing mud in?”

Marek pulled off a boot.

“As long as we walk.”

Eva wanted to answer.

Did not.

Because that too was not an argument.

They entered the kitchen.

Inside it had become warmer, but not dry. The wood by the wall smelled of forest. The jackets steamed. The water in the bucket stood black and still until Lina dipped a ladle into it. Apple peels lay on the table. On the shelf by the side wall the jar stood under cloth, almost forgotten for several minutes.

Almost.

And that was good.

Thomas noticed it first.

Looked.

Did not go over.

Sofia saw it and for some reason felt gratitude.

Not even toward him.

Toward the distance he had held.

Lina put a pot on the stove.

“What will there be?” Nick asked.

“Food.”

“Specific and terrifying.”

“Porridge.”

“Already better. Fear has received form.”

David sat by the window.

Opened the notebook.

The four words from the morning remained there:

Stove.

Wood.

Door.

Water.

Now he added a fifth:

People.

Looked.

Then drew a line from people to door.

From door to warmth.

From warmth to stove.

From stove to wood.

From wood to forest.

He did not draw the line from forest to water.

Not because there was no connection.

Because the hand stopped.

He looked at the sheet and suddenly, almost frightened, closed the notebook.

“What?” Sofia asked.

“Nothing.”

“David.”

He ran his palm over the cover.

“If I continue, everything will be there.”

Sofia did not answer.

Neither did Nick.

Even Nick.

Beyond the window the forest stood close. Did not approach. Did not retreat. Simply was. Branches held water. Sometimes a drop fell after a delay, as if the tree were thinking whether to let it go or not.

At some point Sofia saw the glint.

Not strong.

Not like a window into another time.

Rather like cold that for a second remembered its old name.

A low dwelling.

Wet walls.

People inside are pushing moss, dry grass, scraps of hide into the cracks. One holds a child closer to the fire. Another comes in from outside and brings on his shoulders water, wind, and the smell of raw earth. Behind him another enters — with wood that has not had time to dry. A third opens the flap to carry out ash, and the cold passes over the floor.

They close the cracks.

Again and again.

The cold enters anyway.

Not only through the walls.

Through hands.

Through hair.

Through wood.

Through water.

Through those who return, because without returning the fire also does not hold.

The woman by the hearth says nothing.

She only takes the wet wood and puts it not into the fire, but beside it.

To wait.

Not because patience is beautiful.

Because wet wood first has to be near warmth before it can become warmth.

The glint disappeared.

Sofia stood by the window.

Before her was glass, beyond the glass — the grey day. In the glass — the reflection of the kitchen: Lina by the stove, Marek by the door, Thomas with a mug, Nora on the bench, Nick trying to dry a sock and preserve dignity at the same time, David with the closed notebook.

Sofia heard a drop fall from a jacket in the entryway.

Then another.

Then the stove sighed briefly.

“You went away again,” Nick said.

“A little.”

“Is it drier there?”

“No.”

“The stability of the world is beginning to tire me.”

Nora looked at her boots by the door.

“It’s wet inside too,” she said.

And immediately lowered her eyes, as if she had said something unnecessary.

Nick raised a finger.

“That I am not going to comment on.”

“Why?” Thomas asked.

“Because sometimes even I feel where the edge is.”

Eva said from the entryway:

“Then wipe it with a rag. The edge is all mud.”

Nick stood.

“There. I have been returned again from metaphysics to floor maintenance.”

“You’re safer there,” Eva said.

He took the rag.

And really began wiping the floor.

Not well.

But with the expression of a person performing an ancient rite that descendants will incorrectly call cleaning.

The porridge took a long time to cook.

Not because the grain was special. The stove was still gaining strength, the wood was giving off water, and the pot at first only thought about boiling. Lina did not hurry it. Stirred, covered it, opened it, stirred again. Eva sliced apples, including the bruised place, which turned out almost entirely savable. Marek carried the wet chips closer to the wall, where the air moved better. Thomas watched his movements and no longer asked at once.

That was noticeable.

Instead of a question he took one of the wet logs and put it beside the stove, not into the fire.

Marek saw.

Nodded.

Thomas nodded briefly in return.

No victory.

Simply the log had ended up where it was better for it to be now.

The jar on the shelf stood calmly.

Nick passed it with a wet rag, stopped, looked, and said:

“Don’t worry. Today not everything revolves around you.”

“Who are you talking to?” Eva asked.

“To the cultural trauma of recent days.”

“Wipe under the bench.”

“As you say, guardian of domestic realism.”

And he wiped.

After the meal the day became wider.

Not lighter. Wider.

Outside the wind had quieted a little, and the forest began giving off sounds separately: drops from branches, the distant cry of a bird, water somewhere lower down, the short rub of a branch against the shed wall. The house answered with its own sounds: the stove, the floorboards, the lid of the pot, the creak of the door, someone’s cough in the far room.

Sofia went into the entryway for dry socks.

Stopped by the door.

The floor was written over with tracks.

Not with words.

With soles.

The mud dried at the edges, darkened in the center where it still held water. Some tracks went in. Some went out. Some lay over one another so that one could no longer tell who came in first, who went out, who brought water, who wood, who simply stood and blocked the passage.

Eva, passing by, sighed.

“Washing again.”

Sofia nodded.

“Yes.”

But did not go for the rag at once.

She opened the door.

Not for long.

Outside the house stood on wet earth.

The forest was close.

Not threatening.

Not tender.

Close.

The water in the bucket by the wall reflected the grey sky. Dark patches showed on the wood. Smoke came from the pipe and at once became part of the wet air.

Sofia closed the door.

Tightly.

Checked the handle.

Then looked down.

The house stood with the door closed.

But on the floor the tracks of the forest darkened.
