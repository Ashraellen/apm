## Prologue

That night, the earth was grinding.

Not with stones.  
Not with millstones.  
Not with iron, as people would later tell it — people for whom it is always easier to believe in a machine than in something alive.

It was grinding deep beneath the snow, beneath roots, beneath dark water, beneath the bones of animals and forgotten people. It ground slowly, without sound, like a heart that does not ask the body for permission to beat.

Above the forest stood the northern lights.

They did not dance. They did not shimmer, as people like to say when they look at the sky with a tourist’s eyes. They hung low and motionless, as if someone enormous had opened a green wound above the world.

At the edge of the frozen lake stood seven men.

Each of them held iron in his hands.

They had not come for gold. Gold lay in other lands, under other curses. They had not come for power. Power already belonged to the one who knew how to make the hungry wait.

They had come for what feeds.

The eldest among them went down on one knee and pressed his palm to the snow.

The snow was warm.

He pulled his hand away at once, as if he had touched not the earth, but a living belly.

“Here,” he said.

The others were silent.

They had walked for thirteen nights. Across ice that could not be walked on. Through a forest where the trees grew with their faces turned inward. Through wind that knew the names of their mothers and spoke them in such a way that the men covered their ears with their palms.

They had reached it.

Now all that remained was to take.

The young one, the one who had laughed louder than the others the whole way, was the first to strike the snow with his iron hook.

The hook went in easily.

Too easily.

Under the snow there was no stone. No frozen earth. No root.

There was something soft.

The young man pulled the hook back. A dark, wet strand hung from the point. It trembled by itself, as if alive.

“This is not metal,” he said.

The eldest looked at the strand and spat.

The spit fell onto the snow and hissed.

“Not everything that feeds has to be metal.”

They began to dig.

The snow parted without resistance. Beneath it the earth opened, but not the kind of earth known to those who plough. It was not black, brown, or frozen. It was warm and dark red. White roots stirred in it, like an infant’s fingers. Water moved slowly between the roots. The water smelled of bread.

One of the men laughed.

Not from joy. From fear.

“We have found it.”

The eldest did not answer.

He was looking into the pit, and his face was growing older and older. Not over years — over a few breaths. As if the pit were returning to him not age, but an understanding he had refused to carry all his life.

Steam rose from the earth.

In the steam stood fields.

They saw them all at once: green fields, golden fields, blue with morning cold. They saw women casting grain into furrows. They saw children pulling the first bread from the oven. They saw animals bending down to water. They saw fish making their way upriver. They saw mushrooms pushing their caps out of the moss. They saw berries growing heavy on branches. They saw blood, milk, rain, salt, warmth, rot, growth.

They saw a world that fed itself.

And then the young man raised the hook again.

“If we take this,” he whispered, “hunger will end.”

The eldest turned to him.

“Hunger does not end for the one who wants to take what he is standing inside.”

The young man did not understand.

Almost no one understood.

People rarely understand a warning when it keeps them from becoming the owners of a miracle.

The hook went deeper.

The earth shuddered.

Not violently. Not enough for trees to fall or ice to crack. Simply, for one instant, the whole forest stopped being forest and became one vast act of hearing.

The wolves fell silent.

The water under the ice stopped.

Far away, beyond the lake, a woman sleeping beside her child suddenly lost her milk.

Above them, the northern lights shivered and folded into a thin line.

The young man pulled.

Something round rose from the earth.

Not a mill.  
Not a chest.  
Not a vessel.  
Not a wheel.

It resembled all of these at once, and none of them. Signs appeared across its wet surface, but everyone who looked saw something different. One saw grain. Another saw an eye. A third saw an open palm. A fourth saw his own mouth, open in a plea.

The eldest stepped back.

“Leave it.”

But it was too late.

They had already seen that a miracle could be pulled out.

And everything a person can pull out, sooner or later, he will call his own.

The young man wrapped both arms around the round thing.

In that same instant, ears of grain grew in his palms.

They broke through the skin without blood. Thin green stalks came out between his fingers, unfolded, filled with grain, and scattered onto the snow. The grains were large and warm. They fell and immediately began to sprout.

The young man screamed.

The others rushed toward him.

Someone tried to tear his hands away from the thing. Someone gathered the grains. Someone wept. Someone laughed.

One of them fell to his knees and began eating the snow together with the sprouted grain.

He had seen, just before, how the eldest man’s spit had hissed on the white crust. He had seen it — and he ate anyway.

Hunger is always faster than memory.

The eldest stood motionless.

He understood first.

Not because he was wiser. But because he had wanted the same thing for too long, and had grown tired of his own desire too late.

The round thing began to turn.

Slowly.

Not around its own axis. Around them.

The forest shifted. The lake drew closer. The sky lowered. Snow began to fall upward from below.

Bread was rising out of the earth.

It grew straight out of the dark-red soil, already baked, hot, with split crusts. Loaves opened like fruit. Their steam smelled of a home many of them no longer had.

The men rushed toward the bread.

And then the earth opened its mouth.

Not a maw. Not a pit. Not an abyss.

A mouth.

So enormous that they had been standing inside it from the beginning.

The eldest closed his eyes.

At last he heard how it was grinding.

Not for them.  
Not against them.  
Not because they had asked.

Simply because to be the world means to feed everything that has not yet learned to give thanks.

The young man, still holding the round thing, stopped screaming.

He looked at the eldest, and there was no longer a human being in his eyes. Only a plea.

“What is this?” he asked.

The eldest wanted to answer: “The world.”

He wanted to answer: “The living.”

He wanted to answer: “That which cannot be taken, because you are already inside it.”

But people remember such words poorly.

So he said the smallest of all possible things.

“Sampo.”

And the earth closed.
