# Chapter Twenty-Two. Recognition

The next day the rain stopped.

Not at once.

At first it spent a long time pretending it was still needed: falling in rare drops from the edge of the roof, hanging on branches, gathering on the underside of the railing, breaking loose from it suddenly, as if remembering yesterday’s work. Then it grew quiet. Not dry. Simply without new rain.

After the rain, the house sounded different.

The boards underfoot answered more softly. The entryway smelled of damp clothes, iron, earth, and that remnant of soup which was no longer food, but still held in the air. By the stove someone had laid wet gloves, and they lay with palms open upward, like small tired animals.

Sofia woke late.

Not because she had slept well.

Because during the night the body had spent a long time sorting yesterday’s work into parts: shoulder, wrist, fingers, knee, back. Each part woke separately and demanded recognition.

She lay on the narrow bed and looked at the gray light in the window.

Her thumb hurt when she bent it.

Under the nail earth still darkened, although in the evening she had washed her hands for a long time, almost angrily, until the water in the basin became lighter. The earth remained as a thin stripe. No longer dirt. A trace.

Sofia lifted her hand to the light.

Turned it.

The fingers were hers.

But yesterday they had held not her affair.

She did not continue thinking.

In the house someone walked down the corridor. A floorboard creaked shortly. Then another. Then somewhere a door opened and closed quietly, without morning determination.

Sofia got up.

The kitchen was almost empty.

Eva stood at the table, gathering bread crumbs from the board into a small bowl. Not throwing them away. Simply gathering them. Nick sat on the bench with a mug and looked at his hands as if suspecting them of treason after yesterday’s stones.

“Good morning,” Sofia said.

“It has not objected yet,” Nick said.

Eva did not lift her head.

“The tea is warm.”

“Not hot?”

“Not anymore.”

“Then the world is honest from the beginning.”

Sofia poured tea. Drank standing.

The tea truly was warm, not hot. There was no promise left in it, only remainder. But after sleep and the pain in her hand it proved sufficient.

“Is the ditch holding?” she asked.

Nick looked at the window.

“I personally have not yet entered into relations with it.”

“Marek looked in the morning,” Eva said. “He said: for now.”

Sofia set the mug down.

The word “for now” no longer sounded like a refusal.

More like the state of things.

She went out.

Not because it was necessary to check. Marek had already checked. Not because she wanted to find something. It was simply difficult for her to remain inside while there was still earth under her nail, and her hand remembered the board better than her head remembered yesterday’s conversation.

The porch met her with the smell of wetness.

Not fresh. Not unpleasant. Simply the smell of wood that had held water all night and was now little by little letting it back into the air. The boards were still dark. In several places sand lay on them, brought in by boots. At the edge a thin fish scale was drying, the one the rain had not washed away entirely. It no longer shone. It had become almost transparent.

Sofia looked at it.

The fish was gone.

And this was no longer news.

By the lower step the earth had been torn up and pressed down by many traces at once: hooves, boots, yesterday’s stones, shovel, water. Everything had mixed so thoroughly that it was no longer possible to say where exactly the animal had been, where Marek, where Thomas, where Nick, where she herself. At first it seemed like dirt.

Then — work.

The ditch ran from the edge of the porch toward the bushes.

Crooked.

Shallow.

With collapsed edges. In one place a board held it, in another — three stones stacked so unevenly they did not look stacked. They simply lay where hands had left them. Water was still flowing along the bottom. Thin, cloudy, with small leaves. Sometimes it got stuck by the root, gathered, then found a crack and went farther.

Not under the house.

Sofia stepped down onto the lower step.

The wood under her foot was slippery, but no longer so treacherous. She crouched and touched the board she had held yesterday.

The board was cold.

Earth clung to its side. Where her fingers had held the edge, there were no marks left. No imprint. No gratitude from the wood. Only the wet rough surface.

Sofia ran her thumb along it.

Dirt got under the nail again.

The same dark as yesterday.

The same as on the potato that had rolled out of Matti’s bag.

The same as in the water David had poured into the bushes.

She wiped her finger on the grass.

It did not help.

By the shed lay the second board Ivar had carried under the shelter yesterday. It stood leaning against the wall, dry on one side, dark on the other. Nearby was the shovel, turned with its iron toward the wall. On the handle, traces of palms were drying. Other people’s. Her own. No longer possible to tell apart.

Sofia walked to the barrel.

Where Nick and Thomas had taken stones, an empty strip of earth remained. The stones around lay differently than before. She did not know how they had been before. But it was visible that the place had been touched. A small disorder left after a right movement.

Behind the barrel in the grass lay a little glove with a hole by the thumb.

The same one Nora had handed to Nick.

Sofia picked it up.

The glove was wet, cold, almost useless. But yesterday it had still been handed over. And for some reason that kept her from calling the glove bad.

She laid it on the edge of the barrel, fingers down, so it would drain.

Farther on was the path to the water.

Sofia did not go all the way to the lake. She stopped where a gray opening could be seen through the trees. The water in the distance lay level. After yesterday’s ditch, the lake seemed too large to have anything to do with their crooked work by the porch.

But the thin water in the ditch was going there too.

Not at once.

Through bushes, moss, earth, roots, a low place, then perhaps into a brook, then somewhere else. Sofia did not know. Nor did she need to know the whole road in order to see the direction.

She turned toward the house.

From outside the house looked smaller than from within.

Not like a shelter.

Like a thing that itself needed to be held: roof, boards, steps, little ditch, firewood under the shelter, the door Eva had opened and half-closed yesterday so that the cold would not enter all at once.

On the porch the basket was drying.

Lina had set it upside down, but one side was still wet. The wicker had darkened. Below, between the rods, a little leaf was stuck. Sofia remembered how three mushrooms and fish scales had lain at the bottom. Then — how potatoes floated in the pot. Then — how bread had been put aside for the morning. Then — how Thomas had held the knife over the last piece and had not cut it.

They were different things.

Basket.

Fish.

Potato.

Bread.

Board.

Ditch.

But they no longer wanted to stand separately.

Sofia frowned.

Not from a thought.

From the fact that thought kept trying to make an order out of it, and every time it came out worse than the wet objects themselves.

“Did you lose something?” Nick asked.

She turned around.

He stood by the door in a sweater, a mug in his hand. His socks were mismatched. One gray, one dark blue. It seemed he noticed this only after Sofia looked down.

“Don’t start,” he said.

“I’m silent.”

“Your silence is looking at my socks with unacceptable precision.”

She smiled.

“I didn’t lose anything.”

“Then you have the look of a person who either understood the arrangement of the world, or remembered where she left something important.”

“Socks?”

“For example. The world often holds on small things.”

Sofia looked again at the ditch.

“I’m just looking.”

Nick stepped out onto the porch carefully.

“Look carefully. Lately everything here answers with action.”

He set his mug on the railing and also looked at the water.

He was silent longer than usual.

“It’s still flowing,” he said.

“Yes.”

“But not there.”

“Not there.”

“Already not bad for morning.”

Sofia nodded.

Nick took the mug.

“David has spread out papers. This is a warning, not news.”

“What papers?”

“All of them. Or almost all. He currently has the face of a person building a dam out of sheets.”

Sofia turned toward the door.

“Where?”

“In the room with the large table.”

“Why?”

“I asked. He said: ‘to look.’ I decided not to stop the word from working overtime.”

Sofia went inside.

In the entryway it was darker than outside. The eyes did not adjust at once. On the shelf lay the paper bag from the potatoes, folded in half. It had dried, but the dark stain on it remained. Beside it stood the empty bucket. On its handle a stripe of mud had dried. Below, on the floor, lay gloves. Someone’s, not hers.

She stopped.

Bag.

Bucket.

Gloves.

Not beside each other in meaning.

Simply beside each other.

But now “simply” was no longer so simple.

In the large room David had indeed spread out papers.

The table was almost entirely occupied. Sheets lay unevenly. Some with bent corners. On one, an old water stain had spread. On another, several arrows had later been crossed out. On a third, a piece of map, where the forest was drawn with a thin line and looked too dry. Beside it lay a sheet with a recipe. Separately — a note about the mark. Another sheet was almost empty; only in the middle stood the word “gift” and after it a short dash, left unfinished yesterday.

David sat before all this and did not write.

This was more noticeable than any notes.

Lina, by the window, was hanging a rag over the back of a chair. She did not look at the papers. Eva was sorting dry towels by the cupboard. Nora sat on the floor by the wall and tied the lace on someone else’s boot — simply because it had come undone and was lying nearby. Nick came in after Sofia, but stayed by the door, as if afraid that with his comment he would turn on the air too early.

“What are you looking for?” Sofia asked.

David did not answer at once.

He ran his finger along the edge of the sheet with the map. Then took the finger away, because pencil dust had remained on it.

“I don’t know.”

“Then why did you spread them out?”

“I wanted to see where it got lost.”

“What?”

He looked at the sheets.

At the map.

At the recipe.

At the word “gift.”

At the old list where there were still arrows from “trace” to “place,” from “place” to an empty circle, from the empty circle to a question mark.

“I don’t know,” he said. “Maybe it did not get lost.”

Nick at the door drew breath carefully, but remained silent.

Sofia came closer.

The old sheets no longer looked funny. And did not look like a mistake. There was effort in them. David’s hands, which then had not yet held a bucket. His desire not to lose what kept moving faster than paper.

On one sheet was written:

TRACE

under it:

trace

Farther on, an arrow.

On another:

MAP

map

Beside it, a small note in uneven handwriting:

the map is not wet

Sofia did not know when he had written this down. Perhaps after the day when they stood over the low place in the forest. Perhaps later, when he understood that the phrase held better than the scheme.

Another sheet:

RECIPE

recipe

And beside it, almost in the margin:

hands do not fit

She touched that sheet not with her fingers — with her gaze.

Hands do not fit.

But yesterday hands entered.

Not into the recipe.

Into board, water, stone, bucket, door.

The sheet was not a lie.

It simply had nowhere to hold the dirt under a nail.

“Did you write all this to find it?” Sofia asked.

“Yes.”

“And now?”

David took the sheet with the word “gift.”

Looked at the dash.

“Now I don’t know where to lead the arrow.”

“Maybe you don’t need to lead it.”

He looked at her.

“Then what is the sheet for?”

Sofia did not answer.

Because the question was good.

And because a good question did not always deserve a quick answer.

Thomas came in from outside, taking off his jacket.

“The ditch holds,” he said.

Then saw the table.

“Oh.”

“Yes,” Nick said. “Paper migration season.”

Thomas came closer.

Took one sheet.

“Is this the old map?”

“Yes,” David said.

“It’s wrong.”

David nodded.

“Not entirely.”

“There’s no ditch on it. No place where the water went under the porch. No...”

He stopped.

Sofia noticed that he could have continued: no boars, no basket, no Matti, no potatoes, no yesterday’s board, no fact that Marek said “for now,” no hands.

But Thomas did not continue.

“It’s smaller,” he said.

David looked at the map.

“Yes.”

“But without it then we wouldn’t have known at all where we were going.”

“We would have known somewhere.”

“That isn’t the same thing.”

“No.”

Thomas put the map back.

Very carefully.

As if it were not truth, but not rubbish either.

“If everything holds only for now,” he said, not looking at anyone, “how can you count on anything at all?”

Nick opened his mouth.

Eva looked at him.

He closed it.

Marek answered from the doorway. He had come in quietly from outside and had managed to hear the question.

“On everything at once — you can’t.”

Thomas turned.

“And what can you count on?”

Marek took off his hat, shook the water from it by the threshold.

“On what you do when it goes the wrong way.”

Thomas looked at him.

“That isn’t a guarantee.”

“No.”

“That is almost nothing at all.”

Marek hung the hat on the hook.

“Sometimes it’s enough.”

He went to the stove and added nothing else.

Lina took the rag from the back of the chair, saw that it was still wet, and hung it back.

“Don’t touch it,” she said to Nora, who was reaching toward the edge. “Let it finish drying.”

Nora drew her hand back.

“It’s already almost dry.”

“Almost is not dry.”

Thomas chuckled quietly.

“The word ‘for now’ has found a relative.”

“A large family,” Nick said.

But gently.

Without victory.

Sofia looked again at the papers.

Trace.

Map.

Place.

Recipe.

Mark.

Condition.

Watch.

Inside.

Exchange.

Lack.

Gift.

Participation.

Each word lay separately.

But beside each one there remained something that did not fit into the word.

The map was not wet.

Hands did not fit into the recipe.

Gift did not know where to lead the arrow.

Sofia felt that she was thinking too evenly.

And stopped.

Let them lie instead.

Papers.

Mud.

Words.

They did not ask to be connected correctly right now.

“May I?” she asked David.

He nodded.

She took the sheet with the empty circle and the question mark.

In pencil beside it was written:

SAMPO?

Below — in Russian:

Сампо?

The question mark was stronger than the word. David then, probably, still thought that the question would help not to confuse the direction.

Sofia laid this sheet beside the one where it said “gift —.” Then beside the map. Then the recipe. Then the water stain on the cover of the notebook.

Nothing came of it.

In any case, nothing resembling an answer.

But when the sheets lay closer, the word “Sampo” began to look strange.

Not more important.

Not clearer.

Strange precisely because it stood apart.

As if it had been left alone not out of precision, but from the habit of looking for something separate.

Sofia stepped away from the table.

In the room it became possible to hear the water outside the window going along the ditch. Not loudly. So quietly that perhaps she only knew the sound was there. In the house someone set down a cup. Nora tied the lace on someone else’s boot again, now tighter. Lina took down the rag, checked it, hung it again. Marek by the stove turned a log. Nick finally wore the mismatched socks and stopped thinking about them. Thomas looked at the map, but no longer demanded from it more than it could give.

For a moment Sofia thought the room had become wider.

Not the walls.

Not the air.

The connections between things.

She blinked.

Everything remained ordinary.

Table.

Papers.

Stove.

People.

Moist light.

And still something was already not the same.

Nick came closer and said quietly:

“You again have the look of a person who either understood the world or decided not to wash socks until spring.”

Sofia looked at him.

“Socks.”

“I knew it. The world is for now saved from excessive seriousness.”

But he too looked at the sheets.

The smile left not at once, but gradually, like water from a slanted board.

“What is wrong?” he asked.

“I don’t know.”

“That’s a popular answer today.”

“It is alone.”

“What?”

Sofia pointed to the sheet.

Nick bent over.

Read.

Sampo?

“The word?”

“Yes.”

“It has always been alone.”

“Yes.”

She could not explain.

And did not.

David was watching her carefully.

“Do you want me to rewrite it?” he asked.

“What?”

“I don’t know. Maybe without the question.”

Sofia looked at the word.

Without the question it would become even worse.

“No.”

“Then how?”

She shook her head.

“I don’t know.”

David nodded as if, for the first time in a long while, this answer did not stop him from sitting.

Outside the window the thin water was still going past the house.

In the house someone was cutting the remaining bread.

There was still earth under Sofia’s nail.

David took the pencil.

Not to write.

Simply held it between his fingers.

On the sheet the word “Sampo” stood separately.

Sofia looked at it and, for the first time, could not understand why it was one.
