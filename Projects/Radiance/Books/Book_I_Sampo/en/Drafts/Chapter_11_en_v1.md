# Chapter Eleven. Rising

In the morning bread was no longer food.

It had become a deadline.

The last loaf lay on the table under a towel, like a person who has been told that too much depends on him. It had not grown smaller in the night, but in the morning it seemed smaller. A crust had been cut from the edge, crumbs lay nearby, a knife, a small stain of butter, and Nora’s overturned mug, which she had for some reason set upside down, like the mug on the porch yesterday.

Sofia entered the kitchen and immediately saw not the bread, but how much of it was left.

It was unpleasant.

Yesterday the word “share” had still been hanging in the air, like the smell of bread on hands. Today it was not being spoken, but was already working. No one said “how much for whom.” No one argued. No one looked at the loaf too long. Exactly for that reason, everyone looked at it too briefly.

Eva stood at the table, sifting flour.

The flour fell into the bowl quietly, dryly, almost like snow, but without winter beauty. There was no miracle in that falling. There was supply. There was necessity. There was tomorrow, which had to be prepared today, otherwise tomorrow would sit down at the table and silently show an empty board.

Lina was taking a jar of yeast from the pantry.

Not solemnly.

Not like a key.

Like a thing it is better not to forget.

“Alive?” Marek asked.

“We’ll find out,” Lina said.

Nick lifted his head from his mug.

“A magnificent beginning to the day. Even the yeast undergoes a viability test. I feel less alone.”

“We won’t test you,” Lina said.

“A pity. The results might surprise you.”

David sat by the stove, already without the borrowed sweater, but still cautious. He looked better. Not well — better. Yesterday had not left his face completely; it had only moved aside. David held his cup in both hands and did not look at the notebook. The notebook lay not by his place, but on the shelf beneath the window. Closed. Visible. Untouched.

Thomas stood beside Lina.

Not close enough to get in the way, but close enough to get in the way with his attention.

“How much do you need?” he asked.

Lina opened the jar and smelled it.

“We’ll see.”

“So it isn’t known exactly?”

“It is known.”

“Then how much?”

She looked at him.

“Thomas.”

“What?”

“Let the yeast wake up first.”

Nick said quietly:

“Not everyone can wake up under protocol.”

Thomas did not answer.

He watched Lina pour warm water into a small bowl, add yeast, a pinch of sugar, and stir. Her movements were precise, but not measuring. She did not count spoonfuls the way David would have counted. She did not press the process down into a number. She simply knew how much of the water had to be warm, how much sugar was enough, how much time was not hers.

“Is the temperature right?” Thomas asked.

Marek, coming in with firewood, stopped by the door.

“For whom?”

“For the yeast.”

“They won’t answer you.”

“I’m asking whether it’s too hot.”

Marek set down the firewood.

“If it’s too hot, you’ll cook them. It won’t be bread, it’ll be stone.”

Nick set down his mug.

“Please enter into the record: the northern bread code opens with an article on stone.”

“Very funny,” Thomas said.

“No. But it helps me not ask it myself.”

Sofia sat closer to the table. The smell of flour was already in the air — dry, simple, almost empty. The smell of what had not yet become food. After all these days, bread could no longer be simply bread. It had had time to become a measure of the hand, a piece, a share, a way not to offend, a way to take offence, proof of participation. And now it was returning back again — to flour, water, yeast, heat.

Back — but not entirely.

Because it is impossible to return to flour. One can only begin a new process.

Eva sifted a little more flour.

“Enough,” Lina said.

“For two loaves?”

“If it rises well.”

“And if it doesn’t?” Thomas asked.

Lina closed the flour sack.

“Then they will be lower.”

“But will it be enough?”

“It will be enough for as much as it will be enough.”

Thomas exhaled.

“That is not an answer.”

“It is dough.”

Nick leaned toward Sofia.

“I am beginning to suspect that dough is a form of answer impossible to accept in civilized society.”

She almost smiled.

Almost.

Because Thomas was funny only from the outside. Beneath his questions was the same movement that had once led him toward the old path, toward the white place on the map, toward the rise between the firs. He did not want greedily. He wanted clearly. He wanted to know that tomorrow there would be bread, that effort would not be lost, that the process would not fail, that if everything were done properly, the result was obliged to come.

The yeast water began to turn cloudy.

A thin foam appeared on the surface.

Nora, who until then had silently been peeling an apple by the window, turned her head.

“It’s moving,” she said.

“The yeast is working,” Thomas said.

“No,” Nora said.

“What do you mean, no?”

She looked at the bowl.

“Moving.”

And went back to the apple.

Nick said quietly:

“The difference between ‘working’ and ‘moving’ is going to cost us our mental health today.”

Lina poured the yeast water into the large bowl where the flour already lay. Eva added salt. Marek poured more water from the ladle. Lina began stirring with a wooden spoon until the flour became a heavy, lumpy mass. Then she set the spoon aside and went in with her hands.

Thomas visibly tensed.

“My hands are clean,” Lina said without raising her eyes.

“I didn’t say anything.”

“You looked.”

“I just...”

“Yes.”

She kneaded.

At first the dough resisted. It stuck to her fingers, stretched, stayed on the walls of the bowl, returned to itself reluctantly. Then gradually it began to gather. Flour, water, yeast, salt, the heat of hands — all this stopped being separate and became something third. Not bread. Not yet. But no longer flour.

Sofia watched Lina’s hands.

Not beautiful hands. Real ones. With short nails, a pale scar near the thumb, flour in the folds of the skin. They did not command the dough. They knew where to press, where to release, where to fold, where to pull again. Again. And again. There was no haste in it, though there was not much time.

“You could do it harder,” Thomas said.

Lina stopped.

Did not answer at once.

Then folded the dough again and pressed with the heel of her palm.

“I could.”

“Then why not harder?”

“Because there’s no need.”

“But faster?”

“Not everything that is faster can be eaten later.”

Marek grinned by the stove.

Nick raised a finger.

“That, by the way, could be embroidered above the door. Or carved on the forehead of everyone who built civilization.”

“Nick,” Eva said.

“I’m already silent. There are just a lot of foreheads.”

David was looking at the dough as if it were doing something unpleasantly familiar to him. Not a map. Not a list. Not a record. But also a process one could observe and still not control completely.

“What if we write down the proportions?” he asked quietly.

Thomas immediately turned to him, as if he had at last found an ally.

“Exactly.”

David raised his palm.

“I’m not suggesting it now. Just... if it turns out well, we could remember.”

“You can remember,” Lina said.

“So next time it will turn out the same?”

She looked at him more softly than she had looked at Thomas.

“No.”

David nodded.

Did not agree.

Just nodded.

Eva said:

“The flour is different. The water is different. The warmth is different. The hands will tire differently.”

“But roughly.”

“Roughly, yes.”

David barely smiled.

“‘Roughly’ is a word that pretends it is more honest than it is.”

Nick looked at him with respect.

“After yesterday, you have grown dangerously as a philosopher of the kitchen.”

“Don’t remind me.”

“I won’t. Almost.”

The dough finally became as smooth as it could. Lina gathered it into a ball, greased the bowl with a thin layer of oil, placed it back, and covered it with a towel.

Everyone looked.

Too obviously.

The bowl stood on the table.

Nothing was happening under the towel.

That was exactly why it was impossible to look away.

“And that’s all?” Thomas asked.

Lina wiped her hands.

“That’s all for now.”

“How long do we wait?”

“Until it rises.”

“And how long is that?”

“That long.”

Thomas closed his eyes.

“That is not an answer.”

“It is yeast.”

Nick said quietly:

“The second session of the bread committee has reached a dead end due to the resistance of facts.”

Marek placed the bowl closer to the stove, but not too close.

Thomas noticed at once.

“Couldn’t it be a little closer?”

“It could,” Marek said.

“Then why not closer?”

“You cook the yeast — you get stone.”

“You already said that.”

“Because you are still asking.”

Thomas exhaled sharply.

“I want to understand where it is right.”

Marek looked at the bowl.

“Here.”

“Why?”

Marek shrugged.

“Because not there.”

Nick lowered his head onto his hands.

“I surrender. Northern logic has defeated us all.”

Ivar sat by the window and repaired a strap on an old bag. He hardly participated. Only from time to time did he raise his eyes when someone wanted too quickly to move the bowl closer to the heat or lift the towel.

Thomas finally turned to him.

“And what should we do?”

Ivar tightened the knot, tested it with his fingers, and said:

“Wait.”

One word.

Not loud.

Not slow.

Not instructive.

But after it Thomas sat down.

Nick looked at him and, for the first time, did not joke.

Sofia felt that the word had remained on the table beside the bowl. Not like wisdom. Like an inconvenient object. It could not be eaten, removed, moved elsewhere. It simply lay there.

Probably ten minutes passed.

Thomas looked at the bowl.

Then at the clock.

Then at the bowl again.

“Nothing is visible,” he said.

“Because of the towel,” Nick said.

“That’s not what I mean.”

“And I am trying to remain within the limits of the visible.”

Nora sat by the window and finished the apple in small bites. She looked at the towel differently from Thomas. She was not waiting for the result. She was rather looking at the place where the result might someday happen.

“It isn’t hurrying,” she said.

“How do you know?” Thomas asked.

“You’d hear it.”

Nick slowly turned to Sofia.

“I am not ready for bread that hurries out loud.”

Sofia smiled.

This time almost truly.

The day began to arrange itself around the bowl.

Not at once. People continued doing ordinary things. Marek went to check the firewood. Lina put away the flour. Eva washed the dishes after the kneading. David did take out the notebook after all, but wrote not proportions, only something short on a separate page, closed it, and put it away. Thomas tried to read a book, but turned the pages too rarely. Nick said the dough had already become the most influential participant in the meeting and required a separate chair.

The bowl stood by the stove.

Under the towel.

Doing nothing.

And that was not true.

Sofia passed by several times and each time felt the desire to look. Not even from hunger. From anxiety. Has it risen? No? Already? What if it hasn’t? What if the yeast is dead? What if there is not enough bread tomorrow? What if it needed to be warmer? What if Lina made a mistake? What if Marek put it in the wrong place? What if “wait” is simply a beautiful word for helplessness?

She did not open it.

And because of that she was irritated.

At herself.

After a while Thomas could not bear it.

He stood, went to the bowl, and carefully lifted the edge of the towel.

“Don’t touch it,” Lina said without even turning around.

Thomas froze.

“I’ll only look.”

“Don’t touch it.”

“But we need to understand whether the process is going.”

“It goes better when you don’t disturb it.”

“That’s superstition.”

Lina turned.

“It’s dough.”

Nick lifted his mug.

“I propose we record this: everything Thomas cannot control is first declared superstition.”

“I’m not trying to control it.”

“You are holding the towel over the yeast like a prosecutor over evidence.”

Thomas let go of the edge.

The towel settled back down.

Too sharply.

Lina looked at him.

He grew embarrassed.

“Sorry.”

“To me or to it?”

Thomas looked at the bowl.

“To everyone, apparently.”

Nick said quietly:

“Collective responsibility before the dough. We are growing up.”

The room became calm again.

Not entirely.

Because after the towel had been lifted, everyone now knew: the dough had risen a little.

Only a little.

But it had risen.

And that was enough for hope to become more impatient.

Before, they had been waiting for something unknown. Now they were waiting for the continuation of something that had already begun. That is worse. When nothing is happening, a person can be angry at emptiness. When something has begun happening, a person already demands tempo from it.

The dough had risen slightly.

So it could rise more.

So it should.

That is where the dog had rummaged, Sofia thought, and immediately grinned inwardly: even here, beside a bowl, a person needs the living thing to confirm his expectation step by step.

After midday Lina decided to check.

Not because Thomas wanted it. Because the time had come.

She came over and removed the towel.

The dough had indeed risen. Not magnificently. Not theatrically. Not so that everyone gasped. It had simply become larger. The surface had tightened, rounded; small bubbles had appeared on it. The dough breathed not with sound, but with volume.

“Normal,” Lina said.

David smiled.

Not broadly. With relief.

Thomas came closer.

“Is that enough?”

“For the first rising, yes.”

“The first?”

Lina looked at him.

“Did you think once?”

Nick said quietly:

“Oh no. The process has a continuation. Thomas, hold on.”

Lina turned the dough out onto the board and punched it down. Firmly, but not cruelly. The air came out. The dough became smaller.

Thomas almost grew indignant.

“Why?”

“So it rises better.”

“But it had already risen.”

“So?”

“You... knocked it down.”

“Yes.”

“But we waited.”

“Now we will wait again.”

He looked at her as if she had just destroyed a bridge they had spent all night building.

Nick whispered to Sofia:

“Thomas has just understood the tragedy of bread for the first time.”

Sofia did not answer.

Because the small kitchen scene struck her too.

The dough had risen.

It was knocked down.

Not because it had made a mistake. Not because it had not been good enough. But because that was necessary so that later it would become better. The living thing did not move in a straight line: up, up, up, result. It rose, fell, gathered again, waited again. And to a person, this was almost insulting.

Thomas said:

“Couldn’t we have left it right away?”

Lina divided the dough into two parts.

“We could.”

“Then why didn’t we?”

“Because the bread would be worse.”

“But faster.”

She looked at him.

“Do you need bread, or victory over waiting?”

The kitchen went quiet.

Even Nick did not find a joke at once.

Marek coughed by the door.

“Good question.”

“Thank you,” Lina said.

“Not you. The dough.”

Nick still could not resist.

“Marek, I am beginning to suspect that you talk to objects more often than to us because they object less.”

“They’re more honest.”

“There. Exactly.”

Lina placed the two rounded blanks into tins, covered them with the towel, and set them again closer to the stove.

Thomas sat down.

“More waiting.”

“Yes.”

“How long?”

“Less.”

“That’s already something.”

“Don’t get used to it,” Nick said.

Sofia went out onto the porch.

Not because she was tired of the kitchen.

Because she suddenly felt cramped among the bowl, the towel, Thomas’s gaze, Lina’s patience, and this strange movement of the living thing: rise, be knocked down, rise again. Outside the air was colder. The day remained grey. The woodshed stood in place. By the grass near the wall the morning work of the ants was no longer visible, but Sofia knew it was still continuing there, only now too small for the eyes.

She sat on the edge of the porch.

After a few minutes David came out to her.

“May I?”

“Of course.”

He sat beside her, not too close.

For some time they were silent.

“I wanted to write down the proportions,” he said.

“I know.”

“And I still do.”

“I know that too.”

He gave a short laugh.

“Is it visible?”

“Not very. But I am already familiar with your way of not looking at the notebook.”

He nodded.

“I don’t like that everything depends on ‘roughly.’”

“Yes.”

“Not because I want to control everything.”

Sofia looked at him.

He grimaced at himself.

“All right. Not only because of that.”

“And what else?”

David looked at the path to the water.

“Because if the bread turns out by chance, we learn nothing.”

“And if it turns out not by chance?”

“Then it can be repeated.”

There it was.

Said quietly.

Almost reasonably.

Almost innocently.

Repeated.

Not found. Not taken. Not divided. Repeated. Made so that it happens again.

Sofia felt: not even in the house, but deeper, in the very movement of their days, something had shifted to a new tooth of the gear.

“Do you want bread?” she asked.

“Yes.”

“Or a method?”

David did not answer at once.

Then said:

“A method so there will be bread.”

“Is that not the same thing?”

“I don’t know anymore.”

And that was honest.

From the kitchen came Thomas’s voice:

“Can we look?”

Lina answered something sharp.

Nick laughed.

David smiled.

“He has it worse than I do.”

“Why?”

“I want to write it down after. He wants to move it now.”

“And is that worse?”

“Faster.”

At that moment something flashed again behind her eyes.

Not a full vision.

Not an open door, as by the fire.

Rather a short blow of stone against stone.

A dim room. Low ceiling. The smell of grain, smoke, sweat. A woman sits by a stone and grinds grain. The upper stone is heavy. It goes in a circle slowly, with a dry rasp. Under her fingers flour appears — coarse, with dark flecks, not white, not beautiful. Beside her stands a boy, and he looks not at the flour, but at the woman’s shoulder.

The shoulder rises.

Falls.

Rises.

Falls.

Not like a song.

Like fatigue.

A man by the door says:

“More.”

The woman does not answer.

The stone goes.

The grain is ground.

The flour gathers at the edge.

Someone says:

“If only there were more hands.”

Another answers:

“Hands eat too.”

A dry little laugh.

Then the boy, still looking at the shoulder, says almost in a whisper:

“And what if it did it by itself?”

The stone does not stop.

Neither does the woman.

But everyone hears.

Not because the phrase is loud.

Because there is rest in it.

Not bread.

Not grain.

Rest from the hand.

From the shoulder.

From waiting.

From the fact that the living and the heavy require you every time.

Sofia blinked.

The porch returned.

David was looking at her.

“What?”

She did not answer at once.

“A stone.”

“What kind?”

“The one that grinds.”

David was silent.

Then said:

“A mill?”

The word sounded too early.

Or just in time.

Sofia felt a chill down her back.

“No,” she said.

“No?”

“Not yet.”

David understood that it was better not to ask.

And still asked:

“What was there?”

Sofia looked at her hands.

“A woman was grinding grain. For a very long time. Someone said: if only there were more hands. And then a boy said, ‘And what if it did it by itself?’”

David repeated quietly:

“By itself.”

The word did not become childish.

It became adult.

Too adult.

From the kitchen came Thomas’s voice again:

“Has it risen?”

Nick answered:

“Thomas, if you ask one more time, it will leave on principle.”

David suddenly laughed.

Not hard.

But alive.

“He needs ‘wait’ written on his palm.”

“He’ll rub it off while checking the time.”

They sat a little longer. Then returned to the house.

The second proofing was coming to an end.

The dough had risen again. More strongly than the first time. In the tins it had become rounded, almost full. The towel was slightly lifted over two soft hills. It was strangely touching and unsettling: under the cloth something was growing not because it had been persuaded, but because its own invisible work was going on inside it.

Lina removed the towel.

“Now into the oven.”

Thomas came closer.

“Can it rise more?”

“It can.”

“Then why now?”

Lina looked at him with such tiredness that Nick raised his hand.

“I will answer for her: because if we wait for the perfect rise, we will die hungry, but with impeccable methodology.”

Marek opened the oven.

Heat struck the face.

Lina cut the tops with a knife. Light slanting lines opened immediately on the dough, like skin that had been given room for future expansion. The tins went into the oven. The door closed.

Now waiting became worse.

Because the smell came before the bread.

At first barely noticeable: warm flour, damp crust, yeast that no longer smelled like yeast. Then stronger. The house filled with something that could not yet be eaten, but could already not be not wanted. People began walking more slowly, speaking more briefly. Nick declared that the aroma of bread was the oldest form of political pressure. Nora said that smell always comes before permission. Marek answered that this was why one cannot open the oven every five minutes.

Thomas stood by the oven.

“How much longer?”

“Until it’s ready,” Marek said.

“You are mocking me.”

“No.”

“All the answers today are like that.”

“Because bread is like that.”

Ivar, sitting by the window, said quietly:

“Because you are asking the wrong one.”

Thomas turned.

“And whom should I ask?”

Ivar nodded toward the oven.

“The crust.”

Nick covered his face with his hands.

“I surrender completely. The crust has been granted voting rights.”

But after a while it was indeed the crust that spoke.

Not with words.

With colour.

With smell.

With a thin dry sound when Lina tapped the bottom of the first loaf taken from the tin. The bread was ready. Not because the clock decided. Not because Thomas finally got a number. Because the crust held the sound Lina’s hands knew.

She set the loaves on the board.

“Don’t cut right away,” she said.

Thomas opened his mouth.

Everyone looked at him.

He closed his mouth.

Nick whispered solemnly:

“A miracle of maturation.”

The bread stood on the table.

Hot.

Alive already in another way.

Not like dough. Not like yeast. Not like flour. It had become what everyone had been waiting for, but it still could not be taken. It was almost funny: even when the process was complete, it still required a little more waiting. One could not simply receive. The crust had to be allowed to settle, the crumb to gather itself, the steam to leave not too quickly.

Thomas looked at the bread as at an interlocutor who had spent too long evading a direct answer.

“Now why wait?” he asked.

Lina took the towel and covered the loaves.

“Because otherwise it will fall apart.”

“It’s already ready.”

“Even what is ready sometimes needs to be left alone.”

Nick said quietly:

“That’s it. I’m going to live in the forest. At least the trees are not that right.”

After a while they cut the bread.

Steam rose softly, densely, almost white. The crumb was not perfect: one loaf had risen better, the other was slightly denser at the edge. The crust crackled. The knife went slowly. Slices lay on the board, and no one took one at once.

That was new.

Yesterday they had divided bread as quantity.

Today they looked at it as an event that might not have happened.

Then Lina said:

“Eat.”

And everyone took.

Nick, of course, was the first to say:

“I officially acknowledge: waiting is sometimes edible.”

Marek spread butter.

“Sometimes.”

Thomas took a slice, smelled it, bit, and closed his eyes.

Not from delight.

From relief.

“It turned out,” he said.

“Yes,” Lina said.

“It turned out well.”

“Yes.”

He opened his eyes.

“And if next time we kept it a little warmer on the first rise?”

Silence.

Very short.

But sufficient.

Nick slowly lowered his slice.

Marek looked out the window.

David closed his eyes, as if tired in advance.

Lina did not get angry. That was worse. She simply looked at Thomas with the fatigue of a person who knows: if it turned out, then now they will ask how to make it turn out better, faster, more, always.

Thomas heard himself.

“I don’t mean...”

“You do,” Nick said.

Not viciously.

Thomas set down the bread.

“I just thought.”

“Exactly.”

Ivar took his slice, looked at the crumb for a long time, then broke off a small piece.

Said nothing.

And that allowed Thomas’s phrase to remain where it needed to remain: not an error, not shame, not a reason for a lecture. A beginning.

Sofia ate the bread slowly.

It was warm, a little sour, with a firm crust and soft centre. Nothing magical. Just bread. And therefore more dangerous. Because if it had not turned out, a person could have taken offence at the yeast, the flour, the warmth, the hands. But it had turned out.

And the hand already wanted to know what exactly to repeat.

Not all of Sofia.

Not necessarily thought.

The hand.

The very one that held the slice.

In the evening, when the bread had cooled and one loaf had been put away for tomorrow, Sofia went out to the woodshed.

Not for the ants. They were almost no longer visible. Not for meaning. She was almost tired of meanings. It was simply that the day had been too warm inside the house, too floury, too full of crust and waiting.

The sky was darkening. It was damp in the forest. Light stretched from the kitchen window. At the table someone laughed — Nick, it seemed, was again arguing with Marek about whether bread could be considered a political participant. Thomas sat by the window with a cup, and Sofia knew he was thinking not even about bread. About the method. David perhaps too. Only more quietly.

Ivar came out onto the porch.

“Cold,” he said.

“Yes.”

He stood beside her, without asking.

For some time they looked at the yard.

“Did it begin today?” Sofia asked.

Ivar did not clarify what exactly.

“Earlier.”

“But today it became visible?”

He was silent for a while.

“Today it smelled of bread.”

She gave a short laugh.

“Is that an answer?”

“No.”

“Good.”

He looked at her.

In the darkness his face was almost ordinary: tired, large, alive, without the fire of a prophet and without the right to the final phrase.

From the kitchen came Thomas’s voice:

“I still think the heat could have been kept more even.”

Nick answered something indistinct, but clearly sarcastic.

Marek laughed shortly.

Sofia closed her eyes.

Before her, the stone flashed again. The woman. The shoulder. The flour. The boy.

“And what if it did it by itself?”

She opened her eyes.

The house shone in the damp northern evening. Not like a refuge. Like a place where people ate bread and were already beginning to search for a way not to depend on the fact that bread requires time.

The dough had risen.

But the person already wanted to know how to make it rise again.
