# Chapter One. A Small Event

At Helsinki Airport, the earth was not grinding.

It was simply enduring.

Suitcases rolled across it, heels struck it, doors beeped, and the air smelled of coffee, wet wool, duty-free perfume, and the tiredness of people who had not yet managed to understand whether they had arrived somewhere or merely exchanged one queue for another.

Beyond the glass wall hung the end of August.

It did not resemble the end of summer in places where summer says goodbye to a person with heat, dust, and weary generosity. Here it was leaving almost unnoticed: with a low grey sky, wet asphalt, and light that was still holding on to the day but already understood that soon it would have to yield earlier.

Sofia stood by the glass wall and watched buses crawl slowly outside. The grey sky hung low, as if someone had forgotten to lift it back up after unloading the baggage.

“Are we sure this is the place?” Nick asked.

It was already the third time he had asked the same thing, but each time in a slightly different voice. First with irony. Then with irritation. Now with the caution of a man beginning to suspect that the trip he had planned to laugh at had started laughing first.

Sofia looked at her phone.

There was signal. There was the map. There was Eva’s message. Everything was so normal that it was becoming suspicious.

> “Exit 4. Grey minibus. Marek will have a sign. Don’t be frightened; he always looks as if he knows where you went wrong in life.”

Sofia showed the message to Nick.

“See? Everything according to plan.”

“What worries me is that the plan includes a man named Marek with a sign.”

“You’re worried by anything that isn’t drawn up as a contract.”

“Because contracts at least pretend the world is manageable.”

“Do you work as a lawyer only in your soul?”

“In my soul I don’t work at all.”

Sofia smiled, but the smile came out brief.

She herself did not fully understand why she had agreed.

Eva had talked about this trip for almost a month. Not insistently. Not with shining eyes. Not like people who have found yet another breathing system, paid for a course, and now have to save everyone they know in order not to feel cheated. Eva spoke calmly. Even too calmly.

“Once a year we go north. There is a house there. Not Ivar’s house, just a house. Ivar comes too. Sometimes he talks. Sometimes he is silent. Sometimes it would be better if he talked.”

“And what happens there?”

“Nothing.”

“Nothing?”

“Yes. That is exactly why people return.”

Then Eva said the phrase because of which Sofia bought the ticket.

“When you are called to Ivar, it is a small event. Not a celebration. Not an initiation. Not a retreat. Just a small event. It may change nothing. But I wouldn’t bet on that.”

Sofia had laughed then.

Eva had not.

That was what had turned out to be most unpleasant.

At Exit 4 there really was a man with a sign.

On the sign was written:

**IVAR**

The man held it as if he had no relation to the inscription and was merely supporting someone else’s mistake for a while. He was tall, thin, in a dark jacket, with a face that was hard to call unfriendly. It was more economical. As if its owner had long ago understood that facial expressions should be spent carefully.

“Marek?” Sofia asked.

“Unfortunately, yes,” the man said. “You are Sofia and Nick?”

“Unfortunately, yes,” Nick replied.

Marek looked at him a little more closely.

“Good. You can tell at once when a person has come voluntarily.”

“It shows?”

“People don’t joke like that under force. Under force they keep quiet.”

Others were already approaching them.

A woman of about forty-five with short silver hair hugged Marek as if they had seen each other yesterday, though her voice carried the distance of a whole year.

“You look like a warning again,” she said.

“And you have come again, Lina.”

“So the warning was not convincing enough.”

Behind Lina came a stocky man with a neatly trimmed beard and a yellow backpack. He was dragging two suitcases at once: one his own, the other, judging by his irritation, someone else’s.

“David lost his luggage,” Lina said.

“I did not lose it,” the man said. “The system lost it.”

“The system is always happy to be guilty,” Marek said.

“At last there will be philosophy on this trip,” Nick muttered.

“Don’t hope,” Lina said. “Philosophy is rare there. Usually one has to look at oneself without translation.”

Nick wanted to answer, but did not have time.

Eva came up.

Sofia immediately felt relief. Eva was a familiar person in this strange arrangement of suitcases, signs, and things left unsaid. She hugged Sofia, then Nick, although Nick looked like a man who would have preferred to limit matters to a diplomatic nod.

“You made it,” Eva said.

“So far only to the sign,” Nick said. “Meaning is still far away.”

“Meaning comes without a guarantee too.”

“Excellent. I love services without a guarantee.”

“This is not a service.”

“That’s already worse.”

Marek counted the people with his eyes.

Sofia noticed it. Not rudely, not openly. But he was counting.

Two more came toward the exit: a young man in expensive glasses who kept adjusting his scarf, and a woman with a dark braid arranged around her head like a wreath, but without any of a wreath’s softness. She walked evenly, unhurriedly, and looked not at people but somewhere between them.

“Thomas,” the young man introduced himself, stretching his hand toward everyone and no one at once. “I am here for the first time.”

“Many try to hide that,” Marek said.

“I don’t see the point. It will be obvious anyway.”

The woman with the braid said nothing.

“This is Nora,” Thomas said. “She speaks only when it is already too late.”

“A convenient quality,” Nick said.

“Not always,” Nora replied.

Her voice turned out to be low and calm.

After that everyone somehow fell silent.

A few minutes later the rest had gathered. There turned out to be twelve of them in all.

Sofia did not notice it at once. Then she noticed — and immediately decided not to attach any importance to it. People attach meaning to numbers far too often because numbers patiently pretend not to care.

Twelve people with suitcases, backpacks, tiredness, expectation, other people’s stories about Ivar, and varying degrees of readiness to admit that they were waiting for something.

Ivar was not among them.

For some reason Sofia liked that.

The minibus stood by the far exit: grey, wet, with one side slightly dented. The driver was smoking beside the open door and looking at the arrivals with the expression of a man who had long been carrying people to a place from which they returned different, but whose tips had not increased because of it.

“The drive is about three hours,” Marek said. “Maybe less. Maybe more.”

“Depending on what?” Thomas asked.

“On the road.”

“That much is clear.”

“Not to everyone.”

The suitcases were loaded into the luggage compartment. Someone immediately took a window seat. Someone gave way too politely, and that made everyone uncomfortable. Nick tried to settle himself so that his knees would not press into a neighbouring backpack, but the north had already begun its educational work.

“Is it always this cramped?” he asked.

“No,” Lina said. “Last year it was worse.”

“What was worse?”

“We were.”

The minibus moved off.

The airport was left behind quickly, almost without resistance. The city held on for a while longer — road signs, petrol stations, glass, concrete, lights, neat junctions. Then everything began to thin out.

Houses did not disappear at once. At first they pretended they would still return. Then they stopped pretending.

The forest entered the windows gradually.

First as separate trees. Then in rows. Then with its whole body.

Sofia looked at the pines. They stood darker than the sky, thin and straight, as if someone had written them in a single stroke and had not bothered to correct it.

From time to time dark bilberry bushes flared along the roadside. Sometimes the road passed an open place where the grass had been flattened by rain, and among the trees stood people with plastic buckets. They did not wave to the bus; they did not look after it. They bent toward the earth with such concentration, as if they were gathering not berries, but small proofs that the world still feeds those who know how to bend down.

Sofia caught herself thinking this and immediately turned away.

She did not like that the thought had not sounded entirely in her own voice.

“So who is he, after all?” Thomas asked.

The question hung in the cabin as if it had been expected.

“Ivar?” Eva clarified.

“No, the driver. Of course Ivar.”

The driver said something in Finnish and grinned without turning around.

“You see,” Nick said, “even the driver understood that the question was inevitable.”

Marek sat in the front row, sideways, so that he could see almost everyone.

“No one knows who he is ‘after all,’” he said. “That is what saves him.”

“From what?” Sofia asked.

“From people who like to know things ‘after all.’”

“Very convenient,” Nick said. “No responsibility for the description.”

“A description is not always responsibility. Sometimes it is an attempt to nail the living thing to a board.”

“And sometimes just a way of understanding where you are being taken.”

“You are being taken to a house by a lake,” Marek said. “There will be food. Firewood. Rooms. Bad internet. Good cold. A few conversations. Possibly silence. Ivar will come, or has already come. That is where the safe part of the description ends.”

“That sounds like the description of a film I would turn off after ten minutes,” Nick said.

“You won’t turn it off,” Eva said.

“Why?”

“Because you are already on the way.”

Lina laughed.

“Don’t worry. Ivar does nothing frightening.”

“Why does that sentence not reassure me?”

“Because the frightening part usually begins where no one is doing anything to you.”

Sofia looked at Eva.

“You didn’t tell me that.”

“If I had, you would have come anyway.”

“I’m not sure.”

“Neither am I. That is why I did not tell you.”

The cabin grew warmer. The windows began to cloud at the edges. Someone took off a hat. Someone opened a bottle of water. Thomas took out his phone, looked at the screen, and put it away again with the look of someone lightly offended.

“Lost signal?” Nick asked.

“Not yet. But it is already having doubts.”

“Is there Wi-Fi there?” Sofia asked.

Marek nodded.

“There is.”

“Good?”

“It performs a spiritual function.”

“So bad.”

“It reminds a person that not every connection is connection.”

“There,” Nick said. “It has begun.”

“It hasn’t begun yet,” Lina said. “This is Marek warming up.”

The minibus turned off the wide road onto a narrower one.

The forest came closer. Not threateningly. Simply closer. Like a person who is not going to attack, but wants you to stop pretending he is not there.

Sofia noticed that the conversations had grown quieter.

Not because no one had anything to say. On the contrary. There appeared in the cabin that special kind of silence in which words still exist, but people begin to choose them more carefully.

“And why ‘a small event’?” she asked Eva.

Eva looked out the window for a long time.

“Because a person usually ruins a large event with expectation.”

“And a small one?”

“A small one can be missed. That is why it sometimes goes deeper.”

“Do you always talk like this after trips to Ivar?”

“No. Before the first trip I talked worse.”

“Did he change you?”

Eva turned to her.

“No.”

Sofia waited for more.

Eva did add:

“I simply stopped believing some of my convenient explanations.”

“And is that good?”

“It is honest. Good is too greedy a word.”

Nick sighed quietly.

“I am beginning to feel that I am flying to the wrong place. I mean, already driving. But inside I am still flying back.”

“That is normal,” Marek said. “Many people begin by going back. The body simply doesn’t keep up with them and arrives first.”

“I’m beginning to understand why you are not invited to speak at corporate events.”

“I have been invited.”

“And?”

“I am not invited anymore.”

The minibus drove for almost three hours.

During that time the forest changed several times. It was dark, then lightened, then thickened again. Rare houses appeared. Red walls, white frames, sheds, boats, stacked firewood. Once they passed a lake so still that it seemed not to reflect the sky, but to be keeping it for better times.

Sometimes there were tables outside the houses, covered with oilcloth. On one of them Sofia managed to notice a basket of mushrooms, overturned buckets, and an old woman in a blue jacket. The woman was cleaning something dark and small with a knife, not looking at the road at all. The movement of her hand was calm, ancient, almost insultingly sure. As if real life had long been continuing somewhere off to the side of the highway, and the people in the minibus simply had not yet reached the place where that becomes obvious.

Sofia felt tired.

Not heavily tired. Strangely tired. As if the road were gradually removing her outer layers: airport, city, messages, conversations, the habit of keeping her face arranged, the habit of understanding why she was doing what she was doing.

Something simpler remained.

And more unpleasant.

“We are almost there,” Marek said.

The word “almost” sounded different in the forest. In the city it means a few minutes. Here it meant that the place already knew about you, but had not yet shown the entrance.

The road became very narrow.

The minibus turned between two moss-grown boulders. Beyond them an opening appeared.

The house stood neither on a hill nor in a hollow. It stood as if it had chosen the place before people invented choosing places.

A large wooden house, dark red, with a long roof, a veranda, and an extension on one side. Behind it water could be seen. Not immediately a lake — at first a pale strip between the trunks. To the left stood a shed. Beside the shed, firewood was stacked neatly. Farther on, closer to the water, the black circle of a fire pit showed in the grass.

By the porch stood rubber boots of different sizes. A wet jacket hung over the railing. Under the awning were two baskets: in one lay mushrooms, in the other something dark blue, almost black. Bilberries — or what Sofia decided to call bilberries so as not to feel like a person who had come north and did not know what grew under her feet.

The house did not look old.

It looked prolonged.

The minibus stopped.

Nick was the first to say what many were thinking:

“Well, at least it doesn’t look like a sect.”

A man came out from behind the house carrying an armful of firewood.

Sofia noticed the firewood first.

Not his face. Not his height. Not that strange pause that passed through the group when the returning guests recognized him before he had time to come closer. First — the firewood. Dry, uneven logs, pressed to his chest so simply, as if the man were carrying not a symbol, not preparation for the evening fire, not a beautiful detail of northern life, but only what would be needed by evening if it became cold.

Only then did she see the man himself.

He was broad and tall — not merely taller than most, but somehow more densely placed in space. Not an old man. Not yet. But there was already in him that weight of age when a person is not so much growing old as ceasing to fuss before the world.

He wore a simple dark sweater, a work jacket, and boots. When he shifted the armful of firewood, Sofia noticed the shaved temples and closely cut sides; the rest of his dark hair, already threaded with silver, was drawn into a long French braid down his back. His face was large, attentive, alive. Not smooth. Not noble in the way faces are noble on book covers where no one ever carries firewood or washes his own cup.

No glow around him.

No solemn entrance.

No look of “I have waited for you all my life.”

He carried the firewood as if the firewood mattered more than the impression he might make.

And that was precisely why he made an impression after all.

Marek got out first.

“Ivar.”

The man with the firewood looked at him, then at the minibus, then at the sky.

“You brought rain.”

At first his voice seemed hoarse to Sofia, as if he had been silent for a long time or had been speaking with something that did not like to answer at once.

“We tried,” Marek said.

“You tried badly. The rain is small.”

Lina came up and hugged him. Ivar hugged her with one arm, because he was still holding the firewood with the other.

“You have grown heavier,” she said.

“The forest gave back the excess.”

“The forest was always greedy.”

“No. Just exact.”

Eva greeted him silently. David shook his hand. Nora nodded. Thomas suddenly became serious and did not know what to do with his face.

When Sofia’s turn came, Ivar looked at her calmly. Not intently. Not penetratingly. That made it worse. A penetrating gaze can be disliked. A calm one is harder to accuse.

“Sofia,” she said.

“I know.”

“Did someone tell you?”

“No.”

She did not immediately understand whether he was joking or not.

Ivar looked at Nick.

“And you are Nick.”

“Did someone tell you that too?”

“No. But you are standing as if you already dislike the answer.”

Nick blinked.

“That is a common posture.”

“Yes. That is why I may have been mistaken.”

Ivar turned to the others.

“Bring the things in. Soup cools faster than people. But not by much.”

And he went into the house.

Sofia stood for several seconds without moving.

“Well?” Nick asked quietly.

“What?”

“Has it changed you forever?”

“So far it only irritates me.”

“It has already begun.”

Inside, the house turned out to be warm.

Not cosy in the hotel sense. Not the kind of cosiness bought with cushions, candles, and matching mugs. Here the warmth was working warmth. The stove heated because someone had brought firewood in advance. The soup smelled because someone had cut vegetables. The floor creaked because it had been holding people for a long time and did not consider it necessary to hide its age.

In the hallway everyone began taking off shoes, looking for their things, asking where to put suitcases. The returning guests moved confidently, but without the gesture of ownership. The newcomers tried not to get in the way, and because of that got in the way slightly more.

“Rooms are upstairs and in the side wing,” Marek said. “Don’t choose too long. The house will settle you by itself anyway.”

“Is that a metaphor?” Thomas asked.

“Not yet.”

“And later?”

“We’ll see where you wake up.”

There really was soup in the kitchen. A large pot stood on the stove. Beside it were bread, butter, fish, salt, bowls. The food was simple and because of that almost defiant. After airports and packaging, simple food always looks like a reproach to civilization.

On the windowsill stood a bowl of berries. Beside it lay a knife with a dark handle, a board on which someone had been cleaning mushrooms, and several thin fish bones neatly placed on the edge of a plate. Nothing decorative. No staging of northern life for guests. The house simply fed those who had reached it, and did so without explanations.

Nick took a piece of bread.

“Is this safe?” he asked.

“As long as you are not trying to own it forever,” Ivar said, passing by with logs.

Nick looked at the bread.

“I like that even the food here comes with conditions.”

“Everything has conditions,” Ivar said. “Bread is simply more honest than people.”

Sofia noticed that Ivar did not sit at the head of the table.

There was no head of the table at all.

People ate wherever they happened to be: standing, by the window, on a bench, leaning against the wall. Conversations went unevenly. Laughter flared and went out. Someone told a story about a delayed flight. Someone asked where to charge a phone. Someone had already managed to forget the Wi-Fi password, although he had not yet learned it.

On the wall in the living room hung an old map.

Not a new tourist map, not a decorative one. Grey, worn, with lines of rivers and lakes. The names on it were in different languages. Some Sofia could read. Some seemed not like words, but like traces of how the earth had once tried to explain itself to a person and changed its mind.

By the fireplace stood twelve different armchairs and chairs.

Sofia noticed it and looked away.

The number again.

“Don’t count,” Eva said beside her.

“I wasn’t counting.”

“Of course.”

“Was it done on purpose?”

“What?”

“Twelve chairs.”

“There is always one missing here.”

“For whom?”

Eva looked toward the kitchen, where Ivar was saying something to the driver.

“For the one who thinks the place was prepared specifically for him.”

Sofia wanted to ask more, but at that moment Marek clapped his hands.

“In twenty minutes, to the fire. Whoever wants a blanket, take one now. Whoever thinks they won’t be cold, take one too. The north loves optimists. They crunch louder.”

“So it is a retreat after all,” Nick said. “Just an honest one.”

The fire pit was closer to the water.

By evening the sky had become lower. The rain never started, but the air was damp, cold, and attentive. Moss sprang softly underfoot. The summer green still held in the grass, but dark autumn water was already showing through it. Somewhere off to the side a bird cried once, briefly, as if it had tested the sound and decided not to spend any more.

The sun did not set solemnly. It simply left earlier than Sofia expected. There was no winter finality in this yet, but neither was there the summer’s impunity of light.

The fire circle was laid with stones.

Inside already lay dry twigs and birch bark. Ivar crouched, struck a match. The fire did not catch at once. First it thought. Then it made a decision.

People settled around it.

Twelve guests.

Ivar remained on his haunches by the fire until the flame grew sure of itself. Then he sat on a low wooden chopping block, slightly to the side, not in the centre. In such a way that no one could decide whether he was leading the circle or had simply ended up closest to the wood.

For a while everyone was silent.

This silence was different, not the minibus silence. In the minibus the silence had been cramped. Here it was wide and cold. It held the lake, the forest, the house behind them, the faint smell of smoke, damp moss, the bowl of berries on the kitchen windowsill, the uncollected fish bones, and all the questions people had brought hoping not to take out.

Thomas was the first who could not bear it.

“We were told you tell stories.”

“Who told you?” Ivar asked.

Now his voice no longer seemed merely hoarse. There was density in it, low and soft, but not soothing. Rather, the kind of density that made a person suddenly hear how much unnecessary noise he had brought with him.

Thomas looked at Marek.

“Different people.”

“Different people rarely know what they are saying.”

“But do you tell them?”

Ivar tossed a thin branch into the fire.

“Sometimes a story itself looks for the one through whom it can pass most easily.”

“And today?”

Ivar looked at the flame.

“Today it has already begun.”

Nick quietly leaned toward Sofia.

“I think I should have stayed at the airport.”

“Too late,” she said.

“That word fits here too often.”

Ivar raised his head.

It seemed to Sofia that he was looking not at any one person, but at the space between them all. At the place where the circle had not yet become a circle, but had already stopped being a collection of people.

“You have all heard this word,” he said. “Some in childhood. Some in a book. Some for the first time today, but will pretend they knew it before.”

Marek smiled.

“A good word. Convenient. Round. It is easy to turn it into a thing. People love things. A thing can be searched for, stolen, sold, lost, returned, locked away, called one’s own. With a thing it is clear what to do. Especially if one does not understand what it means.”

The fire cracked.

Not loudly.

But for some reason Sofia flinched.

“Sampo,” Ivar said.

The word fell into the circle and stayed lying between them like a small warm stone.

“They usually say it was lost. Or stolen. Or broken. Or that it was a mill that ground wealth, salt, grain, gold — depending on what the teller lacked.”

He looked at Nick.

“A person always forces a miracle to produce what hurts in him.”

Nick did not answer.

Sofia noticed this.

She was beginning to understand that something unpleasant was happening to Nick: not faith, no. He would not reach faith even with a map and a written invitation. But his jokes had begun to appear half a second later. As if between thought and phrase someone third had appeared and was asking: “Are you sure this is the way you want to hide?”

“But Sampo was never lost,” Ivar said. “People simply stopped recognizing it.”

The fire rose higher.

For one moment the flame became neither yellow nor red, but dark green, like the northern lights seen too close.

Sofia blinked.

When she opened her eyes again, the fire was ordinary.

Almost ordinary.

Ivar spoke more quietly:

“The first mistake of people was not that they wanted bread. Hunger is not a sin. Hunger is the language of the body. The mistake began later. When they decided that everything that feeds must belong to whoever first managed to pull it out of the earth.”

Lina lowered her eyes.

David stopped chewing a dry blade of grass.

Nora sat motionless, as if she were listening not to Ivar, but to something behind him.

“Isn’t that how it is?” Thomas asked.

Ivar looked at him.

“No.”

“Why?”

“Because you cannot own what you are standing inside.”

The wind passed around the circle.

Not strong. But the fire leaned toward everyone at once.

Sofia smelled bread.

There was no bread at the fire pit.

Only smoke. Moss. Lake. Cold. People.

And still the smell was there.

Warm crust. A broken loaf. Steam above the crumb. Something morning-like, although it was already growing dark. Something homelike, although Sofia did not know whose home it was. Something so simple that it became almost painful.

She looked at Nick.

He had smelled it too.

His face changed only slightly. Not fear. Not surprise. Rather the irritation of a person who has just been deprived of the right to consider what is happening mere atmosphere.

Ivar closed his eyes.

“That night, the earth was grinding,” he said.

The fire sank lower.

And they all heard how beneath them, deep under the moss, under the roots, under the water, something slowly turned.
