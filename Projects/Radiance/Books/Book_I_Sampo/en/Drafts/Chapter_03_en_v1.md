# Chapter Three. The Slice

In the morning the bread lay on the table as if nothing had happened in the night.

That was almost insolent.

Sofia stopped in the kitchen doorway and for several seconds looked at the loaf without going in. The house had already woken, though no one had announced a rising. Steps creaked on the stairs, water moved angrily through the pipes, somewhere a door slammed, someone coughed, someone set a cup on the table too carefully. The night had gone, but had not cleaned up after itself.

The bread lay in the middle of the long wooden table.

Dark crust, uneven side, the mark of yesterday’s knife, a few crumbs near the board. Beside it were butter in a small ceramic bowl, salt, a knife with a dark handle, berries in a wide bowl, and mushrooms already fried, smelling of the forest so directly that it was as if the forest had not agreed to remain outside.

Everything was ordinary.

And that was precisely why Sofia could not enter at once.

Under the nail of her thumb there was still a thin dark strip of earth.

She had washed her hands twice. First in the evening before lying down. Then in the morning, when she woke before Eva and stood for a long time at the little sink under the slanted ceiling. The water was cold, the soap smelled of something coniferous, the towel scratched her skin. The earth had almost disappeared. Almost. But the thin line remained where the nail passed into the finger.

Sofia could have cleaned it out with a brush.

She had not.

Not out of respect. Not from mystical trembling. She simply could not decide at once whether she wanted to erase the evidence or preserve the possibility of doubting that it had been evidence.

“Good morning,” Eva said behind her.

Sofia started.

“Do you always walk like that?”

“Like what?”

“Without sound.”

“No. Only when a person is standing in a kitchen doorway and looking at bread as if it were a suspect.”

Sofia wanted to answer, but said nothing.

Eva passed her, took two cups, and set them on the table. Her hair was gathered carelessly, and an old wool sweater lay across her shoulders. She looked like a person who had slept badly, but had long ago made a truce with bad sleep.

“Tea?” she asked.

“Is there coffee?”

“There is. But Marek says the coffee here does not wake you up; it reminds the body of previous mistakes.”

“Then tea.”

“Wise.”

Lina was already sitting on the bench by the window. She was peeling a small apple with a knife. The peel came off in a long thin ribbon, almost without breaking. Lina had the hands of a person who does not make unnecessary movements.

Nora stood by the stove and looked into the fire. Not warming herself. Simply looking.

David came in after Sofia, already dressed, with wet hair and the expression of a person who has decided to begin the day with order and discovered that order had woken before him and gone off about its own business.

“The shower water changes from hot to ice-cold without warning,” he said.

Marek, sitting at the edge of the table with a notebook, did not raise his head.

“The warning hangs on the water itself.”

“Where?”

“In its character.”

Lina smiled without taking her eyes from the apple.

Sofia sat closer to the window. From there she could see the lake between the trees. The morning was grey, damp, not fully awake. The sky held low, as if during the night the house had risen a little higher than allowed and was now pressing its roof into the clouds. The grass by the porch was dark with dew. Behind the shed stood birches, and their white trunks seemed not bright, but washed by cold.

Nick was not there.

For some reason this was troubling.

Then he came in.

He came in the way people do when they are prepared in advance to prove that the day has not broken them. Hair still wet, shirt fastened crookedly at the collar, face cheerful with that dangerous cheerfulness that rests on the inner command not to think.

“Good morning to all participants...” he began, and stopped.

Marek raised his eyes.

“Already better.”

“I haven’t said anything yet.”

“Exactly.”

Nick sat down beside Sofia, but not too close. Yesterday by the fire he had sat shoulder to shoulder with her, as if ordinary closeness could protect against the unusual. In the morning he kept a small distance.

“Did you sleep?” he asked quietly.

“Almost.”

“Me too. The ceiling turned out to be a conversational partner of medium difficulty.”

“And what did it say?”

“Mostly creaked. But with a position.”

Sofia looked at his hands.

There was nothing on his fingers. No earth, no crumbs, no trace of the night. He noticed her glance and placed his palms on the table too calmly.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

“That word is now under observation here.”

She turned away toward the window.

Thomas was the last of those who came straight to the table. His glasses sat a little lower than usual, and there were shadows under his eyes. He looked like a person who had spent the night not in fear, but in calculations.

“Has anyone seen Ivar?” Thomas asked.

“Seen him,” Marek said.

“Where?”

“Here.”

“Now?”

“No.”

Thomas stood for several seconds.

“I was asking in order to know.”

“I was answering in order not to lie.”

Nick took a cup, looked into it, discovered it was empty, and put it back.

“Morning is showing signs of life.”

Ivar appeared through the side door.

He did not enter like the master of the house. Did not enter like the one everyone had been waiting for. He simply appeared with an armful of thin logs, as if the house had asked and he had heard it before the others. He wore a dark sweater, his hair braided in a long plait thrown behind his shoulder. The silver in it, in the morning light, was not age but the cold shine of water. He set the wood by the stove, chose two logs, opened the door, and laid them inside so that the fire would not flare abruptly, but accept the new fuel calmly.

Nick looked at Sofia.

Very quickly.

Ivar said nothing.

He closed the stove, took an empty kettle, poured water from a jug, and set it on the range.

Just like that.

Without a symbol.

And because of that the kitchen itself began to search for what it was.

Lina placed the apple peel on the edge of a plate.

“Shall I cut the bread?” she asked.

The question was ordinary. Necessary. Morning-like.

Nick reached for the knife too quickly.

“I can.”

Lina looked at his hand.

“You can.”

She did not hand him the knife.

She simply moved her own palm away.

Nick took the knife.

And began cutting the bread.

Sofia understood at once that he was not cutting bread.

He was establishing a boundary.

The first slice came out thin. Too thin. Almost transparent at the edge. The second — even thinner. The knife moved slowly, evenly, with almost legal precision. Nick did not hurry, did not joke, did not look around. The crust yielded unwillingly, the crumb compressed under the blade, and still he drew the line with such exactness, as if the question of whether the world could once again become measurable depended on that line.

“If you continue,” Marek said, “in ten minutes we’ll be able to see through breakfast.”

Nick stopped.

“I respect the resource.”

“You are interrogating it.”

Lina quietly set the butter on the table.

Thomas took one of the thin slices.

“May I?”

Nick handed him the knife.

Thomas cut himself a piece.

Thick.

Not crudely. Not greedily in the usual sense. Simply too reliably. The slice came out firm, dense, with a large crust and a heavy side. It landed on the plate with a sound as if it had taken a position.

Thomas heard it himself.

He looked at the slice, then at the loaf, then at the knife in his hand. His movement froze at the place where a person can still pretend that everything is normal.

Nick did not say a word.

Ivar stood by the stove, pouring boiling water into the large teapot. He was not looking at Thomas. Or he was looking not in the way one looks with the eyes.

“You may take,” he said.

The kitchen became quieter.

The kettle continued to ring thinly from the hot water.

“The earth is not greedy,” Ivar said, closing the lid. “It simply remembers who took in order to eat, and who took in order to hide.”

Nick slowly laid the knife on the board.

Thomas looked at his slice.

David frowned.

“The difference is not always obvious.”

Ivar raised his eyes to him.

“That is why a person was given a hand.”

“A hand?”

“It often knows before the head does.”

He said nothing more.

Thomas’s hand was still lying beside the thick piece. He did not move it away. Nick took one of his own almost transparent slices and began spreading butter so carefully that the butter did not so much settle on the bread as try to negotiate with the surface. Eva set a cup before Sofia. Lina ate slowly, calmly, without any demonstration of measure. Nora took a very small piece, then looked at it and broke off a little more. Not for beauty. It simply happened that way.

Sofia did not reach for the bread.

She looked at her fingers.

The thin strip of earth under the nail seemed darker.

“Won’t you have any?” Eva asked.

“I will.”

Sofia took the knife.

The handle was warm from other people’s hands.

In the warmth there was a chain: Lina, Nick, Thomas, now her. Each had held the same object and through it, unwillingly, had told the table something of himself.

Sofia set the blade to the crust.

The bread resisted.

Not strongly. As good bread should resist. The crust held its form, the crumb beneath it was dense, moist, alive. The knife entered with a quiet crunch.

She cut a medium slice.

Not thin.

Not thick.

And at once became angry with herself for noticing.

Nick noticed too.

“A compromise slice,” he said, almost without voice.

“Be quiet.”

“Yes, that’s better.”

“What is better?”

“Being quiet.”

He really did become quiet.

And that was more useful than a joke.

At that moment Thomas placed his thick slice at the edge of the plate and did not eat it.

He took berries.

Then mushrooms.

Then tea.

The slice remained.

Not forgotten. Too visible to be forgotten. It lay beside the plate like something taken for later and suddenly deprived of the right to be innocent.

Sofia saw that Ivar had noticed too.

He said nothing.

Breakfast continued.

People gradually began to talk. Not about the night. About the shower water, about the weather, about whose socks were drying, about the fact that signal could be caught only on the top step of the porch and only if one held the phone like a sacrificial dish. Normal speech returned to the house cautiously.

“This morning I stepped out onto the porch,” David said, “and my phone showed three bars.”

“And?” Nick asked.

“I raised my hand — they disappeared.”

“The phone does not like gestures from above.”

“I sat down — they appeared.”

“Then the phone respects fatigue.”

“Very scientific.”

“I try to match the level of the connection.”

Lina laughed softly.

The laughter passed lightly. It did not destroy the tension, but allowed it not to become a pose.

Sofia ate her slice slowly.

The bread was good.

If it had been strange, bitter, too warm, smelling too much of earth, one could have been frightened and set down the plate. But it was simply good. Dark, dense, a little sour, with a thin sweetness of grain and a dry crunch of crust. The bread did what bread is supposed to do: it fed.

And that was precisely why everything became more complicated.

Because last night the man in the snow had also wanted to be fed.

He had not wanted to become a villain.

He had wanted the child not to cough on the bench, the sack not to be too light, the wife by the stove not to count in silence how much remained. He had wanted stability. Wanted warmth. Wanted not to depend on a neighbour’s door.

Sofia saw his hand on the warm snow.

And her own — on the bread.

There was a difference.

There had to be.

But it was no longer so convenient.

“Sofia?” Eva asked.

“What?”

“You are far away.”

“Not very.”

“Good.”

Eva did not try to bring her back farther than that.

Meanwhile Ivar took the empty basket by the door and went out.

Without an announcement. Without “after breakfast we will do such and such.” He simply left, the way a person leaves when, for him, morning consists not of meanings but of tasks.

Marek stood after him.

“We need firewood by the stove. Who isn’t afraid of the outside world?”

Nick raised his hand.

“I’m afraid, but culturally.”

“That is not a qualification.”

“Then I will spiritually support the others from a safe distance.”

“Sofia?” Eva asked.

Sofia understood the question was not about firewood.

“I’ll go.”

“I’m coming with you,” Nick said.

“No,” she said faster than she had meant to.

Nick fell silent.

She saw she had hurt him, and regretted it at once.

“I don’t mean it that way.”

“What way do you mean?”

“I don’t know. I need... by myself.”

He nodded.

“Take gloves.”

“Why?”

“So there won’t be a heroic epic later about a splinter.”

“Thank you.”

“I have a modest practical role today.”

Sofia took the gloves.

Marek gave her a smaller empty basket.

“The shed is behind the house. Dry ones on the right. Don’t take the lower ones; they’re still damp.”

“How do I tell?”

“With your hand.”

She looked at him.

He bore her gaze calmly.

“Of course,” Sofia said.

“Not everything can be understood with the eyes.”

She went out.

The morning air met her not even with cold, but with dampness. It was full of water, but not rain. Water held on the grass, on the porch railing, on the stones by the path, on the grey bark of the old birch. Breathing was easy and heavy at the same time, as if every inhale brought inside not only air, but also a part of the wet world.

Behind her the house made muffled noises. Voices, dishes, steps. Ahead stood the shed, low, dark, with a door that hung a little crooked and therefore looked more honest than most doors.

Sofia walked toward it along the narrow path.

The grass soaked her boots almost at once.

Yesterday evening she would have noted this as a domestic inconvenience. Today the wet grass touched her feet precisely, coldly, without malice. It simply reminded her: here everything touches. Nothing remains a clean picture.

By the shed it smelled of resin, damp bark, and old iron.

The word iron came by itself.

Sofia stopped at the door.

She placed her palm on the plank.

The plank was cold.

Ordinary.

She almost became angry at that.

What nonsense, to be angry at a plank for being a plank.

Inside the shed it was dark. Light passed through the gaps between the boards in thin grey strips. Firewood lay along the wall: on the right the dry wood, as Marek had said; on the left, darker, heavier, with damp bark. She took the first log from the right, then the second, the third. Dry wood was lighter than it looked. In the hand it sounded different: not with a voice, of course, but with weight.

Dry — ready for the fire now.

Damp — still holding water.

She placed several logs in the basket.

On the lower shelf lay thin kindling. She took a handful, then froze.

Too much?

The question was ridiculous.

Kindling. Not bread. Not a winter store. Not the fate of the world. Just a few thin dry splinters for the stove.

But her hand stopped.

She heard Ivar:

“The earth is not greedy. It simply remembers who took in order to eat, and who took in order to hide.”

Sofia put part of the kindling back.

Then took one again.

And almost laughed.

If in the morning a person begins negotiating with kindling, then the night truly had not been wasted.

“They will all get mixed up later anyway,” Nora said beyond the door.

Sofia started and struck her shoulder against the upright.

“Do you always appear like that?”

Nora stood outside in a grey sweater and rubber boots. In her hands was an empty metal bowl.

“No.”

“Is that your common answer here?”

Nora thought.

“Yes.”

Sofia exhaled.

“What will get mixed up?”

“The kindling. Dry, not dry. Necessary, extra. Someone will take the wrong one anyway.”

“That is comforting.”

“I wasn’t comforting you.”

Sofia adjusted the logs in the basket.

“Do you need something?”

“I’m here for berries. Lina asked.”

“In the shed?”

“Behind the shed. There are bushes.”

“Isn’t it late for berries?”

“Some of them don’t know.”

They went out together.

Behind the shed there really were bushes. Low, tangled, with dark leaves already touched by rust. Berries remained on them: small, dark red, almost black at the base. Nora crouched and began gathering them into the bowl. She did it slowly and a little unevenly. Sometimes she reached for a berry, then changed her mind. Sometimes she picked too quickly and winced when the berry burst in her fingers.

Sofia watched.

“You’re not taking all of them.”

“No.”

“Why?”

Nora lifted one shoulder.

“I don’t know. It’s calmer that way.”

“For whom?”

“For me. For the bush, probably. For the birds. I don’t know.”

She looked at her fingers, stained with dark juice.

“And besides, if you gather everything, the bowl becomes too full. Then I start thinking where to put the extra. I don’t like thinking about extra things in the morning.”

Sofia smiled.

“Have you been coming here long?” Sofia asked.

“Third time.”

“And is it always like this?”

“No.”

Sofia gave a small laugh.

“Of course.”

Nora picked another berry, looked at it, and put it in the bowl.

“The first time I thought I had come to listen to Ivar.”

“And it turned out?”

Nora chose the next berry for a long time.

“That after him you start hearing all kinds of nonsense.”

“For example?”

“How a bucket knocks wrong. How a person sets down a cup too hard. How you lie to yourself that you don’t want to take the last piece. Nothing beautiful.”

Sofia looked at the house.

Laughter came from the kitchen. Probably Nick. She could not make out the words, but recognized the attempt to make the morning lighter.

“Last night,” Sofia said, “did it not seem to you...?”

“What?”

“The snow. The people. The warm earth.”

Nora wiped her fingers on the grass.

“I wish it had.”

“That it had seemed?”

“That one could decide.”

“Decide what exactly?”

“Whether it seemed or not.”

She stood with the bowl.

“And it does not get decided.”

Sofia looked at her basket of firewood.

“I thought it would be simpler in the morning.”

“So did I.”

“And?”

Nora looked into the bowl.

“I took too many sour ones.”

Sofia unexpectedly laughed.

Nora smiled too. A little, almost guiltily.

Behind the shed the land sloped toward the forest. There, farther on, between the trees, was the path to the fire pit. By day it looked shorter. Night knows how to stretch distances, especially those along which a person returns changed.

“I’ll take the firewood back and go to the fire,” Sofia said.

“Alone?”

“Yes.”

Nora nodded.

“Then don’t trust the first appearance.”

“What does that mean?”

“I don’t know. It was said to me that way.”

She went toward the house.

The phrase remained not like an instruction, but like a wet branch in the grass: not very convenient, but already difficult to step around.

Sofia took the basket and walked back.

Thomas stood on the porch.

Without a jacket, with a cup in his hands, he looked as if he had stepped out for a minute and already regretted it, but did not want to admit defeat before the air.

“Were you going to the shed?” he asked.

“Already coming from it.”

“Ah.”

He looked at the basket.

“I wanted to help.”

Sofia did not know whether that was true.

“You can carry it.”

He was slightly thrown, but took the basket.

The weight was greater than he expected. It showed in his shoulder. He straightened at once, as if weight had no significance.

“Dry?” he asked.

“The dry ones were on the right.”

“Did you check?”

“With my hand.”

He looked at her.

There was not surprise in that look, but recognition. The word hand no longer fully belonged to them.

“I left the bread,” Thomas said suddenly.

“I saw.”

“I didn’t want to.”

“What exactly?”

He gave a dry smile.

“Good question.”

Sofia did not help him.

Thomas set the basket on the step.

“I took more than I meant to.”

“After last night, that is understandable.”

“Understandable doesn’t mean pleasant.”

“No.”

He looked toward the lake.

“I do that often. Take extra. Information, things, time, explanations. In case there isn’t enough later. It is not greed.”

He said the last part too quickly.

“I didn’t say it was greed.”

“But it resembles it.”

“Maybe because sometimes fear stands very close.”

Thomas looked at her carefully.

“Very close to what?”

Sofia did not answer at once.

“To the hand.”

He did not like that.

But he nodded.

“I’ll take it in.”

“Thank you.”

He went into the house.

Sofia remained on the porch.

She needed to go back to the kitchen. Instead she walked toward the fire pit.

The path was short, but the morning had managed to fill it with details that had not been there at night. On the right, an old stump stuck out of the grass, with small yellow mushrooms growing on it. On the left lay a stone covered with lichen that resembled an old map of a country where no one lives anymore. Thin spiderwebs stretched between the trees; drops of water hung on them, each holding the grey sky in miniature.

Sofia walked slowly.

Not because she was afraid.

Almost.

The fire pit looked smaller by day.

That was the first insult.

At night the circle of stones had seemed ancient, deep, almost a border. In the morning it was simply a circle of stones. In the centre lay grey ash, several black coals, and the burned branch Marek had not bothered to finish off. Around it moss. Damp. Dense. With drops of dew. No traces of warm snow. No signs that yesterday the earth beneath it had been a body.

The lake beyond the trees was silent.

The trees were silent.

The stones, of course, were too.

Sofia felt anger.

At what, it was unclear. At everything being ordinary. At the fact that the ordinary did not cancel the night. At the fact that now she herself would have to carry this mismatch inside her while the world around pretended to be innocent.

She crouched by the stone where she had touched the moss the night before.

She placed her palm down.

The moss was cold.

Wet.

So ordinary that it was almost frightening.

She pressed her fingers harder.

The cold went into her skin, under the nails, into the joints. No warmth. No breathing. No answer. Only water, moss, earth, morning.

And still her hand did not calm.

Because yesterday it had already known something else.

Cold was no longer proof.

It was surface.

Sofia took her hand away.

Small dark dots of earth and green wet dust of moss remained on her palm. She looked at them and suddenly remembered Thomas’s thick slice on the plate.

Out of place.

But more precise than she wanted.

A branch cracked behind her.

Sofia turned.

Nick stood on the path, holding his jacket in one hand and his cup in the other. He had not put on the jacket; the cup, apparently, he had forgotten to finish.

“I’m not following you,” he said at once. “I just chose the wrong direction badly.”

“Nick.”

“All right. Chose it well, but too late.”

“I told you I wanted to be by myself.”

“Yes. That is why I am standing over there, almost like a bush.”

“Bushes are silent.”

“I’m working on myself.”

She wanted to get angry.

It did not work.

He did not come too close. He stopped beside another stone.

“Warm?”

Sofia shook her head.

“Cold.”

“Excellent. So the night did not pass certification.”

She looked at him.

“Weak joke.”

“I know.”

He crouched and touched the earth too. Quickly. Almost angrily. Then removed his hand and wiped his fingers on his trousers.

“I hate it when the ordinary looks more suspicious than the strange.”

“You didn’t sleep either?”

“I slept. In a dream I argued with a man who had empty hands. He was winning because he did not present any documents.”

Sofia smiled.

“You can make even nightmares procedural.”

“That is not a talent. It is character trauma.”

He squatted there, looking at the ash.

“I understand him.”

Sofia did not ask whom.

There was no need.

Nick continued:

“Not in the sense that I approve. I just understand. If winter is ahead, if a child is coughing, if a neighbour closes the door... a person begins looking for a place where no one can put him in that posture anymore.”

He lifted his palm and looked at his fingers.

“Empty hands are humiliating.”

Sofia sat down beside him on a root.

“But he could still have left.”

“That’s the vile part.”

“You said that yesterday.”

“Because it’s true. I don’t like truth that repeats itself.”

For a while they were silent.

Sofia heard drops falling from the branches. Somewhere far away, from the direction of the house, a door clapped. A bird cried briefly and immediately fell silent.

“This morning I took a medium slice,” Sofia said.

Nick looked at her.

“You noticed too?”

“Yes.”

“I cut the bread like a man filing a complaint against chaos.”

“And?”

“Chaos did not accept it.”

Sofia laughed.

Quietly, but with real laughter.

Nick smiled too, but then at once grew more serious.

“Thomas didn’t eat his.”

“I know.”

“He didn’t leave it because he didn’t want it.”

“I know.”

“That’s why I don’t like this house. Even the bread here starts behaving like a person who has understood everything and keeps silent.”

Sofia looked at the circle of stones.

“Maybe it isn’t the house.”

“I was very much hoping you wouldn’t say that.”

They walked back together.

Not quickly. Not slowly. The way people walk when they have not finished speaking, but have already understood that now is not the time. By the porch Nick stopped.

“Let’s have no big conclusions before lunch.”

“I’ll try.”

“I mean it. Meaning should have a schedule.”

“First tea, then meaning?”

“And meaning in small portions.”

The kitchen had grown warmer.

Thomas had already returned to the table. The basket of firewood stood by the stove, the logs neatly stacked. Ivar had disappeared somewhere. Marek was washing the knife at the sink. Lina was pouring berries into a small bowl. Eva was talking with David by the window. Nora sat on the bench, holding a mug in her palms, not drinking from it.

On Thomas’s plate lay the slice.

Sofia saw it at once.

And became angry with herself for it.

It lay where it had before. Thick, dense, with an uneven edge. At first glance, ordinary. Perhaps a little darker from the air. Perhaps she was simply looking too intently.

Thomas sat beside it and pretended to be reading something on a phone without signal.

Nick went to the table, took his cup, found it empty, and reached for the teapot. His hand accidentally brushed the edge of Thomas’s plate. The plate shifted slightly. The slice rocked.

Nick automatically steadied it with his fingers so it would not fall.

And immediately pulled his hand back.

Not sharply.

Not theatrically.

But Sofia saw.

He froze for half a second, then took the teapot with his other hand and poured himself tea as if nothing had happened.

Thomas raised his eyes.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

Sofia heard that word like the strike of a small wooden hammer.

Nick did not look at her.

The slice was warm.

She knew it, though she had not touched it.

Thomas did not understand at once. Then he looked at the bread. Then reached out his hand — and stopped.

Between the desire to check and the fear of checking.

Marek turned off the water at the sink.

The noise disappeared.

Only the faint crackle of the stove remained in the kitchen.

Ivar stood in the doorway.

Sofia had not heard him enter.

In his hands he held one log. Small, dry, cleanly split. He looked at the table, at Thomas, at the slice, at Nick, who was drinking tea too diligently, and said nothing.

Then he went to the stove, opened the door, and put the log inside.

The fire did not accept it at once.

First it licked the edge.

Then stopped.

Then slowly moved along the dry wood.

Thomas was still looking at the bread.

“I didn’t want to hide it,” he said quietly.

Nick lowered his cup.

Nick did not joke.

Ivar closed the stove.

“Then eat it,” he said.

Thomas raised his eyes to him.

The phrase was simple. Almost rough in its simplicity. No comfort. No analysis. No explanation that “to hide” does not only mean to hide physically. No lecture on measure. Only an action that returned the bread to its first purpose.

Thomas took the slice.

This time he did not stop.

He brought it to his mouth and bit off a piece.

Chewed slowly.

Sofia saw that it was hard for him.

Not because the bread was bad.

Because it was good.

Because warm bread that you took “for later” still remains bread. It does not turn into an accuser. Does not become a monster. Does not demand confessions. It simply continues to feed.

Thomas ate another piece.

Nick exhaled quietly.

David turned toward the window.

Lina placed butter in front of Thomas without saying anything.

Eva looked at Sofia.

Sofia looked at her own hand.

The earth under her nail had become almost invisible.

Almost.

Ivar set the poker back in its place.

“After breakfast,” he said, “we will go to the water.”

It was the first announcement for the whole day.

Nick raised his head.

“All of us?”

“Those who can go without going for an answer.”

“And if I go for air?”

Ivar looked at him.

“Air is usually more patient than answers.”

Nick nodded.

“I’ll try not to disappoint the air.”

This time several people smiled.

Quietly.

Without commotion.

Sofia finished her tea.

Crumbs remained on the table.

Ordinary.

Uneven.

Pale on the dark board.

Before, crumbs had been for her something to brush away. Small rubbish after food. Now each crumb looked like a trace: the bread had been whole, then became a slice, then entered hands, mouth, body, warmth. Nothing had disappeared. Everything had passed farther.

Nora gathered the crumbs with her palm and poured them into a small bowl by the window.

“For the birds?” Sofia asked.

Nora nodded.

“If not for birds, then for someone small.”

“And if no one comes?”

Nora looked at the bowl.

“Then we’ll see tomorrow.”

Sofia smiled.

Nick came up to her and said very quietly:

“I didn’t touch it.”

“You did.”

“Well, all right. Almost didn’t touch it.”

“Was it warm?”

Nick looked at Ivar by the stove, then at Thomas, who was finishing his slice now without his earlier defence, then back at Sofia.

“Too warm.”

“Hot?”

“No.”

“Then what?”

He was silent for a moment.

“As if it had been waiting.”

Sofia did not answer.

Because that was exactly the word she had lacked.

The bread did not accuse.

Did not frighten.

Did not prove.

It waited.

Like earth under snow.

Like moss under cold water.

Like a tale under a familiar name.

Like everything living that a person had taken too long for a store.
