# Chapter Nineteen. Lack

After lunch, the house was left alone.

Not at once.

First someone was still walking between the kitchen and the entryway. Someone was looking for dry socks. Someone was checking whether a jacket had fallen from the bench. Lina covered the jar with cloth, but already without the solemnity that had appeared in the house after the loss. Marek closed the door to the entryway and looked at the sky through the glass.

“No rain yet,” he said.

Nick raised his head from his mug.

“Is that a forecast or a hope?”

“Not now.”

“Northern meteorological style: say only what no one can later hold against you.”

Marek did not answer.

He took the old basket by the door. Not a large one. Dark wicker, worn along the edges. Then he took from under the bench a knife with a wooden handle, put a small rag into the basket, and looked at Sofia.

“Coming?”

“Where?”

“To look.”

“At what?”

“What there is.”

Sofia nodded before she understood why those words made her feel lighter.

Not “to gather.”

Not “to search.”

To look.

Thomas rose almost at once.

“Me too.”

Marek did not object.

Nick sighed.

“I feel that if I stay in the house now, I will be made to wipe something philosophical.”

“Then go,” Eva said.

“Was that a trap?”

“No. Practice.”

Nick stood up.

Nora sat by the window and looked out at the yard. Sofia thought she would go too, but Nora only lowered her eyes and began tying the lace on her boot, which was already tied. Then she lifted her head.

“I’ll stay.”

Nick looked at her.

“A wise person chooses a roof over their head.”

“No,” Nora said. “I just don’t want to.”

“Even wiser.”

David stayed too. He sat at the table with the notebook closed and did not pretend he was working. That was almost a new quality already. Before, he at least depicted the intention to write down the order of the world. Now he sometimes simply sat, like a person who had understood that order does not always come when called.

“If we find a lot,” Thomas said, fastening his jacket, “we should sort it at once.”

Marek put on his hat.

“If we find.”

“But there’s usually some here?”

“Usually there is.”

“So we’ll find some.”

Marek looked at him.

Not sternly.

Not even tiredly.

“Usually is not a contract.”

Nick said quietly:

“Put that on the coat of arms of the house.”

David raised his eyes.

“I won’t.”

“You are growing frighteningly fast.”

They went out.

The air was cool, but without malice. After the night rain the earth had grown soft, and the smell of the forest had risen higher than usual. It no longer lay at the roots. It stood between the trunks, in the wet grass, in the moss, on the lower branches, on the sleeves of jackets. The house behind them seemed warmer after only three steps, although the door had closed tightly.

Marek led them not to the path they had taken yesterday for water, but slightly aside, along the shed, past the old barrel and low bushes. The earth there was darker. Leaves stuck to the soles. Underfoot it sometimes squelched so quietly, as if the forest did not want to admit it could be heard.

Sofia walked behind Marek.

Then Thomas.

Nick brought up the rear and periodically looked at his boots with the expression of a person negotiating with reality through poorly authorized representatives.

“Do people walk here?” Thomas asked.

“Yes.”

“Often?”

“When needed.”

Thomas exhaled.

“Everything here is ‘when needed.’”

Marek bent by the first stump and lifted a branch.

Under it was a mushroom.

Not large.

A pale cap, a wet edge, the stem in the earth firm and clean. Marek looked at it, then cut it low and placed it in the basket.

The sound was small.

The mushroom touched the bottom and remained there alone.

Thomas bent over.

“Good?”

“Good.”

“So there are some here.”

“There is one.”

Nick came closer.

“Thomas, don’t pressure the mushroom with statistics. It has already done everything it could.”

Thomas did not answer.

He looked around.

At first the forest seemed full. Every hummock promised. Every stump looked suspiciously generous. The leaves were raised as if something under them was waiting for a hand. The moss darkened softly. Dry grass lay in circles. The whole world resembled a place where something could be found.

But farther on it became quieter.

Under one bush there was only an old, spreading mushroom, already soft around the edge. Marek touched it with the knife and left it.

Under another — two small ones, too small.

By a birch Thomas found a beautiful red mushroom and called Marek.

“This one?”

Marek looked.

“No.”

“Why?”

“Beautiful.”

Nick smiled.

“A convincing northern classification.”

“And it can’t be taken?”

“It can’t.”

Thomas straightened.

“Beauty is suspicious here?”

“Sometimes.”

Marek was already walking on.

A few minutes later Sofia found three mushrooms together. One was good. The second was wormy. The third had already darkened under the cap, although from above it looked firm. Marek showed her where to cut, and put only one in the basket.

For some reason Sofia felt sorry for the other two.

Not strongly.

Foolishly.

As if they had failed selection not because they were spoiled, but because the world had not wanted to be full.

“Leave them?” she asked.

“Leave them.”

“Will someone eat them?”

Marek shrugged.

“Someone.”

Nick looked at the wormy mushroom.

“It already has tenants. Let’s not arrange an eviction.”

Thomas moved aside and suddenly called:

“There are more here!”

They came over.

At the root of a spruce there really were several caps. Five. Maybe six. Two good. One cracked. One too old. One looked as if something had already bitten the edge.

“An animal?” Thomas asked.

Marek looked.

“Maybe.”

“So they eat too.”

Nick raised his eyebrows.

“Thomas, you just said that in a tone as if the animals had violated a license.”

“I just asked.”

“No, you almost opened a case.”

Thomas wanted to object, but stopped.

Marek cut two caps and put them into the basket. Now the bottom was no longer empty. There were four mushrooms there and one more, Sofia’s, with a slightly curved stem.

Five.

Not many.

But no longer nothing.

And because of that something changed in the air.

Sofia noticed it in herself first. The basket in Marek’s hand became not simply a basket. It became a promise. Not a large one. Not festive. But a promise. In the house, it would be possible to put a pan on the stove. Lina would think of something. Eva would slice. Nick would say some nonsense about mushrooms that had not signed consent.

But it was already possible to imagine.

That “possible” turned out warmer than the mushrooms themselves.

Farther on they found berries.

Low bushes stood in a clearing where the earth rose slightly. The berries were dark, almost black, with a bluish bloom. Not many. They had to be searched for between wet leaves. Fingers quickly became cold and red.

Marek picked one and tasted it.

“Sour.”

Nick took a berry, ate it, and immediately made the face of a person to whom the world has informed the truth without mitigating circumstances.

“Yes,” he said. “Very honest berries.”

Thomas squatted.

“Take them?”

“A little.”

“If they’re sour?”

“They can go with porridge. Or with fish.”

“Will there be fish?”

Marek looked toward the water.

“We’ll see.”

Nick leaned toward Sofia.

“The word has reported for duty again.”

They gathered the berries into the rag. Not a full handful — two, maybe three. More leaves than berries. But the dark dots on the white cloth looked almost rich, until one remembered how many people were sitting in the house.

Thomas asked after all:

“Not enough for everyone.”

“Enough for something,” Marek said.

“For what?”

“We’ll find out.”

Thomas smiled without joy.

“Do you answer that way on purpose?”

“How?”

“Without closing it.”

Marek thought.

“I don’t know what will close.”

It was not beautiful.

And therefore it remained.

They came out to the water through a low passage between pines. There the earth grew lighter, then suddenly dropped downward. The lake lay motionless, but not smooth. A fine ripple moved across the surface, as if the water were stirring not from wind, but from its own thought. Leaves floated near the shore. Old stones were wet to black.

Marek had a simple tackle there.

Not a net.

Not a proper fishing device.

Just a cord, a stick, a small keepnet tied by the stone. He checked it silently. Bent, pulled it up, the water rustled.

Something living struck inside the keepnet.

Once.

Hard.

Thomas stepped closer at once.

“Anything?”

“There is.”

Marek pulled out the keepnet.

Inside was one fish.

Not large.

Silvery, with a dark back, cold and strong in its final displeasure. It beat against the mesh, opened its mouth, threw water onto the stone. Sofia stepped back. Not even out of pity. Out of surprise. Living resistance is always larger than the size of the body.

“One,” Thomas said.

“One,” Marek said.

“Usually?”

Marek untied the keepnet.

“Sometimes more.”

“Today one.”

“Today one.”

Nick stood a little farther off, hands in his pockets.

Did not joke.

Sofia noticed this and, for some reason, looked at the fish more closely. It was already beating less. Water ran from the mesh. On the stone there remained a silver scale, thin as a piece of cold light.

“Take it?” Sofia asked.

Marek looked at her.

“That is why we checked.”

She felt how unnecessary the question had been.

Not because it was foolish.

Because behind it stood something childish: whether one may take what is alive.

But they were already taking.

Mushrooms.

Berries.

Warmth.

Water.

Now fish.

Marek wrapped the fish in a wet cloth and laid it on top of the mushrooms in the basket. The cloth darkened at once. The basket became heavier by very little. But the feeling changed more than the weight.

Now this was supper.

Nick was the first to say what no one wanted to say too directly:

“Well then. Not a feast yet, but not a philosophical hunger strike either.”

Thomas nodded.

“Enough for the evening.”

He said it calmly.

Almost with relief.

And Sofia felt something inside her nod as well.

Enough.

Not much.

But enough.

That word lay down in the basket on top of the fish, mushrooms, and sour berries. No one saw it, but it became easier to carry.

They walked back faster.

They did not hurry, but already wanted home. Marek carried the basket. Sofia steadied the edge when the path grew slippery. Thomas looked around as if checking whether they had missed one more place. Nick once stopped by a stump, lifted a leaf, found nothing, and said:

“The world is secretive, but not obliged to report.”

Then fell silent.

At the house, the first thing they had to do was take off their boots.

The porch was wet. The boards darkened from moisture. Under the shelter someone’s jacket was hanging, dripping from the edge. On the bench lay two stacks of firewood that Marek had said in the morning not to bring in right away. The door to the entryway was open; from inside came the smell of warmth and something sour — perhaps the cloth over the jar, perhaps wet sleeves.

Eva looked out from the doorway.

“Found something?”

Thomas answered faster than Marek:

“A little. But enough.”

Eva smiled.

Not broadly.

Just with the corner of her mouth.

“Don’t bring it into the house right away,” she said. “It’s wet enough in there as it is.”

Marek set the basket on the edge of the porch, under the shelter, closer to the wall.

“We’ll sort it later,” he said.

“Does the fish need to be done now?” Thomas asked.

“Boots now.”

“And the fish?”

“It’s lying there.”

“In wet cloth.”

“It’s used to it.”

Nick snorted quietly.

“The fish would probably like to dispute the quality of care.”

But he too began taking off his boot.

The boot did not want to come off.

Sofia set her foot on the edge of a board and pulled at the heel. Mud stretched from the sole in threads. One forest leaf had stuck to a lace. Thomas was already standing in one sock and holding the second boot in his hand, clearly trying at the same time to watch the basket and not step in a wet place.

From inside someone called Eva.

“Coming!” she called and went into the house.

Lina appeared on the threshold and looked at the basket.

“Wet?”

“Yes,” Marek said.

“Let it stand.”

“The fish is there,” Thomas said.

“I see.”

“It needs to be...”

“Later.”

The word again closed not an answer, but a door against unnecessary hurry.

Thomas wanted to say something else, but Nick finally defeated the boot and nearly sat down on the wet boards. Sofia caught him by the sleeve.

“My thanks,” he said. “You have saved the north from an unnecessary spectacle.”

“The north would survive.”

“But I wouldn’t.”

At that moment something fell inside the house. Not loudly, but enough for everyone to turn.

“That was me!” David shouted from inside. “Nothing terrible.”

“The most alarming phrase of domestic life,” Nick said.

Marek went in first.

Thomas after him.

Sofia lingered on the porch for a second. Looked at the basket. The fish in the wet cloth lay on top. The edge of the cloth moved slightly in the wind. The berries in their bundle had left a dark stain. The mushrooms were visible on the side, pale, damp, uneven.

Enough for the evening, she thought.

Not even in words.

The body thought it.

The stomach.

The hands.

The tiredness in the legs.

Then she went into the house.

The house took them in for a short while.

There really had been a pot lid that fell. David had set it too close to the edge while helping Eva take something out. Nothing broke. But the lid rolled under the bench, Nick crawled after it and hit his shoulder. Thomas began explaining that if the bench stood five centimeters farther away, the passage would be more convenient. Eva said that if people explained less, the passage would be more convenient too. Lina asked Marek to bring a clean board for the fish.

And then Thomas remembered.

“The fish.”

He said it as if the fish could be personally offended by the delay.

Marek was already going to the door.

“Now.”

They went out almost together: Marek, Thomas, Sofia, Nick behind them because, as he said, “if there is a new form of household humiliation, I must be a witness.”

On the porch the basket lay on its side.

Not neatly fallen.

It was overturned as if it had been shoved from below and from the side at the same time. The rag with berries lay by the step, opened. Berries had scattered over the boards and into the mud. Some of the mushrooms lay nearby, some were crushed. One good mushroom, the very first one, pale and firm, was trampled into the wet earth at the edge of the porch.

The fish was gone.

At first Sofia saw only that.

The empty place.

The cloth lay separately, wet, with a dark tear at the edge. A scale remained on the board. Several silver dots stuck to the raw wood. By the step the mud was churned by short, strong movements. At the edge of the porch — a track. Wide. Smeared. Not human.

It smelled of fish.

And raw wool.

And forest earth, too close to the house.

Nick stopped behind Sofia.

She waited for him to say something.

He did not.

Thomas stepped forward.

“Who left it like this?”

Nick looked at him.

But still said nothing.

“We all left it,” Sofia said.

She had not meant to speak.

The words came out by themselves.

Thomas turned to her.

“No. Who put it on the edge?”

“I did,” Marek said.

“Why not higher?”

Marek looked at the tracks.

“Because they usually don’t come this close in the daytime.”

“Usually.”

Thomas’s word struck harder than he had meant.

Marek did not answer.

He crouched by the step, touched the mud with the tips of his fingers, looked at the ground by the porch. Then he stood and walked a little to the side, toward the bushes. There the earth was turned more deeply. Leaves overturned. Moss pressed down.

“Boars?” Thomas asked.

Marek looked down.

“Looks like it.”

“One?”

Marek shook his head.

“No.”

Nick finally exhaled.

Not a joke.

Simply air.

Sofia felt anger.

Not large.

Not clean.

Ugly.

It rose from her stomach, dry and fast, although everything around was wet.

She felt hurt as if someone had taken a plate from her table.

Although there had not yet been a plate.

That made it shameful.

Lina came out of the house with a clean board in her hands.

Stopped.

Looked.

Set the board on the bench.

“All right,” she said.

And this “all right” was not a conclusion. Not reproach. The beginning of work.

She crouched by the basket.

“This one can be used.”

She took one mushroom, whole, only dirty.

“This one too.”

A second.

“Not this one.”

She put the crushed mushroom aside.

“This one, rinse.”

“The fish is gone,” Thomas said.

“I see.”

“We should have brought it in right away.”

Lina raised her eyes to him.

“Now we need to sort through it.”

“But if we had...”

“Thomas.”

He fell silent.

Not because he agreed.

Because in her voice there was no room for “if we had.” There was room only for what lay on the boards.

David appeared in the doorway.

In his hands was the notebook.

Closed.

He looked at the basket, at the mushrooms, at the scale.

Then lowered the hand with the notebook along his body.

“I had already counted it differently,” he said quietly.

Nick looked at him.

And again did not joke.

That silence suddenly became the most unpleasant part of the scene.

Marek brought the wet cloth from the edge of the porch. Smelled it, grimaced.

“They carried the fish off.”

“Far?” Thomas asked.

Marek looked at the forest.

“Far enough.”

“Can it be found?”

“You can follow the track.”

“And?”

“Find the place where it is already gone.”

Thomas clenched his fists.

Not strongly.

Just enough for his fingers to feel occupied.

“So that’s it?”

“No,” Lina said.

She lifted the basket.

At the bottom remained three mushrooms. One good. One small. One with a dirty side that could still be cut away. Beside them a wet leaf had stuck. In the corner lay two berries, crushed into a dark stain.

On the board the fish scale shone.

“Here,” Lina said.

“That is not supper,” Thomas said.

The words came too quickly.

Sofia felt how they struck not only Lina. Everyone.

Lina did not take offense.

She rarely spent strength on offense when she had to hold dirty mushrooms in her hands.

“It is part of supper,” she said.

“A small part.”

“Yes.”

Nick finally spoke.

Quietly.

“The forest sent the bill faster than we did.”

Nick himself understood that he had said it too beautifully and winced.

“Please consider that a draft phrase,” he added. “I am not ready for public responsibility.”

But no one smiled at once.

Then Eva, standing in the doorway, said:

“Wash your hands and help.”

“Now I recognize the world,” Nick said.

And went to wash his hands.

Marek lifted the crushed mushrooms and carried them to the edge of the porch.

“Where does this go?” Sofia asked.

“Not here.”

“Behind the shed?”

“Later.”

He put them on an old little board so as not to smear them over the steps. His movements were so calm that Sofia felt even worse. She wanted someone else to be angry too. Not strongly. Just enough to confirm that the loss had been a loss.

But Marek was not angry outwardly.

Lina was not angry outwardly.

Eva had already brought a bowl of water.

Thomas stood and looked at the scale.

David held the closed notebook.

Nick was washing his hands too thoroughly.

Sofia bent down and lifted the pale mushroom out of the mud.

The very first one.

It was crushed on one side. The stem had split. The cap was pressed full of earth. Not long ago it had lain in the basket first, and because of it the bottom had stopped being empty.

“This one?” she asked.

Lina looked.

“No.”

“Completely?”

“Completely.”

Sofia held it for another second.

Then put it on the little board with the others.

For some reason, this mushroom was harder to let go of than the fish. The fish had disappeared. It was not there. But the mushroom was here, in her hand, almost like food, but no longer food.

Thomas said:

“We should have closed the basket.”

Marek answered:

“Yes.”

“Hung it up.”

“Yes.”

“Brought it in right away.”

“We could have.”

“Then why...”

Marek lifted his eyes to him.

“Because we didn’t.”

It was rough only because it was exact.

Thomas fell silent.

Sofia expected him to flare up. He did not. He looked at the basket as if it were a problem in which all the right actions had become clear only after the answer.

“I don’t like this,” he said at last.

“What?” Eva asked.

“When the lesson appears after the mistake.”

Nick, drying his hands, said quietly:

“Lessons are generally impolite people.”

This time someone almost smiled.

Almost.

Lina sorted through everything that remained. Three mushrooms went into a clean bowl. Several berries went there too, although one was crushed and stained the water dark. She did not touch the fish scales at once. First she washed the board, then the step, then the edge of the basket. The scales held to the wet wood stubbornly, like small silver evidence.

“Leave it,” Marek said.

“Why?” Thomas asked.

“It will wash away.”

“With rain?”

“Or in the evening.”

Thomas looked at the sky.

“So now we wait for rain.”

“No.”

“Then what?”

“Not everything has to be rubbed at once.”

Thomas smiled crookedly.

“Today, apparently, that is the common theme of the house.”

Lina looked at him.

“Not the house.”

He fell silent.

The sentence did not continue.

And because of that became better.

Inside, what remained was put on the table.

Lina did not put the basket away at once. She set it by the wall to dry. At the bottom lay three mushrooms, a fish scale, and a wet leaf.

Sofia sat beside it.

She still wanted the supper she had already managed to imagine.

And that desire was more unpleasant than hunger.

Nick sat opposite.

Silent.

Then said:

“I wanted to joke about a boar restaurant.”

“Why didn’t you?” Sofia asked.

He looked at the basket.

“There wasn’t enough.”

She did not understand at once what he meant.

Enough joke.

Enough food.

Enough air.

Enough of them.

Probably a little of everything.

Lina took the bowl with the three mushrooms.

“There will be soup,” she said.

Thomas raised his head.

“From this?”

“And from something else.”

“From what?”

“We’ll see.”

He almost laughed.

Not cheerfully.

But no longer angrily.

“Of course.”

Marek took off his jacket and hung it by the door.

“I’ll check the tracks in the evening.”

“Why?” David asked.

Marek shrugged.

“To know where they walk.”

“And what will change?”

“We won’t leave the basket there.”

David nodded.

Opened the notebook.

Then looked at the basket.

Closed it.

“Later,” he said.

Nick said quietly:

“Everything today is later. Except the boars. The boars work immediately.”

And this time Eva smiled first.

Briefly.

Tiredly.

But she smiled.

Outside the window fine rain began to fall again.

Not heavy.

The kind that solves nothing at once, but gradually changes the look of boards, earth, tracks, and silver dots on the step.

Sofia went out to the threshold once more.

Not for food.

Simply to look.

The porch was darkening. The tracks by the step were already spreading. Where the fish had lain, only the smell remained, which the rain had not yet had time to press down. By the edge, near the mud, a broken piece of mushroom stem showed pale.

Sofia wanted to pick it up.

She did not.

She stood and watched the rain make it smaller.

Then she returned to the house.

The basket was set by the wall.

At the bottom lay three mushrooms and fish scales.

The rest remained outside.
