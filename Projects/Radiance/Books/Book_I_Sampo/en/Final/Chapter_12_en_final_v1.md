# Chapter Twelve. Recipe

The next day the bread had cooled.

And that changed it at once.

Yesterday it had been an event. Hot, fragrant, almost impossible, the kind that could not be cut at once, could not be hurried, could not be received before its time. It had stood on the table, covered with a towel, and the whole house had moved more slowly around it, as if around a sleeping animal.

In the morning it lay on the board differently.

The crust had become harder. The crumb denser. The smell had gone deeper. The slices cut more evenly. The bread had stopped being a miracle of waiting and had become proof.

It had worked.

That was enough for the next thing to begin.

Sofia understood it almost at once, even before Thomas opened his mouth. He stood by the table with a mug in his hand and looked not even at the bread, but at the place near the stove where yesterday the bowl of dough had stood. At the empty place. There was more tension in it than there had been in the full bowl.

An empty place is always more convenient for thought.

On it one can build everything that the living thing had interfered with yesterday.

“How much flour was there?” Thomas asked.

Lina did not raise her head. She was cutting bread.

“Enough.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“The morning has begun with a crime against precision.”

Thomas ignored him.

“I’m serious. About how much?”

“About enough.”

“Lina.”

She set the knife on the board and looked at him.

“Did you eat?”

“Yes.”

“Was there bread?”

“There was.”

“Then there was enough flour.”

“That is not a recipe.”

“No.”

“There.”

“And I didn’t say it was a recipe.”

Nick said quietly to Sofia:

“I think Thomas came not to have breakfast, but to interrogate yesterday.”

Sofia took a slice. The bread was cold, but not dry. The crust held the knife, the crumb sprang back. She spread butter on it and suddenly felt: here it was, the very dangerous place that had only begun yesterday. When bread was hot, it had to be waited for. When bread cooled, it could be studied.

Thomas sat down.

David was sitting opposite him, already with the notebook.

Not with the same tension as before. The notebook was open, but not to the map. The pencil lay across a clean page like a small bridge between desire and caution. David was not writing. Yet.

Eva set tea on the table.

“You too?” she asked David.

“Not yet.”

“But you are going to.”

“Possibly.”

Nick raised his hand.

“May I officially state that with David, ‘possibly’ means ‘yes, but I’m ashamed,’ so that we don’t lose time?”

David looked at him.

“You may. But I can also officially state that your irony is a way of not participating while you are participating anyway.”

Nick lowered his hand.

“Unpleasant. Continue.”

David almost smiled and still picked up the pencil.

“I don’t want to turn this into a map.”

“That is already good,” Sofia said.

“But if the bread turned out, we can at least write down what there was.”

“What there was,” Nora repeated by the window.

She was sitting there with a cup, her legs tucked under her, looking at the grey yard. She barely looked at the table. But she heard everything.

Thomas came alive.

“Exactly. Not to control. To understand.”

Lina took up the knife again.

“Understanding is possible.”

“Then tell me.”

“What?”

“How much flour.”

“I didn’t weigh it.”

“How much water?”

“As much as the dough took.”

“What does ‘took’ mean?”

“It means what it means.”

“Dough does not take. It is mixed.”

Lina looked at him for a long time.

“Did it hear you yesterday?”

“What?”

“When you asked every ten minutes.”

Thomas opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Nick said quietly:

“The score is one to zero in favour of the dough.”

David wrote: “Flour — enough.” He looked at the line, grimaced, and crossed it out.

“You see,” Eva said.

“What?”

“You are already dissatisfied with the recipe.”

“It isn’t a recipe.”

“Exactly.”

The kitchen was bright, but not sunny. The grey morning held on to the windows; the wet grass by the porch darkened; the woodshed stood slightly crooked, as if after yesterday’s conversations about bread it too had decided not to give straight answers. Towels were drying on the stove. By the wall stood the clean bowl, the very one in which the dough had risen yesterday. It still smelled a little sour, a little warm, although the bowl had already been washed.

Thomas looked at it too often.

“How long was the first rise?” he asked.

Lina broke off a small piece of crust for herself.

“Until the first rise.”

“That is mockery.”

“No. It is memory.”

“Whose?”

She showed her hands.

“This one’s.”

David looked at her fingers. Nothing special. Short nails, flour still in one fold of skin, a scar near the thumb. Yesterday Sofia had already watched those hands. Today, for some reason, they seemed even more important. Not because they knew more than paper. Because they did not separate knowledge from participation.

“You can write ‘until it begins to hold itself,’” Eva said.

David raised his eyes.

“What?”

“The dough. Yesterday, when Lina kneaded it. At first it spread out. Then it began to hold itself.”

Lina nodded.

“Yes.”

Thomas said:

“That is also imprecise.”

“But true,” Eva answered.

Nick lifted his mug.

“The greatest confrontation of our age: imprecise, but true.”

“Nick,” Lina said.

“Yes, yes. I’m already tired of myself. But the event is historical.”

David wrote: “knead until it begins to hold itself.”

The line looked strange.

Almost funny.

Almost frightening.

Because on paper it became an instruction. Yesterday it had been the movement of a hand.

Nora turned from the window.

“It won’t remember the hands.”

Silence.

The phrase was spoken so quietly that at first it seemed possible not to take it seriously. But it lay exactly where it needed to: between David’s notebook and Lina’s fingers.

“What won’t?” Thomas asked.

“The recipe,” Nora said.

“A recipe isn’t supposed to remember hands. It is supposed to describe an action.”

“There,” she said.

And turned back to the window.

Nick looked at her for several seconds.

“Nora, sometimes you say something in such a way that I want to object, but in order to do that I would have to become stupider.”

“Don’t.”

“Thank you. I’m holding on.”

David did not cross out the line.

But beside it, a little lower, he wrote: “hands?”

With a question mark.

Sofia saw it and for some reason felt tenderness toward him. Not a great one. A careful one. David still wanted to write it down. But now at least he saw that paper could not contain everything. Before, he might have rejoiced at the sheet itself. Today the sheet was beginning to resist.

Thomas moved closer.

“May I look?”

David immediately tensed.

And they both noticed it.

After yesterday, the notebook could no longer simply open.

“You may,” David said after a pause.

He turned the notebook so Thomas could see the page, but did not let it go from his hands.

A compromise.

Not trust.

But already not a lid.

Thomas read:

“Knead until it begins to hold itself.”

“Hands?”

“This cannot be used,” he said.

“It can,” Lina said.

“How?”

“With hands.”

Nick quietly groaned.

“We are being destroyed by domestic precision.”

“I am not against hands,” Thomas said.

“You are against the fact that they don’t stay on paper,” Sofia said.

The words came out faster than she had time to decide whether they should be spoken.

Thomas turned to her.

Not sharply.

Rather in surprise.

“Possibly,” he said.

And that was better than arguing.

Eva sat down at the table.

“You can write things down. And sometimes you must. Otherwise every time you begin from zero.”

“There,” Thomas said.

“But a recipe does not bake bread.”

“No one says it does.”

“Your hand says it,” Nora said, without turning around.

Thomas closed his eyes tiredly.

“Has everyone decided to speak in riddles today?”

Marek, who until then had been silently cleaning a frying pan by the sink, said:

“No. You simply ask as if the answer is obliged to be transferable.”

“And it isn’t transferable?”

Marek set the pan to dry.

“Not all of it.”

Ivar came in from outside closer to noon. He had a damp jacket on his shoulders. The hair at his temples had darkened from the fine rain. He went to the stove, hung up the jacket, looked at the table: bread, notebook, pencil, Lina’s hands, Thomas’s face, David’s question mark beside the word “hands.”

“Writing?” he asked.

“A recipe,” Thomas said.

Ivar nodded.

Did not approve.

Did not condemn.

Simply accepted the word as an object on the table.

Thomas was apparently waiting for more.

“If everything is written down, it can be repeated,” he said.

Ivar took a slice of bread.

“Paper does not rise.”

And sat down by the stove.

Nothing more.

No continuation.

No explanation that would make the phrase convenient.

Thomas looked at the notebook.

David — at the paper.

Lina — at her hands.

Nick almost opened his mouth, but changed his mind.

Sofia felt that Ivar’s phrase had not closed the conversation. On the contrary. It had left it open in the most unpleasant place. Because Thomas was right: writing is necessary. David was right: without writing, memory spreads apart. Eva was right: one cannot begin from zero every time. But Ivar was right too, if it can even be called being right: paper lies there and does not breathe.

And yesterday the bread had not risen on paper.

Lina suddenly stood.

“Come,” she said to Thomas.

“Where?”

“To look at what you didn’t write down.”

He grew wary.

“Is that a threat?”

“Almost a lesson.”

“That’s worse,” Nick said.

Lina took a small clean jar from the shelf. Not new. There were scratches on the glass, the lid was slightly dented, and inside it smelled of sour bread and something old, warm, not spoiled, but having lived longer than one day.

“Yesterday I left a piece,” she said.

Thomas turned at once.

“What piece?”

“Of dough.”

“When?”

“Before the tins.”

“Why didn’t you say?”

“You didn’t ask.”

Nick said quietly:

“The historic error of science: no one asked about the jar.”

Lina set the jar on the table. Inside, under a cloth, lay a small piece of yesterday’s dough. Not beautiful. Grey-beige, soft, slightly settled, with a damp surface. It did not look like the future. More like a forgotten remnant.

And still it smelled differently from the empty bowl.

More sour.

Quieter.

More persistent.

“What is that for?” Thomas asked.

“For later.”

“But there is a recipe.”

Lina looked at him.

“There is.”

“Then why this?”

“So there is something to begin with.”

“With flour, water, and yeast.”

“Sometimes.”

“And this?”

“This already knows yesterday.”

David raised his eyes from the notebook.

“What does ‘knows yesterday’ mean?”

“It means what it means.”

“It doesn’t know.”

Lina opened the cloth a little wider.

“Does it smell?”

David leaned closer.

Nodded.

“Yes.”

“There.”

“Smell is not knowledge.”

“For you — no.”

Nick said quietly:

“I am delighted. We have begun an argument between epistemology and sour dough.”

Thomas took the notebook from David with his gaze, but not with his hands.

“This also has to be written down.”

“Write it,” Lina said.

“How much should be left?”

“A little.”

“How much exactly?”

“Enough.”

“Lina.”

“Thomas.”

“Will it spoil?”

“If forgotten — yes.”

“Then it is unreliable.”

“Alive.”

“Then it is unreliable.”

Lina covered the jar with the cloth.

“Then it requires care.”

Sofia looked at the small piece under the cloth. It seemed almost funny beside the notebook. The notebook was neater. The pages were straight. The lines could be aligned. The words could be read again. The pencil could be sharpened. A mistake could be crossed out. Paper does not sour, does not ask for warmth, does not require feeding, does not die of forgetfulness.

But paper does not rise.

And this small piece, ugly, damp, alive, could.

Not by itself.

Not without a person.

Not like a button.

But it could continue.

“Does it need to be fed?” Nora asked.

Lina nodded.

“Yes.”

“With what?”

“Flour, water. Warmth. Time.”

“And that’s all?”

“And not forgetting.”

Nora looked at Thomas.

“Did you write that down?”

He was already writing.

Too quickly.

David watched his hand and for some reason did not stop him. Perhaps because now he was not the one writing. Perhaps because it became easier for him from the fact that another hand also wanted to make the impossible neat.

“‘Not forgetting’ is not a point in a recipe,” Thomas said.

“It will become one,” Nick answered.

“Very funny.”

“No. But necessary.”

Thomas wrote it down anyway:

“Not forgetting.”

And that line suddenly turned out to be the most precise.

After lunch the rain grew stronger.

Not strong, not a real downpour, but an even northern rain that does not fall so much as occupy the air. The house became darker. People scattered: Marek went to check the boat shelter, Eva sorted dry herbs by the window, Nora sat on the floor with a cup, Nick tried to read and every five minutes declared that the book was losing to the jar of dough in dramaturgy.

Thomas and David remained at the table.

The notebook lay between them.

Not entirely David’s.

Not entirely common.

After yesterday, that could no longer be settled by one word.

An attempt at a recipe had appeared on the page:

Flour — roughly.

Water — warm.

Yeast — alive.

Sugar — a pinch.

Salt — a little.

Knead until it holds itself.

First rise — until it rises.

Punch down.

Second rise — less.

Bake — until the crust says.

Do not cut at once.

Leave a piece.

Feed.

Not forgetting.

Thomas looked at the page like a person who had received a document, but had not received power.

“This is a terrible recipe,” he said.

David nodded.

“Yes.”

“But better than nothing.”

“Yes.”

“It is impossible to repeat exactly with it.”

“Yes.”

“Then what is it for?”

David thought.

“So we remember where to ask.”

Thomas looked at him.

“Whom?”

David nodded toward the kitchen, toward the jar under the cloth, toward Lina’s hands, toward the stove, toward the rain beyond the window.

“Not only the paper.”

Thomas was silent.

Then said:

“You have become very inconvenient.”

“I’m trying.”

“It doesn’t look like it.”

“Then it’s working.”

Nick, without looking up from the book, said:

“I request that it be recorded: the group has moved from cartographic illness to bread uncertainty, with signs of recovery and complications.”

“Nick,” David said.

“Yes, understood. Too precise. I withdraw.”

Thomas still took the pencil and added a small question mark beside the line “until the crust says.”

David looked.

“Leave it.”

“Why?”

“Because it really did say.”

“The crust does not speak.”

“Yesterday it did.”

Thomas wanted to object.

Did not.

The rain beyond the window grew thicker.

And then Sofia saw something else again.

This time not bread.

Not dough.

Stone.

The same low house, perhaps another, perhaps the same one. Grain in a leather sack. A stone that moves in a circle. The woman is no longer alone. Beside her a man with a thin stick is making notches on a wooden board. He is not writing words. He cuts short marks.

One handful.

Two.

Three.

A circle.

Another circle.

Another.

The woman’s shoulder moves.

The boy who yesterday said “And what if it did it by itself?” now says nothing and looks at the notches.

The flour gathers slowly.

Little.

Always little, if counted in advance.

“So much grain,” says the man with the stick.

A new mark.

“So many circles.”

A new mark.

“So much flour.”

He looks at the board as if not memory has appeared on it, but a promise.

The woman wipes her forehead with the back of her hand.

“And?”

The man does not answer at once.

He looks at the sack, at the stone, at the flour, at the marks.

“If we repeat the same number of times, will the same amount come out?”

No one laughs.

Because the question is too tired to be stupid.

And too reasonable to be safe.

The stone stands.

The woman looks at the board.

The boy looks at the stone.

There is no machine in the air.

Not yet.

Only the first belief that labour can be caught in sameness.

Sofia blinked.

The kitchen returned.

David was looking at her.

“Again?”

She nodded.

“Notches.”

“Where?”

“On wood. How much grain, how many circles, how much flour.”

Thomas straightened.

“And?”

“Someone asked: if we repeat the same number of times, will the same amount come out?”

Thomas did not understand at once why his breath caught.

David understood faster.

“That is a normal question,” Thomas said.

“Yes,” Sofia answered.

“A very normal one.”

“Yes.”

Nick closed the book.

“That is exactly what is alarming. The most dangerous things usually begin with a normal question.”

Nora, without raising her eyes from her cup, said:

“Normal does not mean small.”

“Thank you,” Nick said. “Now I feel worse, but with better quality.”

Thomas stood.

Walked around the kitchen.

Returned to the table.

“What is wrong with repetition?”

Nick looked at Sofia.

Sofia — at David.

David — at the jar under the cloth.

“Nothing,” Lina said from the stove.

She had been listening all this time.

“Then why are you all looking like that?”

Lina came to the table, took the jar with the living piece, checked the cloth.

“What is bad is not repetition.”

“What is?”

“When you think repetition belongs to you.”

The phrase could have become too large.

Lina saved it by immediately carrying the jar to the stove and setting it on the shelf where it was warm, but not hot.

She did not look at Thomas.

Did not wait for a reaction.

Simply set it down.

And by doing that she said more than if she had continued.

Thomas sat down.

Looked at the recipe again.

“I don’t want to own repetition.”

Nick said quietly:

“No one does. At first.”

David touched the pencil.

“I thought the same thing about the map.”

It was said without self-accusation.

And therefore stronger.

Thomas heard it.

Sofia saw how he heard it.

He did not answer.

The rain beat on the roof almost evenly. Not quite. If one listened for a long time, one could notice: somewhere faster, somewhere heavier, somewhere drops struck the metal edge of the gutter and made a different sound. Even the rain did not repeat itself exactly, though it seemed the same.

Nick suddenly said:

“Can we have a recipe for rain?”

“Nick,” Eva said.

“I’m serious. Water — roughly, sky — warm or cold, wind — until it holds itself...”

“Stop.”

“I already have. I only wanted to help Thomas feel the scale of the disaster.”

Thomas unexpectedly smiled.

“I felt it.”

“There. I’m useful.”

“Don’t write it down,” Nora said.

“Too late. My soul has already entered it into the archive.”

By evening the recipe had been copied onto a separate sheet.

David insisted on a clean page. Thomas insisted on margins for clarifications. Eva added that “until the crust says” was better not replaced by a number, otherwise it would turn out worse. Lina did not argue. She only sometimes corrected a word if it led too far away from the hand.

“Mix” was replaced with “combine.”

“Wait an hour” was crossed out.

“Wait until it rises” remained.

Thomas put brackets beside it: “roughly?”

Nick wrote in pencil at the bottom: “if the bread objects, address the crust.” Lina looked at him in such a way that he erased it himself.

Nora approached the sheet at the very end.

Read it.

Did not touch it with her finger.

“It has become neat,” she said.

“Is that bad?” Thomas asked.

“No.”

“Then why did you say it like that?”

“Because the piece by the stove hasn’t.”

Everyone looked at the small jar.

The cloth over it had puffed up slightly.

Only a little.

Perhaps from warmth.

Perhaps from the living movement inside.

“It needs to be fed in the morning,” Lina said.

Thomas took the pencil.

“Write it down?”

Lina grinned.

“You can.”

“What exactly?”

“That in the morning it needs to be fed.”

“How much?”

“You’ll see.”

Thomas set the pencil down.

“That is impossible.”

“No,” Lina said. “That is tomorrow.”

The phrase remained hanging softly, but firmly.

Tomorrow.

The thing paper cannot do today.

Sofia took the recipe and looked at it. The page really was neat. David’s handwriting was even, Thomas’s notes a little angular, Eva’s corrections here and there, a small tea stain in the right corner. It was already an object. It could be folded, kept, passed on, hidden, copied. It promised that yesterday’s bread had not disappeared entirely.

And still, beside it, by the stove, stood the jar.

In it lay not a promise.

An obligation.

It required not reading, but participation.

And that was heavier.

And more honest.

Late in the evening Ivar came out onto the porch. Sofia saw him from the kitchen: a large shadow in the doorway, silver in his hair, wet boards under his boots. He did not call. But she went out.

“Cold again,” she said.

“Autumn is close.”

“You can feel it already.”

“It does not ask.”

They stood in silence for a while.

In the house behind their backs lay the recipe.

By the stove stood the jar.

The rain had grown quieter, but had not ended.

“Is it bad?” Sofia asked.

“What?”

“Writing things down.”

Ivar looked at the dark yard.

“No.”

“Then what is bad?”

He did not answer at once.

Then said:

“Forgetting that you did not write everything down.”

She nodded.

It was not an explanation.

Rather a log laid by the stove: it would be useful later.

Thomas’s voice came from the kitchen:

“And if in the morning it is too sour?”

Nick answered:

“Then we will officially declare that the piece has developed character.”

Lina said something. It could not be made out. Nora laughed softly.

Sofia looked at the light in the window.

There, inside, on the table lay paper, which asked for nothing.

By the stove stood the jar, which tomorrow would need to be opened, smelled, fed, warmed, and waited for again.

The recipe was ready.

And that which had to rise again stood by the stove under a cloth.
