# Chapter Six. The Trace

After the rain, the house did not let them outside for a long time.

It did not lock them in, of course. The door stood where it had stood, the hooks held the jackets, the mat darkened from the water brought in, the stove breathed evenly, and no one said that going out was forbidden. But after the rain there appeared in the house a special heaviness — not cosy and not hostile, but the kind wet clothing has after it has been taken off and hung up and still continues to be part of the body.

The things arranged after the pantry had not disappeared back into ordinariness.

The blankets lay on the bench.

The lamps stood by the wall.

The wooden bowls were stacked on the table, and one, the same one, remained under the towel. Eva later removed the towel, saying it was damp anyway, but the gesture had already happened, and the bowl did not become what it had been. Now it was empty even when no one looked at it.

Nora sat by the window and held her enamel mug in both hands, although there was no tea in it. The mug had dried almost completely. At the chip there remained a thin dark line, as if the water had gone, but the place where it had lingered still remembered.

Thomas stood by the windowsill, near the box of nails.

He was not touching it.

He looked as if a thing one does not touch can still leave something on the fingers.

David sat at the table and tried to open a map on his phone. The phone showed a grey rectangle, then white, then grey again. There was a kind of mocking honesty in this: even the device did not want to turn the wet forest into an understandable scheme.

“There’s no signal,” David said.

“You’ve already said that,” Nick replied.

“I’m checking.”

“Does the signal love the persistent?”

“Sometimes.”

“Then it has poor taste.”

David did not answer. He ran a finger over the screen. The map jerked, showed a piece of road, then disappeared again.

Sofia sat at the edge of the table and looked at his finger.

After what had happened with the things — she already mentally assigned it not to one event, but to the whole day, although the day had broken apart into separate places — David’s finger on the screen seemed like a continuation of the same mistake. The hand was looking for a direction. Not a thing yet. Only the path to a thing. But if a thing could already exist, if the rumor had received a place where it could wait, then somewhere there had to be a path.

And if the path did not show itself, a person began stroking the empty screen harder.

Marek came in from outside.

His boots were muddy, the sleeve of his jacket wet almost to the elbow. In his hands he held a short hatchet and a coil of rope.

“The lower boards have been washed out,” he said.

“What boards?” David asked too quickly.

Marek looked at him.

“The wooden ones. Across the wet place behind the firs. The water has risen there.”

“Is it far?”

“If you walk normally — no. If you’re looking for adventure — far.”

Nick raised a hand.

“I am officially against adventure. Especially wet adventure.”

“I’m going to look,” Marek said. “Need to remove a branch and check whether the board holds.”

“I’ll come with you,” Thomas said.

He said it too quickly.

He understood that himself.

His shoulders tensed slightly, as if he wanted to take the words back, but the words were already standing between him and the door.

“Why?” Marek asked.

“I’ll help.”

“There’s one branch.”

“Then I’ll look.”

Nick slowly turned his head toward Sofia.

“‘I’ll look,’ after recent events, is also a phrase of increased dampness.”

“Are you going?” Sofia asked.

“Me? No. I am a man of the stove, the table, and cautious scepticism.”

Outside the window, a drop broke from the roof and struck the metal bucket by the porch.

The sound was short, sharp.

Nick sighed.

“All right. I’m going. Only so no one starts finding ancient signposts without counsel for the defence.”

Nora set the mug on the table.

“I’m going too.”

“Naturally,” Nick said. “Without you the forest might say something strange, and we would not know that it was strange.”

Nora looked at him.

“The forest is not obliged to speak.”

“That is exactly what I’m afraid of.”

Ivar sat by the stove, shaving thin kindling, although the fire was already burning. Small pale curls fell onto his knee. He did not raise his head.

“You’re not going?” David asked.

“I am,” Ivar said.

“With us?”

“Behind.”

For some reason this did not sound like an answer about distance.

Nick fastened his jacket.

“Behind is good. There is less responsibility for direction there.”

Marek took a second rope from the hook and tossed it to Nick.

“Then carry this.”

“I have just been philosophically defending the rear.”

“Defend it with rope.”

A few minutes later they went out.

The rain had almost ended, but the forest did not yet know this. It fell from the branches more often than from the sky. Water gathered on needles, grew heavier, broke loose, struck grass, jackets, faces. The air was thick, damp, full of the smell of soaked earth, wet bark, and mushrooms that had not yet appeared but were already thinking about it.

The porch creaked underfoot.

By the steps the earth had softened. Tracks were printed there — their own, morning, daytime, fresh, old, laid over one another. Someone’s heel. Someone’s broad tread. Marek’s track, deep, sure. Nora’s small track, almost immediately filled with water. Beside it a smeared strip where someone had slipped.

Thomas stopped.

“Nothing can be made out here anymore,” he said.

“A good beginning,” Nick observed.

David crouched.

“No, some can. This is Marek. This is probably Sofia. This is mine.”

“You recognize your own track?” Nora asked.

“By the sole.”

“And if the sole lies?”

David raised his head.

“A sole does not lie.”

“It simply leaves what it can.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“Put it on record: suspicion has now fallen on footwear.”

Marek was already walking toward the path.

“If you’re done with the mud, the boards are still waiting.”

They followed him.

Sofia did not walk immediately behind Marek, but closer to the middle. Ahead were Thomas and David, almost side by side, though in the morning they had held themselves differently. Nick walked on her right with the rope over his shoulder and pretended it weighed more than it did. Nora walked a little behind Sofia. Ivar really did walk behind. His steps could hardly be heard.

The path had changed after the rain.

In the morning it had been damp. Now it had become a recording surface.

Every foot left an imprint. Every stone shone. Every root protruding from the earth had a dark side and a pale one, where water had already drained off. The moss had swollen, lifted, softened, and because of that seemed not a covering of the earth, but its skin. Where the grass was pressed down, water lay in narrow tracks like thin glass.

Sofia tried to look ahead.

It went badly.

Her gaze lowered by itself.

A trace.

A puddle.

A broken branch.

A dark spot in the moss.

Sand shifted by the toe of a boot.

All of it could simply be a consequence of rain.

And now all of it looked as if it might be pointing.

That made her ashamed.

Not because she believed in indications. No. Precisely because she did not want to believe and still saw how something inside was searching for the form of direction.

“Is there an old path there?” Thomas asked.

He pointed left, where between the firs there really was a narrow dark strip without tall grass.

Marek did not turn around.

“All paths here are old.”

“Where does it lead?”

“Into the forest.”

“I’m serious.”

Marek stopped.

“So am I. People went for firewood. For mushrooms. To the water. Just like that. Moose walk there too. People walk. Then grass does not stand up at once. That’s all.”

“But if something was being sought,” Thomas said, “it could have left paths like that too.”

Nick stopped adjusting the rope.

Sofia felt Thomas’s words settle onto the wet air.

Not loudly.

But heavily.

Marek did not answer at once.

“It could.”

And went on.

That turned out to be enough.

Not confirmation.

But not prohibition either.

And to a person who has already begun to search, sometimes it is enough that he was not forbidden.

The boards were lower down, behind the firs, where the path crossed a wide wet place. In spring, perhaps, there was water here. Now — black earth, moss, sedge, hummocks, and several boards laid crosswise. One board had slipped sideways. On it lay a branch, thick, wet, with bark stripped from one end.

Marek went down first, set his foot on the nearest hummock, tested the board.

“It holds, but it’s slippery. Nick, the rope.”

Nick handed it over.

“I still maintain that this is not my spiritual function.”

“It’s a rope.”

“Exactly. It is already working too literally.”

Sofia stood at the edge of the wet place and looked down.

In the mud beside the board was a track.

Not human.

Split, deep, with water inside. Beside it a second, a little ahead. Then another, already less clear. The tracks went toward the firs, where the grass was pressed down.

“Moose?” she asked.

Marek looked.

“Yes.”

David crouched.

“Big.”

“Enough.”

Thomas bent down too.

“It went there.”

“It did not write a note,” Nick said.

“I’m just saying.”

“No, you are already not just saying.”

Thomas straightened.

“And you are already afraid of every direction.”

Nick smiled.

“At last you’ve begun to notice.”

Nora stood beside Sofia and looked not at the track, but where it went.

“A track does not know how it will be read,” she said.

“Whom is that accusation for right now?” Nick asked.

“No one.”

“Then everyone.”

Marek lifted the branch from the board and dragged it aside. The branch left a dirty stripe on the wet wood. The board was clear now, but there was a new trace on it — not of a foot, not of a hoof, but of the movement of a hand.

Ivar came up to the moose track and looked.

“A track says someone passed,” he said. “Not what he found.”

He said it so quietly that the phrase had to be picked up from the air rather than heard.

Thomas did not answer.

Nor did David.

But Sofia saw that the phrase had not closed their question.

Only made it more cautious.

They crossed the boards one by one. The board bent, slipped, water squelched beneath it. Nick slipped on the second board, waved his arms, and held on.

“Everything is under control,” he said, clutching a branch.

“You almost fell,” Sofia said.

“Control sometimes looks dramatic.”

Beyond the boards the path rose to firmer ground, but after the rain there was no dry ground anywhere. The earth simply became harder. The forest here was older. The firs stood more densely, the trunks darkened, the lower branches were dry, grey, like hands that had long since stopped asking for anything. Between the trees there were large stones.

On one of them grew a thin green strip of moss, pale, almost luminous from water. It ran along a crack from top to bottom, as if someone had drawn a green finger down the grey stone.

David stopped by the stone.

“Look.”

Everyone stopped too quickly.

And that was unpleasant.

David felt it himself and became embarrassed.

“I don’t mean it that way. The moss is just strange.”

Thomas came closer.

“It looks like a sign.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“It has begun.”

“I said looks like.”

“That’s the first stage.”

Sofia looked at the moss.

It was simply moss.

Very beautiful, wet, alive, stubborn. It had found the crack because water, dust, shade, possibility lingered there. It was not obliged to mean more.

But now, after the empty bowl, after the box, after the word “thing,” a crack in a stone could no longer be only a place for moss. One wanted to read it.

Sofia felt that desire not as a thought, but as the tilt of her head.

The body itself was searching for the direction of the line.

From top to bottom.

Or from bottom to top.

Where does it lead?

A foolish question.

A dangerous question.

“Don’t touch it,” Nora said.

Thomas withdrew his hand.

He had not even noticed that he had wanted to touch it.

“I wasn’t going to.”

“You were.”

Nick snorted.

“Today everyone knows very well who was going to do what.”

“Hands before heads,” David said, and understood himself where it had come from.

Ivar stood a little farther off, by a fir, and was looking not at the stone, but at them.

Marek went on.

“If we study every wet moss, we’ll rot here before supper.”

“Evidence,” Thomas said quietly.

Sofia heard.

He had not wanted anyone to hear.

But the word had already come out.

Evidence.

Not even a sign.

Not a trace.

Evidence.

The world had begun turning not simply into a map, but into a case.

They went on along a low ridge of stones. In one place there was an old notch on a birch. Shallow, darkened, almost overgrown. Perhaps from an axe. Perhaps from a broken branch. Perhaps someone had indeed once marked the tree in order not to lose the way.

David saw it first, but this time only stopped and looked at Marek.

Marek sighed.

“Yes, old.”

“Who made it?”

“A person. Or a branch. Or a person with a branch. Enough?”

Nick smiled with one corner of his mouth.

“I love precision without use.”

David came closer, but did not touch it.

“It is on this side.”

“The tree does not care from which side you want to understand,” Marek said.

David fell silent.

Sofia felt sorry for him.

Because he was not ridiculous. He was honest in his desire for the world to answer clearly. If there is a notch, someone marked it. If someone marked it, it was for a reason. If for a reason — then possibly a path. If a path — then not everything dissolves into wet moss and accident.

Accident was heavy.

A trace was lighter.

A trace at least pretends that someone has already passed through uncertainty before you.

Nora stood by another tree, a little to the side.

“There is one here too,” she said.

Everyone turned.

There really was a mark on the bark. But it was not a notch. Only a place where the bark had peeled and pale wood showed underneath. The spot was elongated, uneven, like the trace of a finger.

“That’s bark,” Marek said.

“Then why did you say it?”

Nora looked at the pale spot.

“So it would be visible how easy it is to be mistaken.”

Nick said quietly:

“That was unpleasantly useful.”

At that moment the forest changed.

Not sharply.

The world did not collapse, did not shine, did not open a secret passage between the firs. Only the sound of the drops became different. They were no longer falling separately. They seemed to begin counting something in different places at once.

A drop from a branch.

A drop on stone.

A drop into an old moose track.

A drop on the notch.

A drop in the moss.

Sofia felt she was standing on the same path, but the path had become thinner.

As if another one had begun to show through beneath it.

Snow came without cold.

At first only white emptiness between the trees. Then a low sky. Then people walking not through forest, but along tracks.

They walked slowly.

Not because they were tired, though they were tired. Because each step had to be checked. Snow hid the earth, but left everything that had disturbed its surface. A bird’s track. A hare’s track. A human track. The stripe from a drag. A place where someone had fallen to a knee. Ash by an old fire, barely dusted over. Dark water in a hollow where the snow for some reason had melted faster.

“It is warmer here,” one said.

He was kneeling and holding his palm above a dark patch of earth.

“Then we are close,” another answered.

“Or simply water underneath.”

“Water is not simple either.”

A third man, old, with his cheek wrapped in cloth, poked the snow with his staff.

“You now make everything close.”

“And you make everything empty,” the young one said.

“Empty is more honest.”

The young one did not answer.

He was already looking farther on.

There, behind the bushes, where the snow was thinner, green grass could be seen. Not much. Three narrow stems pressed to the ground, but alive.

“Do you see?”

People came closer.

The grass was real.

It lived because a warm stream ran under the snow. The stream was small, almost hidden. It flowed under ice, under roots, under wet earth, and did not know it had become proof.

“Here,” someone said.

“No,” said the woman with the empty bowl. “It is only water.”

“Only?”

“I said what I see.”

“You don’t want to find it.”

“I want to eat.”

“Then look more carefully.”

She looked.

More carefully.

And that made it worse.

Because when a person looks too hungrily, any living stem begins to look like a signpost.

They went on.

Where the snow was thinner.

Where the stone sounded hollow if struck with an axe.

Where the water did not freeze by the shore.

Where an old man swore he had seen light under a root.

Where someone found an old boot track leading toward the hill.

The track was not theirs.

That made it more important.

“Someone went before us,” the young man said.

“Everyone before us was going somewhere,” the woman answered.

“No. Here.”

He stood over the track as over a word.

The track was deep, old; its edges had crumbled, snow lay inside it. It could have been from yesterday. It could have been a week old. It could have been left by a man who was looking for a goat, had lost a knife, was walking home, was walking away from home, was going anywhere at all. But when for many days people had already been saying “it,” another person’s track became almost a confession.

“Then he knew,” someone said.

“Or did not know.”

“But he walked.”

And that turned out to be enough.

They followed the track.

The track ended at a stream.

It led neither to a stone bowl, nor to a box, nor to an iron heart under the earth. It simply went into water and disappeared.

People stood by the stream, and no one wanted to be the first to say that was all.

Then the young man bent down, touched the water, and said:

“He deliberately walked through the stream.”

“Who?”

“That one.”

“Why?”

“To hide the track.”

The woman with the bowl closed her eyes.

Because now even disappearance was working for the search.

What did not lead farther began to seem hidden.

What was silent seemed to know more than anyone.

Sofia felt cold in her fingers.

Not from snow.

From that thought.

She was standing at once in the forest after rain and by the winter stream, where people looked at the place where the track had gone and could not leave themselves.

Then one of the ancient people lifted a wet branch from the bank.

There were three scratches on the branch.

Entirely accidental. From stone. From ice. From the teeth of an animal. From anything.

“Look,” he said.

And everyone looked.

The modern forest returned with a jerk.

Thomas stood by the birch with the notch.

David had already raised his phone, but had not managed to take the picture. On the screen the notch almost disappeared into the wet bark.

“It’s hard to see,” he said.

“Good,” Nick replied.

David lowered the phone.

“Why?”

“Maybe the photo doesn’t want to participate.”

David looked at the screen once more and put the phone away.

He did not delete it.

But he did not take a second photograph either.

Marek called them farther on. The boards had been checked, the branch removed, but he decided to go a little farther to the old turn in the path, to make sure the water had not undercut the edge. Nick protested that the task was expanding during execution, but went.

The path here grew narrower.

The forest compressed around them. Underfoot were roots, moss, stones. Sometimes the path disappeared, and Marek walked not by a visible way, but by memory. This irritated David.

“How do you know where the path is?” he asked.

“With my feet.”

“Seriously.”

“I am serious.”

“But if it isn’t visible?”

Marek stopped.

“Then don’t run.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“Unexpected, but useful.”

Marek went on.

David now looked underfoot even more attentively.

Sofia noticed Thomas did too. They were both looking for the path, but not in the same way. David was looking for signs of the right way: pressed grass, mud, old tracks, a gap between trees. Thomas was looking for something that would confirm not the path, but the possibility. The possibility that the forest knows how to hide what a person has decided to find.

Nick tried to look to the sides.

Not down.

It was noticeable.

He seemed to be resisting the sickness of the gaze. He lifted his eyes to the trunks, to the sky between branches, to drops hanging on the needles. But still, from time to time, his gaze fell to the earth, to a trace, to a root, to a strange curve of moss.

Sofia saw this.

And because of that Nick felt closer to her.

Not because he was stronger than the others.

But because he too was being infected and hated it.

At the old turn in the path lay several large stones in a half-circle. Or almost a half-circle. Perhaps someone had once placed them there. Perhaps they had come out of the earth that way. Moss covered their edges, roots had grown over them, and the rain had made their surfaces dark.

The half-circle was too convenient for the eye.

Thomas stopped first.

Then David.

Then Sofia.

Marek swore quietly.

“Just stones,” he said.

“You don’t yet know what we’ll ask,” Nick said.

“I know.”

David walked around the half-circle, but without his earlier heat.

“Did people sit here?”

“Maybe,” Marek said.

“People?”

“Or stones.”

“Marek.”

“People used to stack firewood here. Maybe they sat. Maybe the earth simply pushed them out. I don’t know. And I’m fine with that.”

Nora crouched by one stone and ran a finger over the moss.

“If you lie long enough, everything around you will begin to look like intention too.”

“Is that about the stone?” Nick asked.

“For now, yes.”

The stones gave nothing more.

Did not change position. Did not open a passage. Did not arrange themselves into a sign. One was higher than the others only because the earth beneath it had risen. Or because it had been placed there. Or because this was again a question without end.

Marek turned them back.

“That’s it. The board holds. The branch is removed. The stones are wet. The house is there.”

On the way back the forest seemed different.

Not because the forest had changed.

Because people had already had time to pass through it with a question.

Sofia noticed that David kept turning around now and then. Not obviously. He simply held his gaze on the notch, on the line of moss, on the place where the path went left. Thomas walked more lightly than before. Not more cheerfully. More lightly. Like a person who had been given nothing, but had been allowed to think he had not been wrong to begin searching.

Nick was silent.

This was louder than his talking.

On the boards he slipped again, but this time did not joke. He simply held on, looked at the moose track in which muddy water already stood, and went on.

Sofia stopped for a second.

The moose track was spreading.

Its edges had collapsed, the water had risen, the form was being lost. A little more — and the track would become simply a small puddle.

For some reason she felt lighter.

Then heavier.

Because if a trace disappears, a person who needs it does not necessarily calm down.

She caught up with the others.

At the house Marek was first up onto the porch and set the hatchet by the wall. Nick hung the rope on a hook, too carefully. David took off his boots and immediately looked at the soles. Thomas lingered by the steps, turned back toward the forest.

The forest stood wet, dark, motionless.

It showed nothing.

Nick noticed Thomas’s look.

“Don’t look at it like that,” he said.

“Like what?”

“As if it owes you.”

Thomas did not answer.

They went in.

The house took them back with the smell of the stove, wet clothes, and bread. The bowls still stood on the table. The towel Eva had used to cover the empty bowl now lay beside it, folded in half. The bowl was uncovered.

Empty.

Ordinary.

Sofia looked at it and suddenly understood: after the forest, the empty bowl had become still more dangerous. Not because there was something in it. Because now emptiness could have a route.

David sat at the table.

Thomas stood by the window.

“We didn’t find anything,” he said.

Marek took off his jacket.

“Because we weren’t looking.”

Thomas turned.

“Were we not?”

Marek looked at him for a long time.

“I wasn’t.”

That was enough for the room to become quiet.

Nora came to the table, took her mug, looked inside, and set it back.

“A trace can be found where it never was,” she said.

Nick sat down on the bench.

“That’s it. I officially want tea.”

“Will that help?” Sofia asked.

“No. But at least a cup is honestly small.”

Ivar entered last.

He took off his wet jacket, hung it on the hook — the same one that had creaked under Sofia’s jacket in the morning. Then he went to the stove and added a log.

“The forest showed nothing,” Sofia said quietly.

She herself did not know to whom.

Ivar heard.

“The forest rarely does that.”

“Then why does it seem that it could?”

He looked at her.

Not the way one looks at a student.

The way one looks at a person already standing inside her own difficulty, and better not interfered with.

“Because you already know what you want to see.”

Sofia lowered her eyes.

On the table lay bread crumbs.

One crumb was at the edge of the bowl. Very small, dry, irregular in shape. If one looked at it long enough, one could decide it resembled a stone. Or an island. Or a sign.

She passed her palm over the table and swept the crumb onto the floor.

Not sharply.

Simply so that the crumb could become a crumb again.

Outside the window, the last heavy drop broke from a branch.

Struck the earth.

Left no trace that could be read.

And yet Thomas stood by the window more lightly than he had gone out.

David was silent as if checking a route inside himself.

Nick drank tea without a joke.

Nora held the mug by the handle, but did not lift it.

And Sofia understood: they had returned without a branch, without a stone, without a sign, without an answer.

The forest had shown them nothing.

But they had already managed to decide that it could.
