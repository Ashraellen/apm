# Chapter Fifteen. Condition

By morning one of the labels had come unstuck.

Not completely.

Only the edge.

The white tape on the glass of the jar had lifted on one side and now stuck out as a small, stubborn tongue. One could still see on it: STARTER. DO NOT THROW. The words held. The glue did not.

Sofia noticed it before the others.

She came into the kitchen when the house had not yet gathered itself into the day. Behind the wall someone coughed. The stove was dark. By the window there was damp silence, and on the table, as yesterday, lay David’s list: ASK BEFORE THROWING. Overnight the sheet had bent slightly at the edges. Not dramatically. Paper had simply remembered that the air was wet.

The jar stood by the stove.

In the new place that yesterday had seemed right.

Not too close.

Not too far.

That was what they had thought yesterday.

Today the cloth on top was dry and stiff. One edge had lifted. On the surface of the mass inside, a thin matte crust had appeared, pale, not frightening, but unpleasantly motionless. Under it, perhaps, something was still moving. Or was not moving. One could not tell by looking.

The labels whitened on the glass.

The English one.

The Finnish one.

ASK BEFORE THROWING on the shelf.

The date.

Everything was labelled.

And still something was wrong.

Sofia did not touch the jar.

After yesterday’s loss, the hand had become more cautious.

Not wiser. Simply more cautious.

She stood and watched the small edge of tape slowly come away from the glass. The movement was almost invisible, but there was an unpleasant persistence in it. The label did not argue. Did not fall. Did not disappear. It simply showed that it too held only under conditions.

David appeared behind her.

“What?” he asked.

Sofia nodded toward the jar.

He came closer.

First he looked at the tape.

Then at the cloth.

Then at the mass under the cloth.

The order of inspection was precise and wrong at the same time.

“It’s peeling off,” he said.

“Yes.”

He carefully pressed the edge of the tape with his finger.

The tape stuck back down.

A second later it lifted again.

David tried once more. Longer. More slowly. Let go.

The tape came away again.

“We need proper labels,” he said.

Sofia did not answer.

He heard himself and added:

“Not only labels. It’s just... the glue won’t hold because of the heat.”

“Or because of the damp.”

“Or because of the glass.”

“Or because not everything is obliged to hold just because we stuck it on.”

He looked at her.

He was not offended.

“That is also possible.”

Thomas came in almost immediately after him, as if the word “labels” had called him from another room.

In his hands were a mug and a pencil.

“What happened?”

“Nothing,” David said.

“You say that when something small but unpleasant has happened.”

Nick said from the corridor:

“That is because David is more humane than most alert systems.”

“The label is peeling off,” Sofia said.

Thomas set the mug on the table.

“I said we should write directly on the glass.”

“You said that in your head,” Nick said, coming in after him.

“Because yesterday everyone was already against the expansion of the system.”

“Not everyone. I was against the birth of the system as a species.”

“Nick.”

“Yes, fine, silent.”

Thomas bent toward the jar.

“And the cloth is dry.”

He said that more quietly.

Lina entered without a sound.

Or perhaps they simply did not hear her behind their own words.

She looked at the jar, then at the stove, then at the window. Not at the labels. At the place.

“It is too warm here,” she said.

Thomas straightened.

“But yesterday it was good here.”

“Yesterday was yesterday.”

The phrase lay in the kitchen flat and dry.

Not like wisdom.

Like temperature.

Lina removed the cloth. Smelled it. Did not grimace. Ran the spoon over the surface. The crust cracked; underneath, the mass was wetter, but denser than it should have been. She added a few drops of water, stirred it, smelled it again.

“Is it alive?” Thomas asked.

“For now.”

“What does ‘for now’ mean?”

“It means it hasn’t died.”

“But bad?”

“It isn’t right for it.”

“What exactly?”

Lina lifted the jar.

“The place.”

“But on it...”

He stopped.

Nick said softly:

“Don’t say ‘but it says on it.’ The day has only just begun.”

Thomas pressed his lips together.

“I wanted to say: we protected it.”

“From a hand,” Lina said. “Not from heat.”

She moved the jar from the old shelf to a low table by the side wall, farther from the stove and closer to the window, but not in a draught. Then she took the cloth, dampened it a little, wrung it out, and covered the jar again.

The labels remained on the glass.

But the jar had to be moved.

This turned out to be more important than Sofia had expected.

The sign kept saying: do not throw away.

The living thing said: it is not right for me here.

And those two speeches did not coincide.

David went over to the list by the door.

“We need to add an item.”

Nick sighed, but this time did not say “no.”

“Which one?” Lina asked.

“Check the place. Not only don’t throw it away.”

“Write it shorter.”

David took the pencil.

Thought.

Wrote:

CHECK PLACE.

Then in Russian:

Check the place.

“And temperature,” Thomas said.

David looked at him.

“We can.”

“Not ‘we can.’ We need to. If it’s too warm, we need to know how much. If it’s too cold, too. And humidity. And exactly where it stands. And how many times a day to check.”

Nick raised his hand.

“I hear the list putting on a uniform.”

Thomas turned sharply.

“And are you suggesting we just guess?”

“No.”

“Then what?”

“I suggest we not pretend that if we write everything down, it will become obedient.”

“I don’t want it to be obedient.”

“You do.”

Thomas fell silent.

Because Nick said it without mockery.

And therefore more precisely.

Lina set the spoon on the edge of the plate.

“He wants not to lose it.”

“Yes,” Thomas said too quickly. “Yes. Exactly.”

“That is not the same thing.”

“What?”

“Not losing it and forcing it to live.”

The silence became denser.

David slowly set the pencil down.

Lina did not develop the phrase. Did not begin to explain. She simply moved the jar a few more centimeters, as if the conversation was important, but the jar’s place was still more important.

Sofia suddenly noticed a circle on the wooden shelf by the stove.

Where the jar had stood during the night.

Not a fresh mark. An old one. Darker than the rest of the wood. An almost correct circle, not from this jar — too wide. Perhaps from a pot. Perhaps from another vessel. Perhaps from many vessels over many years. Around the circle the wood was slightly worn, as if long ago someone had put something warm there, taken it away, put it back again, waited, forgotten, checked, lost.

Sofia ran her finger along the edge of the circle.

The wood was smooth.

“What’s there?” David asked.

“A trace.”

“From the jar?”

“No.”

Lina looked, but did not come closer.

Marek, who had come in with firewood, glanced at the shelf.

“It was always there.”

“What?” Thomas asked.

“The circle.”

“From what?”

Marek shrugged.

“From what was put there.”

“Who?”

“People.”

Nick said quietly:

“The most irritating and precise answer.”

Sofia was still looking at the circle.

She felt uneasy.

Not frightened.

The way it happens when one sees a trace that should mean nothing, but for some reason means more than it is ready to say.

The old circle on the shelf was not a sign. Not a label. No one had written on it. It had simply remained from conditions: heat, vessel, time, repetition, a human hand. The trace did not explain what had stood on it. It only said: something was already held here.

And that was almost a phrase.

Almost.

“I’ll go out,” Sofia said.

“Running away again?” Nick asked.

“Yes.”

“I approve.”

Nora rose from the window.

“I’ll come with you.”

Nick looked at them, then at the jar, at the list, at David with the pencil, at Thomas, who still clearly wanted to pronounce the word “thermometer.”

“Me too. Before I’m entered into the humidity-check schedule.”

“You’ll be entered separately,” Marek said.

“In what section?”

“Do not feed after midnight.”

Nick looked at him with respect.

“A rare strong blow, Marek.”

They went out through the back door.

The porch was damp. There was no sun, but the rain had stepped back, and the light had become more even, wider. The air smelled of water, moss, stove smoke, and cold bark. After the kitchen with its tapes, lists, and attempts not to lose everything at once, the yard seemed almost simple. Though it was not simple. It simply demanded nothing from them in the language of bullet points.

Nora was the first to go toward the water.

Not to the lake yet, but to the narrow path that went down between wet bushes. Nick walked behind and kept checking where he put his feet.

“The forest is clearly lacking warnings,” he said.

“You already said that,” Sofia said.

“And I will keep saying it until roots start signing themselves as threats.”

Nora stopped by a low stone almost completely covered in moss.

“Look.”

Sofia came closer.

There was a circle on the stone.

Not like the one on the shelf. Not wooden, not darkened by heat. Here the moss grew around a bare patch, almost round, a little uneven. In the center the stone was open, grey, wet. As if something had lain or stood there for a long time, not letting the moss cover the surface. Then it had been removed, and the moss had not yet returned.

“From what?” Nick asked.

Nora squatted.

“I don’t know.”

“I am beginning to value honesty as a separate kind of navigation.”

Sofia looked at the circle.

Inside, it grew quiet.

Not empty.

Quiet.

A wooden shelf. A glass jar. A stone in the moss. A circle. A place where something had stood. A condition that had left a trace but had not explained itself.

“Strange,” Nora said.

“What?” Sofia asked.

Nora did not answer at once. She was looking at the circle in the moss.

“We seemed to be going to Ivar.”

Nick said quietly:

“I was going for strange stories and free psychological discomfort.”

Nora did not smile.

“And did we arrive?” Sofia asked.

“I don’t know.”

She stood.

“Sometimes it seems we arrived at ourselves. And Ivar is simply holding the place so we don’t scatter too early.”

Nick stopped fussing with the branch under his foot.

Sofia looked at Nora.

She herself seemed to regret having said it too exactly.

“I don’t know,” she added.

“You do,” Nick said. “It’s just inconvenient for you.”

“Possibly.”

Sofia shifted her gaze to the water between the trees. A narrow strip of lake was grey and smooth, without shine. No revelation. No music. Just water that had stood there before their arrival and would stand after.

Ivar was not leading them.

The thought came not as a conclusion.

As the position of an object in a room.

He was not walking ahead with an answer. Not pulling. Not persuading. Not closing the door if they wanted to leave. He seemed to be holding the place long enough for them not to run away from what rose in them by itself, like dough in a too-warm jar — wrongly, with a crust, but still alive.

Sofia did not say that.

It would have been too smooth.

They went down closer to the water.

By the shore lay old stones, flat, wet, grey. On one of them was a dark circle, almost invisible. Not moss. Not a trace from a vessel, probably. Perhaps from a fire. Perhaps from a wet bucket. Perhaps from anything.

The circles had begun appearing too often.

That was exactly why Sofia stopped trusting them.

When a sign repeats, a person too quickly wants to make a system out of it.

She bent and touched the stone. Cold entered her fingers.

And then the glint came.

Not as an image at once.

First as a smell.

Dry heat.

Smoke.

Sour mass under cloth.

A clay vessel with a rough mark on its side. Not a written sign yet — a cut, a scratch, so it would not be confused. The vessel stands by the hearth. Closer than yesterday. Because yesterday it was cold, and the man with the notches decided that nearer the fire would be safer.

The mark is intact.

No one has touched the vessel.

No one has thrown it away.

No one has confused it.

The older woman removes the cloth.

Inside, a dry crust.

A crack.

A warm sour smell in which there is no longer movement.

The man says:

“I put it there so it would be protected.”

The woman is silent for a long time.

She is not angry.

That is worse.

Then she says:

“Fire does not read.”

He looks at the mark.

At the scratch.

At the vessel.

At the hearth.

And for the first time understands that he preserved the sign.

Not life.

The glint disappeared so quietly that Sofia did not understand for a while that she was hearing the water again.

Nick stood beside her.

“You went somewhere again,” he said.

“Yes.”

“Was it better there?”

“No.”

“Comfortingly stable.”

Nora was looking not at Sofia, but at the stone.

“Fire can’t read,” she said.

Sofia turned sharply.

Nora raised her eyes.

“What?”

“What did you say?”

“Fire can’t read.”

“Why?”

Nora shrugged.

“They moved the jar, didn’t they.”

Nick looked at Sofia, then at Nora.

“I officially do not participate in this coincidence.”

“You heard it,” Sofia said.

“I heard it. But I do not participate. Those are different levels of responsibility.”

Nora looked at both of them.

“What is wrong?”

Sofia wanted to say: I just saw that.

She did not say it.

Because the moment she said it, the coincidence would become an object. They would start considering it, checking it, labelling it. Perhaps even making a separate list: “phrases that leaked through.”

She simply said:

“Nothing.”

Nora did not believe her.

And did not press.

That was good.

They stood a little longer by the water. Then Nick got cold first, although he claimed it was not he who was cold, but his “social shell.” Nora went up the path silently. Sofia walked last and kept thinking how little it takes to confuse care with the right place.

Put it closer to the fire.

Put it farther away.

Cover.

Open.

Warm.

Do not overheat.

Dampen.

Do not flood.

Feed.

Do not crush by feeding.

Everything living was not only “do not throw away.”

It was “holding it in the right condition.”

And that was far more frightening than a label.

When they returned, the kitchen had become quieter.

Thomas had still taken out his phone and was trying to understand what the temperature was in the room. The connection was weak, the app worked badly, the phone confidently showed numbers with an accuracy the kitchen did not deserve. David stood nearby and did not write anything down. That was noticeable.

Lina sat at the table, cutting bread.

The jar no longer stood by the stove, but on a small shelf near the side wall. The labels remained on the glass. The edge of the English tape was again coming away slightly, but less. The cloth on top was damper. Under it the mass looked almost the same as in the morning, but for some reason Sofia felt that now it was no longer cramped.

That was a strange word.

Not cramped.

As if the jar had become easier to breathe.

“We found a circle,” Nick said, taking off his jacket.

Marek looked at him.

“Where?”

“In the moss. Then on a stone. I am not claiming this is a conspiracy of circles, but if they start appearing in the soup, I’m leaving.”

“You won’t leave,” Eva said.

“Yes, but I will loudly declare it.”

Nora went to the jar.

Looked at it.

“Better.”

Lina raised her eyes.

“Yes.”

“Why?” Thomas asked.

Lina answered:

“Because it is better for it here.”

“By what signs?”

She looked at him.

“By the ones you have not written down yet.”

Nick said quietly:

“Painful, but fair.”

Thomas was not offended.

He had grown tired of being offended by what did not fit into the method.

“I want to learn,” he said.

Lina stopped cutting bread.

“Then look.”

“I am looking.”

“Longer.”

He nodded.

Not like a student.

Like a person who, at last, had been offered not an answer, but labor.

Ivar came in closer to evening.

This time he had not been part of the morning argument. Perhaps deliberately. Perhaps he had simply been doing something else. He brought several wet logs, set them by the wall, took off his jacket, and only then looked at the new shelf.

“Moved it,” he said.

“It was too warm here,” Lina answered.

Ivar nodded.

“Yes.”

Thomas turned to him almost quickly.

“You knew?”

“No.”

“But you said ‘yes.’”

“Now I see.”

Thomas opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Nick whispered:

“Right now, the court of appeal inside him is collapsing.”

Sofia barely smiled.

Ivar went to the jar. Did not touch it. Only looked.

“The label remained,” David said.

“Good.”

“But the place had to be changed.”

“Yes.”

“Should this be written down?”

Ivar looked at him, not sternly.

“If you write it down, still look tomorrow.”

David nodded.

“Yes.”

And that turned out to be enough.

In the evening they ate bread without the new starter.

Old bread. What was still there. They cut it thinner than before. Not from economy, although from that too. After the last days, a slice had stopped being just a slice. It had become something that requires continuation but does not promise it.

Thomas held his piece for a long time before eating it.

“What?” Nick asked.

“Thinking.”

“Dangerous.”

“Yes.”

“About what?”

Thomas looked at the jar by the wall.

“That ‘do not throw away’ turned out to be the simplest part.”

Nick did not answer.

Because it was true.

Later, when it was already dark, Sofia came again to the shelf. Not to the old one by the stove, but to the new one. The jar stood quietly. The labels on the glass looked a little ridiculous, a little touching. White pieces of tape on the living mass behind the glass. Words that protected from one human mistake, but not from heat, not from dryness, not from time, not from inattention.

Nearby, on the old shelf by the stove, the circle darkened.

Nick would have said: “the scene of the crime.”

But it was not the scene of a crime.

More like a place of learning that did not ask for consent.

Sofia looked at the circle, then at the jar.

And it seemed to her that between them lay not two steps, but a whole human attempt to understand that the living thing cannot simply be left under the right name.

It has to stand in the right world.

At least a small one.

At least on a shelf.

At least for today.

The label remained on the glass.

The jar already stood in another place.
