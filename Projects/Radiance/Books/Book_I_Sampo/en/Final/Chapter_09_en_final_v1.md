# Chapter Nine. Return

In the evening the house fed them too diligently.

Not because anyone wanted a celebration. There was no celebration. After the hollow, celebration would have looked foolish, like a clean tablecloth on a bog. But the stove was working, water boiled, knives knocked on boards, Marek brought a sack of potatoes from the pantry, Eva found dried mushrooms, Lina set a jar of berries on the table, and all of it gradually assembled itself into dinner.

Not festive.

Not cosy.

Rather obligatory.

As if the house had said: you have returned — eat. The body has the right to demand its own, even when the head is busy with the map, the hollow, wet moss, and what had not become an answer.

The kitchen smelled of mushroom soup, stove, wet wool, drying socks, earth under boots, and the slightly sour berry infusion Marek produced without announcement. The bottle was dark, without a label. He set it near the edge of the table as if it were not a drink, but another tool for a northern evening.

“Is that medicine?” Nick asked.

Marek poured himself very little.

“No.”

“Astonishing honesty. Usually people say ‘for warming up.’”

“If you need to get warm, sit closer to the stove.”

“And if one needs to forget the hollow?”

“It won’t help.”

“Then why?”

Marek lifted one shoulder.

“Taste.”

Nick looked at the bottle with respect.

“That is almost more frightening than medicine.”

David sat at the table and held his spoon too evenly.

Sofia did not notice it at once. First she was looking at his boots by the door. On the newspaper beneath them, dark damp had spread out. Earth was falling from the soles in heavy lumps, looking like something that had not wanted to leave the forest but had already been carried into the house. Then she saw the notebook. David had placed it beside him, but he was not opening it. The grey cover had dried almost completely. One corner was still slightly warped from moisture.

The map was back in the house.

So were they.

Only the hollow had remained outside.

And that was not entirely true.

Nora had hung her jacket closer to the stove, but a dark stripe from wet bushes remained on the sleeve. Nick sniffed his palm several times, then angrily wiped it with a towel. Thomas stood by the window and looked into the dark behind which that same rise between the firs should have been. Marek came in with an armful of firewood and left several bits of bark on the floor. Sofia took off her socks and understood that her feet smelled of wet earth even after she had washed her hands.

The hollow did not enter the house like a guest.

It entered in pieces.

Nick sat opposite David.

“I propose we do not discuss the map today.”

David raised his eyes.

“I wasn’t going to.”

“That is exactly why I am proposing it in advance.”

“All right.”

“What do you mean, ‘all right’?”

“We won’t discuss it.”

Nick narrowed his eyes.

“You agreed too easily. Now I am more nervous.”

David tried to smile. It did not work. He took bread, broke off a piece, then put it back. Then took another, smaller one. Sofia noticed he was eating not because he was hungry. It was as if he were trying to place something dense inside himself, so that it would press down what would not close.

The mushroom soup was thick.

Not heavy in itself, but after the raw forest, cold feet, the wet hollow, and half a day spent trying not to think about the white place, it settled in the stomach almost like a decision. There were potatoes in it, mushrooms, onion, pepper, sour cream, something herbal that Lina had thrown in without asking. The bread was dense, from yesterday, but near the stove it had softened again. The butter no longer resisted the knife. It surrendered at once.

“There shouldn’t have been so many mushrooms,” Nick said, looking into his bowl.

Lina looked at him.

“They are normal mushrooms.”

“I do not doubt the mushrooms. I doubt the normality of everything else.”

“Eat your soup.”

“That sounds like an ancient northern sentence.”

Ivar sat at the edge of the table, eating slowly and silently. He did not look at David. Did not look at the notebook. Did not look out the window. He simply ate soup, like a man for whom food is neither symbol, nor lesson, nor occasion.

It irritated and calmed at the same time.

Thomas was the last to sit down.

“There really is a rise there,” he said, as if continuing a conversation no one had begun.

Nick lifted his spoon.

“I remind everyone: we signed a verbal agreement not to resume cartographic work.”

“I’m not talking about the map.”

“Of course. You are talking about the rise. The rise, presumably, came into your head on its own and requested political asylum.”

“Nick.”

“That’s it, I’m silent.”

David did not interfere.

That was unusual. That morning he would have said something calm, useful, clarifying. Now he ate soup and looked into his bowl as if there too there was a surface on which one could draw a line and make a mistake.

Sofia felt that she did not want to eat.

But she ate.

After the hollow, food seemed not gratitude, but an obligation to the body. It had walked over wet moss, kept its balance, grown cold, drawn in the smell of rotten wood, carried her back to the house. Now it demanded warmth, salt, bread. Without asking what she had understood.

That was even honest.

Nora took a very small sip of the infusion and immediately grimaced.

“Sour.”

“Berry,” Marek said.

“Berries can be angry too.”

Nick looked at her.

“Today I am entirely on the side of berries.”

David suddenly said:

“I didn’t want it to become a search.”

Nick lowered his spoon.

No one answered at once.

David was looking into his bowl. His voice was even, but the evenness held too tensely.

“I really wanted to close it. Just close it. So it wouldn’t drag farther.”

“Close it with what?” Eva asked.

“Verification.”

The word sounded bad.

Not because it was a lie. Because it was too neat for what they had brought on their boots.

“The verification did not close it,” Thomas said.

Nick turned to him.

“Thank you for your contribution. We noticed.”

David nodded.

“It didn’t.”

He took bread, dipped it in the soup, but did not bring it to his mouth. The piece of bread softened and began to crumble between his fingers.

“I thought if I saw the place...”

He stopped.

Put the bread back into the bowl.

“If you saw there was nothing there?” Sofia asked.

David looked at her.

“Yes.”

“But there was.”

“There was.”

“Just not that.”

He was silent.

Nick said quietly:

“That ‘just’ is the size of a house right now.”

Marek poured David water.

“Drink.”

“I’m not...”

“Drink.”

David took the glass. Drank half. Set it down too carefully.

“I thought if I corrected the map, it would become more honest.”

“It will,” Ivar said.

Everyone looked at him.

He was eating soup and did not raise his voice.

“More honest for the map.”

David frowned.

“And not for us?”

Ivar set down his spoon.

“Those are different works.”

Nick ran his palm over his face.

“I asked not to discuss the map. We lasted almost seven minutes. A record for the doomed.”

David took his spoon again. Ate two spoonfuls in a row, quickly, as if he wanted to break off the conversation physically. Then pushed the bowl away.

“I’m fine.”

Nick said nothing.

Sofia heard in those two words the same thing she had heard in the morning in “I am not looking for Sampo.” Too quickly. Too much in advance. Too much before the question.

“You’re pale,” Nora said.

“I’m tired.”

“That isn’t the same.”

“Nora.”

“What?”

“Don’t.”

She nodded and lowered her eyes.

Marek stood, took a small bowl of salt from the shelf, and set it closer to David. Why, it was unclear. Perhaps habit. Perhaps he simply wanted to do something without words.

Outside the windows it was growing dark. Not sharply, but slowly, like water into which ink is being added. The forest disappeared, but did not leave. The house became brighter inside, and because of that everything beyond the glass seemed still denser. On the hooks the wet jackets moved slightly from the heat. Beneath them water dripped into the iron basin: rarely, but stubbornly.

Drop.

Pause.

Drop.

Pause.

David listened to that sound.

Then said:

“It comes back anyway.”

“What?” Thomas asked.

David blinked, like a person who had not meant to speak aloud.

“Nothing.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

She shook her head slightly: not now.

But David had already continued.

“I thought if... if I corrected it. Not to find. Not to go farther. Just to correct it. So it would be right. But it still...”

He made an uncertain movement with his hand near his stomach.

“What still?” Thomas asked.

“I don’t know.”

David gave a short laugh — short and unpleasant to himself.

“Wrong way.”

“What went the wrong way?” Nick asked, without mockery now.

“Everything.”

He swallowed.

“The map is dry. But here...”

He touched his stomach again, then seemed immediately ashamed of the gesture and took his hand away.

Sofia felt the kitchen air change. Until then they could still pretend they were speaking about the map, the place, honesty, error. Now speech suddenly fell lower. Not into the depth of meaning. Into the body.

David closed his eyes.

“I shouldn’t have mixed it.”

“What?” Lina asked.

“I don’t know. Soup. Infusion. The hollow. Everything.”

Nick slowly pushed his own bowl away.

“David, are you sick?”

“No.”

His face said yes.

His hands said yes.

His shoulders said yes before everyone.

He tried to stand up neatly, but the bench creaked too loudly. The table trembled, a spoon rang against a plate. Thomas got up at once, but did not know what to do. Eva stood too. Nora was already at the door, taking a jacket from the hook — whose, it was unclear.

David took one step and stopped.

“Air,” he said.

The voice was not his.

Ivar rose.

Not quickly.

Not slowly.

He came over and placed his palm on David’s shoulder — not supporting him, but returning direction.

“First go outside.”

And nothing more.

Marek was already opening the door.

Cold came in at once.

Not frosty, not winter cold, but sharp after the stove and soup. Damp northern air smelling of wet wood, earth, dark grass, and the woodshed. David went out first, too quickly, but still trying to keep dignity. Marek followed him. Ivar remained at the door. Nick started after them, stopped, then went out after all. Sofia rose too.

“Not everyone,” Ivar said.

He did not order.

He said.

Sofia stopped at the threshold.

The door remained half-open.

Outside one could hear David crossing the wet porch boards. Then the steps went toward the woodshed, where the earth was darker and where no one usually stood without a reason. Then a short, broken sound.

Sofia turned away.

Not from disgust.

Out of respect for shame.

But the sound was in the house anyway.

It entered together with the cold and the sour smell, barely noticeable, but enough for everyone to stop being beautiful. Thomas stood with a white face, holding a napkin he had for some reason taken from the table. Nora was looking at the floor. Eva was pouring water into a mug. Lina was covering the pot with a lid, as if the soup too had to stop participating.

Nick said something quietly outside.

Not a joke.

Marek answered shortly.

Then came the sound of water from the bucket. Not a splash, rather a careful movement. The way people help a person without calling it help.

Sofia thought that probably the worst thing is not vomiting.

Worse is when someone nearby is forced to pretend he did not hear your body refuse to be yours.

The door opened wider. Nick came in first. His face was strange: gathered, almost angry, but without an address.

“Everything is fine,” he said.

Never had that phrase sounded so unconvincing and so necessary.

Eva held the mug out to him.

“Water.”

“Marek gave him some.”

“More.”

He took it.

David came in several minutes later.

Without his jacket. The jacket remained with Marek, who held it aside like a wet thing that, for now, needed not a place but time. David was grey, damp at the temples, lips pressed tight. He did not look at anyone. And fortunately, no one looked directly at him.

Except Ivar.

But Ivar did not look at him as if he were shame itself.

He looked at him as one looks at a person who has returned.

“Rinse,” Marek said, and gave him a glass.

David took it. His hand trembled very slightly. He noticed it, clenched the glass harder, and because of that the trembling became more visible.

Nick turned toward the window.

Thomas pretended to adjust his glasses.

Nora set a chair beside David.

“Sit.”

“I...”

“Sit.”

He sat.

There was no tenderness in it. And that helped more than tenderness would have.

David rinsed his mouth, spat into the bowl Marek held so ordinarily, as if this were just another kitchen task. Then he rinsed again. Set down the glass. Looked at his hands for a long time.

“Sorry,” he said.

The word fell onto the table worse than everything else.

Eva immediately said:

“Don’t.”

Marek, almost at the same time:

“For what?”

Nick was silent.

And that was right.

David raised his eyes only for a second.

“I didn’t mean to.”

“People usually don’t,” Marek said.

Nick coughed quietly, but did not release the joke.

Ivar sat back in his place. The soup had cooled. The bread lay sliced. David’s notebook remained by the plate. Pencil beside it. The map inside. Everything was in place, but the place had changed.

David looked at the notebook as if it were now not merely dry. Too dry.

“Put it away?” Sofia asked.

He understood what she meant.

Slowly shook his head.

“No.”

A pause.

“Just... not now.”

He closed the notebook himself and moved it away from him.

It was a small movement.

Not victory.

Not understanding.

Simply distance.

And sometimes distance is the only thing the body can do for a person.

The rest of dinner passed not quietly, but carefully.

Silence would have been too solemn. They did not know how, and should not have known how, to sit as if in a temple around someone else’s nausea. Marek removed the extra plates. Lina warmed tea again. Nick did say that mushroom soup was officially relieved of the post of comforter, and no one laughed, but the air let go a little. Nora ate a piece of bread without butter. Thomas drank water in small sips, although he was not the one who had been sick.

David sat wrapped in someone else’s sweater.

It seemed to be Marek’s. On David it looked too broad, like a temporary body issued to a person after the previous one had failed him.

“Do you want to lie down?” Eva asked.

“No. I’ll sit.”

“Sit.”

Ivar did not speak.

That was best of all.

Not because silence explained more. Because it did not take from David the right to ordinary humiliation. Sometimes a person has the right simply to sit after he has vomited, and not become material for someone else’s wisdom.

Sofia thought this and almost frightened herself with the thought.

Material.

The word came too early.

She did not unfold it.

At night she slept badly.

The house breathed around her: wood crackled, the stove cooled, someone walked in the corridor, water quietly ran from the jackets into the basin, branches rustled beyond the window. Several times Sofia felt as if she heard the hollow again — not even a sound, rather a wet presence. Then David by the woodshed came back to her. Bent back. Hand on the wall. Nick standing beside him without a joke. Marek with water. And Ivar’s phrase:

“First go outside.”

It was too simple to become an answer.

That was why it did not let go.

Toward morning there was no rain, but everything outside was wet, as after a long conversation no one had finished. Sofia woke before the others. The room was grey. The air smelled of wood, other people’s blankets, and weak smoke. She lay for several minutes, listening to someone cough quietly behind the wall, then got up.

There was no one in the kitchen.

The stove had almost gone out. Mugs from the evening stood on the table. David’s notebook had disappeared. Or been put away. On the windowsill lay a spoon, for no known reason. By the door, water from the jackets had gathered in the basin, and a small black fir needle floated in it.

Sofia put on her boots and went out.

Not for meaning.

For air.

She said this to herself sternly enough to almost believe it.

The porch was slippery. The boards had darkened from the night damp. The air stood cold, with a thin smell of wet ash, earth, and mushrooms. Behind the house, grey light was beginning. Not morning in the beautiful sense — simply the darkness had begun holding objects less tightly.

Sofia went toward the woodshed.

Not immediately.

First she pretended to check the bucket by the porch. Then straightened a log that had fallen from the edge of the woodpile. Then understood she was going there anyway.

The place was around the corner of the house, by the old wall of the shed, where the grass grew more poorly because of the shade and where the earth was mixed with chips, coals, sand, and small bits of bark. David had stood there in the evening. Sofia remembered, though she had looked away.

Now there was no event there.

That turned out to be unexpected.

There was no dramatic stain, no sign, no human humiliation lying on the ground as evidence. Night, damp, and earth had already done part of their work. What remained was indistinct: darker grass, a flattened edge, a little blurred mush by the wood chips, a faint sour smell that held close to the ground and quickly dissolved in the cold air.

Sofia stopped.

She felt awkward, as if she had come to look at someone else’s wound without permission.

She was already about to leave when she saw the ants.

Not many.

Several.

They were moving from the lower edge of the woodpile toward the dark place in the grass. One was dragging something almost invisible. Another stopped, turned, collided with a third, and for one second all three resembled people at the table over the map. Then the resemblance vanished, because the ants were not discussing what exactly they had found. They were working.

Sofia crouched.

Her knees immediately grew cold.

She watched one ant climb onto a chip, another disappear beneath a wet leaf, a third try for a long time to move a small pale particle, then abandon it, go away, and return not alone. Their movement was businesslike, without disgust, without interest in human meaning. They did not know there had been shame here in the night.

That was what struck hardest.

Not the ants.

Not the remains.

Not the sour smell.

The fact that they did not know.

For David, this had been “sorry.”

For the kitchen — a pause, averted eyes, water, a bowl, someone else’s sweater.

For Sofia — an awkward memory of a bent back and a hand on the wall.

For the ant — work.

She felt disgust.

Then shame for the disgust.

Then anger, because the shame turned out to be useless. The earth did not become cleaner because a person was embarrassed. The ants did not become nobler because a person tried to call their work the natural cycle. Everything was much simpler and therefore heavier.

One ant pulled the pale particle toward a crack under a log.

The earth nearby was wet. Water had soaked into it. The water had carried the smell farther. The chips had darkened. A thin blade of grass had stuck to the earth and had already begun to rise. On the edge of the spot sat a small fly, motionless, as if it too were choosing from which side to begin.

Sofia remembered yesterday’s hollow.

Moss holds water.

Rotten wood becomes earth.

Mushrooms grow from what has stopped being wood.

An ant carries what a person could not hold inside himself.

She almost heard the word “Sampo” inside.

Not as a name.

As a circle too large, one a person had kept wanting to imagine more beautifully.

Behind her a board creaked.

Sofia turned.

Nick was standing at the corner of the house, in a sweater, with a mug in his hand. His hair was dishevelled, his face still sleepy. He looked at her, then at the ground, then at her again.

“I’m not asking what you’re doing,” he said.

“Thank you.”

“Because the answer might turn out to be true.”

He came closer, but not too close. Looked down. Saw the ants. His face changed almost imperceptibly.

“Oh,” he said.

And that was all.

For Nick, that was a great deal.

Sofia stood. Her knees hurt from the cold.

“They found it,” she said.

“They have a frighteningly small number of problems with the concept of finding.”

He said it quietly, without his usual shine.

The ant disappeared under the log.

Nick took a sip from the mug and grimaced.

“The tea is cold.”

“Why did you come out?”

“Couldn’t sleep. And perhaps I wanted to make sure the world had not died of awkwardness.”

“It hadn’t.”

“Yes. Moreover, it got down to business. Indecently briskly.”

They were silent.

Sofia looked at the dark place by the wall. She wanted to cover it with something: earth, leaves, a board, a correct thought. The desire was almost the same as David’s with the map. Correct it. Remove it. Make it more honest. Or at least more decent.

She did not.

Nick noticed.

“You want to clean it up too?”

“Yes.”

“Me too.”

“Why don’t you?”

He looked at the ants.

“Because I am afraid of ruining their event.”

Sofia almost smiled.

Almost.

Ivar came out from around the corner of the house.

He was already dressed, as if he had been awake for a long time. In his hands was an empty bucket. He stopped by the porch, saw them near the woodshed, and came over. Did not ask. Set the bucket on the ground.

Nick lifted his mug.

“We weren’t looking.”

“I know.”

“This is beginning to become a suspicious tradition.”

Ivar looked at the earth by the wall.

For a long time.

Without squeamishness.

Without approval.

Without that special expression people put on when they want to show they are above disgust.

He simply looked.

“Is David asleep?” Sofia asked.

“Yes.”

“Is he better?”

“His body is better.”

“And him?”

Ivar lifted the bucket.

“That takes longer.”

He went to the water barrel.

Nick followed him with his eyes.

“I hate it when it is short and seems to be enough.”

Sofia crouched again, not as low this time. The ants continued walking. One climbed onto the edge of the dark spot, stopped, cleaned its antennae, and went on. No reverence for human drama. No disgust. No pause.

And suddenly the kitchen of the previous evening stepped back.

Did not disappear.

Simply grew thinner.

In its place for one second appeared another yard — not this one, not entirely this one. A low house, dark earth, smoke, morning after a hungry night. A woman carries out a bowl of spoiled porridge. The porridge has gone sour. It cannot be given to the children. It cannot be placed on the table. It cannot be called food, though yesterday grain, water, fire, and hope had been put into it.

The woman pours it out behind the house.

Not beautifully.

With irritation.

Almost with anger at the porridge, at herself, at the cold, at the grain that had not become salvation. Then she goes back inside, because someone in the house is coughing.

By evening birds come there.

In the morning — ants.

After a few days the grass by the wall becomes a little darker.

No one says: miracle.

No one says: lesson.

No one even notices.

The earth does not pass over material just because a person has called it failure.

The image disappeared so quietly that Sofia was not sure whether it had existed at all.

But the sour smell, the wet chips, and the ants remained.

And that was enough.

“What?” Nick asked.

“Nothing.”

“That word has become very overloaded among us.”

“Spoiled porridge,” Sofia said.

Nick looked at her attentively.

“Is that an answer or breakfast?”

“I don’t know.”

“Excellent. Then it is an answer.”

She shook her head.

“No. Just... what failed to become food still became something.”

Nick was silent.

Then said:

“David had better not formulate that today.”

“I know.”

“And neither had I.”

“You especially.”

“Thank you for the targeted care.”

They did not return to the house at once.

First Ivar passed by with a full bucket, set it by the porch, and told Marek, who had appeared in the doorway, that the water in the barrel was colder than yesterday. Marek answered that water was not obliged to improve overnight. It sounded so normal that Sofia suddenly felt grateful to everyday words. They held the world back from turning into meaning too directly.

Someone was already moving in the kitchen. Lina was cutting bread. Eva was making tea. Nora sat at the table, cleaning a small knife of yesterday’s stuck-on dirt. Thomas had not yet come out of his room. David was not there.

The notebook lay by his place.

Closed.

Sofia noticed it at once and almost grew angry with herself. Only yesterday she would have looked at bread. At water. At hands. Now the gaze found the notebook on its own. Traces of habits appear quickly.

Nick noticed it too.

“Don’t touch it,” he said.

“I wasn’t going to.”

“I was talking to myself.”

He sat farther from the notebook.

Nora raised her eyes.

“Did you see?”

Sofia understood she was not asking about David.

“Yes.”

“And?”

Nick said:

“The world was busy.”

Nora nodded, as if that were an exact answer, and returned to the knife.

After a while David came out.

He looked better.

Not good.

Better.

His face was no longer grey. His hair was damp from washing. He was still wearing someone else’s sweater. He stopped in the kitchen doorway, saw everyone, and for one second wanted to apologize again. Sofia understood this by his lips.

He did not say it.

And that was almost a feat.

Marek set tea before him.

“No mushrooms,” Nick said.

The silence became dangerous.

Then David unexpectedly smiled.

Very weakly.

“Thank you.”

Nick breathed out carefully, as if he had crossed the boards and had not fallen through.

David sat. Looked at the notebook. Did not touch it.

“Did I say anything completely terrible yesterday?” he asked.

“No,” Eva said.

“Is that not true?”

“It is true enough.”

He nodded.

Nick added:

“You almost formulated that the map is dry, and the stomach is not. I think it deserves preservation, but not today.”

David covered his face with his palm.

Not from laughter.

But not only from shame either.

“Sorry,” Nick said more quietly.

“No. It’s all right.”

“That word is forbidden today,” Nora said.

David removed his hand.

“I feel awkward.”

Marek set salt on the table.

“Good.”

David looked at him.

“Good?”

“Means you’re alive.”

“Very comforting.”

“I’m not comforting you.”

“That is noticeable.”

Nick said quietly:

“Comfort in this house passes through severe customs.”

Ivar came in last. Brought firewood, placed it by the stove, sat down. No one asked him about the previous night, or about the ants, or about what to do with the map now. That was good. Not because there were no questions. Because they did not yet have the right to become words again.

Breakfast was simple: bread, butter, tea, a few berries, cheese, boiled eggs. After yesterday’s soup all food looked cautious. As if the house itself had reduced complexity.

David ate little.

But he ate.

Sofia watched his hands. They no longer trembled. When he took bread, a crumb fell onto the table. No one noticed except Sofia. Then the crumb was noticed by a small black dot at the edge of the board.

Not an ant.

Just a speck.

Sofia almost laughed.

From fatigue.

From the fact that now even a speck could be suspicion.

She took the crumb and ate it.

Nick saw and raised an eyebrow.

“Prophylaxis against symbolism?”

“Yes.”

“I approve.”

David suddenly looked at the notebook.

Everyone noticed, but no one gave any sign.

He placed his palm on the cover.

Did not open it.

“I still want to correct the map,” he said.

Nick closed his eyes.

Eva looked into her cup.

Thomas, who had entered the kitchen just at that moment, stopped in the doorway.

David continued:

“Not now.”

That was different.

Not victory.

Not refusal.

But different.

“And probably not in order to close it.”

He heard himself say “probably.” And did not correct it.

Ivar took bread.

“Then later.”

“And if I still want to close it?”

“You will.”

“And what then?”

Ivar spread the butter too thin, almost transparent.

“You will look to see who wants it.”

Nick gave a quiet groan.

“There. We have almost returned to the head. I knew the body would not hold us for long.”

David unexpectedly laughed.

Shortly.

Weakly.

But truly.

The laugh came out and immediately became a cough. Eva moved water toward him. He drank.

“Not everything passes through the head,” he said.

Himself.

Not Ivar.

Sofia felt how the phrase could have become too beautiful. But David said it hoarsely, after water, someone else’s sweater, and yesterday’s shame. It did not sound like wisdom. It sounded like a person who still had a sour taste in his mouth.

That was why the phrase remained alive.

After breakfast Sofia went again to the woodshed.

Not alone. Nora walked beside her, asking nothing. Nick stayed on the porch with a mug. Pretended he was simply warming himself, although the porch warmed no one.

By the wall there were more ants.

Not a crowd.

Simply more.

They had already laid a tiny road between the chips. The earth had darkened more widely. The night damp had finally mixed everything with the grass. Nothing looked clean. Nothing looked finally dirty. It was unpleasant. It was ordinary. It was busy.

Nora crouched.

“They are quick.”

“Yes.”

“They do not ask.”

“No.”

Nora looked at Sofia.

“Good that they do not ask.”

Sofia did not answer at once.

“Why?”

“We would explain.”

And that was the whole horror.

They stood a little longer. Then Nora went for water, and Sofia remained.

She did not feel lighter.

Did not feel cleaner.

Did not feel it had become more beautiful.

She did not feel gratitude to the cycle of life. She did not want to say that everything is needed, everything sacred, everything accepted. That would have been too fast and too convenient. Too much like David wanting to correct the map, only in another language.

She simply looked.

At the ants.

At the wet earth.

At the grass that made no distinction between dew, rain, soup, shame, and what had returned from the human body.

At the chips that were darkening and would gradually also become something else.

At the tiny work no one had announced.

She did not feel lighter.

But it became clear: this was unnecessary only for a human being.
