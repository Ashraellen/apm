# Chapter Five. The Thing

The house met them not with warmth.

There was warmth too, of course. It stood by the stove, held in the boards, rose from the floor in a slow wave, smelled of damp wool, bread, ash, and other people’s sleeves. But the first thing Sofia felt was not warmth.

She felt the number of things.

Hooks by the door. A shelf for wet hats. A wooden bench on which everyone in turn set a foot to pull off boots. A bucket by the wall. A narrow brush with stiff bristles. A tray with mugs. A knife forgotten at the edge of the table. A jar lid lying by itself, like a person who had lost his reason for being there. An old mat accepting water from boots without objection. A hanger bent under the weight of jackets. A grey rag on the back of a chair. A small iron hook near the stove, from which the poker hung.

Before, all this had simply been the arrangement of the house.

Now every thing seemed to stand a little closer to the eyes.

Not as a symbol.

Worse.

As a thing.

Nora came in last and set the enamel mug on the edge of the kitchen table. Not upside down, as on the porch, but on its side, so the water could drain. The mug lay leaning on its handle and tapped softly against the wood while the last drop looked for somewhere to go.

The drop flowed from inside, passed along the blue rim, paused at the chip, and fell onto the table.

A dark point spread in the wood.

Nick noticed.

“The mug is still continuing the event,” he said.

“It is drying,” Nora replied.

“I sometimes call my condition that too.”

Nora looked at him without a smile.

“You usually make noise.”

“That is protective ventilation.”

“Then you dry badly.”

Nick opened his mouth, closed it, then nodded.

“Accepted.”

Sofia took off her jacket and hung it on a free hook. The hook swung slightly under the weight of the wet fabric. Metal creaked in the wood. The sound was small, but after the water she heard it too clearly.

The hook held.

The mug held.

The shelf held.

The bench held.

The house was full of things that did what the palm by the water could not do: they did not let go at once.

And because of that they became more suspicious.

Eva brought another towel from the hallway and threw it over the back of a chair.

“Someone got wetter than he admits,” she said.

“We have a democracy of dampness,” Nick replied. “Everyone gets wet according to the measure of his inner convictions.”

“Your sleeves are wet.”

“Those are not convictions. Those are consequences.”

Marek came in from outside through the side door. In his hands were a small bunch of keys and an old tin box of nails. He set the box on the windowsill. It clinked as if what lay inside it were not nails, but small evil thoughts.

Thomas looked at the box.

Did not immediately look away.

Sofia noticed.

After the landing, after the rusty nail, after the hand that wanted to throw and did not throw, iron could no longer simply lie there. Even if it was in a box. Even if the box stood on the windowsill and promised nothing to anyone.

“We need to bring a few things in from the pantry,” Marek said. “Before the rain starts.”

“Will it start?” David asked.

Marek looked at the window.

“It is already deciding.”

Beyond the glass the sky had indeed grown lower. The grey had thickened, the forest darkened, and the lake behind the trees had disappeared almost completely, as if it had been covered with something wet and heavy.

“What things?” Eva asked.

“Bowls, candles, spare blankets. And the big box with the lamps.”

Nick slowly turned to Sofia.

“Do you hear? After the water business, we have been issued bowls and a box. The world has stopped even pretending to be subtle.”

“Be quiet,” Sofia said.

“With pleasure. But the world continues.”

Ivar stood by the stove, checking the fire. He did not turn around.

“Those who can, take something,” Marek said. “The pantry is under the stairs.”

Nick looked at his sleeves.

“I can morally.”

“Then you’ll carry blankets,” Marek said.

“That is morally?”

“Blankets don’t argue.”

“You don’t know them well.”

Several people followed Marek.

Sofia went too.

Not because she wanted to help. Though perhaps also because of that. But after the lake, staying in the kitchen beside the mug, the box of nails, and Thomas’s empty plate was harder than moving. Movement gave a person the appearance of choice. Even if the choice was between a wet kitchen and a pantry.

The pantry was under the stairs, but larger than it seemed from the outside. The door did not open at once. Marek put in the key, turned it, pulled the handle; the wood creaked but did not yield.

“It sticks,” he said.

“The door?” Nick asked.

“No, the philosophy of space.”

Marek hit it with his shoulder.

The door opened.

From inside came the smell of cold wood, dust, dried herbs, wax, and something iron. Not rust, no. An older smell. That is how things smell when they have lain in place for a long time and have stopped being surprised by people.

Sofia stopped on the threshold.

Inside the pantry it was dark, but not entirely. A narrow window under the ceiling let in grey light. In that light stood shelves. On the shelves were jars, stacks of plates, candles, boxes, coils of rope, old lanterns, several tin mugs, wooden bowls, empty bottles, a small clay pitcher without a handle, a bunch of keys without an understandable door, iron hinges, lids, folded fabrics, lamps that smelled of kerosene although they had probably not been used for a long time.

Everything was in its place.

And everything seemed temporarily hidden.

“There,” Marek said, pointing to the lower shelf. “Blankets there. Lamps here. Bowls carefully, they’re old.”

“All bowls are old,” Nick said. “Some just pretend it is style.”

Lina, who had appeared beside them without a sound, took a stack of wooden bowls.

“These are not style. These have survived more breakfasts than you have decisions.”

“That was personal.”

“No. That was about bowls.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“In this house even the dishes have a biography.”

Sofia wanted to smile, but could not.

She was looking at the lower shelf.

There stood a small wooden box with a lid. Low, dark, with metal corners. The lid was closed, but not locked. On the front side was the trace of an old label or plaque, long since torn off. The wood around the trace had darkened, and the pale rectangle looked like a place where there had once been a name.

Thomas was looking at the box too.

And this time Sofia was certain: he was not simply looking. He already wanted to know what was inside.

“What’s in there?” David asked.

Marek turned around.

“Where?”

“The box.”

“Probably empty.”

“Probably?”

Marek lifted one shoulder.

“There is a lot of empty probably here.”

He took a bundle of candles from the shelf and handed it to Eva.

David leaned toward the box.

“Can I look?”

Marek looked at Ivar, though Ivar was not in the pantry.

Then, apparently noticing this himself, he frowned.

“Look.”

David pulled at the lid.

It did not open.

“Stuck.”

“Everything sticks today,” Nick said. “Maybe that’s the kind of day it is.”

Thomas unexpectedly reached out.

“Give it here.”

David yielded.

Thomas took the box carefully, but not gently. As one takes a thing already suspected of an answer. He ran his thumb along the edge of the lid, found the place where the wood had lifted slightly, and pressed.

The lid clicked.

Too loudly.

Everyone fell silent.

Even Nick.

Thomas lifted the lid.

There was nothing inside.

Emptiness lay calmly at the bottom, in the form of dark wood, dust, and one dried wing of some insect. Nothing more. No note, no key, no old ring, no hidden object that could have begun some indecently direct new thread of events.

Sofia felt relief.

And almost at once — irritation with herself.

Why relief?

Because an empty box demanded nothing?

Or because an empty box still remained a box?

Thomas looked inside longer than necessary.

“Empty,” David said.

“Yes,” Thomas replied.

But did not close it.

Nick leaned over and looked in.

“Magnificent. At last, a thing honestly shows its contents.”

“Are you sure?” Nora asked from the doorway.

She was standing with two blankets in her arms.

“What?”

“That emptiness is content.”

Nick looked at her.

“I am too wet today for such questions.”

“So am I.”

She passed by and placed the blankets on a chest by the wall.

The chest was large, wooden, with iron bands and a heavy lid. It stood under the stairs so long that the dust around its legs had gathered not like dirt, but like a boundary. Old folded blankets lay on its lid, and because of that the chest looked not mysterious, but tired.

Sofia looked at it.

She did not want to open the chest.

That was important.

She had wanted the little box.

The chest — no.

A large thing did not promise an answer. A large thing promised responsibility for whatever would be found inside.

“Should we look in that too?” David asked.

Marek said sharply:

“No.”

Everyone turned.

Marek himself was surprised by his tone. He adjusted his sleeve.

“Old fabrics in there. Moths. Nothing interesting.”

“After that phrase,” Nick said quietly, “the level of interest has risen by forty percent.”

“Do not touch it,” Marek said.

And no one touched it.

Steps sounded in the corridor.

Ivar came to the open pantry door, but did not enter. He stopped on the threshold. Light from the kitchen lay on his shoulder, while his face remained in half-shadow.

Thomas was still holding the small open box.

Ivar looked at it.

“Found it?” he asked.

Thomas gave a small smile.

“Emptiness.”

“It keeps well.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“Now I need a blanket.”

Ivar came in, took the box from Thomas, looked inside, closed the lid, and set it on the little table by the door. Not solemnly. Without a gesture. He simply removed the thing from the passage.

Then he said:

“A rumor rarely remains a rumor.”

David raised his head.

“What do you mean?”

Ivar took a wooden bowl from the shelf. Dark, smooth, worn by hands.

“First they hear. Then they look for something to hold.”

He handed the bowl to Lina.

Lina took it with both hands.

The pantry became tighter.

Not physically.

The words simply took up space.

Sofia looked at the box by the door. It stood closed. Empty. And still now it seemed fuller than it had a few minutes earlier.

The door had already opened.

Not into the pantry.

Into another place.

The grey light under the ceiling darkened.

The smell of wax and dust became sharper.

Sofia heard not a sound. Rather, a change in silence, as if someone behind the wall had begun sorting dry grain.

The pantry remained the pantry.

And still the shelves moved back.

The wooden bowls in Lina’s hands darkened. The tin mugs lost their domestic lightness. The coils of rope by the wall stretched like sleeping snakes. Empty bottles on the upper shelf held grey light inside them.

Sofia blinked.

And saw people walking along a winter road.

Not the seven from the warm snow.

Others.

Or the same ones, but later.

The snow was old, packed down, grey by the path. The wind dragged fine pellets across it, and those pellets struck faces not painfully, but constantly, as a reminder that the world knows how to be not an enemy, but a condition.

The people did not walk as a crowd.

In twos, threes, separately. From different directions. One had a sack on his back; another, a child wrapped so tightly that no face could be seen. An old man led a goat, though the goat could barely stand. A young man carried something wrapped in cloth under his coat and kept checking with his hand whether it was still there. A woman with dry lips pressed an empty bowl to her chest.

At first they were not asking about a thing.

“Where is the warm earth?”

“Where is bread under the snow?”

“Where is the place that does not end?”

“Where is the root that feeds?”

The answers were different.

Someone pointed north.

Someone east.

Someone said: by the old river.

Someone: beyond the black forest.

Someone: under the mountain where the stone sounds hollow.

Someone laughed and closed the door.

The rumor moved faster than people.

It passed through villages, through low houses, through smoky kitchens, through nights spent in entryways, through the exchange of bread, through refusal, through envy, through fear. A rumor had no legs, so it did not grow tired. A rumor had no throat, so it did not go hoarse. Everyone who repeated it added one of his own needs to it.

First they said: there is a place.

Then: there is that place.

Then someone said:

“It is there.”

And that changed everything.

Because a “place” cannot be put into a sack.

“It” — one can try.

Sofia did not hear who had said it first. Perhaps no one. Perhaps the word had fallen by itself from the mouth of a hungry person when he had grown tired of explaining a place he had not seen. But after that, people began asking differently.

“Who has seen it?”

“What is it like?”

“Large?”

“Heavy?”

“Wooden?”

“Stone?”

“With a lid?”

“With a handle?”

“Do they carry it?”

“Do they hide it?”

“Who has it?”

The old woman by the stove, the same one or one like the one who had sorted grain the previous night, sat before a clay bowl and sorted the last grains. A man with a frostbitten ear came to her and asked:

“Have you heard?”

“Everyone has heard,” she said.

“They say it is a thing.”

The old woman did not raise her head.

“It is easier for people that way.”

“Why?”

She gave a dry snort.

“One can look at a thing. One can hide it. One can quarrel.”

“I don’t want to quarrel.”

“Not yet.”

“I want to find it.”

“Everyone says that.”

He shifted at the door, squeezing his hat.

“If I find it, I will bring it home. Feed the children.”

The old woman took one grain, crushed it between her fingers, and blew away the dust.

“Find it first,” she said. “Then you can be kind.”

He left.

He was not bad.

He was hungry.

And in his hands there already lived the form of a thing he had not yet seen.

In another village two brothers argued by a barn door.

One said:

“If it exists, then someone is holding it.”

The other answered:

“If someone is holding it, then it can be traded for.”

“Or taken.”

“Quiet.”

“Why quiet? If it feeds, it should not lie with one person.”

“And if you had it?”

The first brother did not answer at once.

The wind pushed the barn door. Inside hung sickles, ropes, an old saddle, an empty basket. Everything by which people held, cut, tied, carried.

“I would share,” he said at last.

The second looked at him.

“With whom?”

“With ours.”

“And the others?”

The first brother took a rope from a nail and began winding it around his hand.

“First it must be found.”

In a third place a child was carving a small round box from soft wood with a knife. Not even for play. It was simply that the hands wanted to repeat what adults were naming among themselves more and more often. Round. With a lid. So there would be something inside.

“What are you making?” the mother asked.

“Sampo...” the child muttered without raising his eyes.

“Nothing makes itself. Hold the knife away from yourself.”

He obediently turned the blade.

The mother took the knife away from him.

The little box was uneven, the lid would not close, the bottom had cracked. But the child held it as if it already knew more than he did.

The rumor began to grow heavy.

Before, it had been like smoke: it moved, changed shape, passed through cracks.

Now it began to knock.

In chests.

In bowls.

In sacks.

In the words “it,” “there,” “they have it,” “find,” “hide.”

People began drawing it in their minds. Someone saw a stone bowl. Someone — a wooden box. Someone — a millstone, though there was no mill nearby. Someone — a bottomless pitcher. Someone — an iron heart under the earth. Someone — a lid that had to be lifted.

And each was sure he almost remembered.

That is how a rumor became an object.

Sofia heard this phrase not in Ivar’s voice.

It arose by itself.

And because of that it was more frightening.

The winter road changed into the inside of a house. Not the northern house where they were now. Another. Low, dark, with a ceiling that held smoke. On the table stood an empty wooden bowl. Around it sat people.

They were not eating.

They were looking.

Because someone had said: if one puts the bowl in the right place, it will fill.

The bowl did not fill.

But no one got up.

One man said:

“Maybe it isn’t the right one.”

The woman beside him answered:

“Maybe this is not the place.”

A third said:

“Maybe a word is needed.”

A fourth:

“Maybe a lid.”

A fifth:

“Maybe iron.”

And every new “maybe” made the empty bowl more important.

Sofia wanted to turn away.

She could not.

Because she knew this feeling.

Not from antiquity.

From life.

When an empty form begins to promise more than a full one. When a closed box seems more honest than an open palm. When a thing that has not yet given anything already becomes hope simply because it can be placed before you.

The pantry returned sharply.

Nick coughed.

Someone dropped a lid. It rolled across the floor, struck the leg of the table, and lay still, ringing for several seconds after it had stopped.

“Sorry,” David said.

He picked up the lid.

Sofia understood she did not know how much time had passed.

A second.

A minute.

Or enough for the rumor to become heavy.

Ivar stood at the pantry door. His face was calm, but not soft.

Thomas looked at the closed box.

“That was...” he began.

“No,” Nick said at once.

Thomas turned.

“What no?”

“Do not say ‘that was like.’ Don’t. I object in advance.”

Thomas unexpectedly did not get angry.

“All right.”

He was silent.

“Then I’ll say it differently. If Sampo is a thing, it is simpler.”

It became very quiet in the pantry.

Not because he had uttered something forbidden. No. The word itself had already sounded here before, had moved among them, lain in bread, water, moss, an empty box. But simpler — that was what made the phrase dangerous.

Nick stopped smiling.

David held the lid in his hand and did not know where to put it.

Nora looked at her enamel mug, which for some reason she had brought with her into the pantry. She held it by the handle, and the mug was empty. Or wet. Or both.

Ivar looked at Thomas.

“Simpler,” he said.

“Isn’t it?”

“Not the same.”

Thomas pressed his lips together.

“But if it is a thing, it can be sought.”

“It can.”

“Found.”

“Perhaps.”

Thomas fell silent.

He did not say the third word.

But everyone heard it.

Sofia looked at his hands. They were empty. But not like the man’s hands in the snow. There, emptiness had been humiliation. Here, emptiness was readiness.

And that was more frightening.

Marek took the box of candles.

“Let’s carry out what’s needed,” he said.

His voice was dry, almost angry. Not from anger. From the desire to return things to their ordinary work.

Lina lifted the bowls. Eva took the blankets. Nick — the box with the lamps. David finally placed the lid on a shelf, though he still had not understood what it belonged to. Nora went out with the mug. Thomas lingered by the small box.

Did not open it.

Only touched the lid with two fingers.

Then took his hand away.

Sofia went out last.

When they returned to the kitchen, the house became a house again. Almost. The blankets were placed on the bench, the bowls on the table, the lamps by the wall. Marek closed the pantry and hung the key on the hook by the stairs. The key swung once, twice, three times. Then stopped.

Nora set the mug beside the bowls.

White enamel, blue rim, chip.

Sofia thought that now the mug looked different among the bowls. By the water it had been a small form before a great source. In the house it became simply one of the things. And this was worse. Because in a house, things quickly acquire the right to be needed.

Ivar took the wooden bowl Lina had brought and set it in the middle of the table.

Empty.

Not as a sign.

Rather because it had to be put somewhere.

But everyone looked.

Too quickly.

Ivar saw it.

“Like that,” he said.

“What?” David asked.

Ivar touched the rim of the bowl.

“The thing lies empty. And the person is already waiting.”

Nick said quietly:

“I don’t like it when objects get ahead of events.”

“They do not get ahead,” Lina said. “They endure.”

“Everyone endures better than I do today.”

“That is not difficult.”

Nick nodded.

“Deserved.”

Sofia looked at the bowl.

The wood was dark, smooth; inside it thin rings of grain could be seen. Someone had once carved it with hands. Someone had held a knife. Someone had removed the unnecessary. Someone had made an empty place inside the wood so that food, water, berries, bread, anything could be placed there. The bowl was not simply wood. It was wood with emptiness inside.

That was enough.

She went to the window.

Outside, at last, rain had begun.

Not strong. Northern rain does not always come as an event. Sometimes it simply makes the air visible. Drops lay on the glass, crawled downward, joined, parted, left transparent paths behind them. Beyond the glass the forest became nearer and farther at the same time.

The lake could not be seen.

Only rain.

Only glass.

Only the reflection of the kitchen over the wet world.

In the reflection the wooden bowl stood on the table as the centre of the room.

Empty.

Sofia turned to Ivar.

“And if people were mistaken not when they wanted to take?” she asked.

He looked at her.

“When, then?”

“When they decided that what must be sought is a thing.”

Ivar did not answer at once.

In the kitchen someone set a lamp on the floor. The wick inside the glass was black, dry. The box of nails on the windowsill clinked again — probably Marek had brushed it. Nora ran a finger along the chip of her mug.

“Sometimes it is the same beginning,” Ivar said.

Sofia nodded.

She was not sure she understood.

But the body understood earlier.

As with water.

As with bread.

As with a thing not yet taken, but already placed in the centre of the table.

The rain grew stronger.

Now it knocked on the roof, on the windowsill, on the overturned bucket by the porch, on the glass. The house filled with small blows. Every drop found a surface. Every surface answered in its own way: wood dully, glass thinly, metal sharply, earth without sound.

Thomas stood by the table and looked at the empty bowl.

“If it is a thing,” he said quietly, almost to no one, “then it can be lost.”

Nick did not answer at once.

“Or found.”

Thomas raised his eyes.

There it was.

Sofia saw it between them.

Not even hope.

Relief.

Eva covered the bowl with a towel.

The gesture was small.

Almost domestic.

But Sofia felt something inside her flinch.

An empty thing had been covered.

And immediately it began to seem there was something beneath the cloth.

Eva noticed her look.

“So dust won’t settle,” she said.

“Yes,” Sofia answered.

Of course.

So dust would not settle.

Nothing else.

People began to disperse.

Someone went to dry things. Someone to look for tea. Someone simply into the corridor, where one could stand without the bowl, the box, and Ivar. David took the lamp and carried it to the window to examine it. Marek put the box of nails higher on the shelf. Nora left the mug beside the bowls and this time did not take it with her.

Sofia went to the table and touched the edge of the towel. Beneath it was a wooden bowl. Empty. Just an empty bowl.

But now emptiness had a form.

And the form endured.

Outside the window, the rain continued knocking.

The lake remained behind the trees.

The bread — on the table.

Nora’s mug — beside the bowls.

The box — in the pantry.

The key — on the hook.

The nails — on the shelf.

The things had taken their places.

And because of that it did not become calmer.

Only clearer where to look.

While Sampo was a rumor, it slipped from the hands.

While it was warmth under snow, bread, water, moss, it could not be placed in front of oneself and said: there.

But a thing can.

Even an empty one.

Especially an empty one.

Sofia raised her eyes to Ivar.

He stood by the stove, his back to the room, and was adding a thin log to the fire.

The flame did not accept it at once.

First it touched the edge.

Then went inward.

On the table, under the towel, the bowl was silent.

The rumor was no longer only a rumor.

It had received a place where it could wait.
