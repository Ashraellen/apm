# Chapter Thirteen. Sour

The rain did not end.

It only got tired.

From morning it hung over the house as a grey, fine, even weight, not properly falling, but not leaving either. The yard had soaked through completely. The gravel by the porch had darkened, the boards on the steps had taken in water, the grass by the woodshed lay lower than yesterday. The forest stood motionless and wet, as if someone had put it there before the beginning of all their words and had now forgotten to remove it.

In the house it smelled of bread, yesterday’s rain, wet sleeves, and something sour.

The sour thing stood by the stove.

The small jar under the cloth had been placed on the warm shelf after supper. In the morning Lina opened it, smelled it, said “normal,” and added a little flour and water. Thomas asked exactly how much. Lina answered: “So it wakes up.” David did not write it down at once. First he looked at Nora. Nora said nothing. Then he did write: “morning — feed.” Without a quantity.

The jar ended up by the stove again.

The cloth over it lifted slightly in one place, as if someone inside were breathing not with a chest, but with patience.

Sofia passed it several times, and each time she caught the smell. It was not pleasant and not bad. Sour. Damp. Warm. Slightly floury. A little like yesterday’s bread before it became bread. If one did not know, one could think something had been forgotten.

If one did know, one could think that too.

That was the unpleasant part.

“Is it really supposed to smell like that?” Thomas asked.

Lina was closing the sack of flour.

“Yes.”

“And if it’s too sour?”

“Then we’ll see.”

“How do you know when it’s too?”

“When it is.”

Nick was sitting by the window, wrapped in a blanket as if he had officially withdrawn from human activity.

“I feel that today the word ‘too’ is going to lose the war to the word ‘we’ll see.’”

“You can help with the firewood,” Marek said.

“I can’t. I’m in a blanket. A blanket is an ancient form of refusing civilization.”

“Then be silent from the blanket.”

“Fair.”

Ivar was outside. In the morning he had gone down to the water with Marek, then stayed by the shed, sorting out some old ropes that had been hanging on a nail since before their arrival. His absence from the house made the room more ordinary. Not empty. Just ordinary. When Ivar was inside, even silence seemed to know it was being heard. When he went out, the house became a house: mugs, wet socks, chips of wood by the stove, bread, crumbs, the recipe on the table, and the jar smelling sour.

The recipe lay beside the notebook.

No longer in the notebook.

On a separate sheet.

David had copied it out cleanly. Thomas had added small notes in the margins, although Lina had warned him that notes would not make the dough obey. A tea stain remained in the corner of the sheet. Nick said it was the first real sign of life on the document. No one answered, and he counted that as almost agreement.

Sofia looked from the sheet to the jar and back again.

On the sheet it was quiet.

In the jar it was not quiet.

Even if it made no sound.

“In the city they do this more simply,” Thomas suddenly said.

Everyone looked at him.

He understood how it sounded at once.

“I don’t mean Sampo. Just... there everything is clear: ingredients, program, time. You put it in, choose, wait.”

Nick slowly pulled his face out of the blanket.

“Now the blanket has stopped protecting me.”

Lina did not look at Thomas.

“Not everything is clear there either.”

“But much clearer.”

“Then why do you eat this bread?”

Thomas sighed.

“I’m not against this bread.”

“You’re against the fact that it doesn’t behave like a program.”

He fell silent.

David was looking at the recipe.

Sofia noticed that he wrote nothing down. Good. The word “program” had already entered the room and laid itself on the table too evenly, too dryly, like an object that had nowhere here to go yet.

At that moment an engine sounded outside.

Not loud. Distant at first. Then closer. Wheels went over the wet gravel at the turn, crunched, slipped for a second, then moved again. The house seemed to tense slightly. Not because of danger. Because of the outside.

The sound of a car in this place was an event.

Marek came in from the porch before the car stopped.

“The caretaker,” he said.

“Caretaker of what?” Nick asked.

“The house.”

“The house has a caretaker?”

“The house has pipes, a stove, a drain, a roof, gas, and people who think a blanket cancels firewood.”

“I feel personally attacked.”

Marek was no longer listening. He opened the door.

Cold air came in from outside, the wet smell of gravel, and the sound of a van door closing. Outside the window stood a grey vehicle with a white side panel and mud on the wheels. Not new. Not old. Working. A van like that did not bring miracles. It brought gas cylinders, towels, tools, sacks, and sometimes silent people.

The man from the van was short, solid, in a dark waterproof jacket and a grey knitted cap. His face was weathered, not old, but long familiar with weather. Short stubble on his chin. In his hands — a plastic crate and a bunch of keys, which, as soon as he came in, he took off his finger and put into his pocket so they would not jingle.

He stopped at the threshold and took off his cap.

“Hello,” he said.

His voice was quiet, a little dry.

Marek answered:

“Hi, Matti.”

The man nodded.

“I check house.”

“Sure.”

He looked at the others the way one looks at temporary people in a permanent house: calmly, without curiosity, but taking them into account. Many people. That meant more water. More rubbish. The stove working more often. Wetter floors. Nothing personal.

“Everything good?” Matti asked.

“Good,” Marek said. “Water okay. Stove okay.”

“Gas?”

“Half.”

Matti nodded.

“I bring one.”

He spoke English in short pieces. Not because he was ridiculous. Because that was enough for the work. Words were tools, like keys. The needed word — he took it. The unnecessary one — he did not.

Eva came to help with the crate.

“Towels?” she asked.

“Yes. Clean. And coffee. Little.”

“Coffee is never little,” Nick said quietly.

Matti looked at him.

Nick raised a hand.

“Sorry. House joke.”

Matti nodded as if he had not understood the joke, but had understood that it did not interfere.

The house immediately became different.

Not foreign — inspected.

Matti went to the sink, opened the water, looked at the stream. Listened. Closed it. Bent under the sink, opened the cupboard door, checked the trap. Tapped the pipe with his fingers. Marek stood beside him and answered short questions.

“Leak?”

“No.”

“Smell?”

“Only wet people.”

Matti looked at him, then almost smiled.

“Normal.”

Nick whispered to Sofia:

“I have officially been recognized as a natural smell.”

“Quiet.”

Matti went to the stove. Checked the damper, the ash, the firewood by the wall, the place under the logs. He moved without fuss. Not like an owner. Like a person who knows that if he does not look now, someone will call in the evening, in the rain, and say the chimney is not drawing or the water is leaking.

Ivar came into the house when Matti was already standing by the stove.

“Matti,” he said.

“Ivar.”

They shook hands. Briefly. No embraces, no hidden meaning.

Ivar did not look like the owner.

Matti did not look like a guest.

They were two people meeting in the same place not for the first time, but for different reasons. One comes to listen and speak. The other — to check the stove and the water.

“Many people,” Matti said.

“This year, yes,” Ivar answered.

“Okay.”

And that was all.

No question why.

No interest in their words, in Sampo, in why grown people were sitting in a wet house and arguing with bread. For Matti, the house was a house. People — people. Stove — stove. Water — water. Smell — smell.

He went to the back door, opened it, looked at the porch, the gutter, the wet boards. Then returned, brought another crate from the van: clean towels, two packs of candles, a bag of coffee, a small packet of salt, several light bulbs, and a roll of rubbish bags.

“Thank you,” Eva said.

“No problem.”

Movement began by itself.

Eva sorted the towels. Marek went with Matti to the gas cylinder. Thomas, for some reason, volunteered to show where yesterday the stream in the bathroom had been weak. David removed the recipe from the table, then put it back on the edge, then moved it again so no one would set a wet crate on it. Nora took the candles and put them on a shelf. Nick rose from the blanket and, despite his own philosophy, went to help with the rubbish bag.

“I am doing this not from a sense of duty,” he told Sofia.

“Of course.”

“But from fear of Marek.”

“That is also a form of participation.”

“An unpleasant one, but functional.”

Sofia smiled.

For several minutes the house became simple.

Almost cheerful.

Wet people, crates, short English words, clean towels, Marek by the door, Eva with coffee, Nick with a bag, Matti with a flashlight under the sink. No Sampo. No ancient memory. Just a house that had to be maintained, because houses without maintenance quickly become cold, wet, and dangerous.

That was exactly why no one was watching the jar.

It stood by the stove.

On that very shelf where it was warm, but not hot.

Under the cloth.

Matti returned to the stove alone.

Not entirely alone — there were people in the house, but everyone was busy. Marek was talking to Thomas by the bathroom door. Eva was looking for a place for the towels. David was straightening the sheet with the recipe so it would not slide off the table. Lina had gone into the pantry for another jar for salt. Ivar stood by the window, looking at the yard and listening to something outside, perhaps the van engine, which had not yet cooled.

Matti bent down to check the shelf by the stove.

He saw the jar.

Lifted the cloth.

Smelled it.

His face creased slightly. Not strongly. He simply registered it.

“Old?” he said under his breath.

Nick, passing with the rubbish bag, heard only the sound, not the word.

“What?”

Matti raised the jar a little higher.

“Bad? Spoiled?”

Nick held the bag, gripping its neck with one hand. Packages, wet wipes, shells, and yesterday’s butter paper stuck out of it.

“I don’t know,” Nick said.

He really did not know. He sort of knew. But not enough to take responsibility for the smell.

“Lina?” he called.

Lina was in the pantry and did not hear.

Marek said from the bathroom:

“One minute.”

Matti smelled it again.

It smelled sour.

For a person who had come to check the house, a sour jar by the stove was not a mystery. Not a living continuation. Not tomorrow’s bread. It was something someone had forgotten to clear away.

He said:

“I clean.”

Nick turned to him.

“Wait, maybe...”

But at that moment Thomas called:

“Nick, can you hold the door?”

The bag began slipping from his hand.

Matti took the jar calmly, efficiently, without irritation. Removed the cloth, looked inside, creased his face again.

“Bad smell.”

He did not throw it into the bag at once. He was careful. He took it to the sink, scraped the contents into a small rubbish bag, rinsed the jar with hot water, and set it upside down by the sink. The cloth went into the rubbish. Then he tied the small bag and put it into the large one Nick was holding.

All of it took less than a minute.

No drama.

No villainy.

Just order.

“Thanks,” Matti said.

Nick looked at the washed jar.

Something in him turned unpleasant.

Not a thought. Not an understanding. Just a small inward cold, like when a person sees that a door has closed and is not sure whether he should have held it.

“Was it...” he began.

But Matti was already checking the kitchen window again.

Nick did not finish.

Lina returned a few minutes later.

In her hands was a small tin box with salt.

“Where is the jar?” she asked.

Nick did not understand at first.

“Which one?”

“By the stove.”

He looked at the shelf.

Empty.

Then at the sink.

There stood the clean jar upside down.

Lina saw it too.

The salt box remained in her hands.

It did not fall.

That would have been too simple.

She simply stopped holding it as a thing and began holding it as weight.

“Where is what was inside?” she asked.

Her voice was even.

Too even.

Nick said:

“I think...”

Matti turned.

“I throw,” he said.

Silence did not understand at once that it needed to become silence.

At first the house was still moving: Marek closed the cupboard door under the bathroom sink, Thomas said something about the tap, Eva put towels on a shelf. Then all of it stopped one thing after another, as if someone were turning off not the light, but actions.

Lina looked at Matti.

“You threw it away?”

Matti did not understand the Russian, but he understood the intonation.

He looked at Marek.

Marek translated briefly:

“She asks: you throw it away?”

Matti nodded.

“Yes. Bad smell. Spoiled. I clean.”

Lina set the salt box on the table.

Very carefully.

Then said in English:

“It was not spoiled.”

Matti froze.

“No?”

“No.”

“I think old. Bad.”

“It was starter.”

He looked at her.

The word passed him by.

“For bread,” Lina said.

Matti understood.

Not immediately, but he understood.

No horror appeared on his face. Only a practical mistake that had suddenly turned out not to be practical. He looked at the clean jar by the sink. At the rubbish bag. At Lina.

“Sorry,” he said. “I think spoiled.”

“I know.”

“I clean.”

“I know.”

That was what hurt.

Not “why.”

Not “how could you.”

Not “who allowed you.”

But “I know.”

Because Lina really did know: he had wanted to remove the bad thing. He had wanted to help. He had wanted to leave the house cleaner than he found it. In his system, a sour mass in a jar by the stove was something that had to be taken out.

He had not broken it.

He had cleared it away.

And that made the disappearance heavier.

Thomas came closer.

“He didn’t know,” he said.

Too quickly.

Not even justifying Matti. Rather saving himself from the need to look at the clean jar.

Lina nodded.

“Yes.”

“He couldn’t know.”

“Yes.”

“It wasn’t written on it.”

Lina looked at him.

Thomas heard himself.

His face changed.

David slowly took the recipe sheet.

Read it.

“Leave a piece.”

“Feed.”

“Not forgetting.”

He turned the sheet over.

There was nothing on the back.

Not a single line: “do not throw away if it smells sour.”

Not a single line: “this is alive.”

Not a single line: “not every bad smell means death.”

He put the sheet back.

Said nothing.

Nora stood by the window.

“He didn’t know that living things sometimes smell like that,” she said.

Matti did not understand the words, but he understood that they were speaking about him. And again he said:

“Sorry.”

Marek briefly translated something to him in Finnish.

Not everything. Not Nora’s phrase word for word. Something simple, domestic, human. Probably: it was starter, for bread, they wanted to keep it.

Matti listened, looking at the floor.

Then he said several words in his own language. Marek nodded.

“He says he is sorry. He thought it was bad. He can bring new yeast.”

Lina closed her eyes.

Opened them.

“We have yeast.”

Marek did not translate at once.

“Lina...”

“We have yeast,” she repeated.

Then Marek translated:

“Yeast we have.”

Matti understood that he had offered the wrong thing.

That was even more painful.

Because he was offering replacement within a system where everything still looked replaceable.

The bad thing was thrown out — we will bring the good one.

The spoiled thing was removed — we will give a fresh one.

There wasn’t enough — we will bring more.

But what had been thrown away was not a quantity.

And not an ingredient.

It was the continuation of yesterday.

Lina went to the sink, took the clean jar, set it in front of herself. The jar was warm from the water and empty.

She looked inside.

Not for long.

Long enough.

Then she set the jar back upside down.

“Lina,” Thomas said.

She turned.

“I should have...”

“No.”

“Yesterday I wanted to write it down.”

“Yes.”

“I should have written it down.”

“What?”

He did not answer.

She did not help him.

That was right.

Because he had to find the impossibility himself.

“That it mustn’t be thrown away,” he said at last.

Lina looked at the clean jar.

“And how would he have understood that this was ‘it’?”

Thomas was silent.

David held the recipe sheet between two fingers.

He did not crumple it.

Just held it too tightly.

“The jar could have been labelled,” he said quietly.

Nick raised his eyes.

“It could.”

“Starter. Do not throw.”

“It could.”

“Then...”

David did not finish.

Because he saw it himself: then this jar, perhaps, would have remained. And another would not. What is labelled gets recognized. What is not labelled ends up in the rubbish again. One can expand the writing. Add warnings. Put labels. Make lists. Clarify. Hang tags on the living thing so it does not die from someone else’s order.

And still, someday, something will remain without a tag.

Sofia looked at the clean jar and felt a sour taste in her mouth.

Not smell.

Taste.

As if the mistake had happened somewhere closer than the sink.

Matti stood by the door, holding his cap in his hands. He had become unnecessary in a house where five minutes ago he had been needed. And that too was unfair. He had not wanted to become the person who threw away tomorrow. He had wanted to check the water, the stove, the gas, the rubbish.

Rubbish.

The word struck Sofia almost physically.

Rubbish is not a property of a thing.

Sometimes it is a name given to it too early.

Ivar had been standing by the window the whole time.

He had not interfered.

Thomas said again, now more quietly:

“He didn’t know.”

Ivar turned his head.

“There.”

One word.

Nothing else.

And it was enough.

Because the meaning was not that Matti was guilty. And not that he was innocent. The meaning was in that “didn’t know,” which turned out to be neither excuse nor sentence, but the very hole through which the living thing disappeared.

He did not know.

And threw it away.

Nora went to the rubbish bag.

Nick said at once:

“Don’t.”

She stopped.

“Why?”

“Because if you find it, it will be worse.”

“And if I don’t?”

“Also.”

She looked at him.

He was not joking.

The bag stood by the door, tied. Inside was wet cloth, shells, packaging, paper, scraps, and somewhere there — the thing that had been fed that morning. Perhaps it had smeared across the bag. Perhaps it still smelled. Perhaps it had already been mixed with other rubbish so that taking it out would only make the loss more visible and helpless.

Lina said:

“Leave it.”

Nora stepped away.

Matti quietly asked Marek:

“I take trash?”

Marek did not answer at once.

Looked at Lina.

Lina nodded.

Not sharply.

Not ceremonially.

Just nodded.

Matti took the bag.

Nick made a movement as if to help, but stopped.

The bag was not heavy.

Matti carried it out himself.

The door opened. Cold came in. The door closed. Outside there were steps on wet gravel. Then a metallic sound — the van container or crate. Then the silence of rain.

Lina sat down.

That was the first real gesture of loss.

Until then she had stood, held, spoken, answered. Now she sat on the bench by the wall and put her hands on her knees. Her fingers were still. On one nail there was still flour. A small white line against the skin.

Sofia looked at that flour and thought that a recipe would not remember the hands.

And now the hands would not bring back what had not been recognized.

Thomas went to the window.

“We can start again,” he said.

The phrase was careful.

Not stupid.

Even kind.

Nick closed his eyes.

David lowered the recipe sheet onto the table.

Lina did not answer at once.

“You can.”

“With new yeast.”

“You can.”

“Or from another bread.”

“You can.”

“So not everything is lost.”

Lina looked at him.

“Everything is never lost.”

Thomas seemed to breathe with relief.

“But that does not mean nothing is lost,” she added.

He lowered his eyes.

“Yes.”

David sat down opposite the recipe.

“That isn’t in the recipe.”

“What?” Sofia asked.

“The difference.”

“What difference?”

He looked at the empty jar by the sink.

“Between starting again and continuing.”

The phrase stayed in the room.

Very quiet.

Very exact.

Sofia felt that perhaps this was the most important thing David had said in all these days. Not because it was beautiful. Because it was painful and simple. One can start again. Continue — no longer.

Matti returned a few minutes later. Without the bag. With the wet cap in his hand. He came up to Lina.

“Sorry,” he said again.

Lina stood.

Not at once.

“I know,” she said.

He nodded.

“I bring yeast next time.”

Lina looked at him and seemed to understand: he still wanted to repair it with the thing he knew how to do. Bring. Replace. Give new. And there was no stupidity in that. It was his work.

“Thank you,” she said.

Not because the yeast would replace it.

Because a person was standing before her with guilt he could not properly name.

Matti nodded.

Collected his tools.

Checked one more window.

Even after the mistake, he continued to do the work. It was almost unbearable and right at the same time. The house still needed checking. The stove still had to draw. Water still had to run. Gas could still run out. The loss of the starter did not cancel a single domestic task.

Life often did exactly that: it did not stop respectfully enough.

When Matti left, the van took a long time getting out of the yard. The wheels slipped on the wet gravel, then caught the solid part, the engine moved farther away, quieter, disappeared beyond the trees. The house remained.

And order remained in it.

Clean towels on the shelf.

A new gas cylinder by the wall.

Coffee in the cupboard.

The stove checked.

The washed jar by the sink.

The recipe sheet on the table.

The absence by the stove.

That was the loudest thing.

Not even emptiness. Absence.

Emptiness is where nothing has been. Absence is where something was and disappeared.

The day stretched on strangely.

Nick barely joked. Once he said he was now afraid of all people who arrive with clean towels, and fell silent at once. Eva cooked soup. Marek chopped kindling too evenly. Thomas stood for a long time by the shelf where the jar had been, then went down to the water. David folded the recipe in half, then unfolded it again, as if he did not know whether he had the right now to leave it neat.

Nora sat on the floor and held her cup with both hands.

“Sour is not always bad,” she said.

Nick looked at her.

“That is a little too close to a moral right now.”

“Then forget it.”

“I won’t manage.”

“There.”

He gave a small smile.

Not funny.

Just acknowledged defeat.

Toward evening the rain finally weakened. It did not stop, but became more transparent. A dull light began to seep through the windows, as if the day, before leaving, had decided to check that they were still inside. Lina rose and went to the shelf by the stove. Nothing stood there. She ran a finger over the wood. Dust and a little flour stayed on her finger.

“No need to bake today,” Eva said.

“I wasn’t going to.”

“Good.”

“We’ll see tomorrow.”

Thomas, sitting by the window, raised his head.

“Can I label the shelf?”

Everyone looked at him.

He flushed.

“I don’t mean... not now. Just if something living is there.”

Lina looked at him for a long time.

Then said:

“You can.”

“Really?”

“Yes.”

“It will help.”

“Sometimes.”

He nodded.

And did not say “but not always.”

That was good.

Because everyone heard it.

Later Sofia went out to the woodshed.

She thought she was going to the place of the morning ants, but her feet carried her a little farther, to the low place behind the shed, where the wet grass gathered water. It smelled of earth, damp chips of wood, mushrooms, and something sour too — not starter, but wet rot, the work of decay. No one threw that smell away. Not because it was pleasant. Because in the forest there was no caretaker obliged to return everything to a clean appearance.

She stood and listened to water dripping from the shed roof.

And then a short glint appeared before her eyes.

Not large.

Not solemn.

An older woman by a low hearth is looking for a small clay pot.

“Where is the sour?” she asks.

The young man, the same one with the stick and the notches, raises his head.

“I cleared it away.”

“Where?”

“Behind the house. It smelled bad.”

The woman does not shout.

Does not strike him.

Does not curse.

She only squats beside the empty place where the pot had stood.

On the table lies the board with the notches.

How much grain.

How many circles.

How much flour.

Everything is in place.

Except the thing that was supposed to begin tomorrow.

The young man says:

“I didn’t know.”

The woman nods.

And that makes it worse for him.

The glint disappeared.

Sofia returned to the house already at dusk.

Ivar was sitting on the porch.

“You saw?” he asked.

“Yes.”

“What?”

“They threw it away too.”

He nodded.

“And also not from malice.”

“Most often, that is how it happens.”

She sat beside him.

Behind their backs, light burned in the house. Someone moved a chair. Nick was speaking quietly, without his usual pressure. Marek was closing the damper. Eva laughed shortly — not merrily, but alive. Lina, it seemed, was washing a cup. Or simply holding it under water longer than necessary.

“How can this not be lost?” Sofia asked.

Ivar did not answer at once.

Then said:

“Sometimes it can’t.”

Sofia was silent.

Not because she understood.

Because the answer did not close anything.

And that was good.

In the evening the recipe lay on the table again.

Nick wanted to put it away in the notebook, but David said, “let it lie.” Thomas did not object. The sheet had become slightly wavy from the damp air. The ink — no, the pencil — held. “Not forgetting” could be seen at the bottom of the page. “Feed” too.

Sofia looked at those words and thought that they were not false.

They had simply proved too little.

By the stove nothing stood under a cloth.

And that was more important than everything written.

The recipe remained.

The continuation had been thrown away.
