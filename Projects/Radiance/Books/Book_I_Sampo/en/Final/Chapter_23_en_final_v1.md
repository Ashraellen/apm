# Chapter Twenty-Three. Not a Thing

After that, no one spoke at once.

Papers lay on the table.

Sheets that until recently had seemed like an attempt to assemble an answer now simply occupied space. Some overlapped others at the corners. The map lay over the recipe. The sheet with the word “gift” almost covered the old arrow toward the empty circle. Nearby, a little askew, lay “Sampo?” — apart, although Sofia no longer understood why it had to be separate.

David held the pencil between his fingers.

Did not write.

The pencil was sharp. Too ready for a hand that did not know where to lower it.

Thomas stood at the edge of the table and looked at the word “Sampo” as if it were behaving dishonestly. It did not answer the question, but also did not leave the sheet.

“Then what are we looking for?” he asked.

Nick stood by the door, still in mismatched socks, a mug in his hand.

“Please note: the question has become more cautious.”

Eva gave him a short look.

“I’m silent,” Nick said. “Almost.”

David did not lift his head.

“I don’t know.”

“But we are looking for something,” Thomas said.

“Probably.”

“Or not anymore?”

David looked at the pencil.

“I don’t know.”

Thomas exhaled.

Not angrily.

With the fatigue of a person who is ready to accept complexity, but not ready to lose the question altogether.

“If it isn’t where we were looking, then where is it?”

The word “where” sounded too clear in the room.

As if a small metal thing had fallen onto the table.

Sofia felt something tighten inside.

Not from fear.

From inaccuracy.

Before, “where” had been a normal word. Even necessary. Where the trace was. Where the map was. Where the place was. Where the house was. Where the water had gone the wrong way. Where to put the stone. Where to hold the board.

But now it demanded a place for what had already stopped standing still.

She looked at the map.

On it, everything had a place.

Except what moved.

Thomas noticed her look.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

“No, not nothing. You look like that when you see that the question is wrong.”

“I don’t know if it’s wrong.”

“But?”

Sofia ran her finger along the edge of the sheet without touching the word.

“It’s small.”

Nick quietly set his mug on the windowsill.

“That is a very dangerous beginning to a sentence.”

“Nick,” Eva said.

“I’m not interfering. I’m protecting all of us from sudden depth.”

Thomas did not react. He was looking at Sofia.

“Small is not an answer.”

“I know.”

“Then how should we ask?”

The question hung there.

Nick did not joke.

David did not write.

Lina, by the window, took the rag from the back of the chair, touched the edge, realized it was damp, and hung it back. She did not participate in the conversation. But from her movement the cloth swayed, and one drop fell from it onto the floor.

The drop was clearer than all the words on the table.

Thomas took the map.

Held it with both hands, carefully, by the edges.

“It was needed,” he said.

“Yes,” David said.

“We wouldn’t have gone there without it.”

“Perhaps.”

“Not perhaps. Definitely.”

David nodded.

Thomas put the map back.

“But what should have been there was not there.”

“There was a place,” Sofia said.

“But not an answer.”

“Yes.”

He took another sheet.

On it was written “след”. An old word, already slightly smudged by fingers. Beside it stood an arrow. Before, it had seemed like a direction. Now — like a gesture that did not know where to come to rest.

“The trace was needed too,” Thomas said.

“Yes,” David said.

“But it did not lead.”

Nick said quietly:

“A trace rarely assumes obligations in general.”

Eva looked at him.

“I know,” he said. “Silent.”

Thomas put the sheet down.

“I don’t like a question that cannot be placed correctly.”

It sounded honest.

Sofia suddenly felt tenderness toward him. Not warm, not beautiful. Simply a brief recognition: it was truly hard for him. Not because he wanted to win the argument. Because without the right question he did not know where to put his attention.

David moved the sheet “Sampo?” closer to himself.

The pencil rose above the paper.

Stopped.

“What do you want to write?” Sofia asked.

“I don’t know.”

“Then why the pencil?”

He smiled almost without sound.

“A habit of threatening emptiness.”

Nick looked at him with respect.

“That is almost a coat of arms already.”

David put the pencil on the table.

Then took it again.

“If we put it in the center,” he said, “everything else will become arranged around it.”

“And?” Thomas asked.

David was silent.

He took the sheet “Sampo?” and placed it in the center of the table.

Very carefully.

Almost solemnly.

Around it he laid out the others: trace, map, place, recipe, mark, condition, gift, participation. Some of the sheets lay crooked. One edge of the recipe bent upward. The map covered half the word “place.” David adjusted it.

Now it looked like a scheme.

Even beautiful.

Too beautiful.

In the center was the word.

Around it — roads toward it.

For several seconds everyone felt easier.

It was noticeable. Even Thomas straightened. Even Nick did not immediately destroy it. Even Sofia felt a small relief: here, finally, at least some form. One could look. One could understand what was around what.

Then the relief left.

Not sharply.

Like warmth from a mug.

Sofia looked at the sheet in the center and felt that something had become untrue precisely because it had become neat.

If Sampo was in the center, then everything else became around it.

Trace.

Map.

Recipe.

Gift.

Participation.

People.

Water.

Ditch.

Mud under the nail.

Bread crumbs in the bowl.

Everything turned into a path toward one sheet.

And yesterday the water had not gone toward a sheet.

The potato had not come to a sheet.

The board had not held a sheet.

Sofia’s hand had not hurt for the sake of the center of the table.

She reached out her hand.

Not at once.

Her fingers still ached slightly.

She took the sheet “Sampo?” from the center and placed it among the others. Not on top. Not below. Between the map and the sheet “gift,” slightly over the corner of the recipe, so that the question mark stood beside the water stain on the notebook cover.

The scheme broke.

There was no answer anymore.

But it became more honest.

David looked at the sheet.

“Is that better?”

Sofia looked.

For a long time.

“I don’t know.”

“But you moved it.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

She wanted to say: because in the center it was lying.

She did not say it.

Because that would have been too certain.

“It was lonely there,” Nick said quietly.

Sofia looked at him.

He shrugged.

“I also sometimes land in meaning by accident. Let’s not pretend it’s a system.”

David took the pencil again.

“If it isn’t in the center, where do the arrows lead?”

Thomas sat down.

“Maybe not arrows.”

Everyone looked at him.

He himself was surprised that he had said it.

“What?” David asked.

Thomas frowned.

“I don’t know. Maybe an arrow demands too much.”

Nick cautiously raised a finger.

“I would like to note: Thomas has just questioned straightness as a civilizational principle. This is a historical moment.”

“Nick.”

“That’s all. Silent. I’m proud silently.”

David looked at the pencil.

“An arrow demands a from and a to.”

“And here?” Sofia asked.

He moved the pencil through the air above the table.

Not on the paper.

Simply above the sheets.

“Here, it’s through, all the time.”

The word was small.

Not an answer.

More like an accidental passage between two boards.

Lina came up to the table to take the knife, which lay between the papers and was in her way. David immediately tried to move the sheets away, but she only said:

“Later.”

“Later what?” he asked.

“You’ll put them away.”

“When?”

She looked at the rag by the window.

“When it dries.”

And left with the knife.

The phrase was entirely domestic.

And precisely for that reason no one began to take it apart.

Marek came in from outside.

There was new mud on his boots. He stopped at the threshold, looked at the table, then at everyone.

“Map again?”

“Not entirely anymore,” Nick said.

Marek took off his jacket.

“The water is going normally.”

“Completely?” Thomas asked.

“No.”

“But?”

“Where it should.”

“For long?”

Marek hung the jacket on the hook.

“For today.”

Thomas exhaled.

“For today.”

“Sometimes that is a measure,” Marek said.

He went to the stove.

Added nothing.

Nick leaned toward Sofia.

“Everyone here has started talking as if writing on stones. I’m alarmed.”

“You too,” Sofia said.

“Me? I protect conversation from excessive granite.”

“In mismatched socks.”

He looked down.

“These are not mismatched socks. This is a dialogue of traditions.”

Thomas suddenly said:

“Then where is Sampo?”

The words returned.

The room did not become quiet.

Rather, everything at once stopped moving.

Even Lina by the window stopped adjusting the rag. David lifted his head. Nick did not draw breath for a joke. Sofia felt the word “where” again trying to put everything into a place.

Ivar stood by the door.

Nick flinched almost imperceptibly.

“I should start wearing a bell so I know when you materialize,” he said.

Ivar did not answer him.

He was looking at Thomas.

Not sternly.

Not softly.

Simply in such a way that Thomas’s question remained standing in place and could not pretend it had not been heard.

“You are still asking ‘where,’” Ivar said.

Thomas slowly turned to him.

“And how should one ask?”

Ivar looked at the table.

At the sheets.

At the pencil in David’s hand.

At the word, which no longer lay in the center.

“For now, not at all.”

Thomas opened his mouth.

Closed it.

“That does not help.”

“No.”

Ivar went to the stove, took one log from the basket, and placed it beside the firebox. Not into the fire. Beside it. Then he stepped back to the wall and said nothing else.

It was almost cruel.

And almost relieving.

Because no one received a ready phrase that could immediately be written down, disputed, or hidden in a pocket.

David looked again at the sheet.

At the word “Sampo?”

Then took a new clean sheet.

Put it beside the others.

Wrote at the top:

Сампо —

Stopped.

The dash stretched after the word like a small bridge one could not yet step onto.

David wanted to put the next word.

It was visible in the hand.

The hand knew the habit: after a dash one must give a definition. After a question — a direction. After a center — arrows.

The pencil touched the paper.

Did not leave a line.

“If I write,” David said, “it will at once become what I wrote.”

“Only on the sheet,” Thomas said.

“That used to be enough for me.”

Nick said quietly:

“That phrase, I fear, will outlive many of us.”

Eva did not stop him.

David did not smile, but did not shut down either.

“You can leave it unfinished,” Sofia said.

“Then it will be unfinished.”

“Yes.”

“You say that as if it is normal.”

Sofia looked at the ditch outside the window. It could not be seen from here, only the wet light and the lower branch from which rare drops fell. But she knew the water was still going past the house. Not perfectly. Not forever. For today.

“For today,” she said.

David looked at her.

Then at Marek.

Marek said nothing.

That, it seemed, was agreement.

Nora came up to the table and stopped beside the edge. She did not touch the papers. Only looked at the clean sheet with the unfinished dash.

“Can it be left?” she asked.

David turned to her.

“What?”

“Like that.”

He looked at the sheet.

Then nodded.

“It can.”

Nora went back to the wall, where someone else’s boot still lay with its lace tied.

Her phrase explained nothing.

And therefore remained useful.

Thomas sat beside David.

“I still don’t like it.”

“Me neither,” David said.

“Good.”

“Why good?”

“Means I’m not alone.”

Nick lifted his mug.

“The great union of people who dislike the absence of an answer. I am ready to join, but without membership fees.”

“You’re already in,” Eva said.

“Excellent. I was accepted without my knowledge. Finally an organization worthy of trust.”

Sofia smiled.

But did not take her eyes off the sheet.

Сампо —

The dash stood after the word.

Not like a road.

More like a held breath.

David held the pencil above the sheet.

He could draw an arrow.

To the map.

To the recipe.

To the word “gift.”

To the empty circle.

To anything.

The hand trembled slightly.

Not from fear.

From habit.

Then stopped.

For the first time Sofia saw a person not do what he knew how to do, not because he had given up, but because doing it now would be inaccurate.

Outside, a drop broke loose from the roof and struck the edge of the windowsill.

Lina by the window finally took down the rag. Checked it.

This time it was dry.

She folded it carefully and laid it on the back of the chair.

“Now it can,” she said.

Not to David.

Not to the sheet.

To the rag.

But no one in the room clarified.

David was still holding the pencil above the sheet.

The arrow did not appear.

And for the first time it looked not like loss, but like caution.
