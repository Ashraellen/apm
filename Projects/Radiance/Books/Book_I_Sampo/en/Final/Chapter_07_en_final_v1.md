# Chapter Seven. The Map

By evening the house had not dried completely.

It had only pretended it had coped.

The jackets hung by the door like heavy dark bodies. The sleeves no longer dripped, but the fabric still held water inside, like a grievance decent people do not show in front of guests. Old newspapers had been laid out under the boots by the bench. The newspapers had gone soft at the edges, the ink had run a little, and now someone’s news from yesterday lay beneath northern mud without the slightest dignity.

The stove worked evenly.

On the table stood mugs, bowls, bread, a knife, and a saucer with butter that had grown too soft near the heat. Nora sat by the window, her palm resting on her enamel mug. Nick had taken a place closer to the stove and held his tea in both hands as if tea were the last official institution of common sense. Thomas stood by the window and still looked toward the forest, although almost nothing could be made out beyond the glass anymore.

David sat at the table with his phone.

The screen again showed grey.

Then white.

Then a piece of road that did not resemble the one they had driven on.

Then grey again.

“The map won’t load,” he said.

“It is wise,” Nick replied. “It has gone into silence.”

“I just need to understand where we were.”

“We were in a wet forest. It was around us. Then we came back. End of geographical report.”

“No, not like that.”

David was not irritated. That was worse. He spoke calmly, almost gently, like a person who is no longer arguing but assembling something necessary inside.

“We went out from the porch, took the main path, then there was the old path to the left, then the boards, then the stone with moss, then the birch, then the place with the stones.”

“You are listing places,” Nick said.

“Yes.”

“Why?”

David raised his eyes.

“To understand how they are arranged.”

The room became a little quieter.

Not much. The stove murmured. A spoon chimed against the mug in Eva’s hands. Somewhere in the hallway Marek set a boot on the floor and swore quietly because newspaper had stuck to the sole. Everything was ordinary.

But the word “arranged” lay on the table beside the bread.

Sofia felt it.

After the forest, after the trace, after the moss that wanted to be a sign not because it wanted to, but because a person had already tilted his head, the word “arranged” was more dangerous than it seemed. It did not say “find.” It did not say “Sampo.” It did not say “path.”

It only said: it can be connected.

That turned out to be enough.

Thomas stepped away from the window.

“The boards weren’t right after the old path,” he said.

David turned to him.

“Yes. There was a turn between them.”

“And the place where the roots came out onto the path.”

“The roots were after the boards.”

“No. Before.”

“Before the boards there were the tracks by the porch, the old path, and the descent.”

“The roots were there too.”

Nick slowly set his mug on the table.

“This is how evenings die.”

Eva looked at him.

“No one is making you take part.”

“That’s the horror. I already remember where the roots were.”

Sofia involuntarily looked at him.

Nick caught her gaze and spread his hands.

“What? They were before the fir stand. Big, wet, one like someone’s hand, but I categorically refuse to develop that.”

“Before the fir stand,” David said, and again moved his finger over the screen, as if the phone could understand a spoken correction.

The screen did not understand.

“Give me paper,” Thomas said.

“No,” Nick said.

Everyone looked at him.

He himself was surprised by the sharpness.

“I mean... why paper?”

“To draw it,” Thomas said.

“Exactly.”

“You’re afraid of paper?”

“Not of paper. Of what people do on it — yes.”

Marek came into the kitchen holding a boot and a piece of newspaper stuck to the sole.

“If someone is afraid of paper, I have a candidate for destruction.”

Nick nodded toward David.

“They want a map.”

“Who is ‘they’?” David asked.

“So far, two. But the contagion is high.”

Marek tore the newspaper from the boot, threw it into the bucket by the door, and looked at the table.

“A map of what?”

David said:

“The path.”

Thomas said almost at the same time:

“The place.”

The two words did not coincide.

And precisely because of that they became worse.

Marek looked first at one, then at the other.

“The path dries by itself. The place is not going to run away.”

“We just want to understand where we were,” David said.

Marek lifted one shoulder.

“Then draw the house. A stick coming out of it. Then mud. Then another stick. Then back. Done.”

“Thank you for the cartographic contribution,” Nick said.

“You’re welcome.”

Marek went to the stove to set the boots closer to the heat.

Ivar sat in the corner by the window, where the light had almost gone out. He was not visible at once. He was repairing the strap on an old bag: needle, dark thread, dense leather, slow movement of the fingers. He did not take part. Sofia even thought he was deliberately occupied with something that required hands, not explanations.

Lina brought a notebook from the next room.

“Here is paper,” she said, and set it on the table.

Nick looked at her reproachfully.

“You too, Lina.”

“The paper isn’t guilty.”

“That is always how they justify it.”

The notebook was old, with a thick grey cover. On the first pages there were grocery lists, some dates, house numbers or perhaps phone numbers, a few words in a language Sofia did not know. Lina leafed closer to the middle and found a clean page.

David took the pencil.

Not solemnly.

Rather with relief.

That was the first thing that truly alarmed Sofia.

Not the pencil.

Not the paper.

The exhale.

He had not drawn anything yet, but he already felt lighter, because the hand had received a surface.

On a map it is not wet.

The thought came before Ivar said it.

He lifted his head from the strap only after several seconds.

“On a map it is not wet,” he said.

And lowered his eyes to the bag again.

Nothing more.

No one in the room answered.

Because the phrase did not argue. It simply laid the wet forest beside the dry page.

David still drew the first line.

Crooked.

“Here is the house,” he said, placing a square.

“The house is not square,” Nick noted.

“It’s a diagram.”

“Exactly.”

“Nick.”

“I’m silent. The forest has simply been issued a format.”

Thomas bent over the paper.

“The porch is here.”

“Yes.”

“The path doesn’t go straight out, first it goes a little to the right.”

“Like this?”

“More.”

David erased the line with his thumb. A grey trace remained on the paper. He drew a new one.

Sofia watched the forest enter the pencil.

Not at once.

Resisting.

First the house became a square. Then the porch — a short stroke. Then the path — a line. Then the old path to the left — another, thinner line. The boards — two little crosspieces. No one drew the moose track, but David placed a dot there.

“What is that for?” Nora asked.

“There was a track there.”

“A moose’s.”

“Yes.”

“The moose is not part of the map.”

David looked at the dot.

“It is a landmark.”

“For whom?”

He wanted to answer.

He did not.

Nick said quietly:

“The moose has been posthumously appointed an employee of navigation.”

“It is alive,” Nora said.

“All the more outrageous.”

Sofia involuntarily smiled. The smile came out small and disappeared at once.

Because the dot remained.

Nora saw this.

Eva set a plate of bread on the table.

“Eat, before the map eats everyone.”

“Far too late,” Nick said.

Thomas took a piece of bread, but did not bite. He held it in his hand and looked at the page.

“The stone with moss was after the boards.”

“Here?” David asked.

“No, farther. After the rise.”

“And the notch?”

“After the stone.”

“Not right away.”

“Almost right away.”

“There was another narrow path between them.”

“The whole forest was narrow.”

“No, especially there.”

David added a dot.

Then another.

Stone.

Birch.

Stones in a half-circle.

House.

Boards.

Old path.

Each thing on the paper received a place. Each place — a name. Each name — the possibility of being connected with another.

Sofia suddenly understood why David was becoming calmer.

On the table there were no wet roots anymore.

No slippery board.

No smell of raw bark.

No water inside the moose track.

No moment when a person does not know where to put his foot.

There was a line.

And the line obeyed.

“Too straight,” Thomas said.

“What?”

“From the boards to the stone. We didn’t walk straight.”

David erased.

“Like this?”

“No. The path went like this.”

Thomas took the pencil.

Nick sighed.

“The transfer of the tool has taken place.”

Thomas drew the line himself. Slower, more surely. His line was not more accurate — Sofia saw that, though she could not have proved it. But Thomas held the pencil as if accuracy were a matter of dignity.

“If the old path goes left from here,” he said, “and the stones are there, then between them there is a section we did not pass through.”

David leaned closer.

“Yes.”

Nick said very quietly:

“There.”

Eva looked at him.

“What?”

“Nothing. An emptiness has just been born, and now it has to be filled.”

Sofia looked at the map.

Between the thin line of the old path and the dot of the stones there really was a white place.

Ordinary clean paper.

Nothing more.

But after Thomas’s words it stopped being clean paper. It became “a section.” A place where they had not been. A space that might contain.

Sofia felt the familiar cold.

The empty bowl.

The empty box.

The empty place on the map.

Every emptiness, once it received a form, began to wait.

“We didn’t go there,” David said.

“Because there may be a bog there,” Marek answered without turning from the stove.

“May be?”

“In the forest, many things may be. That is not an invitation.”

Thomas ran his finger over the white gap without touching the paper.

“But if the old path goes around that place...”

“It may be going around a wet pit,” Marek said.

“Or something important.”

Marek turned.

“Thomas. People usually go around a pit not because it is important. But because it is wet.”

Nick raised his mug.

“To wet pits that make no claim to metaphysics.”

Nora said:

“But a person can make the claim for them.”

Nick looked at her.

“That was very unkind.”

“I know.”

Sofia looked at the white place.

It was small.

Smaller than a palm.

On real ground it could be a patch of forest, a wet hollow, a few bushes, hummocks, roots, water under moss, mosquitoes if it had been another season, or simply an inconvenient piece of the way. On paper it was a place where one wanted to put a question.

And the question was already there.

Although no one had written it.

Ivar tightened the knot on the strap. Checked the seam with his finger, bit off the thread, and set the bag beside him.

“On a map it is not wet,” Nick repeated quietly, but not mockingly.

Rather like a person who had only now understood why the phrase was unpleasant.

Ivar did not answer.

Beyond the windows, evening grew thicker. The forest disappeared not at once, but in layers. First the separate branches went, then the spaces between the trunks, then the boundary of the path itself. Only dark masses remained, and the glass began to reflect the kitchen: the table, faces, map, mugs, stove-light.

Now the map no longer lay simply on the table.

It was reflected in the window over the forest.

A white page — on dark trunks.

Pencil lines — where beyond the glass it was wet and nothing wanted to be a line.

Sofia turned away.

Too late.

The page already held her.

David placed another dot.

“The water should be here.”

Marek said:

“The lake is in a completely different direction.”

“I don’t mean the lake. A stream or low ground. The water went under the boards and farther on.”

“Water usually goes farther on.”

“But if its direction coincides with the old path...”

“It does not coincide. You drew it in.”

David fell silent.

The line had indeed appeared only now. It had not been in the forest. In the forest there had been water under the board, wet moss, mud, low ground. On the map, David connected them with one faint line.

It looked convincing.

That was exactly the bad part.

“Erase it,” Sofia said.

She was surprised by her own voice.

David raised his eyes.

“Why?”

“We didn’t see that.”

“But the water had to go somewhere.”

“It had to. But not where it is convenient for us.”

Nick looked at her attentively.

Thomas set the bread back on the plate.

David slowly erased the line. A trace of graphite remained on the page. Barely visible. But it did not disappear entirely.

“Even when you erase, it remains,” Nora said.

Nick closed his eyes.

“Nora, I am asking you to be less right sometimes.”

“I wasn’t trying.”

“That’s the problem.”

Sofia looked at the grey trace of the erased line.

It was almost invisible.

But now the eye knew where to look.

Erasing had not been enough.

And that too was like the forest.

Like a trace that would spread into a puddle, but remain in the head of the one who had managed to see the direction.

The air in the kitchen grew denser.

Not dramatically. Simply everyone had been looking at one page for too long. The tea was cooling. The butter had finally softened completely. Crumbs were drying near the edge of the plate. The stove crackled, as if reminding them that there are things that work without a scheme.

Lina came up to the table, glanced at the map, and said:

“The house is drawn too close to the forest.”

“It stands close,” David answered.

“No. When you are walking from the forest wet, it is farther.”

Nick raised a finger.

“That is a serious cartographic observation.”

“I’m not joking.”

“Neither am I.”

David looked at the square of the house. Then at the window. Then back at the square.

He did not correct it.

And that was right.

Because a distance measured by wet clothing cannot be placed on a page.

Sofia thought this and immediately felt how the thought wanted to become beautiful. She stopped it.

No need.

Let it remain wet clothing.

Not a formula.

The map on the table trembled slightly from a draft. The paper lifted at one corner, then lay back down. Thomas automatically pressed it with his fingers.

Too quickly.

The way one holds not paper, but a possibility.

And then the kitchen went away.

Did not disappear.

Stepped aside.

The light of the stove grew redder, deeper, like coals in a low winter house. The table remained, but became another: rougher, lower, with cracks clogged by flour, ash, and old dirt. Instead of the notebook, a board lay on it. Charcoal dots had been placed on the board.

Not a map.

Not yet.

An attempt.

Around the board sat people.

Those who had walked through snow, who had seen grass under thin ice, who had stood by the stream where the track disappeared, who had heard that somewhere there was it. Their faces were dark from smoke and weariness. One woman had an empty bowl on her knees. A young man held a small stick and moved it between the charcoal dots.

“Here the snow was thin,” he said.

He placed a black dot.

“Here the water did not freeze.”

A second dot.

“Here the stone sounded hollow.”

A third.

“Here the track went into the stream.”

A fourth.

“Here the old man saw light.”

The old man sitting by the wall coughed.

“I said I might have seen.”

“You said you saw.”

“I said my eyes were tired.”

“But was there light?”

The old man was silent.

The young man placed a fifth dot.

The woman with the bowl looked at the board without trust.

“You are drawing what is not there.”

“I am connecting what was there.”

“There was snow. There was water. There was a track. There was an old man who was cold.”

“And all of it is near.”

“Near on the board.”

He raised his eyes.

“And in the world?”

The woman did not answer.

Because in the world everything was different.

In the world, between snow and water there were days of walking, wet feet, quarrels, a lost knife, a stranger’s door, a night without fire, a coughing child, a goat that lay down and did not rise, fear, exhaustion, a phrase misunderstood, a forgotten turn, a track that was no longer there in the morning.

On the board, between the dots, there was a line.

The line was lighter.

The young man drew it in charcoal.

Once.

Twice.

Three times.

The dots became order.

People bent closer.

Not because they had seen the truth.

Because order warmed.

For one short minute.

“If we go this way,” someone said, “we’ll come to it.”

“To what?” the woman asked.

“To what holds.”

“Did you draw what holds?”

“I drew the way.”

“No. This way you are not so cold.”

He struck the table with his palm.

The bowl on the woman’s knees jumped.

“You always speak against it.”

“I say the board is dry.”

“What?”

“Your feet don’t freeze on it. That’s all.”

The young man looked at the board.

At the charcoal dots.

At the lines.

At the place between them, where, if one looked long enough, it began to seem that the missing thing was about to appear on its own.

He took a handful of grain from a small pouch and placed one grain where the lines almost met.

“Here,” he said.

Never had the word “here” sounded so empty.

And so convincing.

The people around him exhaled.

Someone put a hand on his chest to keep his heart in place. Someone wept without tears. The old man by the wall turned away.

The woman with the bowl looked at the grain.

The grain lay calmly.

It did not know it had become a place.

It did not know that a road was already being built around it.

It was grain.

And therefore, perhaps, the only living thing on the board.

Sofia smelled charcoal.

Her fingers clenched by themselves.

The kitchen returned.

On the table lay the notebook.

Instead of grain — a crumb of bread.

The crumb had accidentally fallen onto the white place between the old path and the stones.

David looked at it.

Thomas too.

Nick managed to see their looks.

“No,” he said.

“What no?” Thomas asked.

“The crumb is not participating.”

Sofia quickly took the crumb and placed it on the plate.

Too quickly.

That made it clear that she had been frightened too.

David exhaled like a person caught not in an action, but in readiness.

“I didn’t say anything.”

“That is exactly why it’s frightening,” Nick replied.

Eva sat beside Sofia.

“Maybe put the paper away?”

David laid his palm on the notebook.

Not sharply.

But he held it.

“I don’t think we should put it away.”

“Why?”

“Because we have already begun. If we put it away, it will only get worse. Everyone will hold his own map in his head.”

This was reasonable.

And precisely because of that it did not calm anyone.

Thomas nodded.

“It’s better to see what we are doing.”

Nick looked at him.

“People sometimes say that before very bad decisions.”

“And sometimes before honest ones.”

“Yes. That is the inconvenience.”

Sofia looked at the map.

It was bad.

Inaccurate.

Almost funny, if one looked soberly: the square of the house, the line of the path, several dots, the boards as two little sticks, the stones as a half-circle, the erased line of water, the white place where they had not been. Any normal person would have said: this is not a map, but a sketch on a wet evening.

But there was no normal person left in the room.

They had all already seen the forest after the map.

And the forest had changed.

Not outside.

Inside.

Now one could think: there, beyond the old path, there is a white place. There, between water and stones, a connection is missing. There, where the moss grew along a crack, perhaps it was not simply a stone. There, where the notch had almost vanished, perhaps someone knew why he made it.

“Perhaps” had begun to work as an instrument.

Sofia suddenly understood that it was sharper than a knife.

A knife at least cuts what it touches.

“Perhaps” cuts what is not there yet.

She wanted to say this aloud.

She did not.

Nick looked at her anyway, as if he had heard.

Ivar rose and came to the table. Everyone moved back slightly, though he had not asked.

He looked at the map.

Not long.

Then at the window, where the same map was reflected over the darkness.

“What should we do?” David asked.

Ivar looked at him.

“With what?”

“With the map.”

“Set it down, if you are tired of holding it.”

“I’m serious.”

“So am I.”

David fell silent.

Ivar did not take the pencil. Did not correct the line. Did not erase the dot of the moose track. Did not say that the map was false. Did not say that the map was dangerous. He simply stepped back to the stove and added a log.

The wood did not catch at once.

First it blackened along the edge.

Then a thin red line appeared inside.

Then the fire went deeper.

Sofia thought: this too is a map.

No.

She stopped the thought.

No need.

Not everything that has a line is a map.

Sometimes wood simply burns.

She stood up and went to the window. Behind the glass, nothing could be seen except their kitchen. The forest had disappeared. But she knew it was there: wet, dark, heavy, not drawn, not reduced, not obliged to coincide with the lines on the table.

Behind her David spoke more quietly:

“If it’s light tomorrow, we can check only this section. Not search. Just look.”

“‘Just look’ has already happened,” Nick said.

“Then walk through it.”

“That word is more honest.”

Thomas said:

“We still need to understand what that place between the old path and the stones is.”

Marek answered from the hall:

“It is wet.”

“You don’t know that for sure.”

“I know enough.”

“Enough for what?”

Marek did not answer.

Sofia stood by the window and felt fatigue rising in her legs. The forest was nearby. The map was nearby. The house was between them, and that did not make it safer. The house knew how to dry clothes, heat water, hold bread, hide things in the pantry. But it did not know how to return innocence to the gaze.

If a person had already seen a white place on the map, it remained.

Even when the paper was closed.

Eva did cover the notebook with her palm after all.

“Enough for today.”

Nick said quietly:

“At last, a normal cartographic measure.”

David did not argue.

Nor did Thomas.

But their silence was not refusal.

Rather, postponement.

As if the map had not disappeared, but had simply entered each of them separately.

Lina closed the notebook.

The grey cover lay over lines, dots, boards, the white place, and the erased water. From the outside, the notebook became simply a notebook again.

Sofia knew: that was not true.

Not because there was anything mystical in it.

Because they had already seen.

And now not seeing was harder.

Beyond the window, the wind moved the branches a little. One drop broke loose and vanished into the dark. On the glass remained a trace of light from the kitchen.

Sofia returned to the table.

Sat down.

Looked at the closed notebook.

Then at the bread, the bowl, Nora’s mug, the knife, the soft butter, the wet newspapers under the boots.

Everything was in place.

Everything was ordinary.

And everything could already end up on a page.

They had found nothing.

Now they had a scheme.

And because of that everyone seemed to feel lighter.

Sofia understood this not as a thought.

As cold under the ribs.

The forest had not become clearer.

It had become smaller.
