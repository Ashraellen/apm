# Chapter Fourteen. Label

In the morning the shelf by the stove was empty.

Not simply free.

An empty shelf is when something can be put on it.

This one was empty differently.

Sofia noticed it at once, before she had even managed to wake fully. The house lived by its small morning things: someone poured water into the kettle, someone moved a chair, someone coughed behind the wall, Nick spent too long looking for his mug and muttered that the house had begun hiding objects as a form of upbringing. But by the stove, on that same warm shelf where yesterday the jar under the cloth had stood, there was nothing.

And this nothing held more firmly than a thing.

The clean jar stood by the sink upside down.

It had dried.

The glass had become transparent and almost guilty, though glass, of course, could not be guilty. On the bottom, if one looked from the side, there remained a thin whitish line from the water. Matti had washed it well. Very well. So well that inside there was no smell, no trace, no yesterday.

The recipe lay on the table.

The sheet had bent slightly from the damp. At the bottom it still said: “feed,” “not forgetting.” The words had preserved themselves better than the thing they referred to.

Sofia did not touch the sheet.

She went to the window and looked out at the yard. The rain had finally stopped, but everything around looked as if it had only gone behind the trees and was standing there, waiting to be called again. The gravel shone, the woodshed had darkened, the wet bark by the shed seemed almost black. The forest had not become refreshed after the rain. It had become deeper.

Behind her, Thomas came in quietly.

Sofia heard not even his steps, but the sound of paper.

In his hands he had a small piece of white tape, a pencil, and scissors. The tape had been torn carefully, but the edges were still uneven. Thomas did not look at Sofia. He looked at the shelf.

“Good morning,” he said.

“Morning.”

He went to the table, put the tape beside the recipe, and wrote in large letters, in English:

STARTER. DO NOT THROW.

He wrote slowly. Too evenly. Then he looked at the inscription like a person who had come on time — and was still late.

Nick appeared in the doorway with a mug.

“Is that a statement of intent or a monument?”

Thomas did not answer.

Nick looked at the empty shelf, at the tape, at the clean jar by the sink.

“Understood,” he said more quietly.

And did not joke anymore.

Lina came in later.

In her hands she had a small sack of flour and a clean spoon. She saw the tape on the table. Read it. Then looked at Thomas.

“Good,” she said.

He seemed to have been waiting for something else.

“Really?”

“Yes.”

“I thought you would say it was useless.”

“Sometimes useful.”

“But not enough.”

“Yes.”

“I know.”

Lina nodded.

Not like a teacher.

Like a person who hears that the other is truly not speaking in order to win.

She took the clean jar from the sink, set it on the table, and looked inside. The emptiness in the jar was no longer yesterday’s. Yesterday it had been the consequence of a mistake. Today it had become a place.

“Will this be new?” David asked.

He stood by the door, not yet coming in. Apparently he too had come to the sound of the tape, or to that morning pull that gathers people around the unfinished.

“Yes,” Lina said.

“New,” he repeated.

“Yes.”

“Not continuation.”

Lina looked at him.

“No.”

He nodded.

Sofia saw how that word passed through him again. Not as yesterday, when it was fresh and cutting. Today it had become quieter, but deeper. One can start again. Continue — no longer.

Lina poured flour into the jar. Not much. Then added water. Warm, but not hot. Mixed it with the spoon. The movement was simple, almost poor. No magic. The flour darkened, the water disappeared, the mass became thick, uneven, lumpy. A new beginning looked worse than hope.

“That’s all?” Thomas asked.

“No.”

“What else?”

“Wait.”

He smiled without joy.

“Of course.”

“And feed.”

“I wrote that.”

“I see.”

Lina wiped the mouth of the jar. Covered it with cloth. Reached for the tape.

Thomas suddenly said:

“Wait.”

Everyone looked at him.

“What?”

“Maybe it should be in Finnish.”

Marek, who until then had been silently pouring himself tea, raised his eyes.

“Matti understands English.”

“Yesterday he didn’t understand everything either.”

“Yesterday there was nothing on the jar.”

“But if someone else comes?”

“Who?”

“I don’t know. Another caretaker. A worker. Someone from the district. Someone who won’t know what starter is.”

Nick set his mug on the table.

“It has begun.”

“What?”

“The expansion of the safety system to the size of the universe.”

Thomas looked at him sharply.

“And are you suggesting we lose it again?”

Nick did not answer at once.

Then said:

“No.”

That was good.

Because a light joke would not have survived that question.

David came closer.

“It can be written in Finnish. Just in case.”

“And the date,” Thomas said.

“What for?”

“So it’s clear this hasn’t been forgotten for a month.”

“Logical.”

“And maybe ‘alive.’”

Lina looked at him.

“In what language?”

Thomas fell silent.

Nora was sitting by the window, almost invisible until then. A cup rested on her knees, but she seemed to have forgotten to drink long ago.

“‘Alive’ doesn’t always help,” she said.

“Why?” Thomas asked.

“People throw away many living things anyway.”

Nick said quietly:

“You could have warned us that this would hurt.”

Nora did not smile.

David took a second piece of tape.

“Marek, how do you say ‘do not throw away’ in Finnish?”

Marek was silent for a moment.

“Älä heitä pois.”

Thomas immediately said:

“Write it.”

“I can say it, but I don't write it well.”

“Still better than nothing.”

Marek took the pencil. Wrote slowly, large, with slight doubt in the letters:

ÄLÄ HEITÄ POIS.

Then looked at it.

“I think that’s it.”

“Think?” Nick asked.

“I’m not Finnish.”

“Splendid. We now have an international starter-protection system with probabilistic spelling.”

“Nick.”

“Silent.”

David took a third piece of tape.

“The date.”

“Write it,” Thomas said.

David wrote the date. Then the time. Then thought and added beside it: “new starter.”

“Why ‘new’?” Sofia asked.

He raised his eyes.

“So it won’t be confused.”

“With what?”

He did not answer.

With the old one, of course.

Which was not there.

There were now three labels on the table. English, Finnish, the date. Lina looked at them without irritation. But without relief either.

“We could also write ‘ask Lina,’” Thomas said.

Nick covered his face with his hand.

“Forgive me, but now this is no longer a jar, it’s a small state border.”

“Very funny.”

“No. Not anymore.”

David said:

“‘Ask before throwing’ is better than ‘ask Lina.’”

“Why?”

“Because Lina may not be here.”

The words came out simple.

And immediately made the room colder.

Lina said quietly:

“Write that.”

David looked at her.

She repeated:

“Not my name. ‘Ask before throwing.’”

Thomas wrote.

ASK BEFORE THROWING.

The piece of tape was longer than the others.

Now the jar was almost surrounded by words.

STARTER. DO NOT THROW.

ÄLÄ HEITÄ POIS.

DATE.

NEW STARTER.

ASK BEFORE THROWING.

Lina took them one by one and did not stick all of them.

The English one — onto the jar.

The Finnish one — beside it, lower.

The date — on the lid, although there was no lid, only cloth; so she stuck it to the rim of the jar.

“ASK BEFORE THROWING” — on the shelf.

She left “NEW STARTER” on the table.

“Why?” David asked.

“It knows it’s new while we remember.”

“And after?”

“After, it won’t matter anymore.”

“Why?”

“Because if it lives, it will stop being new.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“I think we have just witnessed the administrative defeat of the word ‘new.’”

Sofia wanted to smile, but did not quite manage it.

Nora came to the table.

Looked at the jar.

Did not touch it.

“A label saves what has already been named,” she said.

Thomas sighed tiredly.

“Again.”

“What?”

“You say it as if that’s bad.”

“No.”

“Then how?”

“Incomplete.”

He looked at the jar.

“Incomplete is better than nothing.”

“Yes.”

He nodded.

That too was good.

Because no one in the room argued that a label could help. Everyone knew: if yesterday something, anything, had been written on the jar, Matti perhaps would not have thrown it away. Or he would have asked. Or at least paused one second longer. Sometimes a second is all the living thing needs in order to be recognized.

But the label did not teach one to see.

It taught stopping where someone had already said in advance: stop.

David took a sheet of paper.

“Maybe we need a list.”

Nick sharply raised his head.

“No.”

“Why immediately no?”

“Because I can hear an office opening somewhere in the distance.”

“Nick, this is practical.”

“All dangerous things are practical at first.”

David pressed his lips together.

“Yesterday we lost something living because there was no practicality.”

Nick fell silent.

David continued:

“I’m not suggesting... I don’t know, a control system for everything. Just a list for the house. What not to throw away without asking. Jars by the stove. Bowls covered with cloth. Infusions. Something with a date. Something near the window. So that if Matti comes again or someone else...”

“He didn’t come to kill the starter,” Lina said.

“I know.”

“Then the list will not be against him.”

“Yes.”

“Against what?”

David did not answer at once.

“Against our forgetfulness.”

Lina nodded.

“Then write it shorter.”

“Why?”

“Long things stop being read.”

Nick lifted a finger.

“That should be carved on the doors of every institution.”

Marek said:

“Shorter.”

Nick lowered his finger.

David wrote at the top of the sheet:

ASK BEFORE THROWING.

Then in Russian:

Ask before throwing away.

Then he looked at Marek.

“In Finnish?”

“Later.”

“Why?”

“Because Matti won’t read a wall newspaper in three languages.”

“It’s not a wall newspaper.”

“Yet.”

Sofia suddenly felt the air in the kitchen thickening again. Not even from meaning. From the human desire not to lose anything anymore. It was understandable. Almost tender. And very dangerous in its neatness. After a loss, one wants to build a room where loss cannot happen again. Close every crack. Label every jar. Stick warnings to every edge. Name every thing by the right name so that no one will ever again confuse the living with rubbish.

But inside that desire something else was already hiding: the wish for the world to be readable in advance.

Let it explain itself.

Let a person not risk making a mistake.

“I need to go outside,” Sofia said.

Nick looked at her.

“Save yourself.”

“Coming?”

He looked at David’s list, at the jar, at the strips of tape.

“Yes. Before I’m entered into the category ‘do not throw away without approval.’”

“A questionable category,” Nora said.

“I think so too.”

Nora stood.

“I’ll go.”

Marek glanced at the window.

“Wet.”

“We noticed,” Sofia said.

“Don’t go far.”

“Yes.”

Lina did not turn away from the jar.

“Take a knife.”

“What for?” Nick asked.

“Mushrooms.”

“Are we going for mushrooms?”

“We are now,” Nora said.

Nick took the knife as carefully as if he had been handed not a tool, but responsibility.

They went out through the back door.

The air outside was damp and cold, but after the kitchen it seemed wide. Not clean. Wide. The wet boards of the porch gave slightly under their feet. Somewhere drops were still falling from the roof. The yard smelled of damp wood, earth, old grass, chimney smoke, and that sour forest smell Sofia had sensed yesterday by the woodshed.

Nick breathed in.

“Everything here is unlabelled. Suspicious.”

Nora went down first.

“Will you read the ground?”

“I’d prefer instructions.”

“Of course.”

They went to the woodshed, then a little farther, to the low strip of forest beginning behind the shed. Not a real walk, just a retreat from the house to the distance where human voices stop being the center. The grass underfoot was wet. Moss on the stones glowed dully, not with postcard green, but with deep moisture. Mushrooms darkened on stumps, thin ones, pale, with almost transparent stems. At the root of a spruce lay a branch half gone into rot. White film of mycelium grew over it.

Nick stopped.

“So, for example, is this rubbish or process?”

“For whom?” Nora asked.

“Wonderful. I was afraid there would be a simple answer.”

Sofia bent toward the branch.

There was no label on it.

Not “rot.” Not “life.” Not “do not touch.” Not “do not throw.”

Just a branch.

And work easily mistaken for spoilage.

“If this were in the house,” Nick said, “I would probably carry it out.”

“In the house it would be different,” Nora said.

“The branch?”

“The mistake.”

He looked at her.

“Sometimes you speak unbearably correctly.”

“Sometimes you notice it.”

“That hurts too.”

Sofia went on. Between the trees there was much that was small and unsigned. A red berry under a leaf. A black berry beside it. One mushroom with a firm brown cap. Another similar, but slightly different, with a thin ring on its stem. Moss one could mistake for softness until stepping on it and realizing there was water underneath. An old cone. Bird droppings on a stone. The print of a small hoof in the mud. A leaf eaten so evenly that it seemed someone had been practicing patience.

Nothing explained itself.

The forest was not secretive.

It simply did not put up signs.

“Which ones can we take?” Nick asked, stopping by the mushrooms.

Nora squatted.

“Don’t take these.”

“Why?”

“I don’t know.”

“Convincing.”

“If you’re not sure, don’t take it.”

“That is a good rule.”

“But it won’t tell you which ones to take.”

“Of course. That would be too convenient.”

Sofia saw a small firm mushroom nearby under the needles.

“And this one?”

Nora looked.

“I don’t know.”

“Do you know mushrooms at all?”

“Some.”

“And this one?”

“No.”

Nick spread his hands.

“We came into the forest for an experience of recognition with a person who honestly says ‘I don’t know.’ That is already better than most guidebooks.”

Nora looked up at him.

“A guidebook is not to blame if people read it instead of the forest.”

Nick slowly inhaled.

“I now feel I again have nothing to object without moral damage.”

Sofia smiled.

This time it worked.

They gathered a little. Not much. A few berries, a couple of familiar mushrooms Nora did recognize, dryish chips of wood under the shelter of an old root, where the rain had not quite reached. Mostly they looked, rather than took. After the morning kitchen, that was almost a relief: not everything had to be protected at once with a sign.

At the low place behind the shed the earth was especially dark. There water stood in a thin film between the grass, and it smelled of sour decay. Nick stopped at the edge.

“This is a place I would definitely label.”

“How?” Sofia asked.

He thought.

“Careful. The world is working.”

Nora looked at him.

“Good label.”

“Really?”

“Useless.”

“Well, at least something stable.”

Sofia looked at the dark water. A grey strip of sky was reflected in it. On the surface floated a leaf, half yellow, half still green. Its edge was already darkening. Not bad. Not good. Not needed. Not unnecessary. Simply crossing over.

If it had to be classified, one would have to lie.

At that moment the glint came.

Not strong.

Like a wet splinter flaring not with fire, but with the memory of smoke.

A low house. The same hearth. After the loss of the sour piece, the older woman sets a new clay pot on a stone near the warmth. The young man brings a board. It already has notches: grain, circles, flour. Now he cuts other signs.

This one do not touch.

This one for tomorrow.

This one throw away.

This one good.

This one bad.

This one for bread.

A child looks at the lines.

“What if the mark wears off?” he asks.

The man stops the knife.

The older woman does not lift her head.

“Then you look.”

“Where?”

She takes the pot, smells it, covers it.

“Here.”

She points not to the mark.

To the pot.

The glint left.

Sofia stood beside the wet place and looked at her own hands. In one was a small handful of berries, in the other a mushroom Nora had said could be taken. She suddenly understood that she had not trusted a label. Nora’s “you can” — yes. But not only that. The color, the smell, the stem, the place, the way Nora had taken the mushroom, without hurrying and still with doubt.

Recognition was not certainty.

More like attention that knows it can be mistaken.

“What?” Nick asked.

“Labels,” Sofia said.

“Did they catch up with us again?”

“They began labelling there too.”

“In the old?”

“Yes.”

Nora looked at her.

“And?”

“The child asked: ‘What if the mark wears off?’”

Nick said quietly:

“Smart child. Dangerous.”

“The woman said: ‘Then you look.’”

Nora nodded.

“There.”

Nick raised his hand.

“I request that the word ‘there’ be banned as too effective.”

“No,” Nora said.

“I tried.”

When they returned, the kitchen had become a little calmer.

The jar stood by the stove. Already with labels. White strips of tape on the glass and shelf looked too bright against the wood, flour, and warm cloth. As if someone had stuck small rectangles of fear to the living thing.

But they were needed.

And that did not disappear.

Lina was sitting at the table, sorting herbs. David beside her was writing a short list on a separate sheet. Not a long one. Just a few points.

ASK BEFORE THROWING.

Do not throw away closed jars and bowls without asking.

Do not throw away what stands by the stove under cloth.

If it smells strange — ask.

Nick came over and read it.

“Amazing. The list is short and almost does not make me want to seize the world.”

David looked at him.

“Thank you.”

“That was a compliment.”

“I understood.”

Thomas took the mushrooms from Sofia.

“Are these definitely okay?”

“Nora said.”

He looked at Nora.

“Definitely?”

Nora came over and looked again.

“These, yes.”

“And if you were wrong?”

“Then it would be bad.”

“And that’s all?”

“What else?”

Thomas was silent.

“We can check with a guidebook,” Sofia said.

“Yes,” Nora said.

“And still?”

“And still.”

Thomas sat down.

“The world is disgustingly arranged.”

Nick said at once:

“Careful. Phrases like that here usually come back later in the form of moss.”

Thomas almost smiled.

“Let them.”

Lina took one mushroom and looked at the underside of its cap.

“We’ll leave this one.”

“Why?” Thomas asked.

“Because she said yes, but I’m not sure.”

“But you know better.”

“That is exactly why.”

She set the mushroom aside.

Nick whispered to Sofia:

“The farther we go, the more it seems to me that real wisdom is a developed form of refusing self-confidence.”

“Write it down.”

“No. Then it will become an aphorism and spoil.”

David raised his eyes.

“Not everything spoils from being written.”

“True,” Nick said. “Only part of it.”

“Which part?”

“The part they start using instead of life.”

David did not answer.

But he was not offended.

After lunch Marek hung the short list by the kitchen door. Not as a law. As a reminder. The sheet was held by two pins and lifted slightly away from the wall at the bottom. Beside it, on the shelf by the stove, stood the new jar. The English label whitened on it. Below — the Finnish one. The edge of the tape had already begun to lift slightly from the warm glass.

“It will peel off,” Thomas said.

“We’ll stick it again,” David answered.

“Or write directly on the glass.”

“We can.”

“Or buy proper labels.”

Nick from the corner:

“And now the little starter has received its own supply department.”

Lina looked at Thomas.

“Let it peel for now.”

“Why?”

“To remember.”

“What?”

“That a label needs care too.”

Nora by the window said:

“Everything that protects the living must not pretend to be dead itself.”

Nick slowly turned toward her.

“I am tired of marking your hits.”

“Don’t mark them.”

“Too late. Inside, there is already a label.”

That was the first joke of the day that almost became light.

In the evening Ivar returned to the stove.

He looked at the jar with the tapes, at the list by the door, at the mushrooms Lina had set aside separately, at the wet jackets by the entrance.

“You labelled it,” he said.

“Yes,” Thomas answered.

Ivar nodded.

“Good.”

Thomas grew wary.

“Just good?”

“Just good.”

“But not enough?”

Ivar looked at him.

“You already know.”

Thomas lowered his eyes.

Not because he had received a lesson.

Because this time the lesson was no longer needed.

Sofia noticed that Ivar did not begin to speak about the forest, about life, about signs, about the fact that the world cannot be labelled in advance. Perhaps he knew all of that too. Perhaps that was why he was silent.

Late in the evening, when the house had grown quiet, Sofia went to the shelf by the stove.

The jar stood where yesterday absence had been. Inside, the new mass had still barely changed. Flour and water. A few bubbles at the edge, perhaps only air from mixing. Cloth on top. Tape on the glass.

STARTER. DO NOT THROW.

ÄLÄ HEITÄ POIS.

ASK BEFORE THROWING.

She ran a finger beside it, not touching the jar.

The label soothed.

And frightened.

Because next to it one immediately began to see everything that had no label.

The forest outside the window.

Mushrooms.

The wet branch.

Rot behind the shed.

The sour smell of earth.

A human face when it errs not from malice.

Words lying on the table and not knowing what exactly they fail to contain.

The jar had been labelled.

Everything else still had to be recognized.
