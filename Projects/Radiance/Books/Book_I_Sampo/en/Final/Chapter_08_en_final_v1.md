# Chapter Eight. The Place

In the morning no one said: let’s go look.

That was important.

The words stood beside them like wet boots by the door, but no one wanted to be the first to put on these particular ones. It was too early in the house for confessions. The stove had not yet driven out the night damp entirely, the windows were cloudy along the edges, and on the table lay the bread that in the evening had seemed a participant in the map, and in the morning was trying again to be simply bread.

The map lay in the closed notebook.

The grey cover was dry, calm, almost innocent. Lina had set the notebook aside from the crumbs, but had not put it away altogether. It lay at the edge of the table, beside the knife, like a thing one can leave untouched and still know where it is.

David drank tea without sugar and did not look at the notebook.

That was important too.

He was very carefully not looking.

Thomas cleaned his glasses with a napkin. Wiped one lens, then the other, then the first again, although they were already clean. Nick watched this with the face of a person who would like to make a joke, but had not yet decided whether the joke would save him or betray him.

Nora sat by the window, a mug in her hands. There was almost no steam. The tea had gone cold, but she held it as if the warmth might return if it were not hurried.

Sofia was cutting bread.

Not because it had to be her. It was simply that the knife was nearby, and the hand did what it could do without a map. The slices lay unevenly on the board. One came out too thin, another thicker than she had wanted. Nick noticed, but said nothing.

That was almost frightening.

Marek came in from outside. He was wearing a dry jacket, but his boots had already managed to gather fresh mud. He set a metal bucket of water by the door and said shortly:

“It’s wet there again.”

“Amazing,” Nick said. “After rain, wet. The north never ceases to astonish the researcher.”

Marek looked at him.

“I said it for those who are going.”

The kitchen fell silent.

Not entirely. The stove crackled, someone set a cup on the table, a spoon slid across a saucer. But the human part of the silence grew dense.

“Who is going?” Eva asked.

She asked softly, but not without understanding.

David raised his eyes.

“I wanted just to look at the section.”

Nick slowly set down his mug.

“Ah. So that is what it is called now.”

“Nick.”

“No, really. Yesterday it was ‘just to understand where we were.’ Today it is ‘just to look at the section.’ Tomorrow, presumably, we will simply build a small iron railway to the misunderstanding.”

“I am not looking for Sampo,” David said.

Too quickly.

Sofia saw Marek turn his head slightly. Nora also raised her eyes.

Thomas stopped cleaning his glasses.

“No one said you were looking,” Eva said.

David nodded.

“I know.”

But now he had said it.

The word came out into the kitchen and did not go back.

Sampo.

After the bowl it had stood in the very thing. After the tracks — in the wet earth. After the map — in the white place on paper. Now it had been named almost as the thing they were not doing.

Nick said quietly:

“Excellent. At last we are not looking for anything aloud.”

Thomas put on his glasses.

“The section really should be checked.”

“Should is a very wide word,” Nick said.

“If the map is inaccurate, it’s better to make sure.”

“Of what? That it is inaccurate?”

“What is there in reality.”

Marek took a piece of bread from the table, bit off, chewed, and only then said:

“In reality, it is wet there.”

“You said that.”

“I can repeat it on site.”

Nora unexpectedly set down her mug.

“I’ll go.”

Nick turned to her.

“Naturally. The forest needs an independent witness of oddities.”

“No.”

“No?”

“I’ll just go.”

And that turned out to be enough.

Marek wiped his fingers on the towel.

“Ivar is not going.”

Everyone looked at Ivar.

He was sitting by the stove and repairing the same old bag as yesterday. Or another one. In his hands were leather, dark thread, a short needle. He did not raise his head.

“I am going,” he said.

Marek frowned.

“Why?”

“Behind.”

“You already walked behind yesterday.”

“Then I know the way.”

Nick raised a finger.

“I am not sure that comforts me.”

Ivar lifted his eyes. In them there was neither invitation nor prohibition.

“Those who want to close a question had better walk slowly.”

“So this is a warning after all?” Nick asked.

“No.”

“Worse. Simply a description.”

The decision was made without a decision.

That is often what happens with things a person later calls inevitable.

Sofia went not because she wanted to. And not because she did not want to. She felt that if she stayed in the house, the place would still go with them — in conversation, in the map, in the way David would later fall silent, in the way Thomas would become calmer or heavier. Better to see with her own eyes than later live inside someone else’s line.

Six of them went out: Sofia, David, Thomas, Nick, Nora, and Marek. Ivar came out last, when they were already standing by the porch. He took neither a stick, nor a bag, nor rope. Only fastened his jacket and closed the door behind him.

The day was lighter than yesterday, but not kinder.

The sky hung low. The rain had left, leaving work behind for everything living: drops still held on branches, the earth still accepted water, the moss still rose, the roots still darkened, mushrooms were still thinking whether to show themselves or not. The air smelled of wet wood, old leaves, stove smoke from the chimney, and something sour, marshy, real.

Nick breathed in.

“Excellent. It smells like a closed question.”

“You can stay,” Thomas said.

“I can’t. Then you will come back with the faces of people who ‘just looked,’ and I will have to believe your words. I am not that religious.”

Marek went first.

This time no one lingered by the tracks under the porch.

And that too was new.

Yesterday the tracks had still been an event. Today they had become a passed stage. A person quickly grows used to what disturbed him yesterday, if a more precise anxiety appears ahead.

Sofia noticed that David was holding the notebook under his jacket.

He did not take it out.

But he held it.

The thin grey cover sometimes showed under the open edge when he adjusted his collar. It was almost funny and not funny at all. The map was going with them, hidden from the rain like a document that must not get wet, because then the world would again become too large.

The way to the boards was familiar now.

This became the first disappointment.

Yesterday every root might have been a hint. Today they already knew: here was the old path, here the boards, here the moose track, here the moss on the stone, here the birch. The places they passed through had received names. Not full ones, not true ones, but enough not to meet them anew.

Sofia thought: this is how possession begins.

Then stopped the thought.

No need.

Let it be a path.

Let it be simply a wet path that is hard to walk on.

But the path no longer obeyed.

It already lay in memory as a line.

At the old path to the left, David stopped after all.

“From here,” he said.

Marek did not turn around.

“From here.”

“If you go straight, does it go around the low place?”

“If you go straight, first you get your feet wet.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“That is the best answer for feet.”

Nick looked at Sofia.

“I increasingly respect Marek as a philosopher of soles.”

“I am not a philosopher,” Marek said.

“Exactly. That is why it works.”

Thomas stood beside David and looked left, where the old path went off between the firs. Not deeply. It simply disappeared faster than the main one. The grass there was lower, the moss darker, the branches closer to the face. Yesterday it had been a branch of the path. Today — the edge of the white place.

“Are we going there?” Nora asked.

Marek nodded.

“A little. Then we turn. I am not taking you through the bog, even if the paper thinks it is smarter.”

David did not object.

That was almost worse than if he had.

They turned.

The old path immediately became narrower. Fewer people had walked here, but they had walked. Not every year, perhaps. Not every month. But the earth was a little lower, the grass a little more obedient, the branches bent in one direction. A human trace remains in the world longer than a human being thinks. Sometimes not because the human being is important. Simply because moss forgives slowly.

Nick walked carefully.

“I have a proposal,” he said.

“No,” Thomas said.

“I haven’t said it yet.”

“Still no.”

“A very mature approach to dialogue. I wanted to propose: if we find simply a wet place, everyone admits Marek was right, and we return without expanding the mythological infrastructure.”

“If there is simply a wet place there,” David said, “I will admit there is a wet place there.”

“You hear? He left the doors open.”

“Nick.”

“Yes, yes. I am silent. But the doors are open. And judging by the humidity, they have been for a long time.”

Nora stopped by a bilberry bush. Several berries still held on the branches — late, wrinkled, dull. She picked one, looked at it, but did not eat it.

“What?” Sofia asked.

“Nothing.”

“Nora.”

“Only that a berry remained.”

“And?”

“And it does not care that we did not come for it.”

Nick sighed.

“I was morally prepared for a wet place, but not for reproach from a berry.”

Nora put the berry in her palm, then ate it after all. The sourness made her wince.

“Late,” she said.

“Everything here is late,” Marek said. “And wet.”

It became harder to walk farther on.

The path did not disappear, but it stopped helping. Soft places appeared underfoot, where the earth did not hold immediately, but first accepted the step inward, as if it wanted to think. Roots ran over the moss, the moss held water, the water hid under the grass, and every step required attention. Not attention to meaning. To the foot.

This was useful.

And almost humiliating.

Because the body quickly returns a person from a scheme to the place where the earth decides not by lines.

David took out the notebook.

“Now?” Nick asked.

“Just checking.”

“The gods of bureaucracy are smiling.”

David opened the page and shielded it from the damp with his sleeve. The pencil lines looked more confident than everything around them. Even the erased water line was visible, if one knew where to look. The white place between the old path and the stones seemed small. On paper, it could be covered with two fingers.

David looked at the page, then at the forest.

It became clear too quickly: they did not match.

Not because the map was wrong. Though it was wrong. But because the place had no obligation to match. On paper, between the lines, there had been a section. Here there were bushes, wet ground, hummocks, invisible water, a branch pushing into one’s face, and an anthill at the root, washed out by rain on one side.

“We should be closer,” Thomas said.

“To what?” Nick asked.

Thomas did not answer.

Marek stopped.

“Here.”

Everyone stopped too.

“Here what?” David asked.

“The place.”

He said it without triumph. As one says: here is the mud. Here is the pit. Here is the wet sleeve. Here is what you asked to come to, not because it wanted to see you, but because yesterday you pointed at it with your finger.

Sofia looked.

The place was.

Not a point.

Not a sign.

Not an emptiness.

A small low hollow between firs and several birches. The earth there had sunk, or had always been lower. Moss lay in a thick layer, dark green, almost black in the depressions. Beneath it stood water. It was not visible at once, but when Marek stepped onto the edge and the moss slowly rose after his boot, a cloudy liquid flashed from underneath. By an old rotten trunk grew small mushrooms, grey and yellowish, like other people’s ears. Sedge stuck up between hummocks. On a branch hung a spiderweb, all in drops. A bilberry bush stretched toward the light that almost did not reach. At the root of a birch lay last year’s leaves, no longer leaves, but not yet earth.

David looked at the notebook.

Then at the hollow.

Then at the notebook again.

“Not like that,” he said.

Nick said quietly:

“It has begun.”

“I mean, not exactly like that. On the map it seemed the section was farther from the water.”

“Water is everywhere here,” Marek said.

“Yes, but...”

“I told you: wet.”

Thomas had already started walking along the edge of the hollow.

“Wait,” Sofia said.

He stopped, but not at once.

“What?”

“Where are you going?”

“To look farther.”

“We came here.”

“Possibly this is not the right part.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“There. The place has failed inspection.”

Thomas turned.

“And what do you suggest? Stand here and say: ‘Yes, this puddle is what we wanted to see’?”

“I suggest admitting that we have come to a puddle we ourselves appointed as a suspect.”

“Not we.”

“Of course. The paper did it on its own.”

David raised a hand.

“I did not appoint it. I simply marked the section.”

“Excellent,” Nick said. “A section. Sounds almost dry.”

Nora stood silently and looked not at the people, but at the moss. Then she took a step, crouched, and put her fingers to the green surface. The moss yielded slightly. Water remained on her fingers.

“It holds,” she said.

“What?” Thomas asked.

“Water.”

“And?”

“Nothing.”

She wiped her fingers on her trousers and stood up.

Sofia came closer. At the edge of the hollow the earth was firmer, but still breathed under the foot. She crouched and touched the rotten trunk. The wood was soft. Her finger went into it almost without effort. Inside it smelled of damp darkness, decay, fungus, future earth. Not death. Work.

She pulled her hand away not from disgust.

From a strange intimacy.

The hollow had too many details.

A bird’s track in the soft earth by the water. Three small imprints, almost immediately blurred. A broken branch, but old, not from yesterday. A yellow leaf stuck to a stone so tightly it seemed part of the stone. A tiny mushroom bitten by someone from below. An ant dragging something pale and not interested in the fact that people were discussing its place.

David looked into the notebook again.

Sofia saw his face and understood: he did not see half of what was before him.

Not because he was blind.

Because the map was already holding his gaze by the collar.

“Here was already here,” Ivar said.

Sofia did not immediately understand that he had come up.

He stood a little behind, by the birch, not in the hollow, not on the path, but in such a way as not to block either the earth or the mistake for anyone. His voice was low, dry, without pressure.

Thomas turned.

“What does that mean?”

Ivar looked at him.

“Nothing for the map.”

Nick said quietly:

“A very unpleasant level of answer.”

David closed the notebook.

Too quickly.

Then opened it again.

“So the section is marked incorrectly.”

Marek sighed.

“David.”

“I am not saying there has to be something here. I am saying that if we wanted to check the white place, we need to understand whether we came correctly.”

“You came where you came.”

“That is not geography.”

“What is it?”

David fell silent.

Thomas continued for him:

“If the map was born from tracks, and the place does not match, then one of the tracks was read incorrectly.”

“Or the map is bad,” Nick said.

“Or we remember badly.”

“Or all of it at once.”

“Or we need to go farther.”

There it was.

The words were not loud. Thomas said them almost calmly. But at once it grew tighter around them, as if the wet forest had leaned closer to hear how far a person was capable of continuing.

“Farther where?” Sofia asked.

Thomas pointed between two firs.

“The ground is higher there. The hollow may continue that way and come out by the stones.”

Marek shook his head.

“People don’t walk there.”

“Why?”

“Because there is no need.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“That is the place’s answer.”

Nick looked at Marek.

“Congratulations. You did become a philosopher of soles after all.”

Marek did not smile.

“The moss is deep there. There is water under it. You can sink up to the knee. You can tear your boot on a root. You can simply get wet and come back angry. Choose what you need to prove.”

Sofia felt the phrase strike not even Thomas, but David.

Because David did not want to prove.

He wanted to close.

It looked more reasonable, calmer, almost more honest. But inside was the same movement: the map had given a white place, the white place had demanded earth, the earth did not match, which meant a little more was needed. A little farther. A little more precise. One more step. One more line.

Nick took off his glove and touched the moss with his palm.

“Cold,” he said.

“Wet,” Marek corrected.

“Those are not mutually exclusive accusations.”

Nick smelled his palm and grimaced.

“It smells... as if the forest did not ask for our opinion.”

Nora looked at him.

“It did not.”

“Yes, I understood, thank you.”

David at that moment stood a little aside and looked again at the map. He did not draw. Only looked. The map had begun to warp from the damp. One corner of the page lifted, and the grey pencil grew a little darker where a drop had fallen onto the paper.

“Put it away,” Marek said.

David raised his eyes.

“What?”

“The paper. It will get wet.”

David looked at the page as if only now he had understood that it too was a thing that could be spoiled by the world.

He closed the notebook and shoved it under his jacket.

Thomas was still looking between the firs, toward where Marek had forbidden them to go.

“The ground is higher there,” he said.

“There are roots there,” Marek answered.

“But if the hollow goes that way...”

“Thomas.”

Marek pronounced his name without irritation.

That stopped more than sharpness would have.

“What?”

“There will be a place there too.”

Thomas was silent.

Marek adjusted the belt on his jacket.

“Wet, dry, with roots, without roots. With a stone. With a pit. With a mushroom. With something else. You can walk until evening. There will be a place every time.”

Nick said quietly:

“That is perhaps the most frightening thing you have said today.”

“I said there are many places.”

“Yes. That is what is frightening.”

Ivar looked at Sofia.

He did not ask.

She suddenly understood that he would not ask. He did not pull an answer out of people like a nail out of a board. He waited until the pain itself stopped holding on by the nailhead.

“It was not empty,” she said.

Not very loudly.

Only so she could hear herself.

David turned.

“The place?”

“Yes.”

“No one said it was empty.”

Sofia looked at him.

He understood.

Too late, but he understood.

Because the white place on the paper had been empty. The point had waited. The line had waited. The section had waited. In their heads all of it had stood and waited for the real forest to confirm that it could be folded into waiting.

But the hollow was not white.

Marek said:

“We’re going back.”

Thomas wanted to object, but did not.

That was not agreement.

It was simply that he was tired of wet feet, wet earth, Marek’s wet “I told you,” the wet impossibility of forcing the place to become what yesterday had been a dry patch on paper.

They went back.

Not at once.

First Nick looked at the hollow once more. Nora broke off one small twig, then put it back, as if she had noticed her own hand too late. David checked whether the notebook had got wet. Thomas looked where the ground rose between the firs. Sofia wiped her dirty fingers on the grass and understood that the smell would remain anyway.

The path back was the same.

But now it did not lead to the place.

It led away from the place.

For some reason that was heavier.

When one goes toward a place, even if one does not admit it, inside there is a small forward movement: now, perhaps, something will become clear. When one goes back, it has not become clear, and still one has to walk. The legs know this before the head. They lift more heavily. Boots come out of the mud louder. Branches catch the sleeve more often.

Nick walked beside Sofia.

“I am angry,” he said.

She looked at him.

“At whom?”

“That is the particularly humiliating part. No one can be selected.”

“At the place?”

“That would be convenient. But it smelled of moss and was busy with its own affairs. It is hard to be angry at wet moss without reputational damage.”

“At David?”

Nick glanced ahead, where David walked beside Marek and was silent.

“No. He wanted to close the question. That is almost a respectable crime.”

“At Thomas?”

“Thomas simply shows the next mistake more honestly.”

“Then at yourself.”

Nick was silent for a moment.

“Sister, sometimes you economize words dangerously.”

She gave a small laugh.

Not merrily.

“You asked.”

“I did not think the trial would be fast.”

They reached the old path. The main one looked wide, almost hospitable. The boards ahead shone with wet wood. The water beneath them moved slowly, without any desire to explain anything.

By the moose track, David stopped.

The track had almost disappeared.

What yesterday had been an imprint had now become a cloudy little puddle of irregular shape. The edges had blurred, the water had risen, the mud had settled. If they had seen it for the first time, no one would have said with confidence: a moose was here.

“There,” Nick said. “The witness has withdrawn his testimony.”

David did not smile.

“But we remember.”

“Yes. That is the problem.”

Marek went on.

“The house is close.”

Nick sighed.

“On the map, I hope, too.”

By the porch they stopped almost at the same time. Not on command. It was simply that after the hollow the house looked different. Not safer. Rather drier. Walls, roof, door, hooks, window, smoke from the chimney — all of it was form. All of it held. All of it said: here one can take off wet things, put down a cup, close the door, lay out bread, pretend that a place has a boundary.

Marek went in first.

“Boots on the newspapers,” he said. “And don’t bring the hollow into my kitchen.”

“Too late,” Nick said.

Marek looked at him.

“Then at least physically.”

In the house it smelled of stove and yesterday’s tea. Eva raised her head from the table, saw their faces, and asked nothing. Lina only set another mug closer to the edge.

David took off his boots and carefully placed them on the newspaper. Then he took out the notebook.

The paper had bowed slightly from the damp, but the map had survived. The lines were in place. The white place too. He opened the page and looked at it for a long time.

Nick stood beside him, wet, muddy, with the smell of moss on his palm.

“Only don’t say it needs correcting,” he said.

David did not answer at once.

Then said:

“It does.”

Nick closed his eyes.

“Of course.”

“Not because we found something.”

“All the more.”

“Because the place was not where we marked it.”

From the hallway Marek said dryly:

“The place was where it was.”

David raised the pencil.

Sofia saw the movement and felt something fall inside.

Not strongly.

Like a drop from a branch.

He wanted not to find. Not to prove. To correct. To make the map more honest. But now the honesty of the map demanded a new line. And a new line demanded a new place. And a new place — a new going out.

Thomas stood by the window.

He looked not at the forest, but at the reflection of the kitchen. As if there, in the glass, one could see the table, the map, the wet jackets, and a piece of forest all at once.

“There is a rise farther on,” he said.

Eva quietly set a mug on the table.

Nick turned to him.

“Thomas.”

“I am not saying now.”

“That comforts exactly not at all.”

Nora took off her jacket, hung it on a hook, and said:

“The place was full.”

Everyone looked at her.

She lifted one shoulder.

“You speak as if there was nothing there.”

And fell silent.

It was the best sentence that could have been said.

Not because it explained everything.

But because it returned to the hollow at least part of what they had tried to take from it.

Sofia sat on the bench. On her sleeves were wet traces of bushes. On her boots — heavy earth. Under her nail — a dark strip. On her fingers, however she wiped them, remained the smell of rotten wood and moss.

They had not returned empty-handed.

And that was strange.

Because they held nothing in their hands.

Earth was on the boots.

Water — in the fabric.

Smell — on the skin.

The hollow — in Nick’s irritation, in David’s silence, in Thomas’s still gaze, in Nora’s short sentence, in Marek’s “I told you,” which now sounded not like victory, but like fatigue.

Ivar entered last.

He took off his jacket, hung it on a free hook, and went to the stove. The wood caught almost at once. He stood looking at the fire, then turned toward the table.

David held the pencil over the map.

“May I correct it?” he asked.

The question was strange.

Not because he was asking permission.

Because it was unclear from whom.

From Ivar?

From Marek?

From the map?

From the place, which had remained outside and was already busy again with water under moss?

Ivar came to the table, looked at the page.

“You may,” he said.

David almost breathed out.

“But?”

“Nothing.”

“Simply may?”

“Simply may.”

David frowned.

“Then why does it sound as if I shouldn’t?”

Ivar looked at him calmly.

“Because you already know it will not close anything.”

The pencil remained above the paper.

Did not touch it.

For several seconds everyone looked at that small distance between graphite and page.

Almost nothing.

Less than the thickness of a fingernail.

But right then more forest fit inside it than in the map itself.

David set the pencil down beside the notebook.

Did not correct it.

For now.

Thomas turned away from the window.

“So what? Just leave it?”

Ivar said:

“For today.”

Nick laughed quietly.

“The most merciful unit of time.”

Marek poured himself tea.

“For today is a good place.”

Nick looked at him.

“You need to be stopped before you become completely dangerous.”

For the first time that morning, Marek almost smiled.

“I said it was wet there.”

“Yes. And you were right. That is especially irritating.”

Sofia looked at the notebook closing. David covered the page with the notebook cover after all. From the outside the map disappeared. The white place disappeared. The hollow disappeared. The tracks, water, moss, rotten trunk, bird print, trembling root — everything disappeared under the grey lid.

But did not disappear.

On Nick’s palm remained the smell of moss.

On Sofia’s sleeves — wet traces of bushes.

On Marek’s boots — earth.

In David’s head — a place that needed correcting.

In Thomas’s eyes — the rise between the firs.

And behind the house the hollow held water, rotted, fed mushrooms, darkened under moss, and was.

Sofia sat closer to the stove and held her palms to the warmth. The earth under her nails dried and tightened the skin. On the table lay bread, the knife, Nora’s mug, the notebook, wet newspapers, soft butter. Everything was again in the house. Everything again had a place.

And only then did it reach her.

The most frightening thing was not that the place had turned out to be empty.

It had not been empty.

It had been full.

It simply had not been their answer.
