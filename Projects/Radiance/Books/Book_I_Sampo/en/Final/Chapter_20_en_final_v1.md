# Chapter Twenty. Gift

They began cooking the soup closer to evening.

Not because the time had come.

Because there was nothing more to wait for.

On the table lay three mushrooms. One good. One small. One with the dirty side cut off, leaving an uneven white wound. Beside them, in a bowl, a few berries darkened. There were so few of them that they looked more like stains in water than food.

The fish scales were not brought to the table.

They stayed on the porch, where the fine rain had already begun to make them less shiny.

Lina set the pot on the stove. She poured in more water than one would want to look at. The water covered the bottom confidently, rising higher than the mushrooms could justify. When Lina threw them in, they floated up at once, one after another, and began to seem even smaller.

Nick stood by the table and looked into the pot with the caution of a person who did not want to offend either the food or the truth.

“Is it soup already?” he asked at last.

Lina turned her head to him.

“This is the beginning.”

“Good. The beginning looks thin, but dignified.”

Eva gave him the knife.

“Slice the bread.”

“Is the bread also a beginning?”

“There isn’t much bread.”

“Then I’ll cut it thin, like hope.”

“Cut it evenly.”

He took the bread. Dark, yesterday’s, with a dense crust. The slices really had to be made thin. The knife went heavily, crumbs fell onto the board and immediately became noticeable. Nick gathered them with his finger and swept them to the edge, as if afraid to lose even what normally no one counts.

Thomas sat opposite the pot.

Not too close.

But so that he could see what was inside.

“How many bowls will there be?” he asked.

Lina stirred the water.

“As many as needed.”

“That isn’t an answer.”

Nick did not raise his head.

“That is already the family crest of the kitchen.”

Thomas did not smile.

“I’m serious.”

“So is she,” Eva said.

Thomas looked at her.

“If there isn’t much soup, we need to understand how to divide it.”

“We’ll understand when we pour it.”

“Then it will be too late.”

Lina put a handful of grain into the pot. Not a full one. A small one. The dry grains quickly went down, and the water again became almost empty on top.

“Too late for what?” she asked.

Thomas opened his mouth.

Closed it.

Looked at the pot.

“For it to be fair.”

Lina did not answer at once.

The stove cracked with a dry chip. Outside the window the rain fell finely and evenly, almost without sound. In the entryway it smelled of wet boots. Somewhere by the door, a drop from a jacket fell into the same place, patiently, as if it too were taking part in the count.

“Fair is not always visible before the ladle,” Lina said.

Nick quietly put the bread on the plate.

“I would like to joke right now, but I’m afraid the ladle will receive the right of objection.”

Thomas stood up.

“Can we add something else?”

“We can,” Lina said.

“What?”

She looked at the shelf.

At the table.

At the bowl with berries.

“Salt.”

“That isn’t what I meant.”

“I know.”

He walked irritably to the window. Not sharply. He simply needed to move his body so as not to argue with the pot. Sofia sat at the edge of the table and looked at his back. Yesterday, perhaps, she would have thought he again wanted to turn everything into a rule. Now it seemed to her that he simply could not bear the moment when the rule appears later than hunger.

Inside her, it was no better.

She was no longer angry.

The anger after the basket had gone somewhere deeper, leaving not even offense, but an empty place that kept checking: what if it becomes more now?

But it did not become more.

Lina stirred the soup.

The water clouded slowly. One mushroom floated to the edge, turned cap-down, then moved back to the middle. The grain began to swell, but there was little of it. Lina had not yet thrown in the berries. They lay separately, dark and sour, as if they could still refuse to participate.

David sat by the window.

The notebook lay closed before him.

He placed his palm on the cover, held it there, then removed it. This time he did not even open it.

Nick noticed.

“You’re not writing down the world today?”

“No.”

“The world is grateful, but will pretend indifference.”

David looked at the pot.

“I don’t want to count ahead again.”

Nick lowered his eyes.

“Yes.”

And said nothing else.

That short “yes” sounded strange. Without defense. Without joke. As if he too remembered the basket by the wall and how quickly the word “enough” had settled where nothing was ready yet.

Nora sat on the bench by the window and picked a wet thread from her sleeve. She almost did not lift her head. Sometimes she looked at the rain. Sometimes at the door. Just a girl who had stayed in the house and now was not asking why the soup looked like water.

Outside, the sound of an engine was heard.

Not close at once.

First low, somewhere beyond the trees. Then closer. Then wheels slapped along the wet road by the house. Nick lifted his head.

“If that’s boars on a tractor, I’m leaving the genre.”

Eva looked out the window.

“Matti.”

Thomas turned around.

“Why?”

“We’ll find out now.”

The car stopped by the shed. The door slammed. A few seconds later heavy steps sounded on the porch. Not hurrying. Practical. Then a short knock at the door, although Matti seemed to be the kind of person who could have entered without knocking if he believed the matter was more important than the form.

Marek opened.

Matti stood in the doorway.

In a wet jacket, hat in hand, with a face that was neither smiling nor gloomy. Only the ordinary readiness to do what he had come to do. A canvas bag hung from his shoulder. In his other hand he held a small paper bag, darkened at the bottom from damp.

“Evening,” he said.

“Evening,” Marek answered.

“Brought the shed key. The one that was sticking.”

He held out the key ring. Marek took it, looked.

“Fixed it?”

“Yes. Opens now.”

“Thank you.”

Matti nodded, as if the gratitude had passed by him and landed where it needed to.

Then he lifted the paper bag.

“This too.”

Marek did not take it at once.

“What?”

“Potatoes. A little. And bread.”

The kitchen grew quieter.

Not strongly.

But noticeably.

Even the soup in the pot seemed to stop pretending to boil and began listening.

Thomas took a step toward the door.

“Did you know?”

Matti looked at him.

“What?”

“About...”

Thomas stopped.

About the fish? About the boars? About the overturned basket? About how they had already managed to imagine supper and lost it before preparing it?

He did not finish.

Matti waited.

“Nothing,” Thomas said.

Matti shifted his gaze to Marek.

“Laura had some left. Said you have many people. I was passing by.”

He said it as if the phrase explained everything that needed explaining.

Marek took the bag.

“Will you come in?”

Matti looked at the floor.

At his boots.

At the rain behind him.

“No. I’m wet.”

Nick said quietly from behind the table:

“That is not a rare condition here.”

Matti looked at him.

Did not understand whether it was a joke.

And, it seemed, decided not to find out.

“There’s also a jar,” he said.

From the canvas bag he took out a small glass jar with a metal lid. Inside, something pale shone in cloudy brine. Cucumbers. Or something resembling cucumbers, only smaller, denser, and angrier.

“What is that for?” Eva asked.

“Laura said she opened the wrong one. It needs to be eaten.”

“By us?”

Matti shrugged.

“By someone.”

Nick put the knife down on the table.

“A magnificent form of assignment.”

Thomas came closer.

“How much do we owe?”

Matti looked at him as if the question was technically understandable, but had been applied in the wrong place.

“Nothing.”

“Why?”

“There was extra.”

Thomas frowned.

“Extra at your place?”

“At Laura’s.”

“But if we take it, she won’t have extra anymore.”

Matti thought.

Not quickly.

“That is why she gave it.”

Nick drew breath as if preparing to say something too successful.

Then did not say it.

Because there were already enough words in the room that could spoil the small paper bag if placed on top of it.

Sofia looked at the potatoes.

Matti set the bag on the bench by the door. One potato fell out of it. Small, with thin soil in its eyes. It rolled toward the threshold and stopped at the crack between the boards.

Sofia felt joy.

At once.

Too quickly.

And that made her embarrassed.

The fish was still somewhere outside. Not here. Not theirs. The good mushroom had been crushed and, by now, was probably darkening under the rain. The basket by the wall still smelled of wet wicker and loss. And she was looking at the small potato as if it had the right to correct the evening.

It did not.

And still it became easier.

That lightness was uncomfortable.

Like someone else’s jacket given to you to throw over your shoulders when you have not yet decided whether you are allowed to stop freezing.

Lina came to the door.

Picked the potato up from the floor.

Ran her thumb over the earth on its skin.

“Good.”

Matti nodded.

“Normal.”

“Thank you.”

He nodded again.

Lina took the bag, the jar, and the bread, and carried them to the table. Not carefully, as if they were holy things. And not carelessly. Simply as something that now had to be washed, sliced, and put where water was boiling.

Thomas followed her with his eyes.

“We have to answer with something.”

Matti was already getting ready to close the door.

“Not now.”

“Later?”

Matti looked at him.

“If it is needed.”

“By whom?”

“By whoever needs it.”

Nick finally could not help himself.

“I would like to express gratitude without turning it into a contract,” he said. “But it seems all of us are bad at that skill so far.”

Matti looked at him again.

For a long time.

Then said:

“Eat it warm.”

And left.

The door closed.

Not sharply.

Marek stood by the door for a second, listening to Matti descend from the porch. Then turned to the table.

“He didn’t wipe his boots again,” Eva said.

Nick looked at the tracks by the door.

“That, apparently, is the signature of the gift.”

“Take the rag.”

“Of course. One cannot accept signatures without processing them.”

He took the rag.

But already without the former performance.

Meanwhile Lina emptied the potatoes onto the table. There were not many. Seven small potatoes and two medium ones, with uneven skins, with dark spots of earth. The bread turned out to be a small piece, dense, almost black. The jar of cucumbers clicked when Lina checked the lid.

“Is all this going into the soup?” Thomas asked.

“The potatoes — yes.”

“The bread?”

“On the table.”

“The cucumbers?”

“Later.”

“How many potatoes do we put in?”

Lina took the knife.

“As many as needed.”

Thomas closed his eyes.

“That word is haunting me.”

“Then you hear it,” Eva said.

“I hear it. I don’t understand.”

Lina washed the first potato in a bowl of cold water. The earth did not come off at once. The water darkened. She did not peel the skin completely, only cut away the dark place and sliced the potato thinly straight into the pot.

The slices fell into the water and disappeared for a second.

Then floated up.

The soup did not become rich because of this.

But it stopped looking accidental.

Sofia watched Lina cut the second potato. Then the third. Every movement was ordinary. No gratitude into the air, no explanations, no “you see.” Knife, potato, water, soup.

And that was all.

For some reason, precisely this was harder to accept than if someone had said a beautiful phrase.

A beautiful phrase could have been objected to.

A knife could not.

David opened the notebook.

Everyone noticed.

Even he did.

He looked at the empty page.

Wrote:

gift

The word came out small.

Too small for the page.

He looked at it, then put after it not a period, but a dash.

The dash hung there.

Nick came over with the rag, saw the page, and stopped.

“I would help,” he said quietly. “But I’m afraid I’d ruin everything.”

David did not smile.

“So am I.”

He closed the notebook without finishing.

Thomas noticed.

“Why didn’t you write it down?”

“I did.”

“There’s only one word there.”

“For now, yes.”

“But you usually...”

“Usually is not a contract,” David said.

Nick lifted the rag.

“I officially testify: the phrase has gone to the people.”

Eva pointed at the track by the door.

“Let the people wipe the floor.”

He went to wipe.

Lina added two more potatoes to the soup. Set the rest aside.

“Why not all?” Thomas asked.

“Tomorrow is also a day.”

“But today the soup is thin.”

“Today the soup will have potatoes.”

“And tomorrow?”

“We’ll see.”

He sat down.

This time he did not argue.

Simply sat, like a person who still wants an answer but is beginning to understand that the answer is not obliged to arrive before supper.

The soup boiled slowly.

The potatoes made the water cloudier and denser. The mushrooms no longer floated alone. The grain rose from the bottom. Lina still did not put the berries into the pot. She crushed them with a small pinch of salt in a separate bowl, then added a spoonful of hot water. A dark sour stain came out, which she set beside the bread.

“What is this for?” Nick asked.

“For the bread.”

“The bread is about to experience character.”

“And you.”

“I already have.”

Nora came to the table and looked at the potatoes set aside.

“Is that for tomorrow?” she asked.

“Yes,” Lina said.

“And if someone comes tomorrow?”

The room grew quiet.

Nora immediately understood that she had said more than she meant to.

“I don’t mean...” she began.

“It’s all right,” Marek said.

He stood by the door, took a wet jacket down from the hook, and moved it farther from the stove so it would not drip onto the floor.

“If someone comes, we’ll see.”

Nora nodded and went back to the window.

The phrase did not become prophetic.

It simply remained the question of a child who had seen that food can leave and arrive not according to plan.

When the soup was almost ready, Thomas stood and went to the shelf with the bowls.

“I’ll set them out.”

Lina looked at him.

“You’ll pour?”

“If I may.”

“You may.”

He took the ladle.

Held it so seriously, as if holding a tool that could not be trusted without inspection. The first bowl came out almost full of water and two slices of potato. Thomas looked at it, then back into the pot.

“Uneven.”

“Stir,” Lina said.

He stirred.

The second bowl came out better. There was a mushroom in it. One of the three. Thomas froze.

“For whom?”

“Set it down,” Lina said.

“But there are three mushrooms.”

“And more bowls.”

“Exactly.”

Nick said quietly:

“Now the mushroom will become a political question.”

“Nick,” Eva said.

“I’m silent. The mushroom does not need politics.”

Thomas set the bowl with the mushroom in the middle of the table, as if setting the decision aside.

Then poured the next one. There was no mushroom in it. Only potato and grain.

The next — the same.

Then one more, where a small mushroom piece floated up.

He looked at Lina.

“What is the right way?”

She took the ladle from him.

Not sharply.

“To pour.”

“But...”

“Then we’ll swap.”

“By what rule?”

Lina looked at the table.

At the people.

At the bowls.

“By the eyes.”

Nick covered his mouth with his hand.

Eva looked at him warningly.

“I’m not saying anything,” he said through his fingers. “It’s just that the rule has finally started looking back.”

Lina poured the remaining bowls. Then truly changed two places. Then pushed one to Nora, one to Marek, one to Thomas, one to Sofia, one to Eva, one to Nick, one to David. She kept the last for herself.

Thomas watched.

“You took less again.”

“It will be enough for me.”

“That’s not...”

He stopped.

Took his bowl.

Looked inside.

There was a slice of potato, grain, a piece of mushroom — not whole, an edge. He took the spoon, lifted the mushroom piece, looked at it, then put it back into the soup.

“We’ll swap,” he said.

“With whom?” Lina asked.

Thomas looked at her bowl.

Then at Nora.

Then at Sofia.

Then at his own.

“I don’t know.”

“Then eat.”

He exhaled tiredly.

And sat down.

It was not defeat.

Rather, it was the first time he could not turn care into the movement of an object from one place to another.

They began eating.

The soup was thin.

That could not be hidden.

The spoon passed through it easily. One had to search at the bottom of the bowl. But the potato was hot, soft, with an earthy taste. The bread was dense, and with the sour berry water it suddenly became better than it looked. The cucumbers from the jar turned out sharp, salty, almost angry. Nick ate one and said:

“This cucumber is not asking for love. It demands respect.”

For the first time that evening, Eva laughed a little louder.

Not long.

But truly.

Matti was already gone. Nick had wiped his wet tracks by the door. The potatoes lay in the soup. The jar stood open. The bread diminished in thin slices. The fish did not return. The basket by the wall was still wet inside.

Sofia ate slowly.

She was still embarrassed by joy.

Not large.

Not bright.

Simply by the fact that the soup had become better than it should have been after the porch. As if she had betrayed her hurt by allowing the potato to change the taste of water.

She looked at Lina.

Lina ate calmly. Without a grateful face. Without the face of a person who had understood a lesson. She simply ate while the soup was hot.

Sofia did the same.

Thomas suddenly said:

“If there was extra, that doesn’t mean we can always count on it.”

Nick lifted his spoon.

“I don’t even know whom you’re defending right now: us, Laura, the potato, or the future.”

“I’m serious.”

“So am I. A rare occasion.”

Marek broke off a piece of bread.

“He’s right.”

Thomas looked at him almost with gratitude.

Marek continued:

“You cannot count on it.”

Thomas blinked.

“That’s what I said.”

“Yes.”

“But...”

“And you have to eat when it’s given.”

Thomas lowered his eyes into the bowl.

Nick said quietly:

“A complicated right: not to count on it, but not to refuse.”

“That you can write down,” Eva said to David.

David shook his head.

“No.”

“Why?”

He looked at the bowl.

“I’m eating for now.”

Nick lifted his spoon a little higher.

“I consider this a great turn in written culture.”

“Eat,” Eva said.

“Yes.”

And he ate.

Outside the window the rain grew quieter. It did not end, but shifted into small work. The boards of the porch darkened. The tracks Matti had left by the entrance had already been wiped. The tracks by the steps, where the boars had shoved the basket, remained outside. No one could see them from the kitchen. But Sofia knew they were there.

When the bowls were almost empty, Lina stood and added more for those who had almost nothing left. Not equally to everyone. For Nora — more water and one slice of potato. For Thomas — a little grain. For Nick — almost only liquid.

Nick looked into the bowl.

“I see my share has become philosophical.”

“You had a cucumber,” Eva said.

“The cucumber was a personality. That doesn’t count.”

“Eat.”

He ate.

Thomas suddenly took the plate with bread and moved it closer to the middle.

“One piece left.”

Everyone looked.

The piece was small. Thin. With crust on one edge.

“Cut it?” Thomas asked.

Nick said carefully:

“Now we will learn whether bread can withstand justice.”

Thomas took the knife.

Then put it down.

“Who wants it?”

Nick raised his hand.

Then lowered it.

“I wanted it until you asked.”

Eva looked at the bread.

“Leave it.”

“For whom?”

“For morning.”

Thomas looked at her.

“That isn’t a person anymore.”

“All the more reason it won’t argue.”

Lina covered the piece of bread with a towel.

And the question ended.

Not because it had been resolved fairly.

Because it had been put off to where it could survive the night.

Later, when the table had been cleared, Sofia went out into the entryway. The basket stood by the wall, turned on its side so it could dry out. Beside it lay the paper bag from the potatoes. Empty. A dark trace from the wet bottom remained on it, and a little earth.

She picked up the bag.

Wanted to throw it away.

Then folded it in half and put it on the edge of the shelf. Not because it was needed. Simply because her hand did not want to send it to the trash at once. It still held the smell of raw potato skin, the road, and someone else’s “there was extra.”

From the kitchen came Nick’s voice:

“Has anyone seen my dignity?”

“Under the bench,” Eva said.

“Thank you. It’s used to being there.”

Sofia smiled.

Quietly.

So as not to make an event out of it.

Then she looked at the door. Behind it were rain, the porch, tracks, the empty place of the fish, the broken piece of mushroom stem she had not picked up. None of it had disappeared.

But on the shelf lay the wet paper bag.

And for some reason it too could not be crossed out.

By evening the soup was still thin.

But in every bowl something floated.
